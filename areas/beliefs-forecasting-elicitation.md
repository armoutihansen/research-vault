## Scope

This area covers the eliciting and evaluating of subjective beliefs and probabilistic forecasts: how a report that is supposed to *be* a belief gets paid for, recovered, and trusted. Its three child topics trace a single arc. It starts with the decision-theoretic foundations — which functionals of a distribution can be elicited at all, and which payment rules make truth-telling optimal (proper scoring rules and elicitability, via convex/Bregman duality). It then asks how those rules survive contact with a real agent in the lab, where risk attitude, ambiguity, and limited comprehension break the risk-neutral premise the theory rests on. And it ends at the foundational worry underneath both — whether there is a coherent, format-independent subjective probability in the head to be elicited in the first place. The area's predictive content hinges on a primitive (a true latent belief) that the cognitive end of the literature is not sure exists.

## Sub-areas / themes

- [[scoring-rules-elicitability|Proper Scoring Rules & Elicitability]] — the statistical decision theory of honest reporting: which payment rule $S$ makes truthful reporting of a distribution (propriety) or a single functional $T(F)$ (elicitability) the unique expected-payoff maximizer, unified by convex/Bregman duality (Savage 1971; Gneiting & Raftery 2007; Lambert, Pennock & Shoham 2008; Gneiting 2011), and where the boundary of elicitability falls (CVaR, the mode — Heinrich 2014).
- [[experimental-belief-elicitation|Incentivized Belief Elicitation in Experiments]] — the mechanisms that pay experimental subjects to reveal beliefs and the long argument over their *behavioral* incentive compatibility under risk, ambiguity, and non-EU preferences: risk-correction curves (Offerman et al. 2009), binarization/binary lotteries (Berg et al. 1986; Karni 2009; Hossain & Okui 2013), fixed-prize frequency reports and partial identification (Schlag & Tremewan 2021; the user's Armouti-Hansen2026b), and the deflating horse-races (Danz et al. 2022; Charness et al. 2021).
- [[subjective-expectations-probability-judgment|Subjective Expectations & Probability Judgment]] — whether people *have* coherent subjective probabilities and how to measure them: expectations as identifying data that relax rational-expectations assumptions in choice models (Manski 2004; Delavande et al. 2011), the cognitive-limits case (Hogarth 1975), and format/representation effects (Gigerenzer & Hoffrage 1995; Edwards 1975).
- **Cross-cutting theme — the report is a *map* of the belief, not the belief.** Every topic separates a latent object (a distribution $P$, a functional $T(F)$, a perception $Q$) from the observable *report* $r$, and the action is in the map between them: a scoring rule's expected-score geometry (theory), a risk-distorted reporting curve $R(p)$ or an identified set $P_S(r)$ (lab), and a format-distorting elicitation map $g(Q,\phi)$ (cognition). The whole area is the study of when that map can be inverted.

## Cross-topic tensions

**1. Proper in theory, broken in the lab — the propriety-vs-behavior gap.** This is the area's spine. The scoring-rules topic proves a rule is strictly proper exactly when its expected-score function is strictly convex (Savage 1971; Gneiting & Raftery 2007) — but propriety holds only under risk neutrality and expected-value maximization. The experimental topic spends its whole effort on what happens once that premise fails: Armantier & Treich (2013) show *every* proper rule is biased for *every* risk-averse EU agent, and the sharpest collision is over the field's workhorse — Hossain & Okui (2013) validate the binarized scoring rule as risk-robust while Danz et al. (2022) show that merely *explaining* its (flat) incentives raises misreporting and pulls reports to center. Theoretical incentive compatibility and behavioral incentive compatibility point in opposite directions, and there is a *comprehension–robustness inversion*: the most theoretically robust mechanisms (Karni 2009, the BSR) are exactly the ones subjects understand worst, while the assumption-light frequency report is understood best. This is where the user's own work sits — Armouti-Hansen2026b recasts the frequency/count-rule class as Manski-style partial identification, trading the point estimate the theory wants for a distribution-free identified set $P_S(r)$ that survives any monotone utility, and frequency-report scoring is the candidate the field's negative results (Danz et al. 2022) leave open as a behaviorally incentive-compatible mechanism.

**2. Point identification vs. partial identification — how much to trade for robustness.** The scoring-rules theory delivers exact point recovery of $P$ or $T(F)$; the experimental literature splits over whether to keep it. Offerman et al. (2009), Hossain & Okui (2013), and Harrison et al. (2014) fight to preserve a point estimate (a correction curve, a robustified rule); Schlag & Tremewan (2021) and Armouti-Hansen2026b deliberately abandon it for a set, on the view that the point estimates are spuriously precise once preferences are unknown. This is the same move Manski (2004) makes in the expectations topic when he turns from a point belief $Q$ to elicited *ranges* to capture ambiguity — partial identification is the connective tissue between the lab-mechanism camp and the survey-expectations camp.

**3. Is there a true belief to elicit at all?** This is the foundational tension, and it undercuts the other two. Scoring-rules theory and the lab mechanisms both *assume* a latent $P$ exists and is stable; the subjective-expectations topic disputes the premise. Hogarth (1975) argues humans are limited-capacity processors "ill-suited" to coherent distributional assessment; Edwards (1975) replies the documented biases are artifacts of impoverished elicitation and demands invariance across response modes before any bias is reified; Gigerenzer & Hoffrage (1995) show base-rate neglect dissolves under natural-frequency framing, i.e. the answer is *constructed from* the representation rather than read off a stable internal competence. If the elicited number flips with format $\phi$, then both the scoring rule's "true $P$" and the lab mechanism's "belief behind the report" are partly fictions — there is nothing format-independent for the inverted map to land on. Hogarth (1975) is the bridge paper here: he gives a critical exposition of strictly proper scoring rules ($S(p,p)>S(r,p)$) and warns many are *insensitive* and *presuppose a unique true distribution* — the exact assumption the scoring-rules topic builds on and the experimental topic relies on.

**4. Which format/rule, and on whose authority.** Each topic has its own unresolved "which one" fight, and they conflict across the boundary. Scoring-rules theory cannot anoint a canonical rule — Selten (1998) axiomatizes the quadratic via neutrality while Gneiting & Raftery (2007) privilege the logarithmic via locality. The expectations topic adds an orthogonal axis: Gigerenzer & Hoffrage (1995) say *frequency* formats fit the mind, yet Manski (2004) and Delavande et al. (2011) elicit single-event percent-chance CDFs — the very format Gigerenzer argues is computationally mismatched. The frequency-report mechanism in the experimental topic (Schlag & Tremewan 2021; Armouti-Hansen2026b) is, in effect, the format Gigerenzer's cognitive evidence recommends, married to the partial-identification stance the lab needs for robustness — a convergence the three literatures never make explicit.

## Open questions

- **A mechanism that is informative *and* behaviorally incentive compatible.** Danz et al. (2022) leave open the constructive characterization of an elicitation that is both transparent about its incentives and truth-revealing; bridging the scoring-rules propriety theory to the lab's comprehension constraint is the central unsolved problem. Whether the fixed-prize frequency report (whose incentives are not flat near the optimum and need no compound-lottery reduction) passes Danz et al.'s weak conditions is the live test, and the natural home for the user's frequency-report scoring rules.

- **A partial-identification reading that spans theory, lab, and cognition.** Armouti-Hansen2026b characterizes the identified set for count rules under any monotone utility; the elicitability theory (Gneiting 2011: variance, CVaR, the mode are not directly elicitable) and Manski's (2004) range elicitation are two more partial-identification stories. A unified question: what is point- vs. set-identified about a latent belief when it is observed only through a rule of known propriety *and* a format-distorting map $g(Q,\phi)$ — turning Heinrich's (2014) non-elicitability, Armantier & Treich's (2013) "report is not the belief," and Gigerenzer's format dependence into one bounds problem.

- **Frequency/distribution methods beyond binary, simple events.** Schlag & Tremewan (2021) prove minimal-precision optimality only for $k=2$; Armouti-Hansen2026b shows two natural count losses (Hamming, Chebyshev) resist tractable bounds and no rule dominates across belief regimes — and the elicitability theory says many multivariate functionals are not elicitable at all. A usable, comprehension-friendly *quantile/distribution* elicitation that is both elicitable in Gneiting's sense and behaviorally robust is still missing.

- **Disentangling genuine non-additivity / ambiguity from measurement artifact.** Offerman et al. (2009) recover residual ambiguity attitudes but cannot fully separate them from elicitation bias; Manski (2004) reads the fifty-fifty heap as ambiguity while Gigerenzer's logic reads some of it as a format-induced default. Whether the latent object is a single $Q$, a set of priors, or a format artifact is the same question asked three ways, and ambiguity-robust elicitation is unsolved across all three topics.

- **Does predictive validity license the structural use the theory wants?** Delavande et al. (2011) show elicited moments predict realized behavior; the elicitability theory and the lab mechanisms aim at *cardinal* recovery of $P$. Whether predictive validity justifies the cardinal, structural use (Manski's identification payoff) or only an ordinal/reduced-form reading is open — and connects the foundational scoring theory to whether any of the elaborate truth serums beats non-incentivized introspection (Charness et al. 2021).

## Scope

This area studies how goods, quality information, and advice move from firms to consumers through markets and the vertical chains, intermediaries, and signals that organize them. It runs from the classical IO of vertical relations and quality signaling — double marginalization, dissipative-advertising signals, lemons and certification — to the modern behavioral turn in which firms price against, discriminate on, and steer *fallible* consumers. Its child topics share one organizing margin: a friction in the transmission of price or quality information ($p$ stacked too high, quality hidden, signals jammed, advice biased) and the contracts, institutions, or regulations that resolve or exploit it.

## Sub-areas / themes

The five topics arrange along a spine from supply-side structure to demand-side fallibility.

- **Vertical structure.** [[distribution-channels-vertical-integration|Distribution Channels & Vertical Integration]] — the make-or-buy / sell-through-whom margin: double marginalization, coordinating contracts (two-part tariffs, quantity/all-units discounts), and strategic decentralization that softens downstream competition.
- **Quality information from the firm.** [[advertising-signaling-quality|Advertising & Signaling of Product Quality]] — how experience-good quality reaches buyers via dissipative advertising, introductory price, and (newly) the *jamming* of noisy reviews; [[adverse-selection-intermediation|Adverse Selection, Quality & Intermediation]] — the lemons unraveling and the certifiers, warranties, and expert middlemen that restore or weaponize trade.
- **Demand-side fallibility and its governance.** [[behavioral-io-naivete-attention|Behavioral IO: Naivete, Shrouding & Attention]] — firms pricing against shrouded add-ons, naivete-based discrimination, and attention shocks; [[advice-misselling-disclosure|Advice, Misselling & Consumer Protection]] — commission-driven cheap talk, suitability standards, and the regulation of biased advice. These two are the field's behavioral-IO frontier, where the intermediary that *cures* information frictions in the classical topics becomes the agent that *exploits* them.

## Cross-topic tensions

The field-level fault line is **does an intermediary or institution cure an information friction or weaponize it — and does competition discipline the answer?** Each topic answers locally and they collide. In [[adverse-selection-intermediation|intermediation]] the expert middleman raises welfare when quality is *given* (Biglaiser 1993) but collapses seller effort to $\underline q$ when quality is *chosen under hold-up* (Biglaiser & Li 2018); in [[behavioral-io-naivete-attention|behavioral IO]] the recommender that should add value instead steers on mistakes and harms *unboundedly*; in [[advice-misselling-disclosure|advice]] the very institution being studied (commissions, refunds) *generates* the adviser's bias. Competition flips sign across topics too: it *softens* in vertical channels (the McGuire–Staelin reversal where decentralization is a strategic asset, not a coordination failure), it can *raise* manipulation in balanced advertising races (Grunewald & Kräkel), and it merely *redistributes* a market-power-invariant deadweight loss under naivete-based discrimination (Heidhues & Kőszegi).

A second cross-topic tension is **whether transparency and disclosure help**. The Grossman–Milgrom unraveling logic that underwrites [[adverse-selection-intermediation|certification]] predicts full disclosure (and would make certifiers redundant), yet [[advice-misselling-disclosure|advice]] finds mandated disclosure can *lower* welfare and [[behavioral-io-naivete-attention|behavioral IO]] finds no firm unshrouds and soft information remedies backfire — while *uninformative* salience reminders durably help. So the field disagrees on whether the binding primitive is information (suppressible) or attention (replenishable).

A third, methodological tension cuts across all five: **a rational-Bayesian benchmark versus fallible consumers**. The vertical-channel and classical-signaling topics assume sophisticated buyers who perfectly discount anticipated bias; the behavioral-IO and advice topics make naivete, forgetting, credulity, and limited attention load-bearing — and concede these primitives are often *not identified* from rational alternatives (search costs, preference heterogeneity) using market data alone.

Finally, **functional form and contract space are pervasively load-bearing**: linear-wholesale-price results (channel softening, "exit hurts the survivor") flip under two-part tariffs, and demand-form rankings reverse (linear vs. constant-elasticity), echoing the regime-dependence of the certification and discrimination results.

## Open questions

- **Sign the welfare of intermediation and competition.** Across [[adverse-selection-intermediation|intermediation]], [[behavioral-io-naivete-attention|behavioral IO]], and [[advice-misselling-disclosure|advice]]: when does adding or sharpening an intermediary (certifier, recommender, commissioned adviser) raise welfare versus enable hold-up, steering, or misselling — and should entry of such intermediaries be *restricted*?
- **Move past the monopoly / single-dyad / duopoly straitjacket.** Signaling and jamming are solved mostly in monopoly, channels mostly in duopoly; $n>2$ firms, competing certifiers, and multi-brand retail remain open and sometimes overturn the benchmark.
- **Identify behavioral primitives from market data.** Disentangle naivete, forgetting, and credulity from rational explanations, and detect naivete-based discrimination, review manipulation, and vague advice empirically — the field is theory-rich and evidence-poor.
- **Strategic / sophisticated consumers.** What survives once buyers infer their own naivete from suspiciously attractive offers, or select among advisers and intermediaries?
- **Design the right remedy.** Reminders vs. shrouding, refund floors vs. commission caps, disclosure vs. standards-of-proof — which instruments are robust across contract spaces, and which technologies implement *value-based* rather than mistake-based steering?

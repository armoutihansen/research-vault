## Scope

This area covers the descriptive economics of choice when the von Neumann–Morgenstern (vNM) expected-utility (EU) and exponential discounted-utility (DU) benchmarks fail: how people value risk, how their reference point and beliefs shape that valuation, and how they trade off the present against the future. Its five child topics form a single program built around one move — adding a *minimal* psychological ingredient (a reference point, a probability distortion, an anticipatory or self-control term) to a classical benchmark and asking what choice-theoretic footprint it leaves. The topics range from the founding descriptive theory of risky choice, through the question of where the reference point comes from (status quo, expectations, beliefs), to the timing of payments and consumption.

## Sub-areas / themes

- [[prospect-theory-loss-aversion|Prospect Theory, Loss Aversion & Framing]] — the founding descriptive theory: exogenous, status-quo reference dependence, S-shaped value, nonlinear probability weighting, and the field tests of whether the bias survives markets and stakes.
- [[disappointment-regret-nonexpected-utility|Disappointment, Regret & Non-Expected Utility]] — the *minimal-departure* non-EU families (disappointment, rank-dependent/anticipated utility, regret) that rationalize Allais-type anomalies, split between transitive and intransitive mechanisms.
- [[expectations-based-reference-dependence|Expectations-Based Reference Dependence]] — the Kőszegi–Rabin program that *endogenizes* the reference point as recent rational expectations pinned down by a personal-equilibrium fixed point, plus its lab/field tests and revealed-preference foundations.
- [[anticipatory-utility-motivated-beliefs|Anticipatory Utility & Motivated Beliefs]] — belief-as-carrier-of-utility: savoring/dread, optimal expectations, ego utility, and the non-instrumental demand for (or avoidance of) information.
- [[intertemporal-choice-present-bias|Intertemporal Choice & Present Bias]] — the temporal margin: quasi-hyperbolic $(\beta,\delta)$ discounting, sophistication/naivete, commitment demand, and the timing of payments.

## Cross-topic tensions

**1. What is the reference point — and is it even exogenous?** This is the field's spine. [[prospect-theory-loss-aversion|Prospect theory]] leaves it as an unmodelled status quo; [[expectations-based-reference-dependence|the Kőszegi–Rabin program]] makes it endogenous rational expectations; [[disappointment-regret-nonexpected-utility|disappointment theory]] uses the within-act lottery mean; [[anticipatory-utility-motivated-beliefs|motivated-belief models]] let the agent *choose* the reference belief to savor. These are not nested, and the data refuse to anoint a winner: Baillon et al. find status-quo and MaxMin referents dominate over expectations-based ones, while field studies of goals vs. par vs. expectations remain unresolved. The whole area's predictive content hinges on a primitive no topic has pinned down.

**2. Belief distortion vs. probability distortion vs. outcome distortion as the load-bearing mechanism.** A single anomaly can be read as a kink in the value function (loss aversion), a bend in the weighting function ($\pi(p)$, rank-dependent $f$), or a distorted *belief*. The sharpest cross-topic clash is that these are frequently observationally equivalent: monotone Kőszegi–Rabin CPE equals rank-dependent $\cap$ quadratic utility (correct-beliefs-nonstandard-utility $\equiv$ distorted-beliefs-standard-utility), Kőszegi's anticipatory agent is EU-indistinguishable absent self-fulfilling expectations or informational preferences, and on the time margin DeJarnette et al. show the *entire* transitive probability-distortion toolkit cannot accommodate a single risk-averse time-lottery choice. The same data territory keeps admitting incompatible mechanisms.

**3. Transitivity and consistency: descriptively safe, or the thing that breaks?** Regret theory abandons transitivity and menu-independence; disappointment, rank-dependent, and KR models retain a real-valued index and force the anomaly into weighting. Loomes–Sugden's $\omega$-vs-$p$ design and the procedure-invariance debate within prospect theory show the field cannot even agree whether observed reversals are intransitivity, non-invariance, or weighting curvature.

**4. Friction or feature, and whose welfare counts?** Present bias and motivated beliefs are framed as pure welfare losses to be disciplined (commitment devices, debiasing) — yet Fahn–Hakenes show present bias can *raise* cooperation, and Bénabou–Tirole show malleable beliefs can be self-control assets. Worse, Gul–Pesendorfer prove commitment demand is observationally identical to a single-self temptation preference with the *opposite* welfare verdict, paralleling prospect theory's unresolved WTA $\neq$ WTP welfare problem. Positive equivalence, normative divergence, runs across all five topics.

## Open questions

- **A theory of reference-point / referent-belief formation** — which expectations, status quo, goal, or self-chosen belief becomes the referent, and *when* (the "moment of first focus"), is unmodelled across the entire area.
- **Identification under observational equivalence** — designs that separate distorted beliefs from nonstandard utility, transitive disappointment from intransitive regret, present bias from temptation, and probability weighting from loss aversion, since static and finite data collapse them.
- **Whose welfare, and is the bias erodable?** A criterion that survives the multiple-selves / single-self ambiguity, and evidence on whether market experience, stakes, and learning attenuate these phenomena.
- **The timing margin as a stress test** — whether any dynamically consistent non-EU model survives Stochastic Impatience, forcing a choice between relaxing intertemporal independence and abandoning impatience.
- **Belief-utility vs. attention** — whether anticipatory disutility (dread, info-avoidance) is affective or attention-modulated, a channel none of the current optimal-belief or reference-dependence models contain.

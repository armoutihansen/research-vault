## Summary
A short but foundational note arguing that the U.S. Supreme Court was wrong to treat integration as illegal *per se* under the antitrust laws without distinguishing vertical from horizontal integration. Spengler shows that while horizontal integration can raise prices and misallocate resources, vertical integration *as such* does not reduce competition and, in an already-imperfect economy, can actually *intensify* it. The mechanism is what later became known as **double marginalization**: when successive monopolistic stages each add their own markup, the final price is inflated and output suppressed; vertically integrating those stages collapses the stacked markups into a single one, lowering price and raising both profit and consumer surplus. The policy upshot is that vertical integration should be judged under the "rule of reason," and only its horizontal elements singled out for remedy.

## Research question
Does vertical integration necessarily reduce competition and harm consumers, as the *per se* illegality doctrine implies? Or can vertical integration, in an imperfectly competitive economy, improve resource allocation and lower prices?

## Method / identification
A stylized partial-equilibrium model of a product passing through three successive production stages $A$, $B$, $C$ before reaching consumers. Variable agents are supplied perfectly elastically, marginal cost equals average variable cost at every relevant output, and non-variable/entrepreneurial assets earn a minimum necessary "profit" per unit. Let $P_a, P_b, P_c$ be selling prices and $V_a, V_b, V_c$ be per-unit variable costs at each stage.

**Benchmark (Section I) — pure competition at every stage:** the final price is just cumulated variable cost plus the cumulated minimum necessary profit, $P_c = V_a + V_b + V_c + \text{(minimum profit)}$. There is no private incentive to integrate, since integration changes neither unit cost nor attainable profit; integration is a matter of indifference.

**Successive monopoly (Section II) — horizontal integration within each stage:** each stage acquires enough market power that its firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve $D_a, D_b, D_c$ and sets a profit-maximizing markup. Each downstream stage treats the upstream price as its input cost: $C_b = P_a + V_b$ and $C_c = P_b + V_c$. Each firm sets marginal revenue equal to marginal cost, so $P_a = C_a + R_a$, $P_b = C_b + R_b$, $P_c = C_c + R_c$, where $R_a, R_b, R_c$ are the realized per-unit profits. The successive markups *cascade* into the final price.

**Vertical integration (Section III):** a single firm spanning $A, B, C$ maximizes total return above variable outlay. Its true marginal cost at the end of stage $C$ is $C' = V_a + V_b + V_c$ (the stacked intermediate markups vanish as internal transfers). It now applies a *single* markup over $C'$, lowering the final price and expanding output.

Spengler also gives an elasticity argument (footnote): with $p = c\,e/(e-1)$ where $e$ is demand elasticity, any cost increment $\Delta c$ (e.g. a monopolistic surcharge) is accompanied by a proportionally *greater* rise $\Delta e$ in elasticity, guaranteeing that the later-stage demand is elastic enough for vertical integration to reduce price.

## Key findings
- **Indifference under competition (Section I):** with perfect competition at each stage, vertical integration is neither cost-reducing nor profit-enhancing; producers and consumers are indifferent to it.
- **Successive-monopoly / double-marginalization result (Section II):** with horizontal market power at each stage, markups stack. In the worked example, $P_a = 30$ (cost 20, profit 10), $P_b = 70$ (cost 50, profit 20), $P_c = 115$ (cost 90, profit 25); output is only $Q = 40$, well below the competitive $Q'' = 128$ that would obtain at price $C' = 60$.
- **Vertical-integration welfare gain (Section III):** integrating the monopolized stages lets the firm price off true marginal cost $M' = C' = 60$. It cuts the final price from $P_c = 115$ to $P' = 100$, expands output from $Q = 40$ to $Q' = 64$, raises aggregate "profit" (return above variable expense) from 2,200 to 2,560, and *also* raises Marshallian consumers' surplus by 780. Both producer and consumer gain.
- **Elasticity guarantee:** within the model the later-stage demand is always elastic enough at the profit-maximizing price that vertical integration lowers price; the greater the upstream "monopolistic" surcharges and the higher later-stage variable cost, the more elastic the integrated firm's demand and the larger the price cut.
- **Rent/surcharge evasion (Section IIIb):** vertical integration lets the high-stage producer evade rent-like monopolistic surcharges — and also "nuisance" taxes, fair-trade laws, farm price supports, and turnover taxes levied at ownership-transfer points — pushing prices toward the ideal.
- **Policy conclusion (Section IV):** competition is *horizontal* in character; vertical integration does not, as such, suppress competition and should be assessed under the rule of reason, with only its horizontal elements treated as potentially restraining trade.

## Contribution
This is the canonical statement of the **double-marginalization** (successive-monopoly) problem and the efficiency case for vertical integration: when an upstream and a downstream monopolist each impose a markup, integration internalizes the vertical externality, lowering price and raising welfare. It supplies the economic-analysis foundation for treating vertical integration under the rule of reason rather than as illegal *per se*, and it anticipates the later transaction-cost and vertical-restraints literatures.

## Limitations & open questions
- The model **rules out economies of scale** by assumption, calling them "irrelevant to the main problem"; the interaction of scale economies with the markup-stacking story is left open (and Spengler notes a rent-evasion-plus-scale combination could overturn the output comparison with pure competition).
- Constant marginal (= average variable) cost and fixed input coefficients are assumed throughout; with substitutable inputs or non-constant costs the clean result may weaken.
- Spengler concedes the model "implies a degree of ease of entry that may appear inconsistent" with the market-power assumptions, flagging an internal tension between free entry and persistent supracompetitive markups.
- Whether the integrated firm's *aggregate* surcharge is larger or smaller relative to entrepreneurial investment "turns on ease of entry, aggressiveness of selling, etc." — left indeterminate.
- He notes transfer prices inside an integrated firm "may be too high" relative to costs, but treats mispriced internal transfers only informally — an opening for the later internal-pricing / transfer-pricing literature.

## Connections
The successive-monopoly mechanism builds directly on the imperfect-competition apparatus of Robinson (1933, *Economics of Imperfect Competition*) and Chamberlin (1938, *Theory of Monopolistic Competition*), both cited for marginal-revenue pricing and rent evasion. The antitrust framing engages Adelman (1949, "Integration and the Antitrust Laws" and "The A & P Case"), Spengler's immediate foil on the *per se* doctrine. The paper is the origin point for the modern double-marginalization literature and the economics of vertical restraints and vertical integration, later formalized in industrial-organization treatments such as [[@Tirole1988|Tirole (1988)]] and Williamson's transaction-cost analysis of vertical integration. Its "rule of reason" conclusion connects to the broader Chicago-school antitrust tradition that vertical arrangements are typically efficiency-enhancing rather than anticompetitive.

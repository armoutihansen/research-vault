> [!abstract] Abstract
> Collective choices are often cyclic and cannot be rationalized by maximization of a single linear ordering. In this paper, we identify conditions under which collective choices, potentially cyclic, can be rationalized by extensive games with perfect information.

## Summary
Xu and Zhou recast revealed-preference rationalizability for *collective* choice. A single decision-maker's choices must be acyclic to be rationalized by one linear order, but groups routinely produce cyclic choices. The authors ask when an observed (single-valued) choice function on a finite set can instead be rationalized as the subgame-perfect-equilibrium outcome of some extensive game with perfect information (a "game tree") in which each alternative is a terminal node exactly once. Their main theorem gives a clean necessary-and-sufficient characterization in terms of two axioms: **weak separability** (a restricted path-independence condition) and **divergence consistency** (a coherence condition on intertwined 3-cycles).

## Research question
Under what conditions can a collective choice function $C$ on a finite set $X$ â€” possibly exhibiting cycles â€” be rationalized by a game tree, i.e. recovered as the SPNE outcome of an extensive game of perfect information whose terminal nodes are the alternatives, with each player holding a fixed linear ordering over $X$? The setting targets opaque institutions where membership and decision procedures are unobserved ("behind closed doors"), so only the aggregate choice function is available.

## Method / identification
Pure axiomatic / revealed-preference theory. $X$ is finite; $C:2^X\setminus\{\varnothing\}\to X$ with $C(A)\in A$. A *game tree* is a pair $(G;R)$: a tree $G$ whose terminal nodes are in one-to-one correspondence with $X$ (each alternative appears once), plus a preference profile $R=(R_i)$ of linear orderings over $X$. For $A\subseteq X$, $G\mid A$ is the reduced tree retaining only paths to terminal nodes in $A$. $C$ is **rationalized by $(G;R)$** iff
$$C(A)=\mathrm{SPNE}(G\mid A;R)\quad\text{for all }A\subseteq X,\ A\neq\varnothing.$$
The authors first derive *necessary* conditions from this representation. Splitting $G$ at the root partitions $X$ into $Y,Z$, forcing $C(Y\cup Z)=C(\{C(Y),C(Z)\})$; iterating over subsets yields **weak separability**: for any $A$ with $|A|>1$ there is a non-degenerate partition $A=B\cup D$ such that $C(S\cup T)=C(\{C(S),C(T)\})$ for all $\varnothing\neq S\subseteq B$, $\varnothing\neq T\subseteq D$. They then analyze cyclic structure: $x,y,z$ form a **3-cycle** if $C(\{x,y\})=x$, $C(\{y,z\})=y$, $C(\{z,x\})=z$; $x$ **diverges before** $y,z$ if additionally $C(\{x,y,z\})=x$. Each 3-cycle pins down a unique branch shape (one player ranks $x$ between $y,z$; another ranks $y,z$ oppositely). Overlapping 3-cycles must cohere, giving **divergence consistency**: for $x_1,x_2,y_1,y_2$, if $x_1$ diverges before $y_1,y_2$ and $y_1$ diverges before $x_1,x_2$, then $C(\{x_1,y_1\})=x_1$ iff $C(\{x_2,y_2\})=y_2$. Sufficiency is proved by induction on $|X|$: weak separability gives a partition $X_1,X_2$ with inductively-constructed trees $G_1,G_2$; a new root player 1's pairwise preferences across $X_1\times X_2$ are shown (via divergence consistency, ruling out four subcases of a hypothetical 2-cycle) to be acyclic, hence extendable to a linear order, completing the tree.

## Data
None â€” this is a theoretical / axiomatic paper with no empirical data. Illustration is by small worked examples (a two-player, three-alternative free-agent-signing story, and counterexamples on $X=\{x_1,x_2,y_1,y_2\}$).

## Key findings
- **Proposition.** If $C$ satisfies weak separability and is acyclic, then $C$ is rationalizable by a single linear ordering on $X$, and conversely. (Defining $xR_Cy\iff C(\{x,y\})=x$ gives a complete, reflexive, antisymmetric relation that acyclicity makes transitive.) This locates weak separability as the cyclic-tolerant relaxation of the classical condition â€” strong separability (the same identity for *all* partitions) characterizes single-linear-order rationalizability.
- **Main Theorem.** $C$ is rationalizable by a game tree if and only if it satisfies **weak separability** and **divergence consistency**. Necessity is shown directly from the SPNE representation; sufficiency by induction with at most $|X|-1$ players and $|X|-1$ non-terminal nodes.
- **Independence of the axioms** (Examples 1, 2): for $|X|>3$, weak separability and divergence consistency are logically independent â€” Example 1 satisfies weak separability but violates divergence consistency; Example 2 satisfies divergence consistency (no 3-cycles) but violates weak separability.
- A 3-cycle's game-tree branch is **unique up to relabeling** of players and terminal nodes.

## Contribution
Extends the revealed-preference program (Houthakker, Arrow, Richter, Sen) from individual rationality to collective choice by allowing the rationalizing object to be a *multi-player extensive game* rather than a single utility maximization. This is strictly more permissive â€” it accommodates cyclic choice â€” yet still falls short of vacuity: the two-axiom characterization shows game-tree rationalizability is a genuine, testable restriction. It connects Plott's path independence (of which weak separability is a restricted form) to game-theoretic foundations of social choice.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems, each a hook for further work:
- **Each alternative once:** the model requires every alternative to be a terminal node exactly once, excluding interesting games (e.g. voting-by-veto). Relaxing to allow repeated terminal labels â€” for three alternatives *any* choice function becomes rationalizable â€” but the general case with **four or more alternatives** has "no satisfactory answer."
- **Single-valued only:** $C(A)$ is a singleton. Extending to multi-valued choice *correspondences* is open and hard, because some correspondences admit essentially different game-tree representations.
- **Minimal number of players:** $|X|-1$ players suffice but need not be minimal; for $|X|=3,4$ at most 2 players are needed. The tightest upper bound is unknown, and fixing the number of players a priori is described as "much more difficult."

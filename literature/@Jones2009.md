> [!abstract] Abstract
> An overview of existing data on imitation in infancy suggests that changes in the direction of imitation research are underway. The widely accepted view that newborn infants imitate lacks supporting evidence. Instead, existing data suggest that infants do not imitate others until their second year, and that imitation of different kinds of behaviour emerges at different ages. The evidence is consistent with a dynamic systems account in which the ability to imitate is not an inherited, specialized module, but is instead the emergent product of a system of social, cognitive and motor components, each with its own developmental history.

## Summary

A narrative review (Royal Society *Phil. Trans. B* theme issue) that reassesses the entire empirical record on infant imitation. Jones argues that the celebrated claim of neonatal imitation, originating with Meltzoff & Moore (1977), is not supported by compelling evidence. The one behaviour newborns reliably "match" -- tongue protrusion -- is better explained as a generic arousal/interest response than as imitation. Pulling together developmental data, she contends that genuine voluntary behavioural matching does not appear until the second year and that different behaviours come online at different ages. She uses this pattern to reject preformationist accounts (innate imitation module, inherited "mirror system") in favour of a **dynamic systems** account in which imitation is the emergent product of separately maturing social, cognitive and motor components.

## Research question

When and how do human infants begin to imitate -- i.e. to voluntarily match the behaviours of others -- across the first two years of life, and which theoretical account of the origins of imitation (a nativist/preformationist module versus an emergent dynamic-systems process) does the evidence support?

## Method / identification

This is a critical literature review, not an experiment, so "identification" is conceptual rather than statistical. The core methodological move is an **interpretive disambiguation**: Jones insists that imitation is never directly observed; what is observed is behavioural matching, which has many possible causes (citing Want & Harris 2002). The review therefore evaluates whether reported matches license an imitation interpretation or are equally consistent with simpler mechanisms -- arousal, chance, stimulus enhancement, or *emulation learning* (matching object affordances rather than actions, following Tomasello 1998).

Several analytic criteria recur:
- **Selective matching of two distinct behaviours** is treated as the evidential bar for ruling out diffuse arousal (the original Meltzoff & Moore logic); Jones shows most studies fail it, with only tongue protrusion reliably matched (Anisfeld's 1996 meta-analysis of 35 studies).
- **Confounds** are dissected case by case: "sidedness" of tongue protrusion confounded with amplitude (hence with rising arousal); head-rotation scored merely as crossing the midline; forehead-touch tasks scored generously (within 10 cm) so that mouthing or looking is miscounted as imitation.
- For the cognitively rich "rational imitation" findings, Jones applies the test of whether *enabling relations* or *intentional vs accidental* contrasts could be produced by non-imitative social learning (Huang et al. 2002) or by moment-to-moment attentional capture.

She also reviews the two mechanistic theories on offer -- Meltzoff & Moore's **Active Intermodal Matching (AIM)** model (supramodal representation + equivalence detector comparing proprioceptive feedback) and the **human mirror system (HMS)** account -- and assesses their psychological reality and developmental plausibility.

## Data

None original. The paper synthesizes existing published studies: newborn imitation experiments and replication failures; non-human primate neonatal "imitation" in chimpanzees (Myowa-Yamakoshi et al. 2004) and rhesus macaques (Ferrari et al. 2006); the author's own prior empirical work showing tongue protrusion to non-tongue stimuli (Jones 1996, 2006, 2007); deferred-imitation and rational-imitation studies; and the AIM/mirror-neuron theoretical literature.

## Key findings

- **No compelling evidence for newborn imitation.** Across studies, only tongue protrusion is reliably matched; Anisfeld's (1996) meta-analysis finds no reliable matching of any other behaviour, including mouth opening. Claims of matching pouting, sequential finger movements, head rotation, and directional tongue protrusion have not replicated.
- **An arousal account fits the tongue-protrusion data.** Newborns protrude their tongues in response to flashing lights, looming objects, an opening/closing box, and even music (the *Barber of Seville* overture in Jones 2006) -- stimuli with no tongue to imitate. Tongue protrusion plausibly reflects oral exploration/arousal and disappears once directed reaching emerges (Jones 1996). Gender differences in finger-movement matching co-vary with heart-rate arousal (Nagy et al. 2007).
- **Primate "neonatal imitation" is transient and anomalous.** Matching occurs only in a brief early window (e.g. macaques on day 3 only) and only for mouth behaviours, despite adults of these species being poor imitators -- inconsistent with an inherited imitation module.
- **Mechanistic theories are unsupported.** AIM lacks empirical confirmation (e.g. Maurer et al. 1999 failed to replicate Meltzoff & Borton 1979 intermodal transfer). The HMS account requires monkey-derived mirror neurons that respond to *goal-directed, meaningful* actions, yet newborns match meaningless simple movements and show no action understanding; visual immaturity (Aslin 2001) further undermines feasibility.
- **Genuine imitation emerges in the second year, staggered by behaviour type.** Direct studies (Masur 1998; Nielsen & Dissanayake 2004; Jones 2007) find little matching before ~12-18 months; in Jones (2007) the two "newborn" behaviours met an imitation criterion only at 16 and 18 months. Much "deferred imitation" is better read as emulation (Barr et al. 1996; Bauer et al. 2001; enabling-relation effects in Wenner & Bauer 1999).
- **"Rational imitation" claims are underdetermined.** Findings that 12-14-month-olds imitate selectively based on a model's constraints (Gergely et al. 2002; Schwier et al. 2006) attribute long inferential chains to infants that simpler attentional accounts can reproduce.

## Contribution

The paper consolidates scattered critiques into a single, sustained case against the dominant nativist narrative and reframes infant imitation as a developmental-systems phenomenon. It supplies a methodological lens -- treat behavioural matching as the explanandum, demand that imitation out-compete arousal, chance, and emulation -- and an explicit alternative theoretical commitment (Thelen & Smith 1994; Gottlieb 2007) in which imitation is not an inherited module but the emergent product of components, each with its own developmental history, that must be separately characterized.

## Limitations & open questions

The author flags numerous open problems that double as research hooks:
- The descriptive developmental record is **patchy and mutually inconsistent**: some studies show "rational" imitation at 12-14 months, others show failure to imitate simple acts well beyond that age. Designs that resolve these discrepancies are needed.
- **Mechanism is under-specified on both sides.** There is little empirical work testing the proposed mechanisms (AIM, mirror system) behind reported matching.
- For rational-imitation studies, **replications with richer behavioural reporting** are needed to establish whether infants differ in probability of imitating versus merely in looking/mouthing, and to rule out attentional confounds (e.g. the door-opening cue in Schwier et al. 2006).
- Even granting constrained-action effects, one must **explain why** infants would construct the specific inference chains attributed to them (e.g. inferring the model could not free a hand) rather than alternative inferences, and why imitation would track only unforced actions.
- A comparative agenda: characterize how newborn chimpanzees and macaques respond to arousing stimuli and what role mouth movements play in their lives, to test whether their matching needs an imitation mechanism at all.

It leans heavily on Anisfeld's (1996, 2005) meta-analytic critique and the author's own arousal studies (Jones 1996, 2006, 2007).

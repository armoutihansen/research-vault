> [!abstract] Abstract
> Subjective beliefs are crucial for economic inference, yet behavior can challenge the elicitation. We propose that belief elicitation should be incentive compatible not only theoretically but also in a de facto behavioral sense. To demonstrate, we show that the binarized scoring rule, a state-of-the-art elicitation, violates two weak conditions for behavioral incentive compatibility: (i) within the elicitation, information on the incentives increases deviations from truthful reporting; and (ii) in a pure choice over the set of incentives, most deviate from the theorized maximizer. Moreover, we document that deviations are systematic and center-biased, and that the elicited beliefs substantially distort inference. (JEL D83, D91)

## Summary
The paper argues that a belief-elicitation mechanism being theoretically incentive compatible is not enough: it must also be *behaviorally* incentive compatible. The authors target the binarized scoring rule (BSR) of [[@Hossain2013|Hossain and Okui (2013)]], which is theoretically truth-revealing for any expected-utility maximizer — indeed for anyone who maximizes the probability of winning a fixed prize — because each report maps to a pair of state-contingent lotteries whose stochastic-dominance ranking makes truth-telling a dominant strategy. Across a sequence of lab experiments, the authors show that providing participants with clear quantitative information about the BSR incentives systematically *increases* false reports and pulls them toward the center of the probability scale, and that these distortions materially change downstream inference (e.g., wiping out a measured gender gap in confidence). ## Research question
Does a theoretically incentive-compatible belief elicitation actually elicit truthful beliefs in practice? Concretely: does giving participants detailed information about the BSR's incentives move reports toward, or away from, the true (induced) probability — and what is the consequence for the economic inferences researchers draw from elicited beliefs?

## Method / identification
The core design is an induced-prior elicitation where the truth is known to the analyst, removing the usual identification problem that subjective beliefs are unobserved. Participants guess the chance that a randomly selected urn is the "red" urn, with a known objective prior $\pi_0$. Under the BSR, a report $q$ on red is incentivized by a state-contingent lottery pair: a $1-(1-q)^2$ chance of an \$8 prize if red, and a $1-q^2$ chance if blue. Because the chance of winning is maximized at $q=\pi_0$, any deviation from $\pi_0$ is labeled a *false report*; crucially, the cost of deviating is tiny (roughly a 1-percentage-point loss in win probability per 10-point misreport), so the incentives are nearly flat near the optimum.

Identification of *behavioral* incentive (in)compatibility rests on a between- and within-subject comparison that holds the mechanism fixed and varies only information about its incentives:
- **Information** (baseline): dominance statement + verbal rule + live on-screen win probabilities for each provisional report + end-of-period feedback.
- **No-Information**: only the qualitative dominance statement; no quantitative detail.
- **RCL**: Information plus a compound-lottery-reduction calculator.
- **Feedback**: starts like No-Information, then gradually reveals incentives via end-of-round feedback (a within-subject dose-response).
- **Description**: No-Information plus a nonquantitative description of the payment rule.
- **Incentives-Only**: the BSR lottery pairs presented as a direct choice, stripped of the elicitation framing, to test whether subjects pick the assumed unique maximizer.

For inference, they formalize *center bias* as a measurement model: the elicited belief $q_i$ equals a fixed center value $c$ with probability $\alpha$ and the true belief $q_i^\star$ with probability $1-\alpha$. They then derive the asymptotic OLS bias when $q_i$ enters a regression as a dependent vs. independent variable, and test the predictions by replicating Niederle and Vesterlund (2007) under Information vs. No-Information.

## Data
Experimental data from the Pittsburgh Experimental Economics Laboratory (PEEL) and an online replication. Each treatment used three sessions of about 20 participants; each session ran ten periods with three sequential guesses (prior plus two Bayesian posteriors after urn draws), yielding 30 elicitations per subject. Sessions averaged 71 minutes and \$20.08 in earnings. End-of-experiment measures included risk-attitude price lists and a cognitive reflection test (Frederick 2005). The NV replication recruited 147 students at UC Santa Barbara (142 identifying as male/female).

## Key findings
- **Information increases false reports.** In the baseline Information treatment, 41.5% of prior reports are false, with no time trend; 85% of participants misreport at least once. Only 15% consistently report the induced prior under Information vs. 50% under No-Information.
- **Distortions are center-biased.** False reports are more likely for noncentered priors and *pull to center*. This bias vanishes under No-Information, so the distortion is caused by the incentive information, not task confusion.
- **The RCL calculator helps but does not fix it**: it removes the pull-to-center but false reports remain frequent and concentrated on noncentered priors — pointing to flat incentives, not just compound-lottery confusion.
- **Dose-response (Feedback).** False reporting starts low (No-Information level) and rises toward the Information level as incentive information accumulates, confirming causality within subject.
- **Posteriors (guesses 2–3) replicate the same patterns.**
- **Direct choice fails too (Incentives-Only).** Most participants do *not* select the lottery pair assumed to be the unique BSR maximizer, with deviations again toward the center — independent evidence the incentives themselves are the root cause.
- **Inference is distorted.** Under the center-bias model, when beliefs are the dependent variable the gender-gap coefficient is attenuated to $(1-\alpha)\,\delta_q$; when beliefs are a control, the asymptotic bias scales with $\beta_q\cdot\delta_q$. In the NV replication, NV-No-Information reproduces the original finding (men more confident; confidence explains the entry gap), while NV-Information shows *no* gender gap in confidence and confidence failing to explain the entry gap — a qualitatively different, policy-relevant conclusion.
- **Diagnostic generality.** The Information vs. No-Information comparison also flags the quadratic scoring rule (QSR), and a Description treatment (qualitative rule only) recovers near-No-Information accuracy.

## Contribution
Introduces *behavioral incentive compatibility* as a design criterion distinct from theoretical incentive compatibility, and operationalizes it through two weak, testable conditions: (i) more incentive information should raise truth-telling, and (ii) in a pure choice over the incentive set, most agents should pick the theorized maximizer. It documents that the field-standard BSR fails both, that the failure is systematic (center bias) rather than noise, and that it can overturn substantive empirical conclusions. The Information–No-Information comparison is offered as a portable diagnostic for any elicitation.

## Limitations & open questions
- The "truth" benchmark is an *objective induced prior*; for genuinely subjective beliefs the true value is unobserved, so the magnitude of bias in field applications is inferred (via the NV replication and Table 3 audit) rather than directly measured.
- The mechanism behind center bias is not fully pinned down: flat incentives, hedging motives, and compound-lottery confusion are all consistent with the evidence, and the RCL result only partially disentangles them. A structural model separating these channels remains open.
- The proposed remedies are unsatisfying: a fully black-box (No-Information) elicitation conflicts with the philosophy of transparent incentivized experiments, and Description leaves it unclear whether subjects actually comprehend the incentives. What an elicitation that is both informative *and* behaviorally incentive compatible looks like is left open.
- No characterization is given of which preference/cognitive types satisfy behavioral incentive compatibility, nor a constructive mechanism-design result delivering it.

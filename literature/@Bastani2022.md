> [!abstract] Abstract
> Workers spend a significant amount of time learning how to make good decisions. Evaluating the efficacy of a given decision, however, can be complicated -- e.g., decision outcomes are often long-term and relate to the original decision in complex ways. Surprisingly, even though learning good decision-making strategies is difficult, they can often be expressed in simple and concise forms. Focusing on sequential decision-making, we design a novel machine learning algorithm that is capable of extracting "best practices" from trace data and conveying its insights to humans in the form of interpretable "tips". Our algorithm selects the tip that best bridges the gap between the actions taken by the human workers and those taken by the optimal policy in a way that accounts for which actions are consequential for achieving higher performance. We evaluate our approach through a series of randomized controlled experiments where participants manage a virtual kitchen. Our experiments show that the tips generated by our algorithm can significantly improve human performance relative to intuitive baselines. In addition, we discuss a number of empirical insights that can help inform the design of algorithms intended for human-AI interfaces. For instance, we find evidence that participants do not simply blindly follow our tips; instead, they combine them with their own experience to discover additional strategies for improving performance.

## Summary
The paper proposes an interpretable-RL pipeline that mines human trace data to produce a single, concise, if-then "tip" that, if adopted, maximally improves a worker's long-run performance at a sequential decision task. The key conceptual move is to interpret *the discrepancy between the human policy and the optimal policy* — weighted by how consequential each action is for cumulative reward — rather than to interpret a black-box policy itself. The method is validated in large pre-registered RCTs (N ≈ 2,800) on a virtual Overcooked-style kitchen-management game on Amazon Mechanical Turk. Algorithmic tips beat no-tip, human-crowdsourced, and naive-imitation baselines, and uniquely let participants reach the optimal policy in a hard "disrupted" scenario. Notably, participants do not blindly comply; they fold the tip into their own learning and recover additional unshown optimal rules.

## Research question
Can machine learning extract "best practices" from logged human trace data and convey them to workers — as interpretable rules — in a way that actually improves their performance at sequential decision-making tasks (where current actions affect future rewards in non-obvious ways)? Two sub-questions: (1) can the algorithm identify *useful* tips, and (2) can humans *understand and operationalize* them?

## Method / identification
The setting is an undiscounted finite-horizon MDP $M=(S,A,R,P)$ with horizon $T$. The human is a policy $\pi_H$ mapping states to (possibly random) actions; the optimal policy is $\pi^*=\arg\max_\pi J(\pi)$ where $J(\pi)=\mathbb{E}_{\zeta\sim D(\pi)}\left[\sum_{t=1}^{T} r_t\right]$. A **tip** is a single interpretable rule $\rho=(\psi,a)$: "if predicate $\psi(s)$ holds, take action $a$; otherwise follow $\pi_H$." The induced policy is $\pi_H\oplus\rho$. The goal is $\rho^*=\arg\max_\rho J(\pi_H\oplus\rho)$.

The estimation problem is that showing a tip shifts the state-visitation distribution from $D(\pi_H)$ to $D(\pi_H\oplus\rho)$, which is unobserved. The authors invoke Lemma 1 (from Bastani et al. 2018), a performance-difference decomposition using the optimal value/Q-functions:
$$J(\pi^*) - J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_{\zeta\sim D(\pi)}\left[\sum_{t=1}^{T} V_t^*(s_t) - Q_t^*(s_t, \pi(s_t))\right].$$
Differencing this for $\pi_H$ and $\pi_H\oplus\rho$ and assuming the marginal state distributions are approximately equal ($\bar{D}_t^{(\pi_H)}\approx\bar{D}_t^{(\pi_H\oplus\rho)}$ — i.e., the indirect distribution-shift effect is small relative to the direct action-change effect) yields the estimable objective
$$\hat\rho = \arg\max_\rho \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k}\sum_{t=1}^{T} Q^*(s_{i,t}, (a_{i,t}\oplus\rho)(s_{i,t})),$$
evaluated over observed human rollouts $\zeta_1,\dots,\zeta_k\sim D(\pi_H)$. Crucially, weighting by $Q^*$ prioritizes *consequential* actions, distinguishing this from naive imitation.

Implementation: $\pi^*$ is learned via policy gradient (a 50-hidden-unit neural net, 10,000 SGD steps, lr 0.001); the Q-function is estimated with a random forest $\hat{Q}$ over featurized states. Tips are restricted to (order, subtask, worker) triples, then post-processed by aggregating over orders into human-readable forms ("assign cooking to chef 2 times"). Candidate tips firing in <10% of states, or disagreeing with $\pi^*$ on >50% of states, are pruned; training is restricted to the bottom-25%-performing humans. The **baseline** competitor replaces $Q^*$ with a visit-count $C^*(s,a)$, i.e., it imitates the optimal policy's frequent state-action pairs without regard to consequence.

Experimental design: pre-registered RCT, two phases. Phase I collects tip-free trace data and elicits a crowd-voted "human tip"; Phase II randomizes new participants into four arms — control (no tip), algorithm, human, baseline — and measures completion time and the fraction reaching the optimal policy. Two configurations: "normal" (3 fully-staffed rounds) and "disrupted" (2 fully-staffed then 4 understaffed rounds, after the chef leaves), the latter forcing participants to un-learn an acquired strategy.

## Data
Human trace data from a custom virtual kitchen-management game (Overcooked-inspired). Phase I: 183 (normal) + 172 (disrupted) AMT participants. Phase II: 1,317 (normal) + 1,011 (disrupted) AMT participants, totaling ~2,800. Workers have deterministic heterogeneous subtask times (e.g., chef chops in 1 tick but plates in 6; server chops in 3, cooks in 12, plates in 1). Optimal completion is 20 ticks (fully-staffed) and 34 ticks (understaffed). Flat participation pay plus tiered performance bonus ($0.15–$1.25/round). IRB-approved; pre-registration on AsPredicted.

## Key findings
- **Algorithmic tips substantially improve performance.** Normal: "Chef should never plate" → 22.5 ticks final round vs control/human/baseline, all significant ($p<10^{-4}$ vs control), and speeds learning by ~1 round; 35% reach optimal vs 24–29%. Disrupted: "Server should cook twice" → 37.1 ticks, significant vs all; strikingly, 19% reach the optimal 34-tick policy vs <1% in every other arm — the algorithm tip *uniquely* unlocks optimality.
- **The naive imitation baseline can backfire.** In the disrupted config the baseline tip ("Sous-chef should plate twice") targets late, non-bottleneck actions and actively *reduces* performance — validating the $Q^*$-weighting over count-based imitation.
- **Human-crowdsourced tips are too vague or wrong.** Normal: humans give "Strategically leave some workers idle" (right direction, underspecified). Disrupted: humans vote "Server should cook once" — correct direction, insufficient magnitude (optimal is twice).
- **Compliance is endogenous and grows over time, trading off against optimality.** Counter-intuitive correct tips ("Server should cook twice," after participants just learned the server is slow at cooking) get lower compliance than intuitive human tips, but compliance more than doubles over the disrupted rounds as participants grasp the rationale. Participants ignore tips they distrust — compliance depends on *semantic* content, not just syntax.
- **Learning beyond the tip.** Via cross-compliance analysis, algorithm-arm participants comply with unshown optimal rules at least as much as other arms, suggesting one good tip conveys information that helps recover the full optimal policy.

## Contribution
(1) A novel interpretable-RL objective that distills the *human-vs-optimal discrepancy* (consequence-weighted by $Q^*$) into a single deployable rule, rather than interpreting a black-box model — bridging interpretable ML and behavioral operations. (2) A large pre-registered RCT demonstrating that ML-derived advice causally improves human sequential decision-making and that it dominates both peer-crowdsourced advice and naive imitation. (3) Design insights for human-AI interfaces: compliance depends on perceived effectiveness; counter-intuitive-but-correct advice faces an adoption penalty; humans augment rather than blindly follow advice.

## Limitations & open questions
- The method **cannot predict adoption rates ex ante** (Remark 1); it optimizes performance *conditional on adoption*, leaving the compliance-vs-optimality trade-off unmodeled. Joint optimization of tip quality and predicted compliance is an explicit open problem.
- Relies on the **distribution-shift approximation** $\bar{D}_t^{(\pi_H)}\approx\bar{D}_t^{(\pi_H\oplus\rho)}$; the authors note it held empirically here but it could fail when a tip substantially changes the visited states.
- Requires a **computable/learnable optimal policy and $Q^*$** — feasible in this engineered game but harder in real domains with unknown dynamics or partial observability.
- Tips are restricted to a **single if-then rule** over a decomposed action space; richer multi-rule tips were tested in pilots but humans could not operationalize them — leaving open how to balance tip expressiveness against human cognitive load.
- The authors suggest **levers to raise compliance** (financial incentives, social-norm nudges) but caution these can have null/negative effects and require context-specific design — an open design question.
- External validity: the study is a stylized game on AMT crowdworkers, not field workers (physicians, drivers) the motivation invokes.

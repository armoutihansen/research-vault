> [!abstract] Abstract
> With complete information, choice of one option over another conveys preference. Yet when search is incomplete, this is not necessarily the case. It may instead reflect unawareness that a superior alternative was available. To separate these phenomena, we consider nonstandard data on the evolution of provisional choices with contemplation time. We characterize precisely when the resulting data could have been generated by a general form of sequential search. We characterize also search that terminates based on a reservation utility stopping rule. We outline an experimental design that captures provisional choices in the pre-decision period.

## Summary
The paper bridges revealed-preference decision theory and search theory. Standard final-choice data cannot tell whether someone chose an inferior option because they preferred it or because incomplete search left them unaware of something better; indeed, with incomplete search any final-choice pattern is rationalizable. Caplin and Dean enrich the data to a **choice process** — the full trajectory of provisional choices $C_A(t)$ as contemplation time $t$ grows — and ask exactly when such data are consistent with sequential search that maximizes a fixed utility. They give axiomatic characterizations for **alternative-based search (ABS)** and its reservation-stopping refinement **reservation-based search (RBS)**, in both deterministic and stochastic forms, all resting on a single acyclicity condition (OWC). They then derive distinctive search-driven signatures of framing, status-quo bias, and stochastic choice, and sketch an incentive-compatible lab design (recording choice at a random hidden instant) under which a proof-of-principle experiment supports both ABS and RBS.

## Research question
When does observed choice reveal underlying preference, given that search is incomplete? Concretely: which enriched data sets on the *evolution* of choice over contemplation time could have been produced by (a) a decision maker who searches alternatives sequentially and always picks the best yet found, and (b) one who additionally stops as soon as an option clears a fixed reservation utility? And can such data be elicited experimentally?

## Method / identification
The primitive is **choice process data**. With $X$ a finite set of alternatives and $\mathcal{X}$ its nonempty subsets, a deterministic choice process is a map $C:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{Z}$ assigning each menu $A$ an infinite sequence $C_A=\{C_A(t)\}_{t\ge 1}$ with $C_A(t)\subseteq A$ — the (set of) best option(s) after contemplating $A$ for $t$ periods.

**ABS representation.** $(X,C)$ has an ABS representation $(u,S)$ if there is a utility $u:X\to\mathbb{R}$ and a nondecreasing search correspondence $S:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{Z}^{ND}$ (so $S_A(t)\subseteq S_A(t+1)$) with
$$C_A(t)=\arg\max_{x\in S_A(t)} u(x).$$
The searched set never shrinks (recall), so choice weakly improves over time; nothing is assumed about *why* or *when* search stops. Identification hinges on an enriched revealed-preference notion: define $x\sim y$ if $\{x,y\}\subseteq C_A(t)$ for some $A,t$ (chosen together $\Rightarrow$ indifferent), and $x\mathrel{C} y$ if $y\in C_A(s)$ but $x\in C_A(s+t)$ with $y\notin C_A(s+t)$ (a switch from $y$ to a later $x$ $\Rightarrow$ $x$ preferred, since $y$ was already searched). The workhorse Lemma 1 (a corollary of Bossert–Suzumura 2009) states that incomplete relations $P,I$ extend to a representing $v$ iff they satisfy **OWC (only weak cycles)**: along any cycle $x_1\,PI\,x_2\,PI\cdots PI\,x_n=x_1$ no link is strict.

**RBS representation.** RBS $(u,S,\rho)$ refines ABS with a reservation level $\rho$: writing $X^\rho_u=\{x\mid u(x)\ge\rho\}$, search of $A$ is complete if $A\cap X^\rho_u=\varnothing$ (R1), and otherwise it must reach an above-reservation item (R2a) and halt the instant it does (R2b). The empirical bite is that menus whose final choice is below reservation must have been fully searched, so final choice itself can reveal preference. The identification machinery defines the **nonterminal set** $X^N$ (objects the DM is seen to abandon) and the **indirectly nonterminal set** $X^{IN}$ (those plus options revealed inferior to a nonterminal one), the smallest admissible below-reservation set; the relation $\mathrel{R}=\mathrel{R}_{X^{IN}}$ augments $\mathrel{C}$ with this final-choice information.

**Stochastic version.** Because search order is unobservable, the authors build a probability space on $\mathcal{Z}$ via cylinder sets and define a stochastic choice process $\tilde{C}:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{P}$. Using "join" sets $J^{xy}$ (x,y chosen simultaneously) and "replacement" sets $R^{xy}$ ($y$ later replaced by $x$), $x\sim_{\tilde{C}} y$ and $x\mathrel{\tilde{C}} y$ hold when $\tilde{C}_A(J^{xy})>0$, $\tilde{C}_A(R^{xy})>0$. Proofs reduce the stochastic case to the deterministic one by **compression** (Definition 15): deleting periods in which choice is constant maps each process to a finite one, expressible as a convex combination of deterministic processes.

**Experimental design.** Subjects see a menu, may click to select any option at any time within a fixed window, and can re-select freely. Crucially, the payoff-relevant choice is recorded at a *random, hidden* instant — so it is optimal to always keep one's current best selected, making the click sequence a credible provisional-choice path.

## Data
None of the authors' own primary data is reported here; this is a theory paper. It describes (Section 6) the design and headline results of a companion proof-of-principle experiment, Caplin, Dean, and Martin (CDM, forthcoming): menus of dollar amounts each shown as a sequence of add/subtract operations, starting selection fixed at the worst ($0) option, 120-second window (binding in ~5% of rounds). In a post-experiment survey, 58 of 76 subjects reported always having their most preferred option selected.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1 (deterministic ABS):** $(X,C)$ has an ABS representation iff $\mathrel{C}$ and $\sim$ satisfy OWC. Four behaviors are ruled out — a preference reversal $\langle x;y;x\rangle$, an indifference-then-strict pattern $\langle x;\{x,y\};y\rangle$, a menu-dependent reversal across $\{x,y\}$ vs. $\{x,y,z\}$, and an indirect 3-cycle.
- **Corollary 1:** characterizes all equivalent ABS representations; search order is only partly recoverable — rejected-along-the-path items were searched, items revealed preferred to current choices were *not* searched, never-chosen items are indeterminate. More switches $\Rightarrow$ tighter inference on search order.
- **Proposition 1 / Theorem 2 (deterministic RBS):** an admissible below-reservation set $D$ must contain $X^N$, be closed under $\succeq_{\mathrel{R}_D}$, and yield OWC; equivalently $(X,C)$ has an RBS representation iff $\mathrel{R}$ and $\sim$ (built from the canonical set $X^{IN}$) satisfy OWC. Corollary 2 gives the equivalent-representation class.
- **Theorems 3 and 4 (stochastic ABS, RBS):** exact analogs — a stochastic ABS representation exists iff $\tilde{C},\sim_{\tilde{C}}$ satisfy OWC; a stochastic RBS representation exists iff $\tilde{R},\sim_{\tilde{C}}$ satisfy OWC.
- **Theorem 5 (framing):** across a family of frames $\gamma\in\Gamma$ inducing frame-specific search orders and reservation utilities but a *common* utility $u$, a unified RBS representation exists iff the cross-frame relations $\tilde{R}(\Gamma),\sim_{\tilde{C}(\Gamma)}$ satisfy OWC. This licenses transporting preference information learned in a low-search-cost frame to a high-search-cost one.
- **Status-quo bias (5.2):** modeling the status quo as the first item searched, RBS predicts an all-or-nothing bias — an above-reservation status quo is always retained; a below-reservation status quo never matters.
- **Stochastic choice (5.3):** search-driven randomness is structured — choice is random only among above-reservation items and deterministic on below-reservation menus; observed stochasticity rises as reservation utility falls.
- **Experiment:** behavior showed switching from lower- to higher-value options (supporting ABS) and was, more strikingly, well approximated by RBS even as menu size and option complexity varied.

## Contribution
Provides the first axiomatic revealed-preference foundation that *incorporates the search process itself*, cleanly separating preference-driven from information-driven departures from utility maximization. It shows that a modest data enrichment (choice over contemplation time) gives the otherwise-untestable canonical search models (Stigler-style sequential search; Simon's satisficing) sharp, OWC-based testable content, unifies deterministic and stochastic treatments, and supplies an incentive-compatible elicitation method — connecting consideration-set, framing, status-quo, and stochastic-choice literatures.

## Limitations & open questions
- **ABS/RBS are not universal.** The framework assumes alternative-based search; attribute-by-attribute (dimension-wise) comparison is excluded. The authors expect ABS to fit when switching attention is costly or options must be grasped holistically, and to fit poorly when alternatives are easy to compare on shared dimensions or easy to identify but hard to compare — characterizing search *style* by environment is left open.
- **Theory–data gap.** The model assumes one can observe *all* best options at each instant; the experiment elicits only a single selection per instant — an acknowledged gap (mirroring tests of standard rationality).
- **Search order only partially recovered.** Never-chosen items remain indeterminate as to whether they were searched; a prior such as list-order search ([[@Salant2008|Salant–Rubinstein 2008]]) could sharpen inference but is not imposed.
- **Stopping rules beyond fixed reservation.** RBS fixes $\rho$; cardinality- or context-dependent reservation rules (cf. [[@Tyson2008|Tyson 2008]]) and optimal/learning-based stopping are only gestured at.
- **Linking to process data.** The authors flag that eye-tracking and Mouselab process data have *not* been characterized in terms of standard choice theory — an explicit gap their approach complements but does not close.
- **Ongoing empirics.** Experimental investigation of choice process data is described as ongoing; richer, conflict-laden choice environments are untested.

## Connections
The paper sits between standard revealed-preference theory and Stigler's (1961) sequential search and Simon's (1955) satisficing, which it makes testable. Choice process data were anticipated by Campbell (1978) and by Block–Marschak's (1960) call to enrich data to separate utility-based from information-based randomness. It is closely related to other "data-enrichment" and limited-consideration programs: [[@Rubinstein2006|Rubinstein and Salant (2006)]] / [[@Salant2008|Salant and Rubinstein (2008)]] on list-ordered choice (where, unlike here, search order is observable), Masatlioglu and Nakajima (2009) on reference-dependent search (their dominating-anchor axiom is weaker than OWC), Masatlioglu, Nakajima, and Ozbay (2009) on attention filters, and [[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti (2007)]], Ergin (2003), Ergin and Sarver (2010) on procedural choice. The status-quo modeling connects to [[@Samuelson1988|Samuelson and Zeckhauser (1988)]]; the consideration-set framing to the marketing literature (Eliaz and Spiegler). Complementary process-tracing work (Payne et al. 1988/1993; Gabaix et al. 2006; Reutskaja et al.; Busemeyer and Townsend 1992; Rustichini 2008) observes search acts but does not tie them to choice implications, which is exactly the link choice process data supply. The companion experiment is Caplin, Dean, and Martin (forthcoming).

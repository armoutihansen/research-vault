> [!abstract] Abstract
> The authors examine how new brand entries affect consumers' consideration sets. A within-subject longitudinal experiment examines several entry positions into existing markets. The results suggest that new brand entries produce changes in consideration sets toward dominating, compromise, and assimilated brands, away from extreme brands in two-brand markets, and toward dominating and away from extreme brands in eight-brand markets. These results are confirmed by a second experiment that utilizes a between-subject design and markets with six existing brands.

## Summary
Lehmann and Pan merge two literatures — context (menu) effects on choice and consideration-set formation — by asking whether the well-documented attraction, compromise, and extremeness effects on *choice* also reshape the *consideration set* (the subset of brands a consumer evaluates before deciding) when a new brand enters a market. Across two laboratory experiments they find that the same context-dependent regularities govern consideration-set membership: a new entry that dominates an existing brand, or that makes an existing brand a compromise, shifts which brands are seriously considered. A striking asymmetry emerges — landing in a disadvantaged position (becoming extreme or dominated) sharply *reduces* consideration, but landing in a favored position (dominating or compromise) does *not* reliably increase it, because consumers cap the size of their consideration sets.

## Research question
Does new brand entry change the membership of consumers' consideration sets in the same context-dependent way it changes choice? Specifically, do asymmetric-dominance (attraction), compromise, extremeness-aversion, and assimilation effects operate at the consideration stage, and do these effects depend on market size (number of incumbent brands)?

## Method / identification
Two paper-and-pencil stimulus experiments using brands described on two or three attributes positioned on the efficient frontier of a product space (attribute values drawn from *Consumer Reports*, fictitious brand names).

**Study 1** — within-subject longitudinal design, $N=112$. A two-period design: in period 1 subjects see incumbent-only markets; after a filler task, period 2 re-presents the same categories with a new entrant added. Two-brand markets use a full factorial of entry positions: 2 (assimilated vs. not) $\times$ 2 (dominating vs. dominated) $\times$ 2 (compromise vs. extreme); eight-brand markets drop the assimilation factor (4 positions: dominated, extreme, dominating, compromise). Consideration set is a two-item measure ("Which brand(s) would you consider seriously?" and "Which are acceptable?"); choice and preference are also collected.

**Identification** is via OLS regression on pooled subject $\times$ category $\times$ before/after cells (4,475 observations). Each brand's position is coded with dummies on five dimensions — assimilated, compromise, extreme, dominating, dominated — with pre-entry and nontarget brands coded 0. Subject and product dummies absorb fixed heterogeneity to handle the partially-repeated-measures structure. The dependent variable is consideration-set membership (averaged two-item index); a companion specification uses the within-subject *change* (after minus before) and interactions of assimilation with each position.

**Study 2** — between-subject design, $N=220$, addressing Study 1's demand-effect and measurement-contamination concerns. Six incumbent brands in three pairs along the frontier, products spanning high/moderate/low involvement with two or three attributes. Two similar entry brands are introduced per condition; only consideration is measured. Adds two coded variables — *compromise-and-asymmetrically-dominating* and *compromise-and-asymmetrically-dominated* — for the middle pair when an entering pair attacks an existing pair. A post hoc split tests whether effects differ when entry attacks the more- vs. less-desirable end of the frontier.

## Data
Experimental stimulus data only. Study 1: 112 northeastern-university students (paid \$10, ~40 min). Study 2: 220 graduate business students (class requirement). Across both, 332 subjects and 18 product categories. Attribute values are anchored to real *Consumer Reports* ratings but brands are fictitious and not physically inspected.

## Key findings
- **Attraction/dominance ($H_1$, partial):** When the entrant dominates target brand B, significantly fewer subjects keep B in the consideration set (35% vs. 54% assimilated; 29% vs. 63% nonassimilated). When B dominates the entrant, consideration rises only weakly. The dominating coefficient is positive and significant; the dominated coefficient is large and negative (e.g., $-.41$, $t=-18.2$ in Study 1).
- **Compromise / extremeness aversion ($H_2$):** Becoming extreme reduces consideration (negative, significant). Becoming a compromise helps in small markets but the gain is asymmetric — a brand that *becomes* a compromise does not reliably increase its own consideration.
- **Assimilation ($H_3$):** Being positioned close to other brands raises consideration; crucially, assimilation *helps weak brands and hurts strong ones*. Being assimilated to a desirable neighbor cancels ~75% of the negative extreme effect but <25% of the dominated effect.
- **Market size ($H_4$, $H_5$):** Dominance, compromise, and assimilation effects appear even in two-brand markets and are somewhat *stronger* in small markets; the compromise effect even flips to marginally negative in eight-brand markets because it is hard to stand out in a crowded frontier.
- **Core asymmetry:** It is easy to push a brand *out* of the consideration set (a dominated entry) but hard to pull one *in*, since consumers limit set size — the entrant itself accounts for almost all the increase in set size.
- **Study 2 replication:** Between-subjects, dominating ($p<.01$) and dominated ($p<.01$) effects replicate; an asymmetrically-dominating position more than offsets a compromise penalty; where the entry attacks (desirable vs. undesirable end) significantly moderates the effects.

## Contribution
First study to show that menu/context effects — attraction, compromise, extremeness aversion, assimilation — operate not only on final choice but on the upstream *consideration set*, and to characterize their asymmetry (easy to exclude, hard to include) and their dependence on market size. It links the violations-of-regularity literature (Huber, Payne & Puto) and the consideration-set literature (Shocker et al.; Roberts & Lattin) into one framework, with managerial implications for new-product positioning (a weak brand benefits from assimilating to a strong one; a dominated entry can knock incumbents out of consideration).

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems: (i) paper-and-pencil task with no physical brand inspection, only two/three attributes and no brand names — effects likely understated in richer settings; (ii) the within-subject Study 1 risks demand effects and contamination across consideration/choice/preference measures; (iii) the design *confounds entry position with the brand's attribute values*, so no clean control condition exists; (iv) "objective" rather than perceptual operationalization of assimilation may weaken its measured impact; (v) where the entry attacks the frontier (desirable vs. undesirable end) materially changes effects and "calls for further research"; (vi) several post hoc sign reversals (e.g., extreme effect vanishing in the desirable area) "require further research"; (vii) process-tracing studies (concurrent/retrospective protocols) are needed to explain *how* entry sequentially affects consideration, preference, and choice.

## Connections
The paper builds directly on the attraction-effect tradition of [[@Huber1982|Huber, Payne and Puto (1982)]] and Huber and Puto (1983), and on [[@Simonson1989|Simonson (1989)]] and [[@Simonson1992|Simonson and Tversky (1992)]], whose two-tendency account (trade-off contrast plus extremeness aversion) it imports wholesale into the consideration stage. The consideration-set framing follows Howard and Sheth (1969) on evoked sets, Shocker et al. (1991), Nedungadi (1990), [[@Roberts1991|Roberts and Lattin (1991)]], and the cost-benefit consideration model of [[@Hauser1990|Hauser and Wernerfelt (1990)]]. Positioning concepts (assimilation, subtyping, pioneering advantage) draw on Carpenter and Nakamoto (1989), Sujan and Bettman (1989), and Kardes and Kalyanaram (1992). Ratneshwar, Shocker and Stewart (1987) supply the moderation-by-meaningfulness logic, and the companion paper Pan and Lehmann (1993) tracks the parallel effect on subjective brand judgments. The asymmetric-dominance and compromise regularities connect to the broader bounded-rationality choice-modeling literature, e.g. [[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti (2007)]] on sequentially rationalizable choice and consideration filters.

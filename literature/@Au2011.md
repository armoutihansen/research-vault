> [!abstract] Abstract
> In this note, we analyze a sequentially rationalizable choice model with a transitive rationale and a standard preference. The model in this note is more restrictive than the Rational Shortlist Method (RSM) model which is proposed in [[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti (2007)]] to capture cyclic behavior. Yet, a decision maker in our model exhibits cyclic behavior in general. We prove that the cyclicity of an indirectly revealed preference is exactly what distinguishes the RSM framework (a sequential choice model with a non-standard preference) from our transitive-RSM framework (a sequential choice model with a standard preference). We also provide a partial identification result on the representation.

## Summary
A short theory note that tightens the [[@Manzini2007|Manzini-Mariotti (2007)]] Rational Shortlist Method (RSM). Au and Kawai ask what happens when both rationales of a two-stage shortlist procedure are required to be "standard": the first rationale (a screening criterion) transitive, and the second rationale (the preference used to pick from the shortlist) complete and transitive (i.e., a strict preference). They show this *transitive-RSM* class still produces cyclic observable choice, but is strictly smaller than RSM, and that the precise gap is captured by a single new axiom, **No Binary Chain Cycles (NBCC)**, which forbids cycles in the *indirectly* revealed preference. They also deliver a partial-identification result: tight bounds on the criterion-preference pair that rationalizes any given choice function, plus a necessary-and-sufficient condition for the representation to be unique.

## Research question
Behavioral anomalies under RSM can stem from two distinct sources: (a) the *procedure* of forming a shortlist before choosing, and (b) a *non-standard* (intransitive/cyclic) underlying preference. The paper asks: if we strip away source (b) by forcing both rationales to be standard, does the procedure alone still generate anomalous (cyclic) choice, and exactly which RSM choice functions survive this restriction? Equivalently, what is the behavioral content of "the anomaly is due to shortlisting, not to a broken preference"?

## Method / identification
The setting is classical revealed-preference choice theory. Let $X$ be finite with $|X|\ge 3$, choice problems $\Omega_X \equiv 2^X\setminus\{\emptyset\}$, and a choice function $c:\Omega_X\to X$ with $c(S)\in S$. The DM has a strict preference $P_2$ (asymmetric, complete, transitive) and a **criterion** $P_1$ (asymmetric, transitive). The shortlist from $S$ is the set of $P_1$-maximal elements $\max(S;P_1)\equiv\{x\in S: \neg\exists\, y\ \text{s.t.}\ yP_1x\}$, and the final choice is $c(S)=c_2(\max(S;P_1);P_2)$, the $P_2$-maximum of the shortlist. A choice function is a **transitive-RSM** if some such $(P_1,P_2)$ represents it. This nests inside Manzini-Mariotti's RSM, where neither rationale need be transitive.

The key conceptual device is a *layered* revealed-preference relation:
- $x$ is **directly revealed preferred** to $y$, written $x\,c_D\,y$, iff $c(\{x,y\})=x$ *but* $c(S)\ne c(S\setminus\{y\})$ for some $S\supseteq\{x,y\}$ (removing $y$ changes the choice, so $y$ mattered via the shortlist).
- A **binary chain** is a set $\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}$ with $x_k\,c_D\,x_{k+1}$ for all $k\le n-1$.
- $x_1$ is **indirectly revealed preferred** to $x_n$, written $x_1\,c_I\,x_n$, iff a binary chain links them.

The central axiom restricts this indirect relation:
$$\textbf{NBCC: } \nexists\ \text{binary chain } \{x_1,\dots,x_n\}\ \text{with}\ x_1=x_n.$$
Crucially, NBCC does *not* forbid cyclic *choice*; it forbids choice functions whose indirectly revealed preference is cyclic. The "if" direction is proved constructively: given an RSM $c$ satisfying NBCC, set $P_2^c$ to a Szpilrajn completion of $c_I$ and define the criterion
$$P_1^c\equiv\{(x,y): c(S)=c(S\setminus\{y\})\ \text{for all}\ S\supseteq\{x,y\},\ x\ne y\}.$$
The proof routes through an auxiliary **Weak-IIA** axiom and a **Reduction** axiom, with Lemma 4 (NBCC: Weak-IIA iff RSM), Claim 5 (Weak WARP + Expansion + NBCC imply Reduction), and Claim 6 (Reduction + NBCC imply Weak-IIA).

## Key findings
- **Cyclic choice survives standardization.** Example 1 ($X=\{x,y,z\}$ with $c(X)=x$, $c(\{x,y\})=x$, $c(\{y,z\})=y$, $c(\{x,z\})=z$) is a transitive-RSM choice function exhibiting a choice cycle, rationalized by $P_1=\{(y,z)\}$ and $P_2=\{(z,x),(x,y),(z,y)\}$. So a fully standard preference plus shortlisting still yields anomalies.
- **Theorem 1 (representation).** An RSM choice function $c$ is a transitive-RSM choice function **if and only if** $c$ satisfies NBCC. Thus the entire gap between the standard-preference model and general RSM is one axiom on the indirectly revealed preference. Example 2 exhibits an RSM violating NBCC (its directly revealed preference cycles $w\,c_D\,x\,c_D\,y\,c_D\,w$), hence not transitive-RSM.
- **Remark 1 (data parsimony).** The criterion can equivalently be recovered from choice sets of size $\le 4$: $P_1^c=\{(x,y): c(\{w,x,y,z\})=c(\{w,x,z\})\ \forall w,z,\ x\ne y\}$ - far less data than the original Manzini-Mariotti construction, which needed $xP_1y$ iff $y\ne c(S)$ for *all* $S\supseteq\{x,y\}$.
- **Theorem 2 (partial identification).** For a transitive-RSM $c$, defining $\underline{P_1^c}\equiv\{(x,y)\in P_1^c: y\,c_I\,x\}$, the set of admissible criteria $P_1(c)$ has tight bounds $\underline{P_1^c}\subset P_1^c\subset \overline{P_1^c}$, and the preferences satisfy $P_2(\underline{P_1^c};c)\subset P_2(P_1^c;c)\subset P_2(\overline{P_1^c};c)$ with $|P_2(\underline{P_1^c};c)|=1$. A *less complete* criterion pins down the preference more tightly; the lower bound captures the "conflict" between the upper-bound criterion and the indirectly revealed preference.
- **Corollary 3 (uniqueness).** The preference is point-identified iff the criterion is point-identified, i.e., $|P_2(c)|=1$ iff $\underline{P_1^c}=\overline{P_1^c}$. Equivalently, the representation is unique iff $c(S)=c(S\setminus\{y\})$ for all $S\supseteq\{x,y\}$ implies $y\,c_I\,x$.
- **Remark 2 / Example 3 (comparative statics caution).** A natural conjecture - that more "conflict" between criterion and preference ($P_1^{c^j}\subset P_1^{c^i}$, same $P_2$) means more cyclic behavior - is **false**: two such DMs can exhibit the same number of cycles (two of length 3, one of length 4), differing only in *which* length-4 cycle appears.

## Contribution
The note isolates the source of choice anomalies in sequential procedures. By proving Theorem 1, it shows that *all* the explanatory power of intransitive/cyclic preferences in RSM is concentrated in violations of NBCC; everything else RSM explains is already reachable with a fully standard preference plus shortlisting. This sharply reframes RSM anomalies as a property of the *attention/screening* stage rather than of preferences per se. The partial-identification theorem and uniqueness corollary give applied researchers tight, testable bounds on recoverable $(P_1,P_2)$ pairs and a clean condition for point identification, while Remark 1 reduces the data requirement to choice sets of size four.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Restrictiveness as a deliberate floor.** Footnote 3 frames the strong assumptions (transitive criterion, complete-transitive preference) as a methodological floor: "if we do not get a unique representation, or comparative static result in this most restrictive class, we never get them in a more general model." This invites work relaxing each assumption and tracking when identification breaks.
- **Comparative statics are unresolved.** Example 3 explicitly refutes the intuitive monotone link between criterion-preference conflict and the amount of cyclic behavior, leaving open *what* observable comparative-static statements (if any) hold across transitive-RSM DMs.
- **Relation to neighboring axioms.** The authors note their NBCC axiom is logically independent of Cherepanov-Feddersen-Sandroni's (2008) acyclicity axiom (neither weaker nor stronger), though NBCC plus Weak WARP implies it - mapping the full lattice of such axioms is left open.
- **Finite-domain restriction.** Everything is stated on finite $X$; extension to infinite alternative spaces (where the lexicographic-semiorder embedding of Manzini-Mariotti, forthcoming, lives) is not addressed.

## Connections
The paper is a direct refinement of [[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti (2007]], *AER*), whose RSM model (characterized by **Weak WARP** + **Expansion**) it nests and sharpens. It is closest in spirit to **Cherepanov, Feddersen, and Sandroni (2008)** "Rationalization," which also builds a shortlist then applies a standard preference, but uses *multiple* criteria and an acyclicity axiom on a (related but distinct) revealed preference. The revealed-preference notion echoes **Masatlioglu, Nakajima, and Ozbay (2010)** "Revealed Attention," whose consideration-set/attention-filter model recovers preferences from the same kind of "removing $y$ changes the choice" observation. Other neighbors generalize rather than restrict RSM: **Apesteguia and Ballester (2009)** (sequential elimination by acyclic rationales), **García-Sanz and Alcantud (2010)** (rationalization by two sequential relations), and **Manzini and Mariotti (forthcoming)** "Choice by Lexicographic Semiorders," of which transitive-RSM is a finite-domain special case. Classic motivation comes from **[[@Tversky1972|Tversky (1972)]]** and **[[@Loomes1991|Loomes-Starmer-Sugden (1991)]]** on intransitive/cyclic choice, with **Luce (1956)** supplying the semiorder background and **Szpilrajn's Theorem** the order-completion tool used throughout the proofs.

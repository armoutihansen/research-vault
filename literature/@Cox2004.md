> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper uses a three-games (or triadic) design to identify trusting and reciprocating behavior. A large literature on single-game trust and reciprocity experiments is based on the implicit assumption that subjects do not have altruistic or inequality-averse other-regarding preferences. Such experimental designs test compound hypotheses that include the hypothesis that other-regarding preferences do not affect behavior. In contrast, experiments with the triadic design do discriminate between transfers resulting from trust or reciprocity and transfers resulting from other-regarding preferences that are not conditional on the behavior of others. Decomposing trust from altruism and reciprocity from altruism or inequality aversion is critical to obtaining empirical information that can guide the process of constructing models that can increase the empirical validity of game theory.

## Summary
Single-game investment (trust) games conflate two distinct motives: a first mover's positive transfer could reflect *trust* (a belief-driven willingness to bear risk) or merely *unconditional altruism*; a second mover's return could reflect *reciprocity* or merely unconditional other-regarding preferences (altruism / inequality aversion). Cox introduces a **triadic design**: the standard investment game (Treatment A) plus two dictator-game controls (B and C) that strip away, respectively, the second mover's ability to return and the first mover's ability to send. Comparing A against B isolates trust; comparing A against C isolates reciprocity. The data reveal statistically significant trust, significant reciprocity, *and* substantial unconditional altruism — establishing that all three motives coexist and that single-game designs cannot separate them.

## Research question
Do observed positive transfers in investment/gift-exchange games actually demonstrate trust and reciprocity, or are they confounded with unconditional other-regarding preferences? More precisely: how can an experimental design discriminate first-mover transfers driven by trust from those driven by altruism, and second-mover returns driven by reciprocity from those driven by unconditional altruism or inequality aversion?

## Method / identification
The core method is a **triadic (three-treatment, across-subjects) experimental design**, paired with a revealed-preference identification argument under the maintained assumption that each subject has a strictly quasi-concave utility function $u(y_k, y_j)$ over own and other's money payoffs.

- **Treatment A** — the Berg–Dickhaut–McCabe investment game. First mover with endowment 10 sends $s_a\in S=\{0,1,\dots,10\}$; the experimenter triples it; second mover then returns $r_a\in R(s_a)=\{0,1,\dots,3s_a\}$.
- **Treatment B** — a dictator game identical to A except the second mover has no decision. The first mover's choice $s_b$ reflects only unconditional preferences, satisfying $u_1(10-s_b,10+3s_b)\ge u_1(10-s,10+3s)$ for all $s\in S$.
- **Treatment C** — a dictator game where the first mover has no decision; "second movers" receive endowments $(10-s_a,10+3s_a)$ inversely linked to a treatment-A first mover's choice, then choose a "return" $r_c$. With no friendly first-mover action, reciprocity cannot operate.

**Trust identification:** if $s_a>s_b$, the amount sent in A is too large to be explained by unconditional preferences. Strict quasi-concavity then implies $u_1(10-s_a,10+3s_a)<u_1(10-s_b,10+3s_b)$ when the second mover returns nothing — i.e. the first mover bore genuine risk, the signature of trust.

**Reciprocity identification:** if $r_a>r_c$, the return in A exceeds what unconditional preferences justify; strict quasi-concavity gives $u_2(10+3s_a-r_a,10-s_a+r_a)<u_2(10+3s_a-r_c,10-s_a+r_c)$, so the second mover incurred a cost to repay. Reciprocity is modeled via a **norm state variable** $\lambda_a=f(s_a)$ entering a norm-conditional utility $u_{2\lambda_a}$, distinct from the norm-free $u_2$.

The appendix formalizes the null hypotheses as the self-regarding model's predictions, $H_0^T: s_a\le s_b$ vs. $H_A^T: s_a>s_b$ (and analogously $H_0^R: r_a\le r_c$), with the discrete-bound versions $s_a>s_b+1$ and $r_a>r_c+1$. Estimation uses two-sample $t$-tests, Mann–Whitney tests, Epps–Singleton distributional tests, and a tobit regression $R_t=\alpha+\beta D_t S_t+\gamma S_t+\varepsilon_t$ (with $D_t=1$ for treatment A) bounded by $R_t\in[0,3S_t]$ and a multiplicative-heteroskedasticity term $\sigma_t=\sigma e^{\theta S_t}$; $\hat\beta$ is the reciprocity effect.

## Data
Original laboratory data collected at the University of Arizona Economic Science Laboratory, November 2000. Six sessions (two per treatment), double-blind protocol: 32 pairs in Treatment A, 30 in B, 32 in C; no repeat participation, subjects filtered against prior similar experiments. Subjects in C were not told their endowments derived from others' Treatment-A choices, to preclude indirect reciprocity.

## Key findings
- **Trust exists.** Mean sent was 5.97 (A) vs. 3.63 (B); the difference is significant by one-tailed $t$-test ($p=0.010$), Mann–Whitney ($p=0.010$), and Epps–Singleton ($p=0.010$). 26 of 32 first movers sent positive amounts in A.
- **Reciprocity exists.** Mean returned was 4.94 (A) vs. 2.06 (C); significant by $t$-test ($p=0.018$) and Mann–Whitney ($p=0.061$), though the Epps–Singleton distributional test was not significant ($p=0.219$). The tobit reciprocity coefficient $\hat\beta=0.680$ is positive and significant ($p=0.034$).
- **Unconditional altruism is substantial.** In B, 19 of 30 first movers (63%) sent positive amounts despite no possible return; in C, 13 of 32 (41%) "returned" positive amounts at a full $1-per-$1 cost. Thus a large share of positive transfers in the raw investment game is *not* trust/reciprocity but unconditional giving.
- The behavior of most pairs is inconsistent with the self-regarding subgame-perfect equilibrium (which predicts zero transfers), but consistent with a richer utility theory rather than a refutation of game theory.

## Contribution
Provides a clean identification strategy — the triadic design plus a revealed-preference test exploiting strict quasi-concavity — that *decomposes* observed transfers into trust, reciprocity, and unconditional other-regarding preferences. It shows that the widely-cited claim that investment/gift-exchange games "document" reciprocity rests on a compound hypothesis that single-game designs cannot test. The paper supplies three concrete prescriptions for utility modeling: utility must not be constant in others' payoffs; beliefs about others' altruism/reciprocity must enter (for trust); and outcome preferences must be conditional on perceived intentions (for reciprocity).

## Limitations & open questions
- **Are preferences characteristics of agents?** A referee challenged the central assumption that A, B, C elicit the same preferences rather than different fairness norms or rules of thumb; Cox explicitly leaves to "authors of subsequent papers" the question of whether preferences are stable agent characteristics across fairness games.
- **Anticipation-dependent utility.** If first movers anticipate emotional costs (anger/betrayal) only possible in A, the trust test becomes conservative — Cox argues this does not threaten the positive findings but flags it as an unmodeled channel.
- The across-subjects design and modest sample sizes leave individual-level decomposition (and the functional form of the norm $\lambda_a=f(s_a)$) unestimated here.

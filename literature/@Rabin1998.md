## Summary

A landmark *Journal of Economic Literature* survey that organizes psychological findings relevant to economics by how radically each challenges the neoclassical core. Rabin sorts the evidence into three escalating tiers: (i) phenomena that only require enriching the utility function $U(x)$ — reference-dependence/loss aversion and social preferences; (ii) systematic biases in *judgment under uncertainty* that make people err when maximizing $U(x)$ — the law of small numbers and confirmatory bias; and (iii) phenomena that undermine the very idea of a stable, coherent, accurately-perceived $U(x)$ — hedonic misprediction, framing/preference reversals, and time-inconsistent (hyperbolic) discounting. The essay is programmatic: it argues that behavioral hypotheses deserve the same rigorous empirical standards economics applies elsewhere, not dismissal on a-priori methodological grounds.

## Research question

What does systematic psychological research teach economists about how real human judgment, behavior, and well-being depart from the standard model of a stable, coherent, rationally-maximized utility function — and how should economics incorporate these departures? The framing question throughout is *how radical* a revision of the economic model each empirical regularity demands.

## Method / identification

This is a narrative *review essay*, not an empirical or theoretical contribution of its own. Its "method" is a taxonomy: classify psychological findings by the depth of the challenge they pose to the maximization-of-$U(x)$ paradigm. Rabin nonetheless restates the formal models economists use to absorb each anomaly:

- **Reference-dependence and loss aversion.** Carriers of utility are changes relative to a reference point, with losses weighted more than equal gains and diminishing sensitivity in both domains. Reference points adjust adaptively, e.g. $r_t = \alpha c_{t-1} + (1-\alpha)\,r_{t-1}$ with $\alpha\in(0,1)$ governing adjustment speed; a rational-expectations agent anticipates this when maximizing long-run well-being.
- **Social preferences.** Simple altruism: $U_1(x) = (1-r)\,\Pi_1(x) + r\,\Pi_2(x)$. Distributive justice generalizes this to $U_1 = (1-r)\,\Pi_1 + r\,W_1(\Pi_1,\Pi_2)$, where $W_1$ encodes the allocation norm (e.g. 50-50, or a maximin norm $W_0 = \min[\Pi_1,\Pi_2]$ over *gains*). Outcome-only models are shown to be inadequate: fairness depends on *intentions*, formalized via psychological games (Geanakoplos, Pearce, Stacchetti 1989; [[@Rabin1993|Rabin 1993]]) where payoffs depend on beliefs about others' beliefs.
- **Hyperbolic / quasi-hyperbolic discounting.** The Phelps–Pollak $\beta$-$\delta$ form: intertemporal utility at $t$ is $U^t = \delta^0 u_t + \beta\sum_{\tau=t+1}^{T}\delta^{\tau-t} u_\tau$, with $\beta,\delta\in(0,1)$. $\delta$ is long-run patience; $\beta<1$ generates the present bias and time-inconsistency. Dynamic choice is modeled as an intrapersonal game among time-dated "selves," with agents classified as **sophisticated** (aware of future self-control problems) or **naive** (unaware) per Strotz (1955).

## Data

None of its own — this is a synthesis. It cites and recounts dozens of others' experiments and field studies (lottery-winner happiness, ultimatum/dictator and gift-exchange labor-market games, the two-hospital law-of-small-numbers item, capital-punishment belief-polarization, hot-hand basketball data, preference-reversal gambles, taxi-driver labor supply, etc.).

## Key findings

- **Reference effects are pervasive and partly irrational.** Status-quo bias, the endowment effect, and asymmetric consumption responses to good vs. bad income news (loss aversion) recur in field and lab data. Rabin argues loss aversion is often a *judgmental bias* rather than rational anticipation of "transition utility," since people demand huge premiums even when long-run consequences dwarf the transitory loss.
- **People are not purely self-interested.** Subjects contribute to public goods, share windfalls, and pay to punish unfair treatment. Disinterested allocation norms favor equal splits or maximin over the "maximal-benefits" criterion. Crucially, *intentions matter*: gift-exchange effort responds to whether a high wage reflected the firm's volition (Charness 1996; [[@Fehr1993|Fehr–Kirchsteiger–Riedl 1993]]).
- **Law of small numbers.** People over-expect small samples to mirror the population, producing the gambler's fallacy, over-inference from short streaks, misperception of regression to the mean (the flight-instructor "praise hurts" illusion), and belief in the non-existent hot hand.
- **Belief perseverance and confirmatory bias.** People anchor on early hypotheses and *misread* ambiguous evidence as confirmation, so identical evidence can drive priors apart (Lord–Ross–Lepper death-penalty polarization). Formalized in Rabin–Schrag (1997).
- **People mispredict their own utility.** Underestimating hedonic adaptation (lottery winners no happier), melioration / ignoring "internalities" (Herrnstein–Prelec), and biased retrospection — peak intensity overweighting, end-effects, and duration neglect (Kahneman 1994).
- **Choices need not reflect stable preferences.** Framing effects, classic preference reversals (choosing the high-probability bet but pricing the high-payoff bet higher), and context/menu effects (asymmetric dominance, compromise) suggest reasons-based rather than utility-based choice ([[@Shafir1993|Shafir–Simonson–Tversky 1993]]).
- **Time-inconsistency is real.** Present bias generates procrastination and self-control problems, and rationalizes observed commitment devices (Christmas clubs, small package sizes, fat farms); people appear closer to naive than sophisticated.

## Contribution

Provides the canonical map of behavioral economics circa 1998, defining the field's research agenda and supplying the now-standard taxonomy (utility-function modifications $\to$ judgment biases $\to$ failures of stable maximization). It popularized the $\beta$-$\delta$ reframing of present bias for economists, the intentions-based view of fairness, and a forceful methodological argument: behavioral hypotheses should be evaluated with ordinary empirical rigor, not pre-emptively dismissed. The piece seeded much subsequent work (O'Donoghue–Rabin procrastination, Laibson hyperbolic savings, distributional/reciprocity models).

## Limitations & open questions

Rabin himself flags many open hooks:

- **Reference-point dynamics are under-identified.** There is "little evidence" on how reference points actually form and adjust ($\alpha$), or how preferences depend on changes in them — so loss aversion and diminishing sensitivity can only be embedded in dynamic models under a "relatively small set of circumstances."
- **Rational vs. irrational loss aversion is unresolved** — whether reference effects reflect genuine experienced "transition utility" or a judgmental bias remains open and consequential for welfare.
- **The structure of social preferences (the form of $W_1$ and the level of $r$) is not pinned down**, and integrating intentions/beliefs into tractable economic models was still nascent.
- **Calibrating sophistication vs. naivety** about one's own time-inconsistency is "sparse"; the degree of self-awareness is inferred only indirectly.
- **Scope omissions** explicitly acknowledged: non-expected utility, status/envy/social comparison, conformity and herding, self-serving/motivated cognition, and crowding-out of intrinsic by extrinsic motivation — each named as economically relevant but uncovered.

## Connections

The reference-dependence and loss-aversion material rests on Kahneman–Tversky prospect theory (1979) and the endowment-effect experiments ([[@Tversky1990|Kahneman–Knetsch–Thaler 1990]]; Samuelson–Zeckhauser status-quo bias). The social-preferences thread anticipates and frames the outcome-based inequity-aversion models (Fehr–Schmidt, Bolton–Ockenfels) while championing the intentions-based alternative built on Geanakoplos–Pearce–Stacchetti (1989) and [[@Rabin1993|Rabin (1993)]]; the gift-exchange evidence is [[@Fehr1993|Fehr–Kirchsteiger–Riedl (1993)]] and Charness (1996). The judgment-bias section draws on Tversky–Kahneman's heuristics-and-biases program (1971, 1974) and the hot-hand work (Gilovich–Vallone–Tversky 1985). The time-inconsistency section connects Strotz (1955), Phelps–Pollak (1968), Laibson (1997), and O'Donoghue–Rabin, and the hedonic-misprediction material links to Kahneman's experienced-utility agenda ([[@Kahneman1997|Kahneman–Wakker–Sarin 1997]]) and Loewenstein's projection-bias work. This survey is directly upstream of modern choice-modeling and social-preference estimation.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Theories of reference-dependent preferences propose that individuals evaluate outcomes as gains or losses relative to a neutral reference point. We test for reference dependence in a large data set of marathon finishing times (n = 9,789,093). Models of reference-dependent preferences such as prospect theory predict bunching of finishing times at reference points. We provide visual and statistical evidence that round numbers (e.g., a four-hour marathon) serve as reference points in this environment and as a result produce significant bunching of performance at these round numbers. Bunching is driven by planning and adjustments in effort provision near the finish line and cannot be explained by explicit rewards (e.g., qualifying for the Boston Marathon), peer effects, or institutional features (e.g., pacesetters). Data, as supplemental material, are available at http://dx.doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.2015.2417. This paper was accepted by John List, behavioral economics.

## Summary
Using roughly 9.5 million marathon finishing times, the authors show that finishers cluster ("bunch") just ahead of round-number times such as 3:00 and 4:00, with up to ~50% more runners in the minute before a round number than the minute after. They argue this bunching is the behavioral signature of reference-dependent preferences (prospect theory), where round-number goals act as reference points. Crucially the effect is psychological rather than incentive-driven: it survives controls for Boston-qualifying cutoffs, pace teams, peer effects, and is reproduced by standard prospect-theory parameters in a calibration exercise. Mechanisms are pacing/planning from the start and dynamic effort adjustment over the final 2.195 km, plus spontaneous adoption of slower round-number goals once an initial target becomes infeasible.

## Research question
Do individuals evaluate outcomes relative to reference points in a clean field setting, and—since the central difficulty in field tests of reference dependence is that reference points are usually unobservable, heterogeneous, and possibly non-stationary (Camerer et al. 1997; Barberis 2013)—can round-number goals serve as *knowable, stable, and incentive-free* reference points that produce a sharp, non-parametric test? Secondarily: through what behavioral channel (planning vs. late-race effort) does reference dependence translate into the observed performance distribution?

## Method / identification
The conceptual core (Section 2) is a deterministic effort-provision model. Redefining performance so agents maximize, let $t = k-\tau$ be how much faster a finishing time $\tau$ is than the worst possible time $k$. Utility is additively separable, $U(t)=b(t)-c(t)$, with costs positive and increasing ($c(t)>0$, $c'(t)>0$). Heterogeneity is carried by a family of cost functions $c_1(t),\dots,c_N(t)$ (ability, preparation, course, weather); the benefit function $b(t)$ is homogeneous and reference-dependent at point $r$ in one of three forms: (1) a jump/notch ($\lim_{\epsilon\to0} v_r(r+\epsilon)\neq\lim_{\epsilon\to0} v_r(r-\epsilon)$); (2) a kink in the first derivative (loss aversion); (3) a kink in the second derivative (diminishing sensitivity). Defining $\delta$-bunching as the set $C(\delta,b(t))$ of cost functions for which $0\le t^*(c(t),b(t))-r\le\delta$, three propositions show each form produces bunching that grows as the discontinuity sharpens: more-discontinuous, more-loss-averse, and more-diminishing-sensitivity benefit functions all yield $C(\delta,b_2(t))\subseteq C(\delta,b_1(t))$. A smooth benefit function plus a smooth cost distribution would instead give a smooth finishing-time density.

Empirical identification is non-parametric and rests on two estimators borrowed from the bunching literature. (i) The **McCrary (2008) density-discontinuity test** is run at every minute mark from 2:40 to 7:00, producing a "running z-statistic" that spikes (local optima) at hour and half-hour marks. (ii) The **Chetty, Friedman, Olsen, and Pistaferri (2011)** excess-mass procedure, by analogy to taxpayer bunching at tax kinks: fit a cubic polynomial counterfactual to the local density (a 16-minute window, ±8 minutes around $r$, excluding the bunching region), measure excess finishers in a 4-minute pre-round-number window, bootstrap (500 iterations) for standard errors, and shift the counterfactual up to equalize total mass. Mechanism analysis exploits split times (10 km, half, 30 km, 40 km), extrapolating each split linearly to a projected finish, and a "normalized pace" measure (last 2.195 km pace ÷ first-40 km pace) to detect late-race effort changes.

## Data
9,524,071 marathon finishes (the published version reports n = 9,789,093) covering 1970–2013 (91.23% from 2000 on) across 6,831 marathon-years—mostly U.S. plus the five largest Canadian marathons and several large international races; all 51 largest U.S. marathons appear over multiple years. A "full split sample" of 868,039 finishes has complete 10 km / half / 30 km / 40 km splits for mechanism tests. RFID chip timing is essential (chip time vs. clock time), since self-reported times would bunch mechanically from rounding. Survey inputs from Sackett, Wu, White, and Markle (2014): 86.3% of marathoners report a time goal, of which 27.8% / 49.4% / 70.5% are divisible by 60 / 30 / 10 minutes, and goals are on average optimistic (only ~25.5% beat them).

## Key findings
- **Excess mass at round numbers.** 51.4%, 21.7%, and 29.6% more finishers in the minute before 3:00, 3:30, and 4:00 than the minute after; smaller but real bunching at 10- and 15-minute marks (left-digit bias is rejected because 3:30/4:30 bunch more than 3:20/3:40 and 15-minute marks bunch despite no left-digit change). The largest count displaced is ~47,363 runners at 4:00; the largest percentage excess (24.6%, t = 41.2) is at 3:00.
- **Propositions 2.1–2.3.** Each of a notch, loss aversion, and diminishing sensitivity in gains independently generates bunching just above $r$, monotonically increasing in the severity of the discontinuity.
- **Robustness / not extrinsic.** Bunching holds across decades, marathon sizes, speeds, countries, and ages with remarkably uniform effect sizes; it is *not* explained by Boston-qualifying cutoffs (e.g., 3:00 has not been a qualifying time since 1989, yet bunches strongly), by pace teams (bunched runners finish at widely dispersed *clock* times; small marathons with no pace teams, n = 291,231, still bunch), or by peer effects (adjacent finishers differ by ~1.5–2.6 minutes).
- **Mechanisms.** Bunching grows along the race (excess mass at the 3-hour mark: 5.1% at 10 km → 13.7% half → 12.4% at 30 km → 14.1% at 40 km → 28.2% at finish), evidencing planning/pacing. Late-race effort matters too: runners on pace for ~4:00 slow only 6–8% over the final 2.195 km vs. 8–11% off-pace; ~35% near a round number speed up vs. ~25% otherwise, and they are less likely to slow by 3+ minutes (the positive 30–40 km vs. final-leg pace correlation, $\rho=.63$, rules out a strategy-mixture artifact).
- **Spontaneous goal formation.** Runners who fall ≥15 minutes behind still bunch at slower round numbers; the extraordinarily hot 2007 Chicago Marathon (98.7% slowed; only 3.2% met goals) still shows significant McCrary jumps at 4:00, 4:30, 5:00—reference points adapt quickly.
- **Calibration (Section 5).** Using the prospect-theory value function $v(x)=x^{\alpha_G}$ for $x>0$ and $v(x)=-\lambda(-x)^{\alpha_L}$ otherwise, with Tversky–Kahneman (1992) parameters $\lambda=2.25$, $\alpha=0.88$ and only $p=.25$ reference-dependent runners (marginal cost $c'(t)=\omega t^\beta$, $\beta=100$, $k=600$, $r=240$), the simulated distribution reproduces the empirical shape (35.0% excess mass). Excess mass is monotone increasing in $\lambda$ and $p$ and increasing as $\alpha$ falls; $\lambda$ and $\beta$ are nearly interchangeable. Grid-search best fits need lower $\alpha$ than lab estimates ($\lambda=2.35,\alpha=0.23,p=.12$ at 3 h; $\lambda=1.77,\alpha=0.15,p=.06$ at 4 h). The Chetty procedure is shown to *underestimate* true excess mass (28.0% actual vs. 22.8% estimated) for kinks but slightly overestimate it for notches.

## Contribution
A uniquely clean, large-sample field test of reference dependence in a setting essentially free of financial incentives, distinguishing it from Pope and Simonsohn (2011) where baseball/SAT round numbers carry direct monetary stakes. Conceptually, it argues the operative reference points are **goals**—related to but psychologically distinct from rational expectations (Kőszegi and Rabin 2006) and from the status quo—thereby broadening the admissible class of non-status-quo reference points and empirically linking the psychology of goals (Heath, Larrick, and Wu 1999) to economic models of goals and self-control (Hsiaw 2013; Koch and Nafziger 2011). It is also one of few studies to document the *temporal* dynamics of effort provision and to show reference points can be adopted spontaneously mid-task.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Internal reference point vs. audience effect.** The authors cannot separate a runner's own loss aversion from caring how a 3:59 vs. 4:01 is perceived by others; they note most field studies share this confound and that either way it is still reference dependence.
- **Source of bunching ("from above" vs. "from below").** Whether mass is drawn from slower runners speeding up or faster runners coasting is "highly sensitive to parametric assumptions" and only partly resolved; both appear to operate.
- **Calibration is deterministic.** It ignores the stochastic control problem runners face; adding uncertainty/risk aversion would smooth the simulated density. Required $\alpha$ is lower than lab estimates, so diminishing sensitivity vs. risk attitudes are not separately identified, and $\lambda$, $p$, $\beta$ are partly interchangeable—the model is "relatively silent about the proportion" of reference-dependent runners.
- **Why round numbers, and why these particular ones.** Why agents adopt round-number goals (cognitive reference points à la Rosch 1975 vs. a coarse partition that defends against present-biased backsliding) is left as speculation; why 3:00 bunches far more than 3:20 (different $p$, $\lambda$, or $\alpha$ across reference points) is not pinned down.
- **Other margins.** Effects on the extensive margin (suggestive evidence that a 4:01 runner is more likely to re-enter next year than a 3:59 runner) and non-round-number reference points are flagged as "beyond the scope of this paper."

## Connections
The empirical lineage runs through field tests of reference dependence in labor supply (Camerer et al. 1997; Fehr and Goette 2007; Farber 2005, 2008; Crawford and Meng 2011) and the disposition effect (Shefrin and Statman 1985; Odean 1998; Genesove and Mayer 2001), which the paper most closely resembles by positing a known quantity as the reference point. Theoretically it sits against Kőszegi and Rabin (2006, 2007, 2009) expectations-based reference dependence—deliberately departing by using non-rational goals—and against expected-utility-with-aspiration axiomatizations (Diecidue and van de Ven 2008; Fishburn 1977). The closest companion is Pope and Simonsohn (2011) on round numbers as goals (0.300 batting averages, SAT retakes); related marathon-specific work includes Sackett, Wu, White, and Markle (2014) and Markle, Wu, White, and Sackett (2014), the latter finding all three reference-dependence forms in satisfaction data. The bunching methodology is imported wholesale from the public-finance tax-kink literature (McCrary 2008; Saez 2010; Chetty et al. 2011; Kleven and Waseem 2013), and the goals-as-self-control framing connects to planner-doer models (Thaler and Shefrin 1981; Hsiaw 2013; Koch and Nafziger 2011).

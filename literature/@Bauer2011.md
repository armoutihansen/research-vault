> [!abstract] Abstract
> This book gives a straightforward introduction to the field as it is nowadays required in many branches of analysis and especially in probability theory. The first three chapters (Measure Theory, Integration Theory, Product Measures) basically follow the clear and approved exposition given in the author's earlier book on "Probability Theory and Measure Theory". Special emphasis is laid on a complete discussion of the transformation of measures and integration with respect to the product measure, convergence theorems, parameter depending integrals, as well as the Radon-Nikodym theorem. The final chapter, essentially new and written in a clear and concise style, deals with the theory of Radon measures on Polish or locally compact spaces. With the main results being Luzin's theorem, the Riesz representation theorem, the Portmanteau theorem, and a characterization of locally compact spaces which are Polish, this chapter is a true invitation to study topological measure theory. The text addresses graduate students, who wish to learn the fundamentals in measure and integration theory as needed in modern analysis and probability theory. It will also be an important source for anyone teaching such a course.

## Summary
A self-contained graduate textbook on abstract measure and integration theory, translated from Bauer's German *Maß- und Integrationstheorie* by R. B. Burckel. It develops the modern (post-Lebesgue) abstract theory from $\sigma$-algebras through the Lebesgue integral, product measures and Fubini, and culminates in topological measure theory (Radon measures on Polish and locally compact spaces). It is the measure-theoretic companion volume to Bauer's *Probability Theory* (Vol. 23 in the same series), supplying the auxiliary results probability theory needs while standing alone as an introduction for analysts and mathematical economists.

## Research question
This is a pedagogical monograph rather than a research paper, so it poses no single empirical or theoretical question. Its organizing aim is: how should one construct, from first principles, a fully general abstract theory of measure and integration that (i) underwrites probability theory, (ii) recovers Lebesgue–Borel measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$ as a special case, and (iii) connects measure to topology via the theory of Radon measures? The unifying methodological thread is the centrality of the convergence theorems and the transformation/representation of measures.

## Method / identification
The "method" is rigorous deductive mathematical exposition, structured in four chapters and 31 sections.

**Chapter I (Measure Theory):** set systems and their generators ($\sigma$-algebras, Dynkin systems), contents, premeasures and measures; the Lebesgue premeasure; Carathéodory-style extension of a premeasure to a measure; Lebesgue–Borel measure on the number line; measurable mappings and image (push-forward) measures; and mapping properties of Lebesgue–Borel measure.

**Chapter II (Integration Theory):** measurable numerical functions on $\overline{\mathbb{R}}=[-\infty,+\infty]$; elementary (simple) functions and their integral; the integral of non-negative measurable functions built by monotone approximation $\int f\, d\mu=\sup\{\int u\, d\mu : u \text{ elementary},\, 0\le u\le f\}$; integrability and the $\mathcal{L}^p(\mu)$ spaces; almost-everywhere properties; the convergence theorems (monotone convergence, Fatou's lemma, dominated convergence); densities and the Radon–Nikodym theorem; signed measures (Hahn–Jordan decomposition); integration with respect to an image measure; stochastic convergence and equi-integrability.

**Chapter III (Product Measures):** products of $\sigma$-algebras and measures, the product measure together with Fubini's and Tonelli's theorems, and convolution of finite Borel measures.

**Chapter IV (Measures on Topological Spaces):** Borel, Borel-regular and Radon measures; Radon measures on Polish spaces; topological properties of locally compact spaces; construction of Radon measures on locally compact spaces; the Riesz representation theorem identifying positive linear functionals on $C_c(X)$ with Radon measures; vague/weak convergence of Radon measures; and vague compactness and metrizability.

## Key findings
The book's "results" are the classical theorems it proves in full generality:
- **Extension theorem:** a premeasure on a ring extends to a measure on the generated $\sigma$-algebra (uniquely under $\sigma$-finiteness).
- **Convergence theorems:** monotone convergence, Fatou's lemma, and Lebesgue's dominated convergence theorem — the technical heart that makes the Lebesgue integral superior to the Riemann integral under limit operations.
- **Radon–Nikodym theorem:** for $\sigma$-finite measures with $\nu \ll \mu$, there exists a density $f$ with $\nu(A)=\int_A f\, d\mu$.
- **Fubini–Tonelli theorem:** integration against a product measure may be carried out iteratively.
- **Riesz representation theorem:** positive linear functionals on $C_c(X)$ correspond bijectively to Radon measures.
- **Luzin's theorem** (measurable functions are "nearly continuous"), the **Portmanteau theorem** (equivalent characterizations of weak convergence of measures), and a **characterization of which locally compact spaces are Polish**.

## Contribution
Provides a clean, complete, and self-contained route into abstract measure and integration with two distinctive emphases: (1) thorough treatment of transformation of measures, parameter-dependent integrals, and the Radon–Nikodym theorem; and (2) a modern, concise Chapter IV on topological measure theory (Radon measures on Polish and locally compact spaces) that is largely new relative to the earlier combined probability/measure volume. It functions as the rigorous foundation for Bauer's probability text and as a reference for analysts, physicists, computer scientists, and mathematical economists.

## Limitations & open questions
As an introductory textbook the author explicitly bounds the scope: the theory is developed "far enough that... the reader can easily press ahead to deeper questions and the manifold applications," but deeper topics are deliberately left out. Topological prerequisites for Chapter IV are sometimes only referenced to standard literature rather than proved. The author notes that "even today the development of measure and integration theory is far from finished," pointing beyond the book toward ergodic theory, spectral theory, harmonic analysis on locally compact groups, and probability theory as live application areas — these are signposts rather than open problems in the research sense.

## Connections
The book is the measure-theoretic companion to Bauer's own *Probability Theory* (Bauer, 1996, Vol. 23 in the de Gruyter series), and is a sibling to the standard graduate references on the subject: Halmos, *Measure Theory* (1950); Billingsley, *Probability and Measure* (1995) and *Convergence of Probability Measures* (1968, the canonical source for the Portmanteau theorem and weak convergence); Rudin, *Real and Complex Analysis* (1987) for the Riesz representation theorem; Folland, *Real Analysis* (1999); and Parthasarathy, *Probability Measures on Metric Spaces* (1967) for the Polish-space material in Chapter IV. The historical lineage runs through Riemann's 1854 Habilitation, Peano and Jordan's theory of content, and the decisive work of Borel (1898) and Lebesgue (1902). For research in choice modeling and social preferences, the relevant downstream machinery is the Radon–Nikodym theorem (likelihoods/densities for estimation) and weak convergence of measures (asymptotics of estimators and limits of stochastic-choice distributions).

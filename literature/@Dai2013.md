> [!abstract] Abstract
> We study a scenario in which a firm designs the compensation contract for a salesperson who exerts unobservable effort to increase the level of uncertain demand and, jointly, the firm also decides the inventory level to be stocked. We use a newsvendor-type model in which actual sales depend on the realized demand but are limited by the inventory available, and unfulfilled demand cannot be observed. In this setup, under the optimal contract, the agent is paid a bonus for meeting a sales quota. Our key result is that it may be optimal for the firm to stock more than the first-best inventory level, because this enables the firm to obtain a more precise indicator of the salesperson's effort. The possibility of stockouts due to limited inventory also leads to several counterintuitive results, including the following: (i) relative to when stockouts are not considered, it may be optimal for the firm to pay a higher bonus even though limited inventory constrains sales; (ii) as inventory becomes more expensive, thereby forcing the firm to lower its inventory, the firm may nevertheless pay the agent a higher bonus; and (iii) if there is a lower probability that the agent's effort exertion leads to high demand, rather than lowering inventory due to the lower sales potential, the firm may increase inventory. This paper was accepted by J. Miguel Villas-Boas, marketing.

## Summary
Dai and Jerath integrate the salesforce-compensation moral-hazard problem with a newsvendor inventory decision. A firm jointly chooses a salesperson's incentive contract and its stocking level; sales are capped by inventory and unmet demand is unobservable ("demand censoring"). The optimal contract is a quota-bonus that rewards clearing inventory. The headline result is that agency considerations push the firm to *overstock* relative to first best, because more inventory makes high demand observable and thus sharpens the signal of the agent's hidden effort. Several counterintuitive comparative statics follow, including higher bonuses when inventory is more expensive and higher inventory when high-demand probability falls.

## Research question
How does the joint presence of (a) unobservable salesperson effort and (b) limited inventory that censors demand alter the optimal salesforce compensation contract and the optimal inventory level, relative to treating either problem in isolation? In particular, does inventory censoring change the structure and power of incentives, and does the agency problem distort the stocking decision?

## Method / identification
A static single-product principal-agent model with a risk-neutral, limited-liability salesperson and a risk-neutral firm. Demand $D$ is discrete with three levels $H>M>L>0$; effort $e\in\{e_H,e_L\}$ ("work" or "shirk"). High effort induces probabilities $p_s=\Pr(D=s\mid e_H)$, low effort $q_s=\Pr(D=s\mid e_L)$, with both summing to one and satisfying the monotone likelihood ratio property (MLRP):
$$\frac{p_L}{q_L}<\frac{p_M}{q_M}<\frac{p_H}{q_H}.$$
High effort costs $\psi>0$ (low effort normalized to 0); reservation utility is 0; pay must be nonnegative (limited liability); $p_L>0$ keeps the support of outcomes invariant to effort. The firm orders inventory $Q$ at unit cost $c$, earns revenue $r>c$ per unit; sales equal $\min\{Q,D\}$. Crucially, if $D>Q$ the firm cannot observe the excess (demand censoring), so it only observes $\min\{Q,D\}$. Timing: the firm jointly commits to $Q$ and the contract; the agent accepts and chooses unobservable $e$; demand and sales realize; the agent is paid. A condition $c<C$ rules out the trivial case $Q<M$. Solution proceeds by analyzing the contract conditional on $Q$ (Cases $Q=H$, $Q=M$, $M<Q<H$) and then optimizing over $Q$. Because the support is effort-invariant, the Mirrlees off-equilibrium-punishment argument cannot apply, so first best is never attainable.

## Data
None - this is a purely theoretical principal-agent/newsvendor model; results are propositions, with motivating field examples (office-products distributor, car dealerships, pharmaceutical detailing) and no estimation.

## Key findings
- **Benchmark 1 (agency, no inventory):** Reward only the top outcome $H$; optimal reward $S^*_{H}=\psi/(p_H-q_H)$ with expected payment $\psi/(1-q_H/p_H)>\psi$ (Result 1).
- **Benchmark 2 (inventory, first best):** Firm stocks $Q^{FB}=H$ if $c<\bar c$ and $Q^{FB}=M$ otherwise, where $\bar c=p_H r$; pays the agent exactly $\psi$ (Result 2).
- **Full scenario (Proposition 1):** The optimal contract is a quota-bonus rewarding the agent only for clearing inventory (highest *observable* outcome). Firm stocks $H$ for $c<\hat c$ and $M$ for $\hat c\le c<C$, where the threshold $\hat c=p_H r+\frac{\psi}{H-M}\big(\frac{1}{1-(q_H+q_M)/(p_H+p_M)}-\frac{1}{1-q_H/p_H}\big)$.
- **Lemma 1:** $\hat c>\bar c$. Hence for $\bar c\le c<\hat c$ the firm overstocks relative to first best.
- **Proposition 2:** The expected payment is weakly higher with inventory than without; the bonus is weakly higher if $p_M<q_M$ and weakly lower if $p_M\ge q_M$ - inventory can raise the *power* of the incentive contract.
- **Proposition 3 (headline):** The optimal inventory in the full scenario is weakly higher than first best; censoring distorts the effort signal, so the firm distorts inventory upward to restore signal informativeness (for medium $c$, not extreme).
- **Propositions 4-5:** As $p_H$ falls, expected payment rises faster with inventory; and if effort cost $\psi\ge\tilde\psi$ the firm *raises* inventory as $p_H$ falls (opposite of first best), because better observability becomes more valuable.
- **Proposition 6:** The first-best-vs-full profit gap (eq. 13) weakly increases in $c$ and weakly increases as $p_H$ falls; this gap equals the maximum the firm should invest to alleviate the moral-hazard/censoring problem.

## Contribution
The paper is among the first to jointly model salesforce incentive design and the inventory/newsvendor decision under demand censoring, bridging the marketing-operations interface. It shows the two problems interact non-trivially: agency motivates *overstocking* (inventory as an effort-information device), and inventory limits motivate *higher-powered* contracts. It provides a clean managerial interpretation of the profit gap as an upper bound on worthwhile monitoring investment, and several testable counterintuitive comparative statics (higher bonus when inventory is costlier; higher inventory when success probability falls).

## Limitations & open questions
- Analysis uses a three-point discrete demand and two effort levels; the authors claim qualitative robustness to any discrete distribution with three or more levels under MLRP and to multiple effort levels, but note full details are "available from the authors upon request" rather than in the paper.
- Overage/underage costs are assumed away (results claimed unchanged qualitatively if added).
- The model cannot handle backordering of excess units, since realized excess demand is assumed unobservable.
- Static, single-period, single-product, single-agent setting - dynamics, renegotiation, multiple products/territories, and continuous demand are left open.
- The first best is never attainable here because the support is effort-invariant; how results change when off-equilibrium punishments are feasible (as in Chu and Lai) is a contrasting open avenue.

## Connections
The paper builds directly on the agency-theoretic salesforce-compensation tradition of Basu, Lal, Srinivasan & Staelin (1985) and surveys by Prendergast (1999), and on quota-bonus and limited-liability contracting results in Park (1995), Kim (1997), and Oyer (2000). It is most closely related to Chu and Lai (2013) on salesforce contracting under demand censorship, sharing the overstocking bias but differing because Chu and Lai allow effort-dependent support (so the Mirrlees argument and first best can apply). The demand-censoring mechanism draws on Braden and Freimer (1991) and Besbes and Muharremoglu (2013), which lack agency. On joint incentive-inventory modeling it relates to Chen (2000, 2005), Plambeck and Zenios (2003), Saghafian and Chao (2011), and Jerath, Netessine & Zhang (2010), and more broadly to the marketing-operations interface literature (Eliashberg & Steinberg 1987, Pasternack 1985, Iyer, Narasimhan & Niraj 2007, Netessine & Taylor 2007). The MLRP and limited-liability machinery follow Laffont and Martimort (2001); the Mirrlees-argument contrast invokes Harris and Raviv (1979).

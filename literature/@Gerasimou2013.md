> [!abstract] Abstract
> A weak (strict) preference relation is continuous if it has a closed (open) graph; it is hemicontinuous if its upper and lower contour sets are closed (open). If preferences are complete these four conditions are equivalent. Without completeness continuity in each case is stronger than hemicontinuity. This paper provides general characterizations of continuity in terms of hemicontinuity for weak preferences that are modeled as (possibly incomplete) preorders and for strict preferences that are modeled as strict partial orders. Some behavioral implications associated with the two approaches are also discussed.

## Summary
When preferences may be incomplete — modeling either an individual's indecisiveness (incomparable alternatives) or the common, conflicting component of a group's preferences (e.g. the Pareto relation) — the standard equivalence between continuity and hemicontinuity breaks down: continuity becomes strictly stronger. Gerasimou closes the gap by introducing two new local conditions — *expanded local transitivity* for weak preferences (preorders) and *continuous local transitivity* for strict preferences (strict partial orders) — that, together with hemicontinuity, exactly characterize full continuity on arbitrary (resp. connected, compact, first-countable) spaces. Along the way he argues that hemicontinuity (and hence continuity) of an incomplete preorder forces *fragility*: arbitrarily small perturbations of strictly ranked alternatives can render them incomparable, which he treats as a descriptively and normatively undesirable behavioral prediction.

## Research question
Under incompleteness, when does the weaker, more "primitive" axiom of hemicontinuity (closedness/openness of upper and lower contour sets) imply the stronger axiom of full continuity (closedness/openness of the graph in the product topology)? And what behavioral content do these topological axioms carry once completeness is dropped?

## Method / identification
Pure axiomatic decision theory / order topology; no data. Let $X$ be a set of alternatives. Weak preference is a *preorder* $\succeq$ (reflexive, transitive), with asymmetric part $\succ$ (strict preference), symmetric part $\sim$ (indifference), and incomparability relation $\bowtie$ defined on the complement of $\succeq$. Strict preference is alternatively taken as primitive: a *strict partial order* $>$ (irreflexive, transitive). Upper/lower contour sets are $U_\succeq(x)=\{y:y\succeq x\}$ and $L_\succeq(x)=\{y:x\succeq y\}$.

Definitions: $\succeq$ is **hemicontinuous** if all $U_\succeq(x),L_\succeq(x)$ are closed; **continuous** if $\succeq$ is a closed subset of $X\times X$. The two novel local axioms:

- **Expands local transitivity** (Def. 4): for all $x,y,z$, if $x\,\%\,z$ and $z\,\%\,y$ (where $\%$ is the complement of $\succeq$) and $x\,\%\,y$, there exist open neighborhoods $N(x),N(y)$ with $x'\,\%\,y'$ for all $x'\in N(x),y'\in N(y)$.
- **Continuously locally transitive** (Def. 6, for $>$): a sequential closedness condition on $\succeq$ (the complement of $>$) ruling out sequences $x_n,y_n,z_n$ with $x_n\,\%\,y_n$, $y_n>z_n$ collapsing in the limit.

Proofs use net/sequence arguments and the order topology generated by taking $\{A: A=U_\succeq(x)\text{ or }A=L_\succeq(x)\}$ as a subbase for closed sets. The strict-order result invokes connectedness, compactness, and first-countability of $X$.

## Data
None — this is a theoretical paper in axiomatic choice theory and order topology; illustrations are mathematical examples (the usual partial order on $\mathbb{R}^2$, lexicographic-type orders, etc.), not empirical data.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1** (known, restated): under completeness, the four conditions — $\succ$ continuous, $\succ$ hemicontinuous, $\succeq$ continuous, $\succeq$ hemicontinuous — are all equivalent (citing Ward 1954, Bergstrom et al. 1976, Bridges & Mehta 1995).
- **Theorem 2** (Schmeidler 1971, restated): a nontrivial bi-hemicontinuous preorder on a *connected* space is complete. Since most economic domains ($\mathbb{R}^n$, the probability simplex, convex function spaces) are connected, this is powerful.
- **Corollary 3**: a hemicontinuous, nontrivial, *incomplete* preorder on a connected space is **fragile** — strictly ranked alternatives can be made incomparable by arbitrarily small perturbations. This is the paper's behavioral critique of imposing hemicontinuity on incomplete preorders.
- **Theorem 4** (main result, weak preferences): for a preorder on an *arbitrary* topological space, $\succeq$ is continuous $\iff$ $\succeq$ is hemicontinuous *and* its complement expands local transitivity. Theorem 1's equivalence is recovered as a special case.
- **Theorem 6** (strict preferences): on a connected, compact, first-countable space, a strict partial order $>$ is continuous $\iff$ $>$ is hemicontinuous *and* its complement is continuously locally transitive.
- **Theorem 5** (via Bergstrom et al. 1976): an *order-dense* strict partial order on an arbitrary space is hemicontinuous $\iff$ continuous; order-denseness follows from non-satiation (consumer theory) or from independence / a "betweenness"-type mixture axiom (choice under risk).

## Contribution
Provides the first general characterizations of continuity via hemicontinuity for *incomplete* preferences in both the weak-preference (preorder) and strict-preference (strict partial order) primitives, on arbitrary or mildly restricted spaces — generalizing the classical complete-case equivalence and the Bergstrom–Parks–Rader open-graph result. It also isolates a sharp behavioral diagnostic (fragility) showing why hemicontinuity is the more "primitive" axiom and why its imposition on incomplete preorders is problematic.

## Limitations & open questions
- The strict-preference characterization (Theorem 6) requires *connectedness, compactness, and first-countability*, unlike the arbitrary-space weak-preference result (Theorem 4); whether these can be relaxed is left open.
- The theory of incompleteness via strict preferences cannot distinguish *indifference* from *incomparability*, a modeling cost the paper flags but does not resolve.
- The novel local-transitivity conditions, while exact, are technical; their behavioral interpretation (especially for $>$) is acknowledged to be less transparent than the contour-set axioms.
- Whether fragility is genuinely descriptively damning, or whether it instead casts doubt on the original strict ranking, is posed as an interpretive question rather than settled.

## Connections
Builds directly on Schmeidler (1969, 1971) on competitive equilibria with incomplete preferences and on the completeness of nontrivial bi-hemicontinuous preorders, and on Bergstrom, Parks & Rader (1976) on preferences with open graphs (their Theorem 2 is shown to be a restatement of the classical equivalence). The order-topology machinery follows Ward (1954) and Bridges & Mehta (1995); the non-transitive consumer is Shafer (1974).

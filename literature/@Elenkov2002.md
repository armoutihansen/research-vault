> [!abstract] Abstract
> This study investigated concurrently the main effects of the transformational- and transactional-leadership behaviors on organizational performance of Russian companies, the moderating effects of support for innovation on the relationship between the transformational-leadership behaviors and organizational performance, and the impact of group cohesiveness on transformational-leadership behaviors in a Russian context. The results demonstrated that transformational leadership directly and positively predicted organizational performance of Russian companies over and beyond the impact of transactional leadership; Russian managers who displayed more transactional-leadership behaviors also made a positive contribution to the achievement of organizational goals; support for innovation significantly moderated the relationship between transformational leadership and organizational performance; and group cohesiveness was positively related to the ratings of transformational leadership. These research findings were, then, used to draw conclusions concerning finding new effective ways to promote organization development (OD) and to achieve better organizational performance results in Russia. The potential benefits for Russian companies of Full Range of Leadership Development Modules, process consultation (PC), sensitivity training, and other OD techniques were discussed.

## Summary
Elenkov tests, in a multi-source survey of 350 small private Russian firms during the post-Soviet transition, whether the Western "full-range" leadership framework (Bass) predicts firm-level organizational performance. Transformational leadership (charisma, individualized consideration, intellectual stimulation) predicts performance positively and incrementally beyond transactional leadership (contingent reward, active/passive management-by-exception); transactional behaviors also contribute positively. Support for innovation amplifies the transformational-performance link, and group cohesiveness raises transformational-leadership ratings. The paper argues this validates importing Western OD techniques into Russian firms.

## Research question
Do transformational- and transactional-leadership behaviors predict firm-level organizational performance in Russian companies, and is the transformational effect incremental over the transactional effect? Two contextual questions follow: (i) does support for innovation moderate the transformational-leadership-to-performance relationship, and (ii) is group cohesiveness associated with higher transformational-leadership ratings? Underlying these is the broader question of whether Western leadership and OD theory transfers to the post-communist, collectivist Russian context.

## Method / identification
Cross-sectional, multi-rater survey design with temporal separation to mitigate common-method bias. Leadership and contextual constructs were rated by subordinates (followers); the organizational-performance criterion was rated six months later by the focal top manager (leader), so predictor and criterion data come from different sources and different time points.

Two estimators are used:
- A **hierarchical (sequential) multiple regression** for Hypothesis 1. Transactional factors are entered first, transformational factors second, and a nested $F$-test asks whether the transformational block adds explanatory power over and above the transactional block. The reported incremental test is $F(3,246)=101.62$, $P<.01$.
- **Partial least squares (PLS)** structural path modeling (Wold, 1985; following Howell & Avolio, 1993) for the individual structural paths (Hypotheses 2a-c, 3, 4a-b), the moderated model (Hypothesis 5, with high vs. low support-for-innovation subsamples), and the group-cohesiveness paths (Hypothesis 6). PLS is chosen as suited to latent-variable structural models with modest samples and non-normal data.

Leadership behavior is measured with Bass & Avolio's (1990) Multifactor Leadership Questionnaire (MLQ) Form 10, deliberately chosen because it contains only behavior items (no effect items), avoiding the same-source contamination Howell & Avolio (1993) criticized in the original MLQ. Items rated on a 0-4 Likert frequency scale. Support for Innovation and an adapted Group Cohesiveness scale were validated with a 30-student US/Russian pilot; questionnaires were translated and back-translated into Russian.

## Data
Stratified random sample (by region and industry) of 350 small single-business private Russian firms ($\le 50$ employees) drawn from the 1998 State Institute for Statistics directory of 50,000+ private companies, across five European-Russia industrial centers (Moscow, Suzdal, St. Petersburg, Novgorod, Petrozavodsk) and twelve industries. With backing from a Russian Chamber of Commerce VP, 950 managers were contacted; 751 usable responses returned: 253 senior managers (84% response) supplying performance ratings and 498 immediate subordinates (77%) supplying leadership/context ratings. Respondents averaged 45.5 years; 91.8% held college degrees. The moderated model uses an $n=160$ subsample.

## Key findings
- **H1 (incremental validity):** Transformational leadership predicts firm performance over and beyond transactional leadership; the nested $F(3,246)=101.62$ is significant. Multiple correlation for transformational factors ($R=.81$) substantially exceeds that for transactional factors ($R=.47$); the combined $R=.85$.
- **H2a-c (supported):** Positive significant paths from charisma ($0.28$), individualized consideration ($0.16$), and intellectual stimulation ($0.15$) to performance.
- **H3 (supported):** Contingent reward positively predicts performance (path $0.19$).
- **H4a-b (rejected):** Contrary to the negative-effect prediction, both passive ($0.11$) and active ($0.12$) management-by-exception are positively and significantly related to performance. Total variance explained $\approx 41\%$.
- **H5 (supported):** Support for innovation moderates each transformational-performance path; paths are significantly stronger under high support for innovation. Intellectual stimulation flips sign — positive under high support, negative under low support ($t=19.87$).
- **H6 (supported):** Group cohesiveness is positively and significantly related to the transformational-leadership dimensions (charisma, individualized consideration, intellectual stimulation).

## Contribution
First study to empirically test, at the company level and over an extended period, whether full-range leadership behaviors predict organizational performance in Russia while modeling contextual moderators. Methodological contributions: temporal and source separation of predictor and criterion to reduce common-method bias; behavior-only MLQ Form 10 to avoid same-source confounds; measurement instruments adapted to Russian managers' cognitive context. Substantively, it counters Luthans et al.'s (1998a) argument against transformational leadership in post-communist firms, attributing the divergence to methodology, and motivates importing Western OD techniques (Full Range Leadership Development, process consultation, sensitivity training).

## Limitations & open questions
- The positive management-by-exception effect contradicts most prior research; the author can only speculatively attribute it to Russia's authoritarian-management legacy, leaving the mechanism an open question (echoing Podsakoff et al., 1984 on fair aversive reinforcement).
- Performance is a perceptual, leader-self-reported criterion rather than objective financial outcomes, despite the temporal separation.
- Scope restricted to small single-business firms to limit organizational-confound threats to construct validity; generalizability to large or multi-business Russian firms is untested.
- Cross-sectional design (one predictor wave, one criterion wave) limits causal identification; no within-firm or panel variation.
- The Luthans et al. (1998a) divergence is unresolved beyond the methodology conjecture.
- Cultural-context claims (collectivism, weak market signals, uncertainty explaining charisma's effectiveness) are interpretive, not directly tested.

## Connections
Builds directly on Bass's (1985) "leadership beyond expectations" and the full-range model (Bass & Avolio, 1990, 1994), and on Burns's (1978) transactional/transformational distinction. The behavior-only MLQ Form 10 design and the support-for-innovation moderator follow Howell & Avolio (1993). The transactional path-goal grounding traces to House (1971, 1977). Charismatic-leadership theory draws on Conger & Kanungo (1987, 1988) and House, Spangler & Woycke (1991). Context-as-moderator arguments connect to Pawar & Eastman (1997) and Pillai & Meindl (1998) on collectivism, crisis, and charisma. Group-cohesiveness-performance links relate to Mullen & Cooper (1995). Within the Russia/transition-management literature it engages Welsh et al. (1993), Holt et al. (1994), Puffer et al. (1994, 1997), Buchko et al. (1998), Banai & Teng (1996), and directly disputes Luthans et al. (1998a) on the "dark side" of leadership in post-communist economies.

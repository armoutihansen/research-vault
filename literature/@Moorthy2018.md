> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper develops a new rationale for decentralization in distribution channels: providing a one-stop comparison shopping experience for consumers. In our duopoly model, when consumers are knowledgeable about their brand preferences, each manufacturer would distribute through its own vertically integrated retail outlets only. When some consumers are unsure about their brand preferences, however, it may be optimal for one of the manufacturers to also distribute through its competitor's outlets. The resulting equilibrium has several interesting properties. First, only one of the manufacturers chooses to add competitor-outlet distribution, not both—even when the manufacturers are symmetric. Second, the manufacturer distributing through its competitor's outlets also distributes through its own outlets, i.e., its distribution strategy is a hybrid strategy, combining vertical integration and decentralization. Third, when the manufacturers' brands are asymmetric, it is the weaker brand that has a stronger incentive to pursue hybrid distribution. Fourth, the competitor's outlets in question welcome the new brand, even when no consumer would actually buy the new brand—a case of pure showrooming. These results highlight the linkages between distribution strategy, shopping efficiency, and retail formats. Shopping costs and consumers' uncertainty about their own brand preferences create a demand for multibrand retailing, and in pursuing this demand, manufacturers may eschew the efficiency advantages of vertical integration in favor of hybrid distribution. However, the fact that only one of the manufacturers chooses to do so suggests that this strategy also has weaknesses, which we discuss in the paper.

## Summary

The paper proposes a *third* rationale for channel decentralization—beyond eliminating double marginalization and the strategic-softening motive of the McGuire–Staelin tradition—namely, providing **one-stop comparison shopping** for consumers who do not yet know their own brand preferences. In a duopoly where each manufacturer is vertically integrated, allowing one manufacturer to *also* sell through its rival's store turns that store into a multibrand outlet, letting uncertain consumers resolve their preferences in a single visit. This shopping-efficiency benefit motivates a *hybrid* distribution strategy (own outlets + competitor outlets), but in equilibrium only one of the two symmetric manufacturers adopts it, and under brand asymmetry the *weaker* brand has the stronger incentive to do so.

## Research question

When (if ever) should a manufacturer distribute its product through a *competitor's* retail outlet, and which manufacturer in a duopoly will do so? More broadly: how do consumers' shopping costs and uncertainty about their own brand preferences shape distribution strategy and the single-brand-versus-multibrand retail format?

## Method / identification

A fully game-theoretic IO/marketing model solved by subgame perfection. Two manufacturers, $A$ and $B$, each own a vertically integrated store. The timing is: (1) choose distribution structure (status quo Case 1, hybrid Cases 2/$2'$, or mutual hybrid Case 3); (2) set wholesale price $w$ on any decentralized brand; (3) set retail price(s). Two side constraints are imposed: competitor-outlet distribution must not make the host competitor worse off (it needs the rival's cooperation), and any hybrid equilibrium must make the deviating manufacturer *strictly* better off than the status quo (complexity must pay).

Demand: a unit mass of consumers, each with downward-sloping demand $D(p)$ ($D(p)>0,\ D'(p)<0$ for $p<\bar v$, $D\equiv 0$ for $p\ge\bar v$). A fraction $\alpha$ are $A$-loyalists, $\beta$ are $B$-loyalists, and $\gamma=1-\alpha-\beta$ are switchers (buy the cheaper brand)—a standard Narasimhan-style loyal/switcher partition. Two innovations: (i) **costly shopping**, each store visit costs $t$, distributed $H(\cdot)$ on $[0,\theta]$ with $H(0)=0,\ H'(0)=0$; (ii) a fraction $\lambda$ of **uncertain consumers** must visit *both* brands' stores to learn whether they are loyalists or switchers, while $1-\lambda$ are knowledgeable. Prices are found by costly sequential search.

Key derived objects: surplus $y(p)=\int_p^{\bar v} D(x)\,dx$ (so a consumer shops iff $t\le y(p^e)$); the decentralized wholesale price $w_d$; monopoly and decentralized retail prices $p^m=p(0)$ and $p^d=p(w_d)$; surplus thresholds $t^m=y(p^m)$, $t^d=y(p^d)$, $t^i=t^m-t^d$; and the double-marginalization efficiency index $\delta_d=w_d D(p^d)/\pi^m$ with $\pi^m=p^m D(p^m)$.

## Key findings

**Lemma 1:** $(\delta_d\to 1)\Leftrightarrow(p^d\to p^m)\Leftrightarrow(t^d\to t^m)$—as double marginalization vanishes, the decentralized channel mimics vertical integration.

**Lemma 2 (Diamond-paradox pricing):** in *every* distribution structure, equilibrium retail prices are monopoly prices $p^m$ for integrated products and $p(w)$ for decentralized ones. A multibrand store optimally prices to steer switchers to its *own* brand, since the store keeps the full integrated channel profit on its own brand but only a share of the smaller decentralized profit on the rival's.

**Lemma 3:** the equilibrium wholesale price through a competitor channel is $w_d$ (rational price expectations).

**Proposition 1:** when all consumers are knowledgeable ($\lambda=0$), the status quo (pure vertical integration) is the *unique* equilibrium—multibrand stores have no value when everyone already one-stop shops.

**Symmetric model (Propositions 2–4):** uncertain consumers shop more under hybrid distribution (Lemma 4: $t^{u2}>t^{u1}$), and the host store always welcomes the rival brand (Lemma 5)—even a "pure showrooming" case where store $A$ stocks brand $B$ but *sells none of it*, because mere presence boosts the uncertain-shopper pool. Deviation to hybrid pays iff $\lambda>0$ and
$$1-\gamma>\frac{H_{u1}}{\min\{H_{u2},\ \delta_d H_{u2}+(1-\delta_d)H_i\}},$$
which favors low $\gamma$ (few switchers) and high $\delta_d$. Two drawbacks limit it: a **cannibalization** effect (the inefficient competitor channel steals integrated own-channel sales) and a **strengthening-the-competitor** effect (all uncertain switchers are ceded to the rival). **Proposition 3:** Case 3 (both manufacturers go hybrid) is never an equilibrium—reciprocating nullifies the first mover's advantage. So equilibrium is either Case 1 or exactly *one* of Cases 2/$2'$.

**Asymmetric model (Proposition 5):** whenever the *stronger* brand would deviate to hybrid, so would the *weaker* brand—the weaker brand has the strictly stronger case. The weaker brand selling through the strong brand's store maximizes uncertain-shopper expansion *and* faces essentially no strengthening-the-competitor loss (it cedes the switchers either way), so its downside is smaller.

## Contribution

Introduces shopping efficiency / one-stop comparison shopping as a novel driver of distribution strategy, reframing "selling through a competitor's outlet" not as decentralization per se but as a deliberate shift in *retail format* from single-brand to multibrand. It rationalizes asymmetric hybrid distribution among symmetric firms, gives a new account of beneficial showrooming (a store gains from showrooming a product it never sells), and links distribution strategy, shopping behavior, and retail format in a single tractable model. It departs from the McGuire–Staelin / Coughlan / Moorthy / Choi tradition, where consumers are reduced-form demand and retailers are single-brand by assumption.

## Limitations & open questions

- Store patronage depends *only* on the number and strength of brands carried; the authors flag that richer store choice (number of categories, service quality à la Iyer & Kuksov 2012) could *overturn* the weaker-brand prescription (the Macy's/Levi's jeans example runs opposite to Proposition 5).
- The monopoly-price result hinges on $H'(0)=0$ and sequential price search; with $H'(0)>0$ or advertised prices, mixed-strategy price equilibria emerge (as in Lal & Villas-Boas 1996), though the authors argue the qualitative results survive because they depend only on $p^d>p^m$.
- Two-part tariffs are assumed impracticable; if feasible, the model degenerates to a single multibrand store, eliminating the puzzle.
- Brand-preference learning is decoupled from price learning (uncertain consumers always complete the two-store learning process); an integrated search model (Wolinsky 1986; Anderson & Renault 1999) allowing mid-search abandonment is left open.
- Costs (manufacturing, retailing, channel-adding) are assumed away; the analysis is a two-manufacturer duopoly, with extension to $n>2$ only asserted.

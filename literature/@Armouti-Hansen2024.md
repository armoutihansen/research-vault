> [!abstract] Abstract
> Many jobs serve a social purpose beyond profit maximization, contributing positively to society. This paper uses a modified principal-agent gift-exchange game with positive externality (prosocial treatment) to study how workers' prosocial motivation interacts with the use of efficiency wages in stimulating effort. We find that prosocial motivation and efficiency wages are independent in stimulating effort: compared to a standard gift-exchange game (GE treatment), the presence of the externality shifts the agents' effort choice function upwards without affecting its slope. Thus, if principals were profit-maximizers, wage offers should be the same in both treatments. However, principals offer higher wages in the prosocial treatment. We show that this is due to principals in the GE treatment highly underestimating agents' reciprocity and thereby offering wages below the profit-maximizing level. Results from robustness-checks further suggest that our findings are unlikely to be driven by a simple efficiency effect.

## Summary
A laboratory experiment embeds a positive social externality (a charitable donation triggered by the agent's effort) into the one-shot principal-agent gift-exchange game. Prosocial motivation and wage-reciprocity turn out to be independent effort motivators: the externality shifts agents' effort-wage function up without changing its slope, so a profit-maximizing principal should offer the same wage in both treatments. Yet principals offer about 10 points more in the prosocial treatment. The driver is biased beliefs: in the standard game principals badly underestimate agents' reciprocity (the slope) and therefore offer below the profit-maximizing wage, whereas the social mission corrects this bias and principals offer near-optimal efficiency wages.

## Research question
Are workers' prosocial motivation and reciprocity (efficiency-wage) concerns complements, substitutes, or independent in stimulating effort, and how does their relationship shape wage contracts? Do principals correctly predict workers' effort responses and therefore offer the "right" wage when the job has a social purpose versus when it does not?

## Method / identification
A modified one-shot principal-agent gift-exchange game. A theoretical benchmark assumes an additively separable agent utility $U(w,m,e;\theta)=w-\frac{c}{2}e^2+M(w,m,e;\theta)$, where $w$ is wage, $e$ effort, $m$ the job's social impact, and $\theta$ a type vector (distributed $F_\theta$) weighting intrinsic benefits. The intrinsic term $M\ge 0$ encodes prosocial motivation ($M_{em}\ge 0$) and reciprocity ($M_{ew}\ge 0$). The principal is a risk-neutral profit-maximizer choosing $w$ to maximize $r\,\bar e^*(w,m)-w$ with $r>0$, knowing $F_\theta$ and the form of $M$ but not $\theta$. Three comparative-statics predictions follow from an exogenous rise in $m$: (1) the lowest acceptable wage falls, $\bar w_m<0$; (2) effort for any wage rises, $\bar e^*_m(w,m)>0$; (3) the profit-maximizing wage moves with the sign of the third cross-partial $\bar M_{ewm}$ — i.e. it rises iff the social mission steepens the expected effort-wage slope, otherwise (Case 1, separability) it is unchanged.

Experimental design: between-subject, six original sessions at the University of Cologne (z-Tree). Stage 1 elicits prosociality (dictator game with charitable donation) and fairness (ultimatum game). Stage 2 is the gift-exchange game. Treatment variation: GE (standard) vs prosocial, where the agent's effort additionally generates a charity donation $\Pi_C=25e$ paid by the experimenter. Wages $w\in\{1,3,5,10,15,\dots,95\}$; effort $e\in\{1,\dots,10\}$ with convex cost (Table 1, $c\approx 0.38$ quadratic fit, $R^2\approx 0.98$). Payoffs on acceptance: $\Pi_P=10e-w+100$, $\Pi_A=w-c(e)+100$; rejection yields (100, 105). The strategy method elicits each agent's full effort-for-each-wage schedule, reconstructing an individual optimal effort function. Crucially, principals' beliefs about agents' acceptance and effort at every wage are elicited incentivized (0.5 points per correct guess) and unannounced to limit hedging; from these a per-pair "expected profit-maximizing wage" is computed.

## Data
Original experiment: 190 students (94 GE, 96 prosocial). Two later robustness treatments (May 2022, same Cologne pool, excluding prior participants): neutral-third-party treatment (90 subjects) and an efficiency treatment that removes the externality but holds total surplus constant by giving the extra surplus to the principal. Behavioral lab data only; no field data.

## Key findings
- Result 1 (supports Prediction 1): the average minimum acceptable wage is marginally lower in the prosocial treatment — more agents accept below-outside-option wages (~17-19% vs ~4%, $p=0.07$).
- Result 2 (supports Prediction 2): for any given wage, effort is higher in the prosocial treatment, but the slope of the effort-wage function is unchanged (interaction term ~0, insignificant). Prosocial motivation and reciprocity are independent — agents' utility is additively separable (the model's Case 1).
- Result 3: contrary to the profit-maximizing benchmark, principals offer significantly higher wages in the prosocial treatment (~36 vs ~26 points, +10, $p=0.01$).
- Result 4: in both treatments principals act as profit-maximizers given their beliefs, but in the GE treatment those beliefs are biased — principals strongly underestimate agents' reciprocity (the effort-wage slope; their expected effort function is about half as steep as the real one, with expected effort ~60% below actual at $w=95$). Hence GE-treatment offered, guessed, and belief-implied profit-maximizing wages are all significantly below the true profit-maximizing wage, while in the prosocial treatment beliefs are accurate and offers are near-optimal.
- Robustness: the neutral-third-party treatment falls "in between" (consistent direction, loses marginal significance, as expected since externality is weaker). The efficiency treatment (surplus to principal, no externality) differs sharply from the prosocial treatment, so results are not a pure efficiency effect — the presence of a third-party externality is necessary; the specific charity identity is not (consistent with Cassar, 2019).

## Contribution
Identifies a new hidden benefit of a social mission: because principals harbor pessimistic beliefs about reciprocity, efficiency wages and an organization's social purpose act as complements in motivating effort even though they are substitutes from the agent's preference side. Provides one of the few incentivized strategy-method elicitations of principals' beliefs in a gift-exchange game, documenting systematic underestimation of worker reciprocity that the social mission corrects. Offers a new explanation for low one-shot gift-exchange efficiency (mispredicted, not absent, social preferences) and a new channel for the profit/non-profit wage differential debate.

## Limitations & open questions
- Cannot fully disentangle externality from efficiency: a positive externality definitionally raises efficiency; the efficiency treatment may introduce principal-specific inequity aversion not present toward a third party. The endogeneity issue is shared with the broader literature.
- One-shot only — no long-term relationships. A natural extension is multiple rounds with rematching to allow learning; the authors conjecture biased beliefs may persist because pessimistic principals never receive disconfirming counterfactual information (cf. Caria & Falco, 2022).
- Social impact is exogenous, agent-chosen, and costless to the employer. Open extensions: endogenous/costly social impact (e.g. costly CSR investment, which could induce CSR-reciprocity or backlash) and allowing agent self-selection into mission-oriented organizations (expected to strengthen results).
- Lab abstraction (strategy method, monetary effort levels); field evidence on gift exchange is mixed.

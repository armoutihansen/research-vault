## Summary
Thaler and Benartzi propose and field-test **Save More Tomorrow (SMarT)**, a *prescriptive* retirement-savings program that exploits behavioral regularities — bounded rationality, present-biased (hyperbolic) discounting, procrastination/inertia, and nominal loss aversion — rather than fighting them. Employees pre-commit to channeling a portion of *future* pay raises into their 401(k); contributions rise automatically with each raise until a cap, and participants may opt out anytime. Across three corporate implementations, take-up and retention were high and saving rates roughly tripled, demonstrating that behavioral economics can be turned into effective real-world policy design ("libertarian paternalism").

## Research question
Why do many employees in defined-contribution plans save below the life-cycle optimum, and can a program built on behavioral principles raise their saving rates substantially without coercion? The standard-theory null hypothesis is that already-optimizing workers would have no interest in a commitment device; the behavioral prediction is that such a program would be attractive and effective.

## Method / identification
The paper is a **field study / proof-of-concept**, not a controlled experiment with random assignment. The core design synthesizes four behavioral building blocks into a four-ingredient mechanism:
1. Employees are solicited well *before* a scheduled raise (long sign-up-to-start lag exploits hyperbolic discounting).
2. Contribution increases begin only with the first paycheck *after* a raise (avoids a perceived nominal loss in take-home pay — loss aversion, money illusion).
3. The contribution rate keeps rising at each raise up to a preset cap (inertia/status-quo bias works *for* saving).
4. Opt-out is allowed at any time (preserves the libertarian character).

Three implementations are reported: (a) a midsize manufacturing firm (1998) with one-on-one financial-consultant meetings and a 3 pp/year increase; (b) Ispat Inland, a steel company, using only a single mailed invitation, 2 pp/year, 18% cap; (c) Philips Electronics (two divisions A and O vs. 28 control divisions), where increases were *not* tied to raises and employees chose a 1/2/3 pp increment (2 pp default), 10% cap. Identification relies on within-group before/after saving-rate comparisons, a control-division comparison at Philips, and the authors' argument that selection ("taste for saving") and information channels cannot explain the results, since SMarT was offered precisely to those who *declined* an immediate 5 pp increase and all consulted employees received the same information. Sections IV–V add **simulations**: Financial Engines software computes median income-replacement ratios for hypothetical workers (ages 25/35/45/55; incomes \$25k/\$50k/\$75k), and a Hewitt Associates dataset (≈539,516 employees in 15 firms) underpins ten-year projections of aggregate saving-rate effects under three rollout strategies.

## Data
- **First implementation:** 315 eligible employees; 286 met the consultant; 207 offered SMarT; 162 enrolled (78%); attrition tracked over four annual raises.
- **Ispat Inland:** 5,817 eligible employees retained May–Oct 2001.
- **Philips Electronics:** 815 test-group employees (Divisions A and O) vs. ~14,458 control; rich demographic/participation table.
- **Simulation inputs:** Hewitt Associates 401(k) records (≈539,516 employees, 15 large firms); Financial Engines projection software; John Hancock and Fidelity benchmarks.

## Key findings
- **High take-up:** 78% of those declining an immediate 5 pp increase joined SMarT in the first firm; ~80% of joiners stayed through four raises.
- **Large saving-rate gains:** In the first implementation, SMarT participants' average saving rate rose from **3.5% to 13.6%** over 40 months (about a quadrupling) — higher than the 9.1%→8.8% of those who took the consultant's one-time recommendation.
- **Inexpensive mailing still works:** At Ispat, 18.1% of current savers joined via a single letter, raising their rate ~2 pp.
- **Decoupling from raises is feasible:** At Philips, where increases were not tied to raises, joiners still raised saving rates (~1.5 pp for existing savers; large jumps for new enrollees), with apparent peer/spillover effects on non-joiners (consistent with Duflo & Saez).
- **Adequacy:** Post-SMarT median income-replacement ratios rise sharply (e.g., 57%→108% for a 25-year-old at \$25k); only one cell exceeds 100%, so workers are *not* induced to oversave.
- **Macro potential:** Each 1 pp rise in the employee saving rate ≈ \$25 billion in additional annual contributions nationally; a 5 pp gain ≈ \$125 billion ≈ 1.5% of disposable personal income — largely *new* saving from low/zero savers.

## Contribution
The paper pioneers using behavioral economics *constructively* — to engineer a welfare-improving institution — rather than merely to document anomalies. It introduces SMarT as a canonical example of **libertarian paternalism** (Sunstein & Thaler), influencing later automatic-escalation defaults and the U.S. Pension Protection Act of 2006. It also rebuts two standard critiques of behavioral economics: reliance on low-stakes lab studies and ex-post storytelling, by deriving an a priori design and testing it in the high-stakes field.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit that:
- None of the implementations is a **randomized controlled experiment**; participants self-select, so causal magnitudes are uncertain (they argue selection and information confounds are implausible but cannot fully rule them out).
- It is **not known which design features are essential** versus merely attractive — e.g., whether linking increases to raises, the sign-up-to-start lag, or one-on-one solicitation is load-bearing; "only a controlled experiment" can decide whether offering a *choice* of increment helps or hurts.
- The **financial-planner channel is effective but costly and confounded** by self-selection (electing to meet a planner may itself signal a taste for saving).
- Optimal **parameter values** (lag length, increment size, cap, education intensity) are not pinned down by theory and must be learned as more firms adopt the plan.
- Long-run persistence and the interaction with **employee turnover** and employer matching remain open (simulations make simplifying assumptions in both directions).

## Connections
The behavioral foundations draw on Laibson (1997) and O'Donoghue & Rabin (1999, 2001) on hyperbolic discounting and procrastination; Strotz (1955) and Ainslie (1975) on time inconsistency; and [[@Samuelson1988|Samuelson & Zeckhauser (1988)]] on status quo bias. The saving framework builds on Shefrin & Thaler's (1988) behavioral life-cycle hypothesis and Thaler & Shefrin (1981) on self-control. Loss aversion and money illusion evidence comes from [[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Kahneman (1992)]], Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler (1990, 1986), and Shafir, Diamond & Tversky (1997). The automatic-enrollment results of Madrian & Shea (1999) and Choi, Laibson, Madrian & Metrick (in press) motivate the default-design comparisons, while spillover effects echo Duflo & Saez (2000). The normative/descriptive/prescriptive taxonomy follows Raiffa (1982), and the policy framing is developed in [[@Thaler2003|Sunstein & Thaler (2003)]] on libertarian paternalism.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> We analyse preferences over finite decision problems in order to model decision-makers with "changing tastes". We provide conditions on these preferences that identify the Strotz model of consistent planning. Building on an example given by Peleg and Yaari (1973), we show that for problems with infinitely many choices, Strotz's representation of preferences may not be well defined. For that case, we propose a well-defined approximation which is empirically indistinguishable from the Strotz preference that is being approximated.

## Summary
Gul and Pesendorfer recast Strotz's "changing tastes" / time-inconsistency model as a *revealed-preference* theory. Instead of treating an agent's expectations about future behaviour as a primitive, they take as primitive a single period-0 preference $\succeq$ over *decision problems* (menus of dynamic continuation choices). They give choice-theoretic axioms that exactly characterise Strotz consistent planning and its Phelps–Pollak $\beta$–$\delta$ subclass, show how to read the agent's implied expectations off a canonical representation, and prove that with continuous consumption the Strotz value function generically fails to exist for horizons of four or more periods. Their fix is to approximate any Strotz preference arbitrarily well by a well-behaved self-control preference, so no finite data set can tell the two apart.

## Research question
Can the Strotz model of decision-making under changing tastes be identified from *observable choice behaviour* alone — without positing how the agent forms expectations about its own future actions — and does the model even have a well-defined representation when the consumption space is continuous?

## Method / identification
The primitive is a complete, transitive preference $\succeq$ over the set $Z_1$ of $T$-period decision problems, built inductively as $Z_t := K(C \times Z_{t+1})$ with $C$ a finite consumption set; each $z_t$ is a finite menu of pairs $(c, z_{t+1})$ (current consumption plus continuation problem). The agent's period-0 ranking of these "assets" is treated as data. The central axiom is **No Compromise (NC)**: $\{h, z_t \cup z_t'\} \sim \{h, z_t\}$ or $\{h, z_t \cup z_t'\} \sim \{h, z_t'\}$ — merging two menus yields the value of one of them, which permits a strict *preference for commitment* ($x_1 \succ x_1 \cup y_1$) while ruling out Kreps-style preference for flexibility. A weak Strotz representation is a value function $W(z) = \max_{\phi \in P^*(\bar V)} U(\phi^T(z))$, where $U$ ranks consumption paths, $\bar V = (V_1,\dots,V_T)$ are period-$t$ objective functions, $P^*(\bar V)$ is the set of consistent plans, and $\phi^T(\eta)$ is the path a plan $\phi$ induces from node $\eta$. To pin down $\beta$–$\delta$ preferences they add **Temptation by Current Consumption (TCC)**, which says that adding an alternative with the same current consumption but a worse continuation than an existing option leaves welfare unchanged. To capture *sophistication* they enrich the primitives with period-$t$ choice functions $C_t$ satisfying Houthakker's axiom (Axiom H, equivalent to rational choice) plus **Independence of Redundant Alternatives (IRA)**: $(c, z_{t+1}) \in C_t(h, z_t) \Rightarrow \{h, c, z_{t+1}\} \sim \{h, z_t\}$. The continuous-consumption analysis replaces $C$ with $D = [0,1]$, requires continuous, strictly increasing $U, U_t$, and introduces **local preference for commitment**.

## Data
None — this is a pure decision-theory / axiomatic paper. There are no empirical data; all "tests" are hypothetical choice experiments over decision problems (e.g. liquid vs. illiquid assets, store A vs. store B), and Example 3 is a worked $\beta$–$\delta$ counterexample.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1** (weak Strotz): $\succeq$ satisfies Axioms P and NC iff it has a weak Strotz representation $(U, \bar V)$.
- **Theorem 2** (canonical representation): any plan optimal under *some* Strotz representation is optimal under the constructed canonical $(U, \bar V)$; hence the canonical representation lets one elicit the set of choices the agent expects to make in each future period.
- **Theorem 3** (sophistication): given Axiom P and rational period-$t$ choice (Axiom H), IRA holds iff there is a weak Strotz representation whose $V_t$ are maximised by the observed $C_t$. So **IRA is the unique observable implication of sophisticated (correct-expectations) Strotz behaviour**; naive agents (O'Donoghue–Rabin) satisfy P and NC but violate IRA.
- **Theorem 4** (Phelps–Pollak): P, NC and TCC together characterise the generalized $\beta$–$\delta$ model, where $U_t(c_1,\dots,c_T) = u(c_t) + \beta \sum_{k=1}^{T-t} \delta^k u(c_{t+k})$ and period-0 $U = \sum_t \delta^t u(c_t)$.
- **Theorem 5** (non-existence): a continuous, monotone PP preference with local preference for commitment can exist only if $T \le 3$. Example 3 ($T=4$, $\delta=1$, $\beta=0.5$) exhibits a problem with no optimal period-1 choice — a sharpening of Peleg and Yaari (1973).
- **Theorem 6** (approximation): any (weak) Strotz preference $(U, V_1,\dots,V_T)$ is represented exactly on $Z_1$ by a Gul–Pesendorfer self-control preference $(U, \alpha V_1,\dots,\alpha V_T)$ for $\alpha$ large enough that $\alpha V > \tilde U$, and this extends continuously to the continuous domain $\tilde Z_1$. Hence no finite data set distinguishes Strotz consistent planning from self-control preferences.

## Contribution
The paper supplies the *behavioural foundations* for the workhorse models of time-inconsistency (Strotz consistent planning; Laibson/Phelps–Pollak $\beta$–$\delta$), turning expectation-based constructs into testable revealed-preference axioms. It cleanly separates *what tempts* the agent ($V_t$) from *whether expectations are correct* (sophistication = IRA), exposes a genuine existence failure of the Strotz program under continuous choice, and offers a time-*consistent* reinterpretation in which observed choices are welfare-maximising from every period's perspective — making welfare and policy evaluation (e.g. a tax discouraging "tempting" high consumption) unambiguous, since one need only ask whether the agent would vote for the policy when it is introduced.

## Limitations & open questions
- The existence result is sharply negative: with continuous consumption and genuine changing tastes, no Strotz value function exists for $T \ge 4$ — the model is *only* approximately, not exactly, well defined in rich choice environments.
- The approximation in Theorem 6 is observationally perfect only for *finite* data sets; the authors stress no finite experiment can separate Strotz behaviour from self-control, leaving open whether any conceivable richer evidence could.
- The full theory presumes the analyst can observe the period-0 ranking over *all* decision problems (not just commitment options); the practical elicitation of such menu preferences is left open.
- Welfare evaluation rests on adopting the time-consistent reinterpretation; the paper notes the time-inconsistent (multiple-selves) reading yields genuinely ambiguous welfare, a tension it reframes but does not resolve.

## Connections
The paper builds directly on Strotz (1955) on consistent planning and on Peleg and Yaari (1973), whose non-existence example under continuous choice it generalizes (Theorem 5). The $\beta$–$\delta$ functional form is from Phelps and Pollak (1968), popularized by Laibson (1997); naive vs. sophisticated agents are framed against O'Donoghue and Rabin (1999). The menu-preference / revealed-preference methodology descends from Kreps (1979) on preference for flexibility, which the No Compromise axiom deliberately inverts (commitment rather than flexibility). The approximation result imports the self-control / temptation preferences of the authors' own Gul and Pesendorfer (2001, 2004), placing this paper at the bridge between the consistent-planning and temptation traditions of modelling self-control.

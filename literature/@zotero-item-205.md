> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper develops a theory of the allocation of formal authority (the right to decide) and real authority (the effective control over decisions) within organizations, and it illustrates how a formally integrated structure can accommodate various degrees of "real" integration. Real authority is determined by the structure of information, which in turn depends on the allocation of formal authority. An increase in an agent's real authority promotes initiative but results in a loss of control for the principal. After spelling out (some of) the main determinants of the delegation of formal authority within organizations, the paper examines a number of factors that increase the subordinates' real authority in a formally integrated structure: overload, lenient rules, urgency of decision, reputation, performance measurement, and multiplicity of superiors. Finally, the amount of communication in an organization is shown to depend on the allocation of formal authority.

## Summary
Aghion and Tirole build a tractable principal-agent model that distinguishes *formal authority* (the contractual right to decide) from *real authority* (effective control over the decision, which accrues to whoever is better informed). The key engine is a two-way interaction: the allocation of formal authority shapes each party's incentive to acquire information, and the resulting information structure determines who actually controls the decision. Delegating formal authority to a subordinate boosts his initiative but costs the principal control; even under centralization, factors such as overload, urgency, reputation, better performance measurement, and multiple principals can hand de facto control to the agent. The paper also endogenizes communication, showing it depends on the authority relationship and on preference congruence.

## Research question
When and why is formal authority over a decision delegated within a hierarchy, and what determines whether the formal holder of authority retains *real* (effective) control rather than rubber-stamping a subordinate's proposal? How does the allocation of formal authority feed back on information acquisition and on the communication of information inside the organization?

## Method / identification
A two-tier incomplete-contracting principal-agent model (in the Grossman-Hart 1986 tradition: projects cannot be described or contracted ex ante; the only contractible object is the allocation of a control right). A principal (she) and an agent (he) face $n \ge 3$ ex ante identical projects; project $k$ yields verifiable profit $B_k$ to the principal and private benefit $b_k$ to the agent, with "no project" normalized to zero. The principal's preferred project yields $B$; the agent's preferred project yields $b$. Congruence parameters $\alpha,\beta\in(0,1]$ capture preference alignment: if the principal's project is chosen the agent expects $\beta b$, and if the agent's is chosen the principal expects $\alpha B$. The agent learns all payoffs with probability $e$ at cost $g_A(e)$; the principal learns them with probability $E$ at cost $g_P(E)$, with $g_i$ increasing, strictly convex, $g_i'(1)=\infty$. The baseline assumes the agent is infinitely risk-averse (unresponsive to monetary incentives), so the wage is constant and no ex post renegotiation of authority arises.

Under **P-formal authority** ("integration"), the principal can overrule the agent; she does so when informed and the agent's proposal is noncongruent, and otherwise rubber-stamps (since $\alpha>0$). Equilibrium efforts solve the reaction curves $(1-\alpha e)B=g_P'(E)$ and $(1-E)b=g_A'(e)$. Under **A-formal authority** ("delegation"), the informed agent picks his project and cannot be overruled, giving $(1-e)B=g_P'(E)$ and $\beta b=g_A'(e)$. The crucial structural feature is that the agent's reaction curve slopes *down*: more principal monitoring crowds out agent initiative, distinguishing this from monitoring / costly-state-verification models where the agent's curve slopes up. Extensions add a multitask participation model, span-of-control / overload, monetary incentives, reputation in repeated play, multiple principals (splitting returns vs. splitting authority), urgency via an optimal-stopping problem, and strategic communication via a congruence-dependent cost shifter.

## Data
None — this is a pure theory paper. Empirical content is confined to illustrative anecdotes (Chandler's histories of Du Pont, General Motors, Standard Oil; General Electric's "dual top management"; U-form to M-form transitions; vicarious-liability case law) used to motivate assumptions and interpret comparative statics; the authors stress that only structural estimation could deliver conclusive evidence.

## Key findings
The **basic trade-off** (Sec. III): delegation raises agent initiative ($e<e^d$, $E>E^d$) but causes the principal a loss of control. **Optimal delegation** (Sec. IV): with participation considerations the delegation rule reduces to the *relative willingness to pay for authority*, $B_k(1-\alpha_k)\,/\,b_k(1-\beta_k) < 1/\mu \Rightarrow$ delegate. Authority is delegated for decisions that matter little to the principal, where the agent can be trusted ($\alpha_k$ high) or the principal cannot help hurting the agent ($\beta_k$ low), and that are "innovative" rather than within the principal's core competency. **Overload result** (Sec. VA): it is always optimal for the firm to operate in a situation of overload, since this credibly commits the principal not to stifle initiative ("playing golf" is not a credible commitment). **Monetary incentives** (Sec. VB): raising the agent's wage *increases his real authority* on two channels — it makes the agent more likely to find a project and it reduces the principal's monitoring incentive. **Reputation** (Sec. VD): a patient superior facing many subordinates builds a reputation for overruling only in important matters, voluntarily relinquishing authority on minor ones — not credible in a one-shot game. **Urgency** (Sec. VE): in the sequential / optimal-stopping variant, a shorter horizon $T$ makes the principal investigate less and rubber-stamp more. **Communication** (Sec. IVE): a subordinate communicates prior information only if $\beta>e$, so under low congruence there is *more* communication by an independent (delegated) agent; centralization can either suppress communication (dissonant objectives) or encourage it (congruent objectives).

## Contribution
The paper's central theoretical contribution is articulating the *two-way* interaction between authority and information — formal authority shapes information acquisition, which in turn determines real authority — rather than treating either as primitive. It bridges the moral-hazard and property-rights literatures (their two polar cases), offers complementary "incentive" and "participation" views of delegation with distinct testable implications, and takes "a modest but first step" toward endogenizing the limits of communication by departing from team theory to allow dissonant objectives. It rationalizes a wide range of organizational phenomena (empowerment, M-form, core-competency centralization, dual top management, vicarious liability) within one framework.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems: (i) endogenizing the *timing* of investigation (simultaneous vs. sequential) is left for future research; (ii) congruence parameters $\alpha,\beta$ are treated as exogenous though the principal could shape them via recruiting, training, or career design; (iii) extending to *multilayered* hierarchies and the complex webs of authority in real organizations; (iv) endogenizing the agent's search timing under urgency, including whether a noncongruent agent strategically delays proposals to signal; (v) the result that communication never hurts an independent agent is conjectured not to be robust once the agent responds to monetary incentives (e.g., blackmail-style holdup); (vi) measuring real authority empirically is acknowledged as hard, with only suggestive proxies (span of control, ownership concentration, overrule counts, "who gets courted"). The paper positions itself explicitly as a first step toward a general theory of authority.

## Connections
The model is built on Grossman & Hart (1986) and Hart & Moore (1990) on property rights as authority conferred by asset ownership, and draws on Simon (1951) and Weber (1968) for the conceptual definition of authority. The observation that information can hurt a principal connects to Riordan (1990), Schmidt (1991), and Crémer (1995), and to Arrow (1975) on vertical integration and communication; the ratchet-effect literature makes a related point. The team-theoretic communication baseline it departs from is the standard cheap-talk/team tradition. Williamson (1975, 1985) supplies the integration-as-arm's-length contrast, and Chandler (1962) the historical motivation for delegation and the M-form. Related contemporaneous work includes Aghion & Tirole (1995) on growth and organizational form, Rotemberg (1993, 1994) on power in organizations, De Bijl (1994, 1995) on delegation of responsibility, and Burkart, Gromb & Panunzi (1994) on large shareholders and monitoring. Tirole (1994) discusses when incomplete- and complete-contract approaches to authority coincide; Shavell (1987) and Strasser & Rodosevich (1993) inform the vicarious-liability application.

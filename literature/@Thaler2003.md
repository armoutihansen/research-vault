## Summary

This short *AEA Papers and Proceedings* essay argues that "libertarian paternalism" is not an oxymoron. Once we accept that some agent or institution must inevitably structure the context in which people choose — for example by setting a default option — paternalism in the sense of *preference-shaping effects* becomes unavoidable. The authors therefore reframe the policy question away from "paternalism: yes or no?" toward "given that we must choose some arrangement, which one promotes people's welfare while preserving freedom of choice?" They illustrate with retirement-savings defaults (automatic 401(k) enrollment, Save More Tomorrow) and a cafeteria-layout parable, and sketch a "toolbox" for sensible planners.

## Research question

Is it coherent to be both a libertarian and a paternalist? More constructively: when an institution must design the choice architecture that affects people's decisions, how should it choose among the available arrangements so as to make choosers better off without coercing them or foreclosing options?

## Method / identification

This is a conceptual/argumentative essay, not an empirical study. The method is a logical argument built on three claims:

1. **The false assumption** that people (always/usually) choose in their own best interest. The authors hold this is "testable and false," citing the behavioral-economics literature (preference reversals, violations of Bayes' rule, framing effects, dynamic inconsistency / self-control problems).
2. **First misconception** — that there is a viable *non*-paternalistic alternative. They argue some agent must choose an arrangement (e.g., the cafeteria director must order the food; the employer must set a 401(k) default), and that arrangement shapes choices, so a form of paternalism cannot be avoided.
3. **Second misconception** — that paternalism necessarily involves coercion. Their definition: a policy is *paternalistic* if "selected with the goal of influencing the choices of affected parties in a way that will make those parties better off," measured "as objectively as possible," explicitly *not* equating revealed preference with welfare. It is *libertarian* paternalism if no choice is blocked and opting out is easy/cheap.

The constructive contribution is a decision toolbox for planners. When welfare can be measured directly, use **cost-benefit analysis**. When it cannot, use indirect welfare proxies via three methods:

- **Market-mimicking** — choose the option the majority *would* choose if forced to make an explicit, revealed choice. Caveats: majority choices may be uninformed, may not track welfare, and may themselves depend on the starting point (a "circularity" problem).
- **Forced/required choice** — make people choose explicitly (used when market-mimicking fails); risk that elicited choices are inadequately informed.
- **Minimize opt-outs** — an *ex post* inquiry: pick the arrangement few people abandon once in place, as evidence they are satisfied (contrasted with the *ex ante* market-mimicking strategy).

## Data

None — this is a four-page essay with no original dataset or estimation. It draws on prior empirical work by others: [[@Benartzi2002|Benartzi and Thaler (2002)]] on investors' (dis)satisfaction with their own portfolios (only 20 percent preferred their own portfolio to the median); Madrian and Shea (2001), where automatic enrollment raised 401(k) participation from 49 to 86 percent; Choi et al. (2002, 2003a) on default and forced-choice enrollment; and Thaler and Benartzi (2003) Save More Tomorrow (savings rates rising from 3.5 to 11.6 percent over about two years).

## Key findings

As a conceptual piece, its "results" are propositions rather than theorems:

- **Paternalism is often inevitable.** Whenever an institution sets a default or an arrangement that prevails "unless people affirmatively choose otherwise," it shapes choices. When paternalism *seems* absent, it is only because the starting point looks so natural that its preference-shaping effects are invisible — "the anti-paternalistic position is incoherent."
- **Defaults are sticky** because of (a) status-quo bias / endowment-loss-aversion effects, (b) the perceived legitimacy of a default that "seems to have resulted from conscious thought about what makes most sense," and (c) inertia and procrastination. For present purposes the exact mechanism does not matter — what matters is that defaults move choices.
- **Even "requiring a choice" is itself a choice** among options (and has a paternalistic element, since many people would prefer not to be forced to choose); it is not an escape from the dilemma.
- **One can go "beyond the inevitable" and stay libertarian** — e.g., placing desserts farther away raises the self-control "transaction cost" of eating them, benefiting those who would resist temptation given a little help, while forbidding nothing.

## Contribution

The paper coins and defends the term **libertarian paternalism**, supplying the conceptual foundation for what became the "nudge" agenda (Thaler and Sunstein's later *Nudge*). Its core move — that *choice architecture* is unavoidable, so the relevant normative question is which architecture best promotes welfare subject to preserving free choice — reframed a long-standing debate in welfare economics and public policy. It also distinguishes welfare from revealed preference and offers an operational toolbox (cost-benefit; market-mimicking; forced choice; minimize-opt-outs) for choosing among arrangements.

## Limitations & open questions

The authors flag several open problems, mostly around the welfare criterion:

- **Measuring welfare without relying on revealed preference.** They concede the planner often "will be unable to make a direct inquiry into welfare," and the indirect proxies are imperfect.
- **The circularity problem.** If majority choices are themselves a function of the default/starting point, the market-mimicking strategy is "doomed" in those cases; they note it survives only where the majority would choose the same way regardless of the starting point.
- **Informational adequacy of elicited choices.** Both market-mimicking and forced choice risk eliciting "insufficiently informed" choices that "would not promote the majority's welfare."
- **Heterogeneity / distributional effects.** A default good for most may hurt some (e.g., highly liquidity-constrained employees made worse off by automatic enrollment); they only "guess" the gains exceed the losses.
- **Where to stop.** The essay does not fully delimit how far "beyond the inevitable" a libertarian paternalist may go before the intervention stops being genuinely libertarian.

## Connections

The essay sits at the intersection of behavioral economics and welfare/public economics. It builds directly on the heuristics-and-biases program (Kahneman and Tversky 2000; Gilovich, Griffin, and Kahneman 2002) and on **status-quo bias** ([[@Samuelson1988|Samuelson and Zeckhauser 1988]]; Kahneman, Knetsch, and Thaler 1991). The savings applications rest on Madrian and Shea (2001), Choi, Laibson, Madrian, and Metrick (2002, 2003), Benartzi and Thaler (2002), and Thaler and Benartzi's Save More Tomorrow. It is in explicit dialogue with companion AEA-session papers on *optimal* paternalism — Camerer, [[@Loewenstein2001|Issacharoff, Loewenstein, O'Donoghue, and Rabin (2001)]] on "asymmetric paternalism," O'Donoghue and Rabin (2003) on optimal sin taxes, and Choi et al. (2003b) "Optimal Defaults" — which formalize welfare-maximizing policy design where this essay argues informally. The line of work culminates in Thaler and Sunstein's *Nudge* and the broad literature on choice architecture and defaults.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Discrete optimization subject to one constraint is attacked by Lagrangian analysis. Incremental allocation schemes are given that generate undominated allocations. In an important special case, the complete family of undominated allocations is generated.

## Summary

Fox studies the singly-constrained separable integer program of maximizing an additively separable objective over nonnegative-integer decision vectors subject to one linear resource (budget) constraint. He shows that a simple greedy rule — spend each marginal unit of resource where it buys the largest incremental return per dollar — produces *undominated* (efficient) allocations, and characterizes exactly when this marginal-analysis logic is justified. The paper unifies several pieces of operations-research "folklore" under Everett's generalized Lagrange-multiplier framework, extends the guarantees from the concave case to non-concave separable objectives (via the least concave majorant) and to nonlinear separable constraints, and bounds the suboptimality of the greedy solution against the true integer optimum. Two military-logistics applications (a flyaway-kit spare-parts problem and a missile-allocation problem) illustrate the conditions. Coverage is full.

## Research question

When is the greedy "marginal analysis" heuristic — allocate each marginal unit of a scarce resource to the use with the highest incremental return per unit cost — provably correct for *discrete* (integer) allocation problems, and which efficient/optimal solutions does it generate? The paper asks precisely under what structural conditions (concavity, monotonicity, equal costs, separability) the heuristic yields undominated allocations and how close those are to the constrained integer optimum.

## Method / identification

This is a constructive mathematical-programming paper. The core problem is
$$\max\{\phi(x): x\in S,\ C(x)\le M\}$$
where $S$ is the set of $n$-tuples of nonnegative integers, the objective is separable $\phi(x)=\sum_j \phi_j(x_j)$, and the constraint cost is separable and linear $C(x)=\sum_j c_j x_j$ with $c_j>0$, $M>0$.

The **incremental allocation procedure** starts from $x^0=0$ and at step $k$ adds one unit of the index $i$ maximizing the per-dollar marginal return $\rho_{kj}=(\phi_j(x_j^{k-1}+1)-\phi_j(x_j^{k-1}))/c_j$, stopping when the cost would exceed $M$. Two variants are given: terminate when the *objective* (not cost) first exceeds a target, and *branch* on ties to enumerate alternative maximizers.

The analysis rests on **Lagrangian relaxation** in the sense of Everett (1963). An integer function is called concave if its first differences are decreasing. For $\lambda>0$, define $T_i(\lambda)$ as the set of integers maximizing $\phi_i(x_i)-\lambda c_i x_i$, and $T(\lambda)=\sum_i T_i(\lambda)$. An allocation $x$ is **undominated** ("efficient") if no other $y$ gives at least as much objective at no greater cost (with strict improvement somewhere). The argument: maximizing the Lagrangian $\phi(x)-\lambda g(x)$ for some $\lambda\ge0$ certifies efficiency (Everett's lemma); the greedy step always lands in $T(\lambda_k)$ for a descending multiplier $\lambda_k$; induction propagates undominatedness along the whole greedy path.

For **non-concave** separable objectives, Fox replaces $\phi_i$ by its *least concave majorant* $f_i$ on the integers and shows (Lemma 2) that a point maximizes the Lagrangian iff it lies in the set $Y_i$ where $f_i$ touches $\phi_i$; the greedy rule over those touching points again yields undominated allocations (Theorem 4). For a **nonlinear separable constraint** with each $C_j$ convex strictly increasing, the marginal-return criterion is replaced by the ratio of objective increment to constraint-cost increment, and the guarantee survives.

## Key findings

- **Theorem 1**: for any $\lambda>0$, if each $\phi_i$ is concave then any allocation in $T(\lambda)$ is undominated (immediate from Everett's lemma).
- **Theorem 2**: if each $\phi_i$ is concave and strictly increasing, *every* allocation generated by the incremental procedure is undominated. As $\lambda$ ranges over $(0,\infty)$ the greedy path recovers all such allocations; with the tie-branching Variant 2 the converse also holds (every undominated allocation with cost $\le M$ is reachable).
- **Theorem 3**: with concave strictly-increasing $\phi_i$, letting $x^1,\dots,x^M$ be the greedy iterates and $z$ a true optimum, the procedure brackets the optimum: $\phi(x^m)\ge\phi(z)\ge\phi(x^{m-1})$ and $C(x^m)\ge C(z)\ge C(x^{m-1})$, with the cost gap bounded by $0\le C(x^m)-C(x^{m-1})\le\max_j c_j$. This yields a practical *a posteriori* optimality check; if too loose, exact dynamic programming can finish the job at much higher cost.
- **Corollary 1**: if additionally all $c_j$ are equal, the greedy procedure is *exactly optimal* ($x^{m-1}\in Z$).
- **Corollary 2**: with equal costs, the optimal value $\sigma(M)$ is concave and strictly increasing in $M$ over the integer grid — diminishing returns to the budget.
- **Theorem 4 / Lemma 2**: the guarantees extend to non-concave separable objectives via the least concave majorant, and (Section 8) to convex strictly-increasing separable nonlinear constraints. A shadow price $\lambda=\max_j[\phi_j(x_j^*+1)-\phi_j(x_j^*)]/c_j$ can be read off any generated allocation for sensitivity analysis.

## Contribution

The paper gives a clean, unifying Lagrangian justification for greedy marginal allocation in discrete problems, stitching together prior special-case results (Gross; Barlow & Proschan; Feeney & Sherbrooke; Karr & Geisler) under one framework while extending the reach to non-concave objectives and nonlinear constraints. Its lasting value is as an early, rigorous statement of the **greedy = optimal/efficient under concavity (diminishing differences) on a separable knapsack** result, with explicit suboptimality bounds and a shadow-price interpretation — a building block for incremental resource-allocation algorithms in inventory, reliability, and military logistics.

## Limitations & open questions

- Guarantees of *exact* optimality require equal unit costs (Corollary 1); with unequal costs the greedy solution is only undominated and bracketed, leaving a gap of up to $\max_j c_j$ in resource that must be closed by costlier dynamic programming.
- Restricted to a *single* constraint and *separable* objective/constraint; the multi-constraint or coupled (non-separable) case is not addressed and is where the marginal logic breaks down.
- For non-concave objectives the least-concave-majorant trick recovers only allocations on the majorant's contact set $Y_i$; integer optima strictly inside concavity gaps are not certified.
- As Fox notes, even when component value functions $F(X)$ and $1-G(Y)$ are convex, their logs need not be concave, so composing separately optimized subproblems (the missile-allocation Application 2) can require brute force — pointing to an open question on when composition preserves the marginal-analysis guarantee.
- Practical usefulness hinges on the *spacing* of generated allocations near the budget; the paper only gestures at this and defers a systematic treatment of step-size control and efficient sorting/updating of marginal-return rankings.

## Connections

The decision-theoretic backbone is Everett's (1963) generalized Lagrange-multiplier method, which supplies the efficiency-certification lemma and the "Lambda" shadow-price theorem. The concavity-based redundancy and reliability results adapt Barlow and Proschan (1965) and the inventory application of Feeney and Sherbrooke (1965); the equal-cost optimality result restates Gross (1956), and the spare-parts marginal analysis traces to Karr and Geisler (1956) and Geisler and Karr (1956). For the linear-objective limit the problem becomes a knapsack, connecting to Gilmore and Gomory's (1963, 1965) cutting-stock work and Balinski's (1965) integer-programming survey. The missile-allocation example draws on Piccariello (1962) and Landi (1964). Within the vault this sits with separable resource-allocation and greedy-submodular optimization, and is methodologically adjacent to any discrete-choice or structural-estimation setting where a separable objective with diminishing differences makes a marginal/greedy allocation rule provably efficient.

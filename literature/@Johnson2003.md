## Summary
A short *Science* Policy Forum arguing that the legal default for organ donation — opt-in (explicit consent) versus opt-out (presumed consent) — drives enormous differences in donation agreement. Combining an online experiment with cross-country and time-series field data, Johnson and Goldstein show that presumed-consent defaults raise effective consent dramatically (near-universal stated consent in the lab; ~60 percentage-point gaps across European countries) and translate into measurably higher actual cadaveric donation rates. The mechanism is "constructed preferences": when preferences are not pre-articulated, the default acts as an implicit recommendation, the effortless choice, and a loss-averse status-quo reference point.

## Research question
Why do organ-donation rates differ by nearly an order of magnitude across otherwise similar countries, and is this gap explained by genuine preference heterogeneity (the classical-economics view) or by the no-action default embedded in consent law? Concretely: do defaults change (a) stated/effective consent and (b) actual donations enough to matter for lives saved?

## Method / identification
Three complementary studies:
1. **Online experiment** ($n=161$): respondents answered one of three randomly assigned framings. Opt-in: "you just moved to a state where the default is *not* to be a donor — confirm or change." Opt-out: identical but default is *to be* a donor. Neutral: choose with no prior default. A single mouse click flipped the choice, so physical effort is largely eliminated by design, isolating the default's suggestive / reference-point role.
2. **Cross-country comparison**: effective consent rates for four explicit-consent (opt-in) versus six presumed-consent (opt-out) European countries, using registry data from Gäbel (25) supplemented by direct contact with national registries to compute the share who had opted in (explicit) or *not* opted out (presumed).
3. **Field identification of actual donations**: a multiple-regression / time-series analysis of cadaveric donations per million across European countries, 1991–2001, with the default policy as the key predictor and controls for transplant infrastructure, educational level, religion, plus year indicator variables to absorb technology/education-campaign trends. A 1999 cross-section from Gimbel et al. (5) over a broader set (including Eastern Europe) serves as a robustness comparison.

## Data
Three sources: (i) 161 online experimental respondents; (ii) registry-based effective-consent rates for 10–11 European countries (Gäbel 2002 plus authors' registry contacts); (iii) panel of actual cadaveric-donation rates per million, 1991–2001, across European countries, with country-level covariates; plus the Gimbel et al. (1999) cross-section. Stated U.S. attitudes come from a 1993 Gallup poll (85% approve, 28% had signed a donor card).

## Key findings
- **Experiment:** effective consent was about *twice* as high under opt-out as under opt-in (roughly 82% vs. 42%, with the neutral condition near 79%). The opt-out and neutral conditions did not differ significantly; only the opt-in condition — current U.S. practice — was significantly lower. Because effort was removed, the effect reflects defaults-as-recommendation and loss aversion rather than switching cost.
- **Cross-country:** opt-in and opt-out countries' effective-consent distributions *do not overlap*; nearly 60 percentage points separate the groups (e.g., Denmark ~4% up to Austria ~100%). Field gaps exceed lab gaps because real switching carries forms/phone/mail costs.
- **Actual donations:** controlling for covariates and year, presumed-consent defaults raise the donation rate by ~16.3% ($P<0.02$), from 14.1 to 16.4 donors per million; Gimbel et al. find a 56.5% increase (10.8 to 16.9). The Netherlands case is striking: a 12-million-letter mailing in a country of 15.8 million failed to move effective consent.

## Contribution
Provides one of the most cited real-world demonstrations that defaults are not behaviorally neutral and have life-and-death consequences, directly countering the classical view (and Caplan's claim that defaults "do not matter"). It links the lab and field, distinguishes *effective consent* from *actual donation*, and frames consent policy as an unavoidable design choice — every policy has a no-action default — thereby anchoring the libertarian-paternalism / nudge agenda in a high-stakes medical domain.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag explicitly: (i) the *mechanism* is unidentified — physical response cost, cognitive effort, and loss aversion are not separated, and "one might draw different conclusions if the effect is due primarily to physical costs than if due to loss aversion"; this calls for research isolating the three causes. (ii) Higher *agreement* does not mechanically yield *donations*: families' objections, physicians' hesitancy to use a default, recipient mismatch, and cultural/religious/infrastructural factors intervene. (iii) Defaults create two-sided misclassification — willing donors not identified versus people made donors against their wishes — and balancing these errors against lives saved raises delicate ethical and psychological questions. (iv) Mandated choice (e.g., Virginia) is an alternative, but ~24% remained "undecided," consistent with constructed preferences.

## Connections
The constructed-preferences premise draws on Payne, Bettman & Johnson (1992), Slovic (1995), and Kahneman & Tversky's *Choices, Values, and Frames* (2000). The status-quo / reference-dependence channel rests on Samuelson & Zeckhauser's (1988) status-quo bias and Tversky & Kahneman's (1991) loss aversion, with omission/default reasoning from Baron & Ritov (1994). Default effects in adjacent domains come from the authors' own work on insurance choice (Johnson, Hershey, Meszaros & Kunreuther 1993) and Internet privacy (Bellman, Johnson & Lohse 2001), and from Madrian & Shea's (2001) 401(k) auto-enrollment study. The policy framing is the nudge / libertarian-paternalism program of Thaler & Sunstein and Camerer, Issacharoff, Loewenstein, O'Donoghue & Rabin (2003). Caplan (1994) is the foil arguing defaults do not matter.

## Summary

This is a comment on [[@Jeuland1983|Jeuland and Shugan (1983)]], who proposed a quantity-discount schedule as a device for coordinating a manufacturer–retailer channel. Moorthy's central point is that the quantity discount is in no way special: a wide variety of manufacturer pricing schemes—including ones with quantity *surcharges* (upward-sloping marginal prices)—achieve channel coordination just as well. The necessary and sufficient condition is geometric and weak: the retailer's effective marginal cost curve must cut his marginal revenue curve from below exactly at the channel-optimal quantity. Reframing the problem as a *decentralization* problem makes this transparent, and it implies that a simple two-part tariff dominates the quantity discount on administrative and legal grounds. Moorthy further argues the channel-coordination rationale for quantity discounts is empirically weaker than the classic price-discrimination rationale.

## Research question

Is a quantity discount schedule *necessary* for coordinating a decentralized distribution channel in the Jeuland–Shugan setting? If not, which of the many coordinating schemes should a manufacturer prefer, and does channel coordination really constitute a new explanation for observed quantity discounts?

## Method / identification

Pure analytical microeconomics; no data. Moorthy adopts Jeuland and Shugan's notation and model: a single manufacturer sells to a single retailer facing consumer demand $Q=D(p)$ with inverse demand $p(Q)=D^{-1}(Q)$; the retailer's and manufacturer's constant marginal costs are $c$ and $C$, with fixed costs $f$ and $F$. Total channel profit is $(p(Q)-c-C)Q-f-F$, so the channel-optimal quantity $D(p^{*})$ solves the first-order condition
$$Qp'(Q)+p(Q)-c-C=0,$$
with second-order condition $Qp''(Q)+2p'(Q)<0$ at $D(p^{*})$.

Jeuland and Shugan's quantity-discount schedule sets the average unit price as
$$t(Q)=k_{1}\bigl(p(Q)-c-C\bigr)+C+\frac{k_{2}}{Q},$$
with $k_{1}\in(0,1)$ and $k_{2}$ governing profit sharing. The retailer's *effective marginal cost* of the $Q$th unit is then $k_{1}\bigl(Qp'(Q)+p(Q)-c-C\bigr)+C+c$, independent of $k_{2}$.

The identification strategy is geometric (Figure 1): the retailer freely chooses quantity, so he equates marginal revenue $Qp'(Q)+p(Q)$ to effective marginal cost. Coordination requires only that these curves intersect, with the cost curve cutting from below, at $D(p^{*})$—which holds because at that point $Qp'(Q)+p(Q)-c-C=0$, forcing effective marginal cost to equal $C+c=$ marginal revenue there. Away from $D(p^{*})$ the effective marginal cost can be almost anything, including upward-sloping segments (curve AB in the figure).

## Data

None — this is a theoretical comment. Empirical claims are anecdotal (e.g. franchise-fee practices, the Morton Salt antitrust case) drawn from the marketing and industrial-organization literatures.

## Key findings

- **Non-necessity / multiplicity result.** A quantity discount is not necessary for coordination. The *necessary and sufficient* condition is that the manufacturer's pricing policy make the retailer's effective marginal cost cut his marginal revenue from below at $D(p^{*})$. Equivalently: (1) effective marginal cost equals $C+c$ at $D(p^{*})$, and (2) effective marginal cost lies strictly below marginal revenue for quantities below $D(p^{*})$. An infinity of schemes—even ones with quantity surcharges—satisfy this.
- **Decentralization reframing.** Channel coordination is a decentralization problem: the manufacturer's pricing scheme is just the instrument that aligns the retailer's privately optimal quantity with the channel optimum. Whether the scheme is negotiated or unilaterally imposed is immaterial.
- **Optimality of the two-part tariff.** Among coordinating schemes, a two-part tariff is best. Setting the constant marginal price equal to the manufacturer's marginal cost $C$ satisfies requirement (1) at *every* quantity, hence requirement (2) automatically; profit sharing is then handled separately by a fixed fee of $k_{1}(p^{*}-c-C)D(p^{*})+k_{2}$. This separation of coordination from profit-sharing, plus lower administrative cost and fewer Robinson–Patman Act antitrust exposures, favors the two-part tariff over the quantity discount.
- **$k_{2}$ is redundant.** The fixed payment $k_{2}$ is needed neither for coordination nor for profit sharing; any desired profit split is achievable by negotiating $k_{1}$ alone.
- **Weaker rationale for quantity discounts.** Because nothing about quantity discounts is special for coordination, but declining marginal prices *are* essential for second-degree price discrimination, an observed quantity discount is far more likely motivated by price discrimination than by channel coordination.

## Contribution

Sharpens the Jeuland–Shugan result by isolating its true economic content: coordination is a generic decentralization problem with a one-point tangency-type condition, not a quantity-discount phenomenon. By doing so it provides theoretical support for the two-part tariff as the natural coordinating contract and deflates the "new justification for quantity discounts" claim. It connects channel theory to the nonlinear-pricing and price-discrimination literatures.

## Limitations & open questions

- The analysis is confined to the deterministic, single-manufacturer–single-retailer, full-information Jeuland–Shugan model; the multiplicity result and the two-part-tariff optimality may not survive uncertainty, asymmetric information, or competing retailers/manufacturers.
- Moorthy concedes quantity discounts are often *implemented* as a menu of two-part tariffs (Oren, Smith and Wilson 1982); disentangling coordination from price-discrimination motives empirically is left open.
- The Robinson–Patman / antitrust comparison is argued informally rather than modeled.
- Jeuland and Shugan's production-shortfall critique of the two-part tariff is rebutted only by appeal to contractible "protection" clauses, not formally.

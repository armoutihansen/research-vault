> [!abstract] Abstract
> Building on the work of Dhar, Menon, and Maach (2004), this commentary describes how the compromise effect models developed in the work of Kivetz, Netzer, and Srinivasan (2004) can be extended to predict complex (business-to-business) purchase decisions and additional behavioral context effects. The authors clarify their general modeling approach and outline how it applies to choices among solutions (augmented products) and group decision making. They then hypothesize about the influence of business-to-business and technology markets on various context effects (e.g., compromise and asymmetric dominance). They show how the models incorporate various context effects and discuss ideas for further research.

## Summary
This is a short rejoinder/commentary in the August 2004 *JMR* exchange with Dhar, Menon, and Maach (DMM 2004), defending and extending the formal compromise-effect models that Kivetz, Netzer, and Srinivasan (KNS 2004) developed in the companion lead article. The authors argue that their "paramorphic" (as-if) choice models — the contextual concavity model (CCM), its normalized variant (NCCM), and the loss-aversion model (LAM) — are general enough to capture not only the compromise effect but a wider family of context effects (polarization, asymmetric dominance, asymmetric advantage, enhancement, detraction) and to extend to complex B2B/high-technology purchase settings, augmented-product ("solution") choices, and multiperson group decisions. They supply two small MBA-classroom field demonstrations (an Economist subscription decoy and a Xerox/IBM B2B decoy and compromise structure) and lay out a research agenda rather than new estimation.

## Research question
Can formal, individual-level choice models built to predict the compromise effect be extended to (i) complex business-to-business and high-technology purchase environments, (ii) choices among augmented products / "solutions," (iii) multiperson group decision making, and (iv) the broader spectrum of trade-off-contrast context effects (polarization, asymmetric dominance/advantage, enhancement, detraction)?

## Method / identification
The paper is a conceptual commentary, not a new empirical study, so the "method" is the articulation of the existing KNS (2004) model class and arguments for its extensibility.

The KNS models are *paramorphic* "as-if" models aimed at predictive validity, not process tracing. The core mechanism: context-independent part-worth utilities of attribute levels are first measured at a global level (e.g., by conjoint analysis), then transformed by context-dependent parameters defined relative to a single reference point drawn from the local choice set. Two related specifications are central:

- **Contextual Concavity Model (CCM/NCCM):** built on *diminishing sensitivity*. Each attribute's value function is concave in distance from a reference point, where the reference point is taken as the *lowest* attribute part-worth in the local choice set. Adding an asymmetrically dominated (decoy) option that lowers the reference point of one attribute creates a utility gain for the option that previously had the lowest level on that attribute, generating asymmetric dominance/advantage and compromise. A concavity parameter $\gamma<1$ governs the curvature; a smaller (more concave) $\gamma$ implies stronger extremeness aversion on that attribute.
- **Loss-Aversion Model (LAM):** built on prospect-theory reference dependence (Tversky and Kahneman 1991). Advantages/gains and disadvantages/losses relative to the reference option are weighted asymmetrically, with loss aversion potentially differing in magnitude across attribute types.

A key claimed advantage is the use of a *single* reference point per attribute rather than "tournament" models (e.g., [[@Tversky1993|Tversky and Simonson 1993]]) in which every option serves as a reference for all others; the authors report the single-reference formulation predicts better and note the theoretical/empirical equivalence of loss aversion and contextual concavity. For group decisions they propose a two-stage procedure: stage 1 estimates a group-level (context-independent) utility space via Arora and Allenby's (1999) approach; stage 2 applies the CCM/LAM context-dependent transformation to that space.

Extremeness aversion is framed as a *continuum*: pure symmetric compromise (equal extremeness aversion on all attributes) at one end, polarization (extremeness aversion on some attributes but not others) at the other, with most real choices in between. The CCM/NCCM and LAM can place context effects along this continuum via their attribute-specific concavity / loss-aversion parameters.

## Data
Minimal and illustrative only. Two small convenience-sample classroom demonstrations with MBA students: (1) an Economist.com subscription triplet with a dominated $125 print-only decoy, and (2) Xerox WorkCentre Pro 35/45/55 and IBM eServer UNIX (entry/midrange/high-end) real-world B2B assortments. No new estimation of the formal models; the lead KNS (2004) article holds the primary empirical applications.

## Key findings
- **Decoy (asymmetric dominance) effect, Economist:** in the real triplet 72% (21/29) chose the $125 print-and-Web option versus only 43% (13/30) in the binary set lacking the decoy ($t=2.4$, $p<.02$).
- **Asymmetric advantage effect, Xerox:** the Pro 45's relative share rose to 89% (25/28) in the triplet with the Pro 55 decoy versus 71% (22/31) in the near-binary set ($t=1.8$, $p<.05$).
- The CCM/NCCM and LAM can theoretically reproduce **compromise, polarization, asymmetric dominance, asymmetric advantage, and enhancement** through their context-dependent parameters.
- **Detraction is the diagnostic exception:** the CCM/NCCM (with concavity $<1$) and standard tournament/relative-advantage models *cannot* capture detraction, but the LAM *can* simultaneously accommodate extremeness aversion and detraction, because the detracted middle option is dominated by the LAM reference option while its disadvantages are of smaller magnitude than the extremes'.
- Group decision making and "being evaluated" are hypothesized to *magnify* context effects (drawing on [[@Simonson1989|Simonson 1989]]; Simonson and Nowlis 2000).

## Contribution
Positions the KNS (2004) model family as a *general* context-effect model — not merely a compromise model — by showing that a single-reference, attribute-specific concavity/loss-aversion parametrization can locate and statistically test a spectrum of trade-off-contrast effects, and by mapping a concrete extension path into augmented-product, B2B/high-technology, versioning, and multiperson decision settings. It also sharpens the conceptual distinction between models via their behavior on detraction.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit that the models are *not* meant to predict all choices. Stated open problems and hooks:
- Incorporating **heterogeneity in the context-effect parameters** (concavity, loss aversion) at the individual level, e.g. via hierarchical Bayes (Rossi and Allenby 2003), combined with experimental work — flagged as "fertile ground."
- Whether **groups** are genuinely more prone to compromise/asymmetric-dominance choices than individuals, and estimating **group utility functions** that weight individual members (Arora and Allenby 1999) before applying the context transform.
- Whether **institutional/B2B buying environments** (decision-unit size, solution complexity, proprietary/customized non-Pareto-optimal offerings, versioning) are more conducive to decoy and compromise effects.
- Empirically testing a **unifying model** that captures the greatest number of context effects across the widest range of choice situations.
- Acknowledged scope limit: large, cogenerated (buyer–seller) solutions purchased over extended cycles may lie outside the models' reach.

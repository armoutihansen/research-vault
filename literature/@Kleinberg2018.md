> [!abstract] Abstract
> Can machine learning improve human decision making? Bail decisions provide a good test case. Millions of times each year, judges make jail-or-release decisions that hinge on a prediction of what a defendant would do if released. The concreteness of the prediction task combined with the volume of data available makes this a promising machine-learning application. Yet comparing the algorithm to judges proves complicated. First, the available data are generated by prior judge decisions. We only observe crime outcomes for released defendants, not for those judges detained. This makes it hard to evaluate counterfactual decision rules based on algorithmic predictions. Second, judges may have a broader set of preferences than the variable the algorithm predicts; for instance, judges may care specifically about violent crimes or about racial inequities. We deal with these problems using different econometric strategies, such as quasi-random assignment of cases to judges. Even accounting for these concerns, our results suggest potentially large welfare gains: one policy simulation shows crime reductions up to 24.7% with no change in jailing rates, or jailing rate reductions up to 41.9% with no increase in crime rates. Moreover, all categories of crime, including violent crimes, show reductions; these gains can be achieved while simultaneously reducing racial disparities. These results suggest that while machine learning can be valuable, realizing this value requires integrating these tools into an economic framework: being clear about the link between predictions and decisions; specifying the scope of payoff functions; and constructing unbiased decision counterfactuals.

## Summary
Using 758,027 NYC pretrial bail cases, the authors train a gradient-boosted-tree predictor of failure-to-appear and ask whether replacing or augmenting judges' release decisions with these predictions would raise welfare. The hard part is not prediction but evaluation: outcomes (labels) are observed only for defendants judges *released*, so naive comparisons are contaminated by the *selective labels* problem, and judges may optimize a richer payoff than the predicted outcome (*omitted-payoff bias*). The paper's contribution is a set of econometric strategies—exploiting the one-sided nature of selective labels, quasi-random assignment of cases to judges of differing leniency, and worst-case bounds on the unobservables—that deliver credible welfare estimates. Even under conservative assumptions, an algorithmic re-ranking rule yields up to a 24.7% crime reduction at the judges' jailing rate or a 41.9% jailing reduction at the judges' crime rate, with gains across all crime categories and reduced racial disparity. ## Research question
Can machine-learning predictions improve on human judges' pretrial release decisions, and—given that the data are generated by the judges themselves and judges may pursue goals beyond the predicted outcome—how can one *credibly measure* the welfare consequences of substituting algorithmic predictions for human judgment?

## Method / identification
The analysis has two steps: train a predictor, then evaluate it as a decision aid. Prediction uses gradient-boosted decision trees ([[@Hastie2001|Friedman 2001]]) fitting $m(X)=\widehat{\Pr}(Y=1\mid X)$, where $Y$ is failure-to-appear ("crime") and $X$ is case characteristics, rap sheet, and age (race, gender, ethnicity excluded). Hyperparameters are chosen by fivefold cross-validation; out-of-sample AUC is 0.707.

The conceptual core is the evaluation framework. Each defendant has true risk $y$ with $\Pr(Y=1)=y$. The algorithm sees $x(X)\equiv E[y\mid X]$; the judge additionally sees private information $z(X,Z)\equiv E[y\mid X,Z]-E[y\mid X]$, so that $E[y\mid x,z]=x+z$. A further unobservable $w$ shifts decisions without predicting risk. Judge $j$'s payoff is
$$\pi_j(y,R) = -\,a_j\,yR \;-\; b_j\,(1-R),$$
with $a_j$ the cost of crime and $b_j$ the cost of incarceration, so the optimal rule releases iff the assessed risk $h_j(x,z,w)<\kappa_j\equiv b_j/a_j$. Comparing any algorithmic rule $\rho^d$ to the judge's rule $\rho^j$ at equal release rates, the welfare difference is proportional to
$$-E[y\mid \rho^d=0,\rho^j=1] + E[y\mid \rho^d=1,\rho^j=0].$$
The first term (defendants the judge releases but the algorithm jails) is *measured*; the second (defendants the judge jails but the algorithm would release) is *unmeasured*, because outcomes for the jailed are missing. This asymmetry is the **selective labels problem**: imputing the jailed defendants' outcomes from released defendants with the same $x$ requires $E[y\mid\rho^j=0,x]=E[y\mid\rho^j=1,x]$, which fails whenever judges select on $z$ (the "gang-tattoo" example).

Both identification strategies exploit the *one-sided* nature of this problem. (1) **Quasi-contraction**: jail those the judge releases whom $m(x)$ flags as high-risk, $R=1 \iff \rho^j=1 \text{ and } m(x)<\kappa_j$—using only measurable cases. (2) **Differential leniency**: under quasi-random assignment of cases to judges (validated by a permutation/$F$-test on a risk index across within-cell judge-leniency quintiles, using borough × courthouse × year × month × day-of-week cells), more vs. less lenient judges hearing comparable caseloads pin down the crime–detention trade-off and identify $\kappa_j$. The unobservables problem is handled with **worst-case bounds**: for re-ranked releases beyond the most-lenient judge's release rate, true crime is set to $\min\{1,\alpha\hat y\}$, with $\alpha$ swept from 1 (selection-on-observables) to $\infty$ (lenient judges detain perfectly).

## Data
1,460,462 NYC arrest records (Nov 2008–Nov 2013); 758,027 subject to a pretrial release hearing; a main working sample of 554,689 split 40% training / 40% imputation / 20% test, plus a 203,338-case "pure hold-out" lock-box untouched until revision to guard against human data-mining. Outcomes: FTA (primary, since NY judges are charged only with flight risk), rearrest, violent rearrest. A national dataset replication appears in the Online Appendix.

## Key findings
- **Riskiest released defendants are genuinely risky.** Judges still release the predicted-riskiest 1% (predicted risk 62.6%) at a 48.5% rate, and those defendants' realized crime rate is 56.3%—predicted risk tracks the 45-degree line, so the high-risk releases are not low-risk on unobservables.
- **Large bounded welfare gains.** A re-ranking release rule cuts crime by up to 24.7% at the judges' jailing rate, or cuts the jailing rate by 41.9% (from 26.4% to 15.3%) at the judges' crime rate. Bounds in Table IV: even at $\alpha=\infty$, crime falls 14.4% and detention falls 18.5%.
- **Gains are broad, not single-judge artifacts.** The algorithm dominates every large-caseload judge; gains hold across all crime categories including violent crime, and *reduce* racial disparities despite race not being an input.
- **ML beats logit at the tail.** In the top 1% of predicted risk, ML and logistic regression agree on only 30.6% of cases; ML-flagged defendants are substantially riskier, indicating real nonlinear/interaction signal (adjusted $R^2$ of 0.51 regressing $m(X)$ on linear covariates).
- **Behavioral diagnostic.** A second model predicting *judges' own decisions* shows judges struggle most with high-risk cases and are excessively noisy; a rule based on the *predicted judge* dominates actual judges, suggesting inconsistency/noise outweighs any private-information advantage (Kahneman et al. 2016).

## Contribution
The paper supplies a template for when and how ML improves human decisions: ideal targets are *prediction* problems (risk is unknown, causal effect of jail is known), but realizing value requires embedding prediction in an economic framework—linking predictions to decisions, specifying the full payoff function, and constructing unbiased counterfactuals. Methodologically it names and partially solves the selective-labels and omitted-payoff biases that invalidate prior human-vs-statistical-rule comparisons (Meehl 1954; Dawes 1979; Grove et al. 2000) and recent CS imputation approaches.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Unobservables can never be fully ruled out**; identification leans on worst-case bounds and a quasi-random-assignment assumption rather than true randomization.
- **Omitted-payoff bias generalizes badly**: bail was chosen *because* the payoff is narrow; for sentencing, hiring, admissions, or policing—where deterrence, retribution, remorse, or many-dimensional preferences enter—outperforming on one predicted dimension need not imply better decisions or judicial misprediction.
- **Cannot separate private information from noise** in judges' unobserved factors; only the net effect is identified.
- **Compound bail decision** (risk × ability to pay) is collapsed to a single release decision; the causal response of judges to a decision aid is itself an open causal question not answered here.

## Connections
The selective-labels framing builds on Bushway & Smith (2007) and is developed algorithmically in Lakkaraju et al. (2017) and Lakkaraju & Rudin (2017); contemporaneous imputation-based bail work includes Jung et al. (2017). It extends the "prediction policy problems" agenda of Kleinberg, Ludwig, Mullainathan & Obermeyer (2015) and the older clinical-versus-statistical-prediction literature (Meehl 1954; Dawes 1971, 1979; Dawes, Faust & Meehl 1989; Grove et al. 2000). The noise interpretation of judicial inconsistency draws on Kahneman et al. (2016) and behavioral overweighting of salient signals ([[@Tversky1974|Kahneman & Tversky 1974]]; Bordalo, Gennaioli & Shleifer 2012). The full-payoff-function point connects to structural models of expert decisions such as Abaluck et al. (2016) and to risk-assessment reviews by Berk (2012) and Zeng, Ustun & Rudin (2016).

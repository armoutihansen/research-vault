## Summary

This is the foundational paper of the conditional (multinomial) logit model and the random-utility maximization (RUM) approach to discrete choice. McFadden's program is to bridge **individual** choice theory and **population** choice data: when alternatives are "lumpy" (qualitative, discrete), systematic variation in aggregate choice must reflect shifts in individual choice at the *extensive* margin, driven by a *distribution* of decision rules in the population. The paper (i) derives the conditional logit selection-probability formula from behavioral axioms, (ii) shows it is exactly the choice probability generated by a population of utility maximizers whose idiosyncratic taste shocks are i.i.d. extreme-value, (iii) develops maximum-likelihood estimation with existence, uniqueness, and large-sample efficiency/normality results, and (iv) applies it to disaggregated shopping-trip mode, destination, and frequency choices in Pittsburgh. Coverage here is `visual` (scanned working paper, 58 pp.) and spans all four sections plus the statistical appendix.

## Research question

How can one build estimable econometric models of *population* choice among a finite set of qualitative (discrete) alternatives from a model of *individual* utility-maximizing behavior, when the econometrician observes only some attributes and unmeasured tastes vary across the population? Concretely: what functional form for the selection probabilities $P(x\mid s,B)$ — choice of $x$ given measured attributes $s$ and available set $B$ — is both behaviorally grounded and computationally tractable, and how should its parameters be estimated and tested?

## Method / identification

A trial draws an individual with attribute vector $s$ facing finite alternative set $B\subseteq X$; the observed choice is multinomial with probabilities $P(x\mid s,B)$. Each individual maximizes utility $U=V(s,x)+\varepsilon(s,x)$, where $V$ is the non-stochastic "representative" taste and $\varepsilon$ is an idiosyncratic stochastic component. The selection probability is the probability that the chosen alternative's utility is largest. The model is pinned down by behavioral axioms on the selection probabilities:

- **Axiom 1 (Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives, IIA / Luce 1959):** $P(x\mid s,\{x,y\})P(y\mid s,B)=P(y\mid s,\{x,y\})P(x\mid s,B)$ — relative odds of two alternatives are unaffected by other available options.
- **Axiom 2 (Positivity)** and **Axiom 3 (Irrelevance of alternative-set effect)**, the latter giving the additively separable form $V(s,x,z)=v(s,x)-v(s,z)$.

These axioms yield the conditional logit form

$$P(x\mid s,B)=\frac{e^{v(s,x)}}{\sum_{y\in B}e^{v(s,y)}}.$$

**Lemma 1** shows this is exactly the population choice probability when the $\varepsilon(s,x_j)$ are i.i.d. with the Weibull / type-I extreme-value (Gnedenko) CDF $P(\varepsilon\le\varepsilon)=e^{-e^{-\varepsilon}}$ (the closed-form integral collapses to the logit). **Lemma 2** is the converse characterization: under mild "translation completeness" of the error CDF, the extreme-value distribution is the *only* i.i.d. error law generating the logit form, so logit $\Leftrightarrow$ extreme-value errors. **Axiom 4** specifies $v$ linear in parameters, $v(s,x)=\sum_k\theta_k v^k(s,x)$, where regressors may be alternative attributes, nonlinear transforms, or attribute–attribute-set interactions (generic/"hedonic" attributes, or alternative-specific dummies); a regressor invariant across the alternative set is non-identified, fixing the normalization.

Estimation is by **maximum likelihood** on the multinomial log-likelihood (eq. 18). The score (eq. 19) sets $\sum_n\sum_j(S_{jn}-R_nP_{jn})z_{jn}=0$ and the Hessian (eq. 20) is the negative of a weighted moment matrix, so the log-likelihood is **globally concave**; under **Axiom 5 (full rank)** the maximum is unique, and **Axiom 6 (an inequality/cone condition, Lemmas 3–4)** is necessary and sufficient for existence (testable via a quadratic program, e.g. Lemke's method). Cheaper alternatives are discussed: a one-step Newton expansion that coincides with the **linear probability model** OLS estimator (inconsistent but useful for screening), and the **Berkson–Theil** weighted-least-squares minimum-chi-square estimator when cell repetitions are large.

## Data

The theory and statistical sections are non-empirical (the small-sample "examples" in Section III are analytic, not data). The **empirical application** (Section IV) uses a 1967 Southwestern Pennsylvania Regional Planning Commission household survey of Pittsburgh trip-makers, supplemented with time/cost data (Charles River Associates, 1972), to estimate three disaggregated decisions: shopping **mode** choice (transit vs. auto, $N=140$ trips), shopping **destination** choice (3–5 zones, $N=63$ auto trips), and shopping **trip frequency** (whether to travel, $N=80$ households).

## Key findings

- **Behavioral characterization:** IIA + positivity + alternative-set-irrelevance imply the logit form (eqs. 4–12); equivalently, i.i.d. extreme-value taste shocks generate it (Lemma 1), and that error law is essentially unique (Lemma 2). This gives logit coefficients a structural utility interpretation.
- **Estimation properties:** the log-likelihood is strictly concave (eq. 20); the ML estimator exists iff Axiom 6 holds, with non-existence probability vanishing as the sample grows (**Lemma 5**); the estimator is consistent and **asymptotically normal/efficient** with covariance the inverse information matrix (**Lemma 6**, eqs. 27–28).
- **Inference tooling:** likelihood-ratio chi-square tests (eq. 29), a likelihood-ratio pseudo-$R^2$ analogue $\rho^2=1-L(\hat\theta)/L(\hat\theta^H)$ (eq. 30), and residual-based goodness-of-fit statistics $D_{in},G,R^2$ (eqs. 31–41) for testing the logit specification and the absence of alternative-set effects.
- **Small-sample behavior:** analytic Examples 1–2 (Figs. 2–3) show ML bias under 10% for sample sizes $\gtrsim$ 20–50 except at extreme selection probabilities; the linear-probability approximation is accurate for small parameter values.
- **Empirical fit:** mode-choice coefficients all have expected signs and imply plausible values of time ($\$5.46$/hr walk, $\$0.95$/hr in-vehicle); Model 1 predicts the actual mode with probability >0.5 for 133/140 trips. Destination and frequency models likewise fit well and chain via an "inclusive price" index — an early nested-structure idea anticipating nested logit.
- **The IIA red flag:** the famous **red-bus/blue-bus** example (p. 12) shows IIA is implausible when alternatives are close substitutes — adding an identical second bus wrongly steals share from auto rather than from the first bus.

## Contribution

Introduces the conditional logit model and, with it, the modern econometrics of discrete choice grounded in random utility maximization. It is the first derivation of the logit choice model from a *theory of population choice behavior* (the author notes this derivation "appears to be new"), unifying Luce's (1959) choice axiom, the Marschak/Block/Luce stochastic-choice tradition, and Thurstonian random utility into an estimable, policy-relevant econometric framework. It established disaggregated, individual-level demand modeling for transportation and beyond, and is the work later recognized by McFadden's 2000 Nobel Prize.

## Limitations & open questions

- **IIA is the central weakness:** proportional substitution across all alternatives fails whenever some alternatives are close substitutes (red-bus/blue-bus). McFadden explicitly limits the model to settings where alternatives are "distinct and weighed independently," leaving open how to relax IIA — the seed of nested logit, GEV, mixed/random-coefficients logit, and probit.
- **No differential substitution/complementarity:** the model cannot represent richer cross-alternative substitution patterns.
- **Small-sample bias** is severe at extreme selection probabilities and the asymptotic normality is only large-sample; finite-sample distributions remain "unknown except for a few special cases," and behavior under mixtures of extreme and non-extreme probabilities is unexplored.
- **Errors-in-variables** from grouping non-replicate observations biases the Berkson–Theil estimator.
- **Identification/normalization** of alternative-set effects requires strong axioms; care is needed where they are implausible.
- The chained inclusive-price construction across mode/destination/frequency is asserted via a "tree" utility structure not derived here — formalizing it became nested logit.

## Connections

The IIA axiom is taken from Luce (1959); the uniqueness argument draws on Marschak (1960), Block (1960), and Luce & Suppes, with the population-revealed-preference foundations in McFadden & Richter (1971). The extreme-value error link situates it in Thurstone's random-utility tradition and contrasts with the probit alternative of Aitchison and Amemiya (1972). The IIA limitation directly motivated later relaxations: nested logit and generalized extreme value (McFadden 1978), mixed/random-coefficients logit (McFadden & Train 2000), and the random-coefficients demand of Berry, Levinsohn & Pakes (1995). Estimation connects to Berkson (1951, 1955) and Theil (1969) on logit regression. The structural-utility reading underpins welfare and counterfactual analysis in modern empirical IO and labor; the disaggregated-forecasting argument (avoiding the "fallacy of composition") frames the model's use in transportation demand and policy simulation.

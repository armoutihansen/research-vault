> [!abstract] Abstract
> Empirical evidence suggests that workers care about the mission of their job, in addition to their wage. This paper studies how organizations can choose a mission to attract, incentivize, and screen their workers. We analyze a model in which a principal offers a contract to an agent for the development of a project and can influence the agent's marginal return of effort through the choice of project mission. The principal's and the agents' mission preferences are misaligned and the agents vary in the intensity of their mission drive. Our main results highlight that how far the organization chooses to move from its preferred mission depends on the contractual environment in which it operates. Missions will be more agent-preferred in environments in which effort is noncontractible. In environments in which agents' drive is unknown, missions will be less agent-preferred and the organization will find it optimal to offer contract menus that may be implemented via scoring auctions when there are competing agents. Our analysis applies to the design and allocation of aid contracts, research funding, and creative jobs.

## Summary
A profit-maximizing principal contracts an agent to develop a project and treats the project *mission* as a strategic, contractible choice variable. The principal's revenue-maximizing mission ($m=0$) is misaligned with the agent's preferences, but moving the mission toward the agent both lowers the wage the agent will accept and (for "driven" agents) raises their intrinsic marginal return to effort. The paper characterizes the optimal mission across four contractual environments defined by two binary frictions — whether effort is contractible and whether the agent's mission drive is observable — and then extends to $n$ competing agents. The central comparative-statics result: noncontractibility of effort pushes the chosen mission *toward* the agent's preferences (mission as incentive device), whereas asymmetric information about drive pushes it *toward* the principal's preferences (mission as a rent-extraction/screening device). With competing agents the optimal menu is implementable via a (first- or second-) score auction, rationalizing observed procurement practice in the aid sector.

## Research question
Two questions drive the paper: (1) Which project mission does a profit-maximizing principal choose? (2) How is that mission choice shaped by the contractual environment — specifically by the contractibility of effort and by private information about the agent's mission drive? More broadly: can missions be understood as an endogenous HR instrument for attracting, motivating, and screening workers, rather than as exogenous job characteristics?

## Method / identification
Pure mechanism-design / optimal-contracting theory; no estimation. Two risk-neutral parties. The principal's payoff is $U^p = M(m)e - p$ and the agent's is $U^a = \theta m e - c(e) + p$, with quadratic cost $c(e)=\tfrac{1}{2}e^2$. The mission $m\ge 0$ shifts the principal's marginal revenue $M(m)$ (decreasing, strictly concave, with $M(0)>0$, $M'(0)=0$, so the principal's ideal is $m=0$) and the agent's intrinsic marginal return to effort $\theta m$ (increasing in $m$). The type $\theta$ ("mission drive") is drawn from $F$ on $[\underline{\theta},\overline{\theta}]$ with $\underline{\theta}>0$; Assumption 1 imposes the monotone hazard rate (the reciprocal hazard $\lambda(\theta)=(1-F(\theta))/f(\theta)$ is strictly decreasing). Assumption 2, $M(m)>\overline{\theta}m$ whenever $|M'(m)|\le\overline{\theta}$, keeps marginal revenue nonnegative and guarantees regular interior solutions.

The contractual environment is encoded by the principal's information set $\Pi$ (does it contain $\theta$?) and the contractible performance measure $\varphi$ (does it contain effort $e$?), generating four cases indexed by superscripts $o/u$ (drive observable/unobservable) and $c/n$ (effort contractible/noncontractible). Full-information cases maximize the social surplus $\Pi(m,e,\theta)=(M(m)+\theta m)e-\tfrac{1}{2}e^2$ directly (with $e$ replaced by the agent's incentive-compatible choice $e^{on}(m)=\theta m$ when effort is noncontractible). Asymmetric-information cases use the revelation principle, the envelope condition $U^{a\prime}(\theta)=m(\theta)e(\theta)$, integration by parts, and pointwise maximization of an *expected virtual surplus* in which the type $\theta$ is replaced by the virtual valuation $\theta-\lambda(\theta)$; the monotonicity (informational-rent-nondecreasing) constraint is imposed and verified ex post. The competing-agents extension adds allocation probabilities $q_i$ and shows the optimum is a "dichotomy": allocation and contract terms separate, the winner's terms coinciding with the single-agent solution.

## Data
None — this is a theoretical paper. Real-world institutions (EuropeAid, USAID, DFID, World Bank IDA procurement; internal vs. external research funding; the 2012 Freelance Industry Report; Google's 20% time) are cited illustratively to motivate assumptions and interpret the comparative statics, not as data for estimation.

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 (observable drive, contractible effort).** Optimal mission $m^{oc}$ equates marginal returns: $-M'(m^{oc})=\theta$, with $e^{oc}=M(m^{oc})+\theta m^{oc}$. Both rise in $\theta$.
- **Proposition 2 (observable, noncontractible).** $-M'(m^{on})=\theta+\frac{\Pi^{on}_e e^{on}_m}{e^{on}}$ with $e^{on}(m)=\theta m^{on}$. The extra (positive) term is the incentive effect of the mission, so $m^{on}>m^{oc}$: when effort cannot be contracted the principal distorts the mission *toward the agent* to motivate effort. Yet equilibrium effort is still lower than under contractible effort ($e^{oc}>e^{on}$).
- **Proposition 3 (unobservable, contractible).** $-M'(m^{uc}(\theta))=\max\{\theta-\lambda(\theta),0\}$, $e^{uc}(\theta)=M(m^{uc})+(\theta-\lambda(\theta))m^{uc}$. Standard $\lambda(\theta)$ downward distortion toward the principal's ideal, "no distortion at the top", and a corner at $m=0$ for sufficiently low-drive (negative virtual valuation) types.
- **Proposition 4 (unobservable, noncontractible).** $-M'(m^{un}(\theta))=\theta-\lambda(\theta)+\frac{V^{un}_e e^{un}_m}{e^{un}}$, $e^{un}=\theta m^{un}$. Here the mission carries a *double distortion*: $\lambda(\theta)$ lowers the agent's marginal return at fixed effort, and $\lambda(\theta)m$ additionally induces lower effort (the channel the principal could otherwise reach directly via the contracted effort).
- **Synthesis (Propositions 1–4).** $m^{uc}<m^{oc}$ and $m^{un}<m^{on}$: noncontractibility of effort raises the mission toward the agent; private information lowers it toward the principal. The ranking of $m^{un}$ vs. $m^{oc}$ is ambiguous.
- **Propositions 5–6 (competing agents).** With $n$ agents and unobservable drive, the optimal allocation gives the project to the highest-drive agent ($q_i=1$ iff $\theta_i>\max_{j\ne i}\theta_j$); there is *nonexclusion* (no reserve price needed — low types' rents are driven to zero by setting $m=0$, $e=M(0)$). By the Laffont–Tirole dichotomy property, the winner's mission/effort match Propositions 3/4.
- **Lemma 1 (implementation).** Under noncontractible effort the optimal schedule is implemented by a *quasilinear scoring rule* $S(m,p)=\varphi(m)-p$, with $\varphi$ equal to the principal's effort-adjusted preferences plus a distortion term $\Gamma(m)$ that bends bids toward the principal's ideal mission; it works under both first- and second-score auctions. A naive rule $S=U^p$ would induce missions too close to the agent's ideal.

## Contribution
The paper is, to the authors' knowledge, the first to treat mission as an *endogenous* contractual choice in a model with motivated agents, and to derive how that choice responds jointly to effort contractibility and to private information about drive. Relative to Besley and Ghatak (2005), who take mission as exogenous, assume output-contingent rewards and full information, and study stable matching, this paper endogenizes the mission, accommodates incomplete contracts and asymmetric information, and derives an implementable allocation mechanism (the scoring auction). It rationalizes several otherwise puzzling patterns: coexistence of freelance and staff contracts within a firm (menus differing in pay vs. flexibility), heavy use of scoring auctions with high design weight in aid procurement, greater researcher discretion under internal than external funding, and the "entrepreneurship" culture in innovation-heavy firms. It also articulates a new "cost of control" rooted not in distrust signaling but simply in misaligned project-design preferences.

## Limitations & open questions
The paper does not contain an explicit "future research" section, but several modeling choices are limiting and constitute natural extension points. (1) Cost and revenue are tied to specific functional forms — quadratic effort cost and a strictly concave, single-peaked $M(m)$ with ideal at $0$ — and Assumption 2 is acknowledged as *sufficient but not necessary*, imposed mainly to keep comparative statics across the four settings clean; relaxing it is open. (2) The mission is one-dimensional and the principal–agent preference conflict is reduced to a single ordering ($m=0$ for the principal, higher $m$ for the agent); richer (multidimensional or non-monotone) mission spaces are unexplored. (3) Drive $\theta$ enters multiplicatively and is one-dimensional with MHRP; multidimensional private information (e.g., joint heterogeneity in ability and drive) is not treated. (4) The competing-agents analysis assumes i.i.d. types and a single contract; correlated types, multiple contracts, or dynamic/relational contracting are left open. (5) The motivating empirical regularities are interpreted narratively rather than tested — the model "generates predictions" (menus of pay-vs-discretion, scoring auctions, internal-vs-external funding discretion) that invite structural or experimental testing.

## Connections
Squarely in the **contract theory with motivated agents** literature (Murdock 2002; Bénabou and Tirole 2003, 2006; Francois 2003; Besley and Ghatak 2005; Delfgaauw and Dur 2007, 2008; Prendergast 2007, 2008). Closest are **Besley and Ghatak (2005)** — mission-matching with exogenous missions and full information, contrasted throughout — **Chau and Huysentruyt (2006)** — competitive tendering between nonprofits yielding ideological compromise but with no effort/incentive channel — and **Besley and Ghatak (2017a)**, which links mission flexibility to organizational form (for-profit/nonprofit/social enterprise). Methodologically the screening problem follows **Mussa–Rosen / Myerson** monopolistic-screening and optimal-auction machinery; the multi-agent dichotomy invokes **Laffont and Tirole (1987)**; and the scoring-auction implementation draws on **Che (1993)**, **Asker and Cantillon (2008)**, and **Nishimura (2012)**. The "cost of control" framing connects to the behavioral control-aversion literature (e.g., Falk–Kosfeld), but here the cost is preference-based rather than signaling-based.

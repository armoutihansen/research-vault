> [!abstract] Abstract
> Among the most important and robust violations of rationality are the attraction and the compromise effects. The compromise effect refers to the tendency of individuals to choose an intermediate option in a choice set, while the attraction effect refers to the tendency to choose an option that dominates some other options in the choice set. This paper argues that both effects may result from an individual's attempt to overcome the difficulty of making a choice in the absence of a single criterion for ranking the options. Moreover, we propose to view the resolution of this choice problem as a cooperative solution to an intrapersonal bargaining problem among different selves of an individual, where each self represents a different criterion for choosing. We first identify a set of properties that characterize those choice correspondences that coincide with our bargaining solution, for some pair of preference relations. Second, we provide a revealed-preference foundation to our bargaining solution and characterize the extent to which these two preference relations can be uniquely identified. Alternatively, our analysis may be reinterpreted as a study of (interpersonal) bilateral bargaining over a finite set of options. In that case, our results provide a new characterization, as well as testable implications, of an ordinal bargaining solution that is previously discussed in the literature under the various names of fallback bargaining, unanimity compromise, Rawlsian arbitration rule, and Kant–Rawls social compromise.

## Summary
The paper gives a single, fully ordinal decision-theoretic model that simultaneously generates the two best-documented violations of rationality in choice-under-conflict — the attraction effect and the compromise effect. The decision-maker is modeled as two "inner selves," each a strict linear ordering (a choice criterion), who reach a cooperative agreement via the **fallback bargaining solution**: score each option by the size of its lower contour set for each self, and select the options whose *minimum* score across selves is largest. Three theorems (i) axiomatize this solution when the two preferences are known, (ii) give a revealed-preference characterization when they are not observed, using "revealed Pareto dominance" and "revealed compromises," and (iii) pin down exactly how non-unique the recovered preferences can be. Because fallback bargaining also has a non-cooperative (alternating-offer) foundation, the same results double as a characterization and testable implications for *interpersonal* ordinal bargaining over a finite set.

## Research question
Is there a single, parsimonious model that explains *both* the attraction and compromise effects in a one-person decision problem (prior models explain only one), and what behavior characterizes it? Concretely: (a) what properties single out a choice procedure that produces both effects when the underlying criteria are *known*; (b) when the criteria/rankings are *not observed*, what necessary and sufficient conditions on choice data permit representing the agent as two selves resolving conflict by fallback bargaining; and (c) to what extent can those two underlying preference relations be *identified* from choices?

## Method / identification
The primitive environment is a finite set of options $X$ and (for the known-preference model) a pair of strict linear orderings $\succ=(\succ_1,\succ_2)\in L(X)^2$. A bargaining problem is a nonempty $S\subseteq X$; a bargaining solution $C$ maps each $S$ to a nonempty $C(S)\subseteq S$. The **score** of $x$ in $S$ along dimension $i$ is the size of its lower contour set,
$$s_i(x,S,\succ)=|\{y\in S\mid x\succ_i y\}|,$$
and the **fallback bargaining solution** is
$$C^{f}_{\succ}(S)=\arg\max_{x\in S}\ \min_{i=1,2}\ s_i(x,S,\succ).$$
Equivalently (Brams–Kilgour reinterpretation): let $E_i(S,k)$ be self $i$'s top-$k$ options and $k^{*}$ the smallest $k$ with $E_1(S,k)\cap E_2(S,k)\neq\varnothing$; then $C^{f}_{\succ}(S)=E_1(S,k^{*})\cap E_2(S,k^{*})$ — keep raising the acceptable rank until both selves' top sets intersect. This is purely ordinal (unlike Nash or Kalai–Smorodinsky) and applies to any finite set; it also coincides with the subgame-perfect outcome of an alternating-offer rejection game (Anbarci 2006).

The **known-preference axiomatization** (Section 4) works at the level of a *bargaining operator* $C$ mapping each profile $\succ$ to a solution $C_{\succ}$, restricted to "regular" operators (neutrality, anonymity, and dependence only on the part of $\succ$ relevant to $S$). The substantive axioms are: **EFF** (no selected option is Pareto-dominated), **ATT — Attraction** (adding a dominated $x$ collapses the solution to previously chosen options that dominate $x$: $C_{\succ}(S\cup\{x\})=\{y\in C_{\succ}(S)\mid y\succ x\}$), **NBC — No Better Compromise** (if $x,y$ are both selected there is no $z$ "between" them, i.e. no $z,i$ with $x\succ_i z\succ_i y$ and $y\succ_{-i}z\succ_{-i}x$), **RA — Removing an Alternative** (a weak IIA: dropping a non-uniquely-chosen option keeps at least one previously chosen option), and **SYM — Symmetry** (selected options have equal "status"). A key inductive Lemma 1 reduces the solution on large sets to its behavior on subsets.

The **revealed-preference characterization** (Section 5) drops regularity (preferences are no longer primitive) and restates every axiom purely in terms of choices. Two definitions carry it: $x$ is *revealed Pareto superior* to $y$ iff $C(\{x,y\})=\{x\}$; $z$ is a *revealed compromise* between $x,y$ iff it is chosen uniquely from the triplet while no pairwise Pareto dominance holds inside it. Beyond restated EFF/ATT/NBC/RA/SYM, three new axioms are needed: **PC — Pairwise Consistency** (transitivity of revealed Pareto dominance), **EC — Existence of a Compromise** (if every pair in a triplet is chosen whole, the triplet's choice is a singleton — this forces the compromise effect), and **OC — Overcoming a Compromise** (a compromise $y$ can be displaced only by an option Pareto-dominating it). The sufficiency proof constructs the two orderings inductively by partitioning $X$ into nested **revealed-Pareto layers** $EFF^{k}$ and, within each, "inner" extreme-to-interior layers $E^{kl}$, adding elements in lexicographic $(k,l)$ order so transitivity is preserved (Lemmas 2–4 and Appendix lemmas).

## Data
None — this is a pure choice-theoretic / axiomatic paper. It is motivated by, but does not itself analyze, the experimental marketing/psychology literature on attraction and compromise effects (Huber et al. 1982; Simonson 1989; Tversky–Shafir 1992; Shafir et al. 1993). Its output is a set of *testable implications* (the axioms) against which future choice data could be confronted.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1 (known-preference characterization).** The fallback bargaining operator $C^{f}$ is the *unique* regular bargaining operator satisfying EFF, ATT, NBC, RA, and SYM. So the five intuitive properties of conflict resolution pin down exactly the max-min-score rule.
- **Theorem 2 (revealed-preference foundation).** A bargaining solution $C$ satisfies EFF, ATT, NBC, RA, SYM, PC, EC, and OC **if and only if** there exists $\succ\in L(X)^2$ with $C=C^{f}_{\succ}$. Thus these eight choice-data conditions are exactly the testable implications of the dual-self fallback model. Examples 1–2 show every one of PC, EC, OC is indispensable (omitting any admits choice data with no consistent preference pair).
- **Theorem 3 (identifiability).** For two profiles, $C^{f}_{\succ}=C^{f}_{\succ'}$ iff $\succ'$ is obtained from $\succ$ by *permuting the two orderings over atoms (of the induced partition) containing at least two elements*. Beyond the trivial relabeling of selves, all remaining multiplicity concerns "irrelevant alternatives": preferences are uniquely identified down to the minimal $C$-dominant sets that Pareto-dominate everything outside them.
- **Mechanism.** Figure 2 shows why: with only $\{A,B\}$ both get minimal score $0$; adding a third option $C$ (asymmetrically dominated, or extreme) raises $B$'s minimal score above $A$'s, so $B$ is uniquely selected — reproducing both effects from one rule. Fallback "favors" compromise over attraction: when a unique option is ranked between all others and only one Pareto-comparable pair exists, that middle option is chosen alone.
- **Contrast rules.** The utilitarian/**Borda** variant $\arg\max_x[s_1+s_2]$ fails: it selects all Pareto-optimal options (no compromise effect) and is not robust to monotone transformations of the ordinal scores.

## Contribution
Three contributions, by the authors' own framing: (1) a *single* model explaining both attraction and compromise effects in an individual decision problem, with characterized testable implications — earlier formal models (Ok et al. 2011; Lombardi 2009; Kivetz et al. 2004a,b; Kamenica 2008) capture at most one effect, or rely on cardinal structure or market equilibrium; (2) a revealed-preference foundation for a *cooperative* (compromise-seeking) dual-self model, in contrast to the non-cooperative dual-self tradition; (3) a new revealed-preference characterization and identifiability result for an ordinal bargaining solution already known under several names (Rawlsian arbitration rule, Kant–Rawls social compromise, unanimity compromise, fallback bargaining), expressed in individual-choice terms (how $C$ changes as options are added/removed at fixed preferences) rather than the social-choice terms of Sprumont (1993) and Kıbrıs–Sertel (2007) (how $C$ changes as preferences vary). The dual interpretation makes the results testable implications for interpersonal bilateral bargaining over finite sets.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit extensions and gaps:
- **More than two selves.** The model extends mechanically to $n$ orderings via $\min_{i=1,\dots,n}s_i$. Proposition 1 shows fallback retains predictive content for any $n$ (RA, EFF, NBC, PC, one inclusion of ATT, a strengthened RA, and a weaker OC all survive), unlike many multi-self models that become vacuous. But ATT, EC, SYM, OC are no longer always valid, and **whether the Proposition 1 axioms are also *sufficient* for an $n$-self fallback representation is left open.**
- **Unequal weights / "weighted" fallback.** Allowing different weights on dimensions raises an open **identifiability concern**: the extra freedom may make the same choice correspondence consistent with too many preference profiles.
- **Intensities / cardinal trade-offs.** The ordinal model cannot capture distance-based effects (e.g., a compromise appearing for $y=(50,50)$ but not $y=(2,2)$). The authors sketch cardinal score variants — summing differences $(x_1-y_1)$ over dominated options, or the family $W^{\alpha}=\alpha\min\{s_1,s_2\}+(1-\alpha)\max\{s_1,s_2\}$ that interpolates between Borda ($\alpha=\tfrac12$) and fallback ($\alpha=1$), or scores depending on the full distribution of lower-contour elements — but **these are explicitly left for future research.**
- **Compromise interpretation.** Footnote 9 notes that any $z$ "between" $x$ and $y$ ordinally counts as a compromise regardless of how close it sits to either endpoint; a richer notion (where $z$ much nearer $x$ is not a genuine compromise) is acknowledged as unresolved.

## Connections
The paper sits between **behavioral choice theory** and **axiomatic bargaining**. It builds on the *reason-based choice* heuristic (Simonson 1989; Tversky–Shafir 1992; Shafir et al. 1993) and the dual-self tradition (Gul–Pesendorfer 2001; Fudenberg–Levine 2006; Bernheim–Rangel 2004; Eliaz–Spiegler 2006), but departs by making the selves *cooperate*. On explaining the effects it contrasts with Ok, Ortoleva and Riella (2011) (reference-dependent, attraction only), Lombardi (2009) (attraction only), Kivetz, Netzer and Srinivasan (2004a,b) (cardinal aggregation), and Kamenica (2008) (market equilibrium). On multi-rationale aggregation it relates to Ambrus–Rozen (2009), Green–Hojman (2007), Manzini–Mariotti (2007), and Kalai, Rubinstein and Spiegler (2002). The bargaining solution itself is the *fallback / unanimity-compromise / Rawlsian arbitration / Kant–Rawls* rule (Sprumont 1993; Hurwicz–Sertel 1997; Brams–Kilgour 2001; Kıbrıs–Sertel 2007), with Anbarci (2006) supplying its non-cooperative foundation à la Rubinstein (1982); the WARP/β-axiom violation framing is Sen (1971). On testable implications of collective choice it is closest to Sprumont (2000) and Eliaz, Ray and Razin (2011), and relates to Chiappori (1988), Chiappori–Ekeland (2009), and Chambers–Echenique (2009).

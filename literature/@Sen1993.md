> [!abstract] Abstract
> Internal consistency of choice has been a central concept in demand theory, social choice theory, decision theory, behavioral economics, and related fields. It is argued here that this idea is essentially confused, and there is no way of determining whether a choice function is consistent or not without referring to something external to choice behavior (such as objectives, values, or norms). We have to re-examine the robustness of the standard results in this light. The main formal result presented here is an extension of Arrow's General Possibility Theorem. This drops the need to impose any condition of internal consistency of social choice, or any internal notion of "social rationality."

## Summary

Sen argues that the standard axioms of "internal consistency" of choice (the weak/strong axioms of revealed preference, Property $\alpha$, Property $\gamma$, binariness) are conceptually confused: acts of choice, unlike statements, cannot on their own contradict one another, so whether a choice function is "consistent" is undecidable without invoking something *external* to choice behavior (objectives, values, norms, social principles). He distinguishes *imposed* internal correspondences (axioms asserted a priori) from *entailed* internal correspondences (implications of an external demand, e.g. maximizing a fixed utility function). The critique is aimed only at the former. As the central formal payoff he re-derives the impossibility of the Paretian liberal and, most importantly, Arrow's impossibility theorem (Theorem 3) using only external-correspondence conditions phrased in terms of *rejection*, dispensing with any consistency axiom on social choice and with any notion of "social rationality"/"social preference."

## Research question

Can the requirement of "internal consistency" of a choice function be justified or even defined without reference to anything external to choice? And if such a priori consistency axioms are dropped, do the standard results of social choice theory — in particular Arrow's General Possibility Theorem — survive?

## Method / identification

Pure axiomatic choice theory; no data. A choice function $C(S)$ assigns to each admissible nonempty (finite) menu $S$ a nonempty choice set $C(S)\subseteq S$. Sen states the two basic consistency axioms — Basic contraction consistency (Property $\alpha$): $[x\in C(S)\;\&\;x\in T\subseteq S]\Rightarrow x\in C(T)$; and Basic expansion consistency (Property $\gamma$): if $x\in C(S_j)$ for all $S_j$ in a class then $x\in C(\bigcup_j S_j)$. Together (for finite sets) $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ are necessary and sufficient for *binariness*: $C(S)=\{x\in S \mid \forall y\in S:\, x R_C y\}$, where the revealed-preference relation is $x R_C y \iff \exists S:[x\in C(S)\;\&\;y\in S]$.

The foundational argument is conceptual: the pair ${x}=C(\{x,y\})$ and ${y}=C(\{x,y,z\})$ violates $\alpha$/WARP, yet Sen exhibits context-dependent rationales under which it is fully reasoned — (i) *positional* choice (don't take the last apple / largest cake slice), (ii) *epistemic value of the menu* (the offer of cocaine reveals something about the host), (iii) *freedom to reject* (fasting differs from starving). Each is still maximization, but of a *menu-dependent* ordering.

The constructive core recasts social-choice conditions as *external correspondences* stated via a rejection operator. For the Paretian liberal: $x P^* y \iff [\forall T:\, x\in T \Rightarrow y\notin C(T)]$ ("$y$ must not be chosen when $x$ is available"). Conditions P* (rejection of Pareto-inferior states) and L* (rejection based on minimal liberty), plus unrestricted domain U*, yield Theorem 2 with no imposed consistency on $C$. For Arrow, Sen works over a single fixed menu $S$ ($|S|\ge 3$) using set-specific Arrovian revealed preference $x P^S_C y$ ($x$ chosen and $y$ rejected from $S$) and defines *rejection decisiveness*: $G$ is decisive over ordered pair $(x,y)$ for $S$ if $(x P_i y\ \forall i\in G)\Rightarrow y\notin C(S)$. Condition I* (*Independent Decisiveness*) weakens Arrow's IIA to: the group's power to reject $y$ in the presence of $x$ must be robust to all rankings of irrelevant alternatives. The functional collective choice rule is $C(S)=F(\{R_i\})$.

## Key findings

- **Foundational claim.** There is no purely internal way to declare a choice function consistent or inconsistent; "consistency" is parasitic on an external reference. Choices are not statements, so $x$ from $\{x,y\}$ and $y$ from $\{x,y,z\}$ are not a contradiction unless an interpretive (external) reading makes them so.
- **Distinction.** *Imposed* vs *entailed* internal correspondences. Revealed-preference axioms are fine when *entailed* by menu-independent utility maximization (as in standard consumer theory), but illegitimate when *imposed* a priori as bare "consistency."
- **Theorem 2 (Paretian liberal, choice-functional).** No FCCR $F$ satisfies U*, P*, L*; proved by exhibiting a menu from which P* and L* jointly forbid every alternative, so $C(S)$ is empty.
- **Theorem 3 (General Choice-functional Impossibility Theorem).** No $F$ satisfies U*, P*, D* (rejection nondictatorship), and I*. Proved via two lemmas: **Theorem 3.1** — decisiveness over any one pair in $S$ implies decisiveness over all of $S$ (field-expansion / contagion); **Theorem 3.2** — any decisive set with more than one member has a decisive proper subset (in fact: in any two-fold partition of a decisive set, one part is decisive — the *ultrafilter* property). Iterating 3.2 on the finite set of all individuals (Pareto-decisive by P*) forces a single decisive individual, i.e. a dictator. This re-establishes Arrow's theorem with *no* imposed internal-consistency axiom on social choice and *no* social-preference/social-rationality structure.

## Contribution

Separates two ideas long conflated in the foundations of rational and social choice: maximization-with-respect-to-context versus context-free "internal consistency." It shows the Arrow impossibility is not driven by collective-rationality/consistency requirements but by the tension between informational parsimony and discriminating social choice — Arrow survives even the most thorough stripping of consistency assumptions. It legitimizes *menu-dependent* maximization (positional, epistemic, freedom-based) as genuinely rational, providing a foundational charter for later behavioral and menu-dependent choice theory, and proposes a research program of re-auditing every axiomatic social-choice result that smuggles in imposed consistency.

## Limitations & open questions

Sen explicitly disclaims several adjacent problems as outside the paper: (1) the *empirical* question of how far actual human choices satisfy standard consistency conditions; (2) the *interpretational* question of whether a person's underlying binary relation (when binariness holds) can be read as their welfare function; (3) the *modelling* question of whether humans are best seen as maximizers of any objective at all (maximization is admitted as one form of external correspondence, not the only one). He flags as open the program of *recharacterizing constructive* social-choice results — utilitarian aggregation, the Rawlsian lexmin rule — through defensible external-correspondence axioms, and warns that any result not so retainable "must be seen as deeply problematic." How best to formulate liberty (relation vs game-form) is noted as unsettled.

## Connections

The target of critique is Samuelson's (1938) revealed-preference program of explaining "behavior without reference to anything other than behavior," with Hicks and Little as fellow travellers. The consistency axioms trace to Samuelson, Houthakker (1950), Arrow (1959), Chernoff (1954), Richter, Herzberger (1973), Plott (1973), and Sen's own (1971) characterization linking $\alpha+\gamma$ to binariness. Theorem 3 extends and supersedes the long line weakening Arrow's collective-rationality requirement (Mas-Colell–Sonnenschein, Brown (1975), Blair–Bordes–Kelly–Suzumura (1976), Blair–Pollak, Blau) and connects to the ultrafilter approach (Kirman–Sondermann (1972), Hansson (1976)) and to Chichilnisky's (1982) Pareto/dictator equivalence. The "impossibility of the Paretian liberal" is Sen (1970, 1976a). Buchanan (1954) motivates eschewing "social rationality." The menu-dependence examples connect to bounded rationality (Simon), framing/anomalies (Kahneman–Slovik–Tversky 1982; Dawes–Thaler 1988), self-command and akrasia (Schelling, Elster, Thaler–Shefrin), and Kanger's background-set-dependent choice. This paper is a foundational antecedent for later menu-dependent and reference-dependent choice modeling and for behavioral revealed-preference work that rationalizes apparent inconsistency via context.

## Summary
Grether and Plott set out, as self-described skeptical economists, to discredit the psychologists' "preference reversal" finding by replicating it under conditions that would satisfy economic objections. Instead they confirmed it: a large fraction of subjects choose the high-probability "P bet" over the high-payoff "$ bet" yet assign a higher monetary selling price to the $ bet. They construct a battery of thirteen rival explanations (economic, psychological, and methodological), design two incentivized laboratory experiments to control for each, and find that all but one explanation can be rejected. The phenomenon survives real cash incentives, randomized-decision payment, the Becker–DeGroot–Marschak elicitation, non-market wording, and order randomization, leaving standard preference theory unable to account for the behavior.

## Research question
Should the preference reversal phenomenon documented by psychologists concern economists? Concretely: (1) Does the phenomenon persist in settings where economic theory is normally applied—i.e., with real, motivating monetary stakes and incentive-compatible elicitation? (2) Can it be explained by standard economic theory or a minor variant thereof (income effects, indifference, misspecified incentives, strategic misreporting)?

## Method / identification
The core anomaly is shown to be a transitivity violation. With $w$ initial wealth, $\$(A)$ and $\$(B)$ the selling limit prices of bets $A$ and $B$, $-$ indifference and $\succ$ preference, by definition of the limit prices $(w+\$(A),0,0)-(w,1,0)$ and $(w+\$(B),0,0)-(w,0,1)$. If the subject states $(w,1,0)\succ(w,0,1)$ (prefers $A$), transitivity forces $(w+\$(A),0,0)\succ(w+\$(B),0,0)$ and hence $\$(A)\succ\$(B)$. The observed reversal $\$(B)>\$(A)$ contradicts this, so it is not merely an expected-utility violation but a deeper inconsistency between preference measured by choice and by valuation.

The identification strategy is elimination by design. The authors tabulate 13 candidate theories (Table 1)—Misspecified Incentives, Income Effects, Indifference, Strategic Responses, Probabilities, Elimination by Aspect, Lexicographic Semiorder, Information-Processing Decision Costs, Response-Mode/Easy-Justification, Confusion, Low Frequency, Unsophisticated Subjects, Experimenters-were-Psychologists—and build features into the experiment that neutralize each.

Two experiments. Experiment 1 used the six P/$ gamble pairs from Lichtenstein and Slovic (1971, exp. 3). Subjects were recruited from economics/political-science classes, told it was an economics experiment, and split into a no-incentive room (flat $7) and an incentive room (one decision chosen at random by bingo cage, payoff added to a $7 credit, max loss $2). The three-part structure—preferences on pairs (1)-(3); selling prices on all twelve gambles via Becker–DeGroot–Marschak; preferences on pairs (4)-(6)—lets them test order effects and the decision-cost story. Probabilities were operationalized with a numbered bingo cage; indifference ("Don't care," resolved by coin toss) was allowed; an explanation, practice gamble, demonstration, and written test addressed confusion. Experiment 2 targeted the strategic-response theory by replacing selling-price elicitation in some sections with "dollar equivalents" (the indifference amount), stripping all market language, and counterbalancing order across two rooms.

## Data
Original laboratory data. Experiment 1: $n=44$ (no incentives) and $n=46$ (incentives). Experiment 2: $n=20$ (group 1) and $n=18$ (group 2). Each subject made preference choices on six gamble pairs and gave valuations on twelve gambles; the gamble parameters are reported in Table 2.

## Key findings
The reversal is robust and asymmetric. In Experiment 1 with incentives, 70% of P-bet choices were inconsistent with announced selling prices versus only 13% for $-bet choices; without incentives the figures were 56% and 11%. Critically, incentives did not reduce reversals—if anything the phenomenon was *stronger* with real money—directly rejecting the decision-cost theory (Theory 8). Reversal rates were the same before and after pricing and invariant to question order, undercutting the anchoring/order story. The BDM mechanism and random-decision payment neutralized income effects (Theory 2) and incentive misspecification (Theory 1); allowing indifference (Theory 3) changed nothing (only 3% of choices). Experiment 2 "shot down" the strategic-bargaining explanation (Theory 4): results from selling prices and from non-market dollar equivalents were essentially identical, and selling prices were not systematically higher than equivalents (higher in only 10 of 24 cases). Because Lichtenstein and Slovic (1971) was replicated, the elimination-by-aspect (Theory 6) and lexicographic-semiorder (Theory 7) accounts—which imply forms of stochastic transitivity violated here—are also rejected. The only surviving explanation is Theory 9 (response mode / easy justification), which the authors call the least satisfactory because it makes choice context-dependent in a way that offers no clear route to repairing the theory.

## Contribution
This paper transplanted the preference reversal anomaly from psychology into experimental economics and gave it credibility precisely because hostile economists, controlling for every standard economic objection, could not make it disappear. It is a foundational demonstration that procedure invariance (valuations and choices should agree) can fail systematically, anticipating later work on context-dependent and constructed preferences, and it established a template of theory-elimination experimental design.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors explicitly conclude that no available alternative theory covers the same broad range of phenomena as preference theory, so they do not advocate discarding it. They flag Theory 9 as unsatisfying: if response mode or wording is a primary determinant of choice, "the way to modify accepted theory is not apparent." Open hooks they leave: How many other simple choice situations harbor such systematic exceptions? Can a theory of choice serve simultaneously as a basis for market theory and welfare economics while accommodating reversals? How should cost–benefit and welfare measures be interpreted once the sign of benefit-minus-cost can flip with the elicitation mode? They issue an explicit challenge: modify the theory to account for the exception *without* rendering it vacuous. They also note the anomaly appears just as preference theory is being successfully extended to non-human choice (Kagel and Battalio).

## Connections

The elicitation rests on Becker, DeGroot, and Marschak's incentive-compatible mechanism. The transitivity-cycle discussion engages Tversky (1969) on intransitive binary choice, [[@Tversky1972|Tversky (1972)]] elimination-by-aspects, and the lexicographic semiorder. The framing references economic imperialism—Becker (1968) on crime, Becker (1973, 1974) on marriage, Hammermesh and Soss on suicide, Fair on affairs—and Kagel and Battalio (1975, 1976) on animal choice. The work is a precursor to the broader anomalies and behavioral-economics literature; later procedure-invariance and constructed-preference research (e.g., Tversky, Slovic, and Kahneman on reversals; Tversky and Thaler) extends it, and it connects to bounded-rationality and menu-dependent choice models such as [[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti (2007)]].

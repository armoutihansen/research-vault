> [!abstract] Abstract
> The purpose of this paper is to understand the effect of strategic interaction on a manufacturer's channel-structure decision. In the model I analyze, if there were no strategic interaction, then the manufacturer would prefer vertical integration to decentralization. With strategic interaction, however, decentralization can be a Nash equilibrium strategy (as McGuire and Staein [McGuire, Timothy, Richard Staelin, 1983a. An industry equilibrium analysis of downstream vertical integration. Marketing Sci. 2(Spring) 161–192.] showed). So the question arises, What is it about strategic interaction that makes decentralization a Nash equilibrium strategy? The answer I give is that strategic interaction makes it possible for a manufacturer's retail demand curve to rise when he decentralizes. I show that this raising of the demand curve on decentralization can happen only if one of the following (mutually exclusive) conditions is satisfied: (1) the manufacturers' products are demand substitutes at the retail level and strategic complements at the manufacturer or retailer levels, (2) the manufacturer's products are demand complements at the retail level and strategic substitutes at the manufacturer or retailer levels. Strategic complementarity at the retailer (manufacturer) level means that if one retailer (manufacturer) raises his price, then the other retailer (manufacturer) would raise his price as well; strategic substitutability is just the opposite. These results show that what is important for decentralization to occur is not how (demand) substitutable the two manufacturers' products are (as one might have thought from earlier works in the literature), but rather the nature of coupling between demand dependence and strategic dependence.

## Summary
Moorthy revisits the puzzle in [[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin (1983a)]]: with a monopolist, vertical integration always beats decentralization (decentralization just stacks margins—double marginalization), yet with two competing manufacturers, decentralization through independent retailers can be a Nash equilibrium. The paper's contribution is a clean mechanism and a sharp characterization. Decentralization "works," when it works, by *raising the decentralizer's own retail demand curve* in equilibrium. This raising occurs only through a specific coupling of two distinct properties—*demand dependence* (a pure demand concept) and *strategic dependence* (a joint demand-and-cost concept in the sense of Bulow, Geanakoplos & Klemperer 1985). The headline result is that high demand substitutability is neither the right nor a sufficient explanation; what matters is the pairing of demand substitutes with strategic complements, or demand complements with strategic substitutes.

## Research question
Why does strategic interaction with a rival manufacturer make decentralization (using an exclusive independent retailer) a Nash-equilibrium channel structure, when a monopolist manufacturer in the same setting would always strictly prefer vertical integration? What structural conditions on demand and costs are necessary for decentralization to be an equilibrium?

## Method / identification
A two-stage game-theoretic channel model. Two identical manufacturers ($M_1,M_2$) each simultaneously choose a channel structure—integrate forward into retailing, or decentralize via an exclusive independent retailer—yielding three structures: II (both integrated), ID (one integrated, one decentralized), DD (both decentralized). After the channel choice, prices are set: integrated manufacturers set retail prices; decentralized manufacturers commit a per-unit wholesale price (linear tariff, price $\geq$ marginal cost), and independent retailers then set retail prices simultaneously. Equilibrium is computed by backward induction (subgame-perfect Nash) within each structure, then the channel-choice game is solved.

The analysis proceeds in two parts. First (§2–§3), Moorthy re-derives McGuire & Staelin's linear-demand result $D_i = 1 - p_i + \theta p_j$ and reframes it via the "raising the demand curve" logic, then presents four worked examples—designed as experimental "treatments" varying one feature at a time: (1) nonstrategic competition (rival's price fixed), (2) constant-elasticity demand, (3) linear demand with retailer economies of scale (decreasing marginal cost), (4) demand complements ($\theta<0$). Second (§4), he generalizes to arbitrary demand and cost functions under regularity Assumptions 1–6 (twice-differentiable, downward-sloping demand; strict quasi-concavity of profit functions; monotone marginal-profit/best-response functions; stability conditions ensuring a unique price equilibrium per structure). Key definitions: product $A$ is a *strategic complement* of $B$ iff $\partial^2\pi_A/\partial p_A\partial p_B > 0$; *demand complement* iff $\partial D_A/\partial p_B < 0$.

## Data
None—this is a pure theory paper. The four numerical examples (e.g. $a=0.7$, $r=m=0.3$ in the economies-of-scale case) are illustrative parameterizations plotted in Figures 2–5, not empirical data.

## Key findings
- **Three-step explanation of McGuire–Staelin.** (Step 1, Proposition 1) Decentralization can benefit a manufacturer only if it benefits the channel as a whole—because the independent retailer extracts a share of channel profit, the manufacturer-profit criterion is *more demanding* than the channel-profit criterion (this is why the critical substitutability $\theta\geq 0.771$ for channel profit becomes $\theta\geq 0.931$ for manufacturer profit). (Step 2, Theorem 1) Decentralization raises channel profit only if it raises the rival channel's equilibrium retail price under demand substitutes, or lowers it under demand complements—because that is what shifts the decentralizer's demand curve up. (Step 3) Unilateral decentralization *always* raises the decentralizer's own retail price (double marginalization, via higher effective marginal cost), but its effect on the *rival's* price depends on strategic complementarity vs. substitutability.
- **Necessary conditions (Theorems 3 and 5).** ID/DD can be a Nash equilibrium only if one of two mutually exclusive scenarios holds: (1) demand substitutes coupled with strategic complements at the manufacturer or retailer level, or (2) demand complements coupled with strategic substitutes at the manufacturer or retailer level. With strategic *independence* at both levels (Examples 1–2), decentralization is *never* an equilibrium, no matter how high the demand substitutability.
- **Examples show demand substitutability is neither necessary nor sufficient.** Example 3 (economies of scale) produces strategic substitutes at the retail level, so DD is never an equilibrium despite arbitrarily high substitutability; Example 4 (demand complements + strategic substitutes) makes DD an equilibrium even though the retail environment is "the opposite of competitive."
- **Theorem 6.** With demand complements, the II price equilibrium dominates the DD price equilibrium for both manufacturers—so when both II and DD are equilibria under complementarity, integration is the predicted outcome. No analogous dominance holds for demand substitutes.

## Contribution
Disentangles two concepts—demand dependence and strategic dependence—that the prior channels literature had conflated, and shows that the strategic decentralization result hinges on their *coupling*, not on demand substitutability per se. Imports Bulow–Geanakoplos–Klemperer strategic complements/substitutes into channel theory and supplies a unifying "raising the demand curve" intuition plus a general (non-parametric) necessary-condition characterization.

## Limitations & open questions
- Results are stated as **necessary, not sufficient, conditions**: $p^{dd}>p^{id}$ is necessary but not sufficient for DD to be an equilibrium (Example 3 shows DD failing even when the price ordering holds). A full sufficiency characterization is left open.
- The price-ordering of $p^{id}$ versus $p^{dd}$ is **ambiguous** when strategic complementarity holds at one level and substitutability at the other (the mixed case of Example 3); Moorthy can only sign it via $|(w^{dd}-m)-(w^{dd}-w^{id})|$ and slope differences.
- Restricted to **linear (per-unit) wholesale tariffs with price $\geq$ marginal cost**. With two-part tariffs allowing below-cost unit prices (as in Bonanno & Vickers 1988; Coughlan & Wernerfelt 1988), the strategic-dependence conditions are no longer needed—an explicit boundary inviting work on which tariff regimes restore them.
- Generality Theorems 4–5 require **constant manufacturer marginal cost**; the case of non-constant manufacturer marginal cost (where the ID problem cannot be mapped to DD) is flagged as unresolved.
- Assumes **commitment to wholesale prices** and **exclusive dealing/territories**; relaxing commitment (per Coughlan & Wernerfelt) may collapse the equilibrium to integration.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> We conduct a field experiment in a naturally occurring labor environment and track whether the performance of workers responds to unexpected wage increases. Specifically, we investigate how the timing of wage increases affects efforts. We find that workers' performance is substantially higher for the same total wage when their wage is increased in two steps as opposed to a single increase at the outset. Moreover, workers are more honest and are more willing to do voluntary extra work after surprising wage increases compared to a baseline condition without increases.

## Summary
A natural field experiment with temporary library-inventory workers tests not just *whether* unexpected wage increases trigger gift-exchange reciprocity, but how the *timing* of a fixed-budget gift matters. Holding the total wage bill constant, splitting an unexpected pay rise into two surprises raises output by about 11% relative to a single up-front increase. The gift effect is persistent (it materializes in the *second* half of the day, contra the fast-decaying effects in prior field studies) and spills over into honesty in self-reports and willingness to do unpaid extra work.

## Research question
Does the *timing* of unexpected kindness (wage increases) matter for the magnitude and persistence of positively reciprocal worker effort? Specifically, can two sequential surprise wage increases elicit more effort than one larger up-front increase that delivers the *same* total pay?

## Method / identification
A between-subjects natural field experiment ($n=98$ temporary workers) embedded in a real library modernization project (affixing barcode labels to ~150,000 books) at the University of Cologne, August 2012. Workers, hired through a trade-fair staffing agency for a one-day freelance job, did not know they were in an experiment; one-shot interactions leave little room for reputation. Three treatments, assigned at the shift/project-day level (so all workers in a shift share a treatment, none in more than one):

- **Baseline:** pre-announced market wage of 8 EUR/hour.
- **Gift_1:** surprise increase to 12 EUR/hour announced before work begins.
- **Gift_2:** same total budget split into two surprises — 10 EUR/hour at the start, then a second surprise raising it to 14 EUR/hour after three working hours. Total fixed payment in Gift_1 and Gift_2 is identical (84 EUR vs. 56 EUR in Baseline).

**Hypotheses.** Standard selfish preferences predict equal effort across all three (Null 1, since pay is unconditional). Gift exchange predicts Gift > Baseline (H1). If kindness sensation is proportional to the monetary gift, uneven timing should not matter (Null 2); but if the *sensation of receiving a gift per se* drives reciprocity, repeated surprises raise effort even at fixed cost (H2).

**Outcomes / estimation.** Primary outcome is output (tags inserted), tracked covertly by counting label rolls collected at the help desk and timestamping. Non-parametric two-sided Mann-Whitney U, Wilcoxon matched-pairs signed-rank, and $\chi^2$ tests for treatment comparisons. Dynamics estimated via random-effects panel regressions of hourly performance with robust standard errors clustered at the shift level, controlling for age, gender, and shift size; specifications use treatment dummies, a linear hour trend interacted with treatment, and a "Hours 4-6" dummy interacted with treatment. Secondary outcomes: net productive working time (gross time minus tracked breaks, plus 20 min if the worker stayed for an unpaid extra sorting task) and honesty, measured as the deviation $D$ between self-reported and actual performance (reports were elicited under an end-of-day bonus scheme for the top 30%, with the bonus deliberately announced only at day's end to avoid confounding reciprocity).

## Data
Field-experimental data on 98 workers across six project days and five libraries. Covertly recorded objective performance (label rolls), tracked break times, voluntary extra-work participation, and self-reported performance. Demographics from the agency (68% female, mostly aged 20-30). Probit checks show no selection into treatments on age/gender; appendix tables confirm balanced shift sizes and demographics.

## Key findings
- **H1 (gifts raise effort):** Output rises from 1,031 tags (Baseline) to 1,071 in Gift_1 (+3.9%, insignificant, $p=0.353$) and to 1,189 in Gift_2 (+15.3%, $p=0.025$).
- **H2 (timing matters):** Gift_2 output exceeds Gift_1 by 11.0% at *identical* labor cost ($p=0.047$); the regression coefficient on Gift_2 is significantly larger than on Gift_1 ($p=0.050$). This rejects Null 2.
- **Persistence / dynamics:** In hours 1-3 the three treatments are statistically indistinguishable (488 / 447 / 486 tags). The gift effect appears in hours 4-6: Gift_1 rises +19.9%, Gift_2 +25.9%, while Baseline falls -5.2%. Both gift-by-hour interactions are positive and significant; the second-half gift effect is significantly above Baseline ($p=0.046$ Gift_1, $p=0.002$ Gift_2). The whole-day Gift_2 vs. Gift_1 difference is significant even though the per-period interaction differences are not (Wald $p=0.196$ in Model 2, $p=0.315$ in Model 3).
- **Productive time:** Gifts raise net productive time by ~3.5% (weakly significant vs. Baseline, $p\approx0.07$-$0.08$), but Gift_1 and Gift_2 do not differ ($p=0.850$). Far more gift-treatment workers stayed for the unpaid extra task (3/21 Baseline vs. 16/38 and 13/39).
- **Honesty:** Over-reporting is common (45 of 98 cases). Average overstatement is 319 tags in Baseline versus 146 (Gift_1) and 114 (Gift_2) — gifts roughly halve cheating ($p=0.021$ and $p=0.002$ vs. Baseline); Gift_1 vs. Gift_2 not significant. The over- to under-report ratio drops from 2.13 (Baseline) to 0.76 (Gift_2), consistent with genuine honesty rather than mere attentiveness.
- **Profitability caveat:** Wage cost per tag rises from 5.4 cents (Baseline) to 7.8 (Gift_1) and 7.1 (Gift_2); the *direct* output gain alone need not make the gift profitable, but timing delivers an ~11% performance gain at zero marginal cost.

## Contribution
First field study to isolate the effect of the *timing* of unexpected kindness, demonstrating that splitting a fixed gift budget into repeated surprises is a budget-neutral lever for raising effort. It also documents unusually *persistent* reciprocity (concentrated in the second half of the day) and extends gift-exchange outcomes beyond output to two novel margins — voluntary unpaid extra work and self-reporting honesty — linking the latter to Greenberg's (1990) finding that pay cuts increase theft, here in the opposite (positive) direction.

## Limitations & open questions
- The authors flag that the second-step effect may reflect **narrow bracketing** of effort to the current wage: Gift_1's second half feels like a "12 EUR job" while Gift_2's feels like a "14 EUR job." They explicitly call for **future work comparing expected vs. unexpected wage increases of the same magnitude** to disentangle the gift-sensation channel from bracketing.
- The mechanism behind delayed responses is not pinned down; they argue (via robustness without hour 1) it reflects higher worker perseverance rather than a learning curve, but this remains interpretive.
- Timing affects work *intensity* but not honesty or total working time; the authors speculate this maps onto a System 1 (fast, affective) vs. System 2 (deliberate) distinction — an untested conjecture.
- Direct effects may not make gifts profitable; whether generosity pays over longer horizons is left open.
- Small sample (98), one-day one-shot relationships, single task; external validity to ongoing employment relationships is uncertain.

## Connections
Builds directly on the gift-exchange field tradition of Gneezy & List (2006) and Bellemare & Shearer (2009), both of which found *transient* effects — the persistence result here is the contrast. The "gift sensation over monetary value" interpretation draws on Kube, Marechal & Puppe (2012) on the currency of reciprocity and Kosfeld & Neckermann (2011) on symbolic awards. Theoretical roots in gift-exchange and reciprocity models: Akerlof (1982), Rabin (1993), Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger (2004), Falk & Fischbacher (2006). The honesty margin connects to Greenberg (1990) on underpayment and theft, and to Gill, Prowse & Vlassopoulos (2012) on workplace cheating. The bracketing conjecture invokes Read, Loewenstein & Rabin (1999), and the System 1/2 framing Kahneman (2011) and Evans (2008).

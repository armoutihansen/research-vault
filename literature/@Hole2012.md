> [!abstract] Abstract
> (no abstract in Zotero) — From the published version: Different approaches to modelling the distribution of WTP are compared using stated preference data on Tanzanian Clinical Officers' job choices and mixed logit models. The standard approach of specifying the distributions of the coefficients and deriving WTP as the ratio of two coefficients (estimation in preference space) is compared to specifying the distributions for WTP directly at the estimation stage (estimation in WTP space). The models in preference space fit the data better than the corresponding models in WTP space although the difference between the best fitting models in the two estimation regimes is minimal. Moreover, the willingness to pay estimates derived from the preference space models turn out to be very high for many of the job attributes. The results suggest that sensitivity testing using a variety of model specifications, including estimation in WTP space, is recommended when using mixed logit models to estimate willingness to pay distributions.

## Summary
This paper compares two ways of recovering willingness-to-pay (WTP) distributions from mixed logit discrete-choice models: the conventional "preference-space" parameterisation, where one places distributions on taste coefficients and computes WTP as a ratio of coefficients, versus the "WTP-space" parameterisation of Train and Weeks (2005), where distributions are placed directly on the WTP quantities. Using a stated-preference job-choice experiment with Tanzanian Clinical Officers, the authors find that preference-space models fit slightly better but produce implausibly large, heavily right-skewed WTP estimates, whereas WTP-space models yield more moderate WTP at a negligible loss of fit. The headline practical message is that the choice of estimation regime materially changes WTP conclusions, so sensitivity testing across specifications (including WTP space) is essential.

## Research question
When using mixed logit models to value non-monetary attributes, does it matter whether one parameterises the random utility model in preference space (distributions on coefficients) or in WTP space (distributions on WTP directly)? Which approach delivers more credible WTP distributions, and at what cost in goodness of fit? The paper is the first such comparison in the health economics literature.

## Method / identification
The utility person $n$ derives from job $j$ in choice situation $t$ is
$$U_{njt} = \alpha_n w_{njt} + \beta_n' x_{njt} + \varepsilon_{njt},$$
where $w_{njt}$ is the wage, $x_{njt}$ the non-monetary attributes, and $\varepsilon_{njt}$ is extreme-value with individual-specific scale $\mu_n$ (variance $\mu_n^2 \pi^2/6$). Dividing through by $\mu_n$ leaves choice behaviour unchanged and homogenises the error variance to $\pi^2/6$, giving the **preference-space** model
$$U_{njt} = \lambda_n w_{njt} + c_n' x_{njt} + \varepsilon_{njt}, \qquad \lambda_n = \alpha_n/\mu_n, \ c_n = \beta_n/\mu_n.$$
Because $\mu_n$ appears in both $\lambda_n$ and $c_n$, the coefficients are generally correlated; assuming them independent implicitly assumes a homoscedastic error. Defining WTP as $\gamma_n = c_n/\lambda_n$ and substituting yields the **WTP-space** model
$$U_{njt} = \lambda_n\!\left(w_{njt} + \gamma_n' x_{njt}\right) + \varepsilon_{njt}.$$
The two are behaviourally equivalent, but standard coefficient distributions in preference space induce awkward WTP distributions: if $\lambda_n$ and $c_n$ are both normal, $\gamma_n$ is a ratio of normals with no defined moments; a log-normal cost coefficient guarantees moments but can yield extreme skew. In WTP space the scale $\mu_n$ is absorbed into the wage coefficient $\lambda_n$ and cancels from the WTP coefficients $\gamma_n$, so the analyst specifies WTP distributions directly. Greene and Hensher (2010) show the WTP-space model is a special case of the generalised multinomial logit of Fiebig et al. (2010). All models are estimated by maximum simulated likelihood (Halton draws; 1,000 for independent-coefficient models, 2,500 for correlated). Coefficients for wage, education, infrastructure and equipment are given log-normal distributions (sign-constrained); the rest are normal. Four preference-space models (a plain logit, a mixed logit with fixed wage, a fully random independent model, and a correlated-coefficient model) are compared with two analogous WTP-space models.

## Data
Stated-preference discrete choice experiment on health-service job choices among Tanzanian final-year Clinical Officer (CO) students. 320 finalists from 10 randomly selected schools (~60% of all CO finalists in Tanzania in 2007) participated; after exclusions the estimation sample is 296 respondents. A D-optimal design produced 32 choice situations split into two blocks of 16; each task was a binary choice between two hypothetical jobs described by seven attributes (wage, education prospects, location, equipment, infrastructure, housing, workload). Response rate ~96%, unusually high for a DCE.

## Key findings
- Substantial preference heterogeneity: standard deviations of several coefficients are large and significant, and a fixed-wage-coefficient model (Model 2) is rejected as too restrictive.
- Model fit improves with flexibility; the correlated-coefficient preference-space model (Model 4) fits best by log-likelihood, AIC and BIC.
- WTP estimates differ markedly across regimes. Preference-space models with random wage coefficients (Models 3–4) yield very high, heavily right-skewed WTP (e.g. mean WTP for decent infrastructure rises from ~237,000 TSH/month in Model 2 to ~466,000 in Model 3, against a CO starting salary just above 200,000 TSH/month). WTP-space models (Models 5–6) give far more moderate means, comparable to the simplest preference-space models, with smaller standard deviations and less skew; their WTP densities are more peaked while preference-space densities have very long tails.
- Preference-space models fit slightly better than their WTP-space counterparts (consistent with Train and Weeks 2005 and Sonnier et al. 2007, though Scarpa et al. 2008 found the reverse), but the difference between the best models in each regime (Models 4 and 6) is negligible. Allowing wage heterogeneity and coefficient correlation affects fit more than the choice of regime; both WTP-space models beat the fixed-wage preference-space model.
- Some qualitative results are regime-dependent: significant heterogeneity in WTP for housing and education-after-6-years appears in WTP space but not preference space, and attribute rankings differ (though education after 2 years ranks highest under all models). The strong correlation among education WTP measures found in preference space does not carry over to WTP space, where education-after-6-years is negatively correlated with the shorter horizons.

## Contribution
First comparison of preference-space versus WTP-space mixed logit estimation in the health economics literature, and an addition to the small set of WTP-space applications in economics more broadly. It demonstrates concretely that the conventional preference-space approach can manufacture implausible WTP distributions through the ratio-of-randoms problem, and provides a methodological recommendation: estimate in WTP space as part of routine sensitivity analysis.

## Limitations & open questions
- The "true" coefficient/WTP distributions are unknown; normal and log-normal are chosen as tractable approximations, and the authors acknowledge results may be artefacts of these distributional choices rather than genuine heterogeneity.
- The correlation structure in the correlated models is imposed subjectively (informed by interviews) rather than estimated under a fully unrestricted covariance matrix, which was deemed too demanding.
- Which estimation regime "performs best" remains an empirical question with no general answer; the paper can only recommend sensitivity testing, not a decision rule.
- WTP-space estimation was not yet implemented in standard software (the authors used a modified version of the Stata `mixlogit` command), limiting adoption.
- Stated-preference (hypothetical) data raise the usual external-validity and hypothetical-bias concerns; the paper does not validate against revealed behaviour.

## Connections
The core methodological reference is Train and Weeks (2005), who introduced the preference-space/WTP-space distinction; Scarpa, Thiene, and Train (2008) and Sonnier, Ainslie, and Otter (2007) provide the comparable applications in environmental economics and marketing, and Thiene and Scarpa (2009) the estimation approach followed here. Greene and Hensher (2010) and Fiebig et al. (2010) place the WTP-space model within the generalised multinomial logit framework that jointly handles scale and coefficient heterogeneity. The mixed logit foundations are McFadden and Train (2000) and the textbook treatment in Train (2009); the estimation command derives from [[@Hole2007|Hole (2007)]]. Within health economics the paper situates itself against Hole (2008), King et al. (2007), Negrín et al. (2008), Regier et al. (2009), Paterson et al. (2008), and Özdemir et al. (2009), the last of which also estimates in WTP space. Meijer and Rouwendal (2006) motivate relaxing the fixed-cost-coefficient assumption, and the application data come from Kolstad (2011).

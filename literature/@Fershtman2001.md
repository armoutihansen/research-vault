> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper proposes an experimental approach to studying different aspects of discrimination. We let participants play various games with opponents of distinct ethnic affiliation. Strategies based upon such ethnic affiliation provide direct evidence of ethnic discrimination. This approach was utilized to study ethnic discrimination in Israeli Jewish society. Using the "trust game," we detected a systematic mistrust toward men of Eastern origin. A "dictator game" experiment indicated that this discrimination was due to (mistaken) ethnic stereotypes and not to a "taste for discrimination." The "ultimatum game" enabled us to trace another ethnic stereotype that reversed the discrimination's direction. One of the surprising results is that this ethnic discrimination is an entirely male phenomenon.

## Summary

Fershtman and Gneezy introduce an experimental method for distinguishing the *sources* of ethnic discrimination — Becker-style taste for discrimination versus belief-based ethnic stereotypes — by having subjects play simple bargaining games against named partners whose ethnicity is signalled by their (typically ethnic) surname. Applied to the Ashkenazic/Eastern divide in Israeli Jewish society, a trust game reveals systematic mistrust of Eastern men; a dictator game shows this is *not* a taste for discrimination; and an ultimatum game uncovers a second, opposite-signed stereotype (Eastern men as more honor-driven). Strikingly, all of this discrimination is a male-only phenomenon.

## Research question

Can a battery of strategic games disentangle whether ethnic discrimination reflects (i) differential treatment by ethnicity at all; (ii) own-group favoritism (group bias) versus systematic discrimination against a particular group; (iii) a *taste for discrimination* (prejudice in preferences) versus *ethnic stereotypes* (beliefs about partners' likely behavior); and (iv) whether those stereotypes are statistically accurate?

## Method / identification

The identification strategy exploits the differing strategic roles of Player B across three games, holding the ethnic signal fixed. The signal itself is the partner's typically-ethnic Hebrew surname written on the decision sheet (non-anonymous; the experimenter sees choices, unlike the double-anonymity of Berg, Dickhaut & McCabe).

- **Trust game** (Berg–Dickhaut–McCabe): Player A receives NIS 20, transfers $t\in[0,20]$; the experimenter triples it, so B receives $3t$ and returns $r\in[0,3t]$. Subgame-perfect equilibrium under money-maximizing preferences is $t=0$. Here B has a strategic role, so A's transfer can be shaped by *beliefs* about B's reciprocation — i.e. by stereotypes.
- **Dictator game**: identical except B's second stage is removed; A simply divides (transfers tripled). B is passive, so stereotypes about B's strategic response are irrelevant. Hence any ethnicity gap in transfers must reflect a *taste for discrimination*. Comparing trust-game to dictator-game gaps thus separates taste from belief.
- **Ultimatum game**: B can accept or reject; rejection gives both zero. B's strategic role re-enters, so A's offer reflects beliefs about B's rejection propensity, isolating a different stereotype (perceived harsh reaction to unfairness).

Tests: two-way ANOVA on transfers (with Mann–Whitney U robustness checks reported in the appendix); a likelihood-ratio test on the distribution of transfers across discrete modes ($0,5,10$, random), fitting MLE parameters on Ashkenazic males and imposing them on Eastern males ($\chi^2$ with 3 df). Statistical discrimination is checked directly by comparing B's returned amounts (trust game) and rejection rates (ultimatum game) across ethnicities.

## Data

Original lab/field-hybrid experiments on Israeli undergraduates. Trust game: 966 participants (483 as Player B selected by ethnic-name lists: 122 Ashkenazic male, 135 Eastern male, 118 Ashkenazic female, 108 Eastern female; Player A recruited at Tel Aviv University). Dictator game: 616 participants. Ultimatum game: 680 participants. Ethnicity assigned by parental country of birth (post-play questionnaire), with a residual "Israeli" category for mixed/unclassifiable.

## Key findings

- **Observation 1 (segmentation):** In the trust game, average transfer to Ashkenazic male partners was 15.15 vs 8.06 to Eastern males ($F(1,256)=61.64$, $p<.001$). Restricting to male-to-male play the gap widens to 17.16 vs 5.62 ($F(1,140)=101.3$): ~80% gave the full NIS 20 to an Ashkenazic man, only 12% to an Eastern man.
- **Observation 2 (no group bias; internalized stereotype):** Mistrust of Eastern men is shared by Ashkenazic, Eastern, *and* "Israeli" male players alike — Eastern men discriminate against their own group. No own-group favoritism.
- **Observation 3 (not statistical discrimination):** Amounts returned by Player B (conditional on amount received) do not differ by ethnicity (Mann–Whitney, not rejected at all four transfer levels). So the mistrust rests on *mistaken* stereotypes.
- **Observation 4 (no taste for discrimination):** In the dictator game average transfers were similar (5.6 Eastern vs 5.1 Ashkenazic); the distribution differs only marginally ($\chi^2$ LR, $p=0.022$). Since the dictator gap is small, taste cannot explain the large trust-game gap — discrimination there is belief/stereotype-driven.
- **Observation 5 (reversed stereotype):** In the ultimatum game Eastern men received *larger* offers (8.4 vs 5.9; LR test $p<.001$), consistent with a stereotype that Eastern men react more harshly to unfair offers. Rejection rates of 5-point offers did not differ by ethnicity (10% vs 8%) — again not statistical discrimination.
- **Observations 6–7 (gender):** No ethnic discrimination toward or by women. Ashkenazic women are trusted less than Ashkenazic men, but Eastern women are trusted *more* than Eastern men. Ethnic discrimination is essentially a male phenomenon.

## Contribution

The paper pioneers using the differential strategic structure of trust, dictator, and ultimatum games as an *identification device* to decompose discrimination into taste, (accurate) statistical, and (mistaken) stereotype-driven components — going beyond audit studies that detect discrimination but cannot diagnose its source. It documents internalized self-directed stereotyping and the male-specificity of ethnic discrimination, and argues experiments are a powerful educational tool for surfacing unrecognized discriminatory behavior.

## Limitations & open questions

- The Arab–Jewish and secular–Orthodox segmentations are explicitly reserved for future research (the companion secular/religious study finds a genuine taste for discrimination).
- Non-anonymous design (experimenter observes choices) departs from double-blind norms and could induce experimenter-demand effects.
- All partners are students; the authors caution that trust/mistrust patterns may depend on partners' education, so results may not generalize to the full population.
- The paper offers *no explanation* for Observation 2 (why Eastern men internalize anti-Eastern stereotypes), only a conjecture about a cycle of prejudice.
- Why discrimination is male-only is documented but not modeled.

## Connections

The method builds directly on the trust game of Berg, Dickhaut & McCabe (1995) and Arrow's (1972, 1973, 1998) distinction between taste-based and statistical discrimination, with Becker (1957) as the taste-for-discrimination benchmark. The ultimatum game traces to Güth, Schmittberger & Schwarze (1982), surveyed in Camerer & Thaler (1995) and Roth (1995). The group-bias check draws on social-identity work by Tajfel (1982) and Tajfel & Turner (1979). The likelihood-ratio approach to discrete behavioral modes echoes Stahl (1996) and Camerer & Ho (1999). The gender findings connect to Eckel & Grossman (1997, 1998), Andreoni & Vesterlund (2001), Croson & Buchan (1999), and Bolton & Katok (1995); the macro trust–growth link to Knack & Keefer (1997). The companion secular–religious experiment is Fershtman & Gneezy (2000).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper considers two distinct procedures to lexicographically compose multiple criteria for social or individual decision making. The first procedure composes M binary relations into one, and then selects its maximal elements. The second procedure first selects the set of maximal elements of the first binary relation, and then within that set, chooses the maximal elements of the second binary relation, and iterates the procedure until the Mth binary relation. We show several distinct sets of conditions for the choice functions representing these two procedures to satisfy non-emptiness and choice-consistency conditions such as contraction consistency and path independence. We also examine the relationships between the outcomes of the two procedures. Finally we investigate under what conditions the outcomes of each procedure are independent of the order of lexicographic application of the criteria.

## Summary
When a decision-maker (an individual, a family, or a society) holds several criteria that conflict â€” the canonical case being the efficiency-equity trade-off â€” a natural resolution is to apply them lexicographically in a priority order. Houy and Tadenuma show that there are two genuinely different ways to do this, and they behave very differently. Procedure $\alpha$ first builds one composite relation $P(R^1,\dots,R^M)$ and maximizes it; procedure $\beta$ maximizes criterion by criterion, narrowing the choice set at each step. The paper gives necessary-and-sufficient conditions for each to satisfy Non-Emptiness, Contraction Consistency, and Path Independence, characterizes exactly when the two coincide, and pins down when each is order-independent. The headline tension: $\beta$ almost always yields a choice but easily violates basic consistency, while $\alpha$ is consistent but frequently chooses nothing â€” a fundamental dilemma between non-emptiness and choice-consistency.

## Research question
Given $M$ criteria each represented by a binary relation on a set of alternatives, how should they be lexicographically aggregated for choice? Specifically, under what conditions do the two natural aggregation procedures ($\alpha$ and $\beta$) yield choice functions that are non-empty and choice-consistent (contraction-consistent, path-independent), when do their outcomes coincide, and when are the outcomes invariant to the order in which criteria are applied?

## Method / identification
Pure axiomatic choice theory on an abstract domain. Let $X$ be a set of alternatives with $|X|\geq 3$, $\mathcal{X}$ the finite subsets, and $R\subseteq X\times X$ a binary relation with strict part $P(R)$ and indifference $I(R)$. A choice function $C:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{X}$ has $C(S)\subseteq S$; $C_R(S)=\{x\in X\mid \forall y\in X,\,(y,x)\notin P(R)\}$ is maximization of $R$. Relations are classified as complete, transitive, quasi-transitive, asymmetric, or acyclic.

**Procedure $\alpha$** composes the relations into the lexicographic composition $P(R^1,\dots,R^M)$: $(x,y)$ belongs to it iff $x$ is $R^1$-superior, or $R^1$ is silent and $x$ is $R^2$-superior, and so on through $R^M$. The choice is then $C_{P(R^1,\dots,R^M)}$.

**Procedure $\beta$** is the iterated choice $C_{R^M}\cdots C_{R^1}$, defined recursively by $C^0(S)=S$, $C^m(S)=C_{R^m}(C^{m-1}(S))$.

The analysis leans on classical rationalizability results: $C_R$ is non-empty iff $P(R)$ is acyclic (Corollary 1, via Blair et al.); $C_R$ satisfies Non-Emptiness and Path Independence iff $R$ is quasi-transitive (Corollary 2, via Plott). A key technical device is the disjoint decomposition $P(R^1,\dots,R^M)=\Gamma^1\cup\cdots\cup\Gamma^M$, where $\Gamma^1=P(R^1)$ and $(x,y)\in\Gamma^m$ iff no earlier $R^{m'}$ ranks the pair and $(x,y)\in P(R^m)$.

## Data
None â€” this is a theoretical paper with no empirical or experimental data. Results are propositions with constructive proofs and counterexamples.

## Key findings
- **Proposition 3 (Non-Emptiness of $\alpha$):** $P(R^1,\dots,R^M)$ is acyclic iff every cycle of $P(R^1)\cup\cdots\cup P(R^M)$ contains a link that is "overruled" by a higher-priority criterion ranking the pair the other way.
- **Proposition 4 (Non-Emptiness of $\beta$, from Houy 2007):** $C_{R^M}\cdots C_{R^1}$ is non-empty iff every cycle of each $P(R^m)$ is broken by some higher-priority criterion $R^{m'}$, $m'<m$, ranking some pair in the cycle.
- **Proposition 5 (nesting):** $C_{R^1}\cap\cdots\cap C_{R^M}\subseteq C_{P(R^1,\dots,R^M)}\subseteq C_{R^M}\cdots C_{R^1}$. Hence (Corollary 3) non-emptiness of $\alpha$ implies non-emptiness of $\beta$, but not conversely (Example 1): $\alpha$ is the more demanding procedure.
- **Proposition 6 (Contraction Consistency of $\beta$):** under Non-Emptiness, $\beta$ is contraction-consistent iff a transitivity-like condition on the $\Gamma^m$ decomposition holds; and whenever $\beta$ is non-empty and contraction-consistent it actually equals $\alpha$. So $\beta$ satisfies even this minimal consistency only when it collapses onto $\alpha$ (Corollary 4; Example 2 shows $\alpha$ can be consistent while $\beta$ is not).
- **Proposition 7 (Path Independence):** the following are equivalent â€” $P(R^1,\dots,R^M)$ is quasi-transitive; $\alpha$ satisfies Non-Emptiness and Path Independence; $\beta$ satisfies Non-Emptiness and Path Independence. Under any of these, $\alpha=\beta$.
- **Order independence (Propositions 8â€“11, Corollaries 5â€“6):** $\alpha$ is order-independent for all permutations iff $P(R^1)\cup\cdots\cup P(R^M)$ is asymmetric (no two criteria ever conflict), in which case $\alpha=C_{R^1}\cap\cdots\cap C_{R^M}$; adding Non-Emptiness requires this union to be acyclic. Order independence of $\beta$ is strictly more demanding, requiring both acyclicity of the union and an extra transitivity-style condition (ii) (Proposition 11, from Houy 2007).

## Contribution
The paper unifies and generalizes scattered earlier treatments â€” Tadenuma's procedure-$\alpha$ work on efficiency-equity and egalitarian-equivalence, and the procedure-$\beta$ tradition of Suzumura, Aizerman, Manzini & Mariotti, and Houy â€” into a single framework for arbitrary $M$ criteria. It delivers the full comparative map: precise N&S conditions for non-emptiness, contraction consistency, path independence, and order independence of both procedures, and the exact conditions under which the deliberate composition ($\alpha$) and the intuitive sequential elimination ($\beta$) agree. The central conceptual payoff is the articulated "gap" between $\alpha$ and $\beta$, offered as a possible explanation of observed inconsistent social/individual choice and of disagreement among people who endorse the same criteria but apply them in different orders.

## Limitations & open questions
- The authors explicitly flag that asymmetry of $P(R^1)\cup\cdots\cup P(R^M)$ â€” required for order independence â€” is "very demanding in economic allocation problems," as it means no two criteria ever conflict, which the efficiency/equity case essentially never satisfies. This leaves open how to obtain order-robust choices in genuinely conflicting settings.
- They frame the non-emptiness vs. consistency tension as a "fundamental dilemma" but do not resolve it; finding intermediate procedures that compromise between $\alpha$ and $\beta$ is left open.
- The closing remark proposes, as future work, using these conditions to examine how multi-criteria choice procedures explain social or individual choice "in concrete problems" â€” i.e., applied and possibly empirical follow-up.
- The framework is non-stochastic and deterministic; behavioral/empirical operationalization (e.g., estimating which procedure people actually use) is not addressed.

## Connections
The procedure-$\beta$ line draws directly on [[@Manzini2007|Manzini & Mariotti (2007)]], whose "sequentially rationalizable choice" characterizes the two-criterion point-valued case via a weakening of contraction consistency â€” the alternative route the authors contrast with their own (they instead seek conditions for the unweakened property). It builds on Houy (2007) on rationality and order-dependent sequential rationality, which supplies Propositions 4 and 11. The choice-consistency apparatus is classical: Chernoff (1954) for contraction consistency, Arrow (1963) and Plott (1973) for path independence, Blair, Bordes, Kelly & Suzumura (1976) for rationalizability without collective rationality, and Sen (1970) and Suzumura (1983) for the broader rational-choice framework. The motivating equity criteria come from Foley (1967) and Kolm (1972) on no-envy and Pazner & Schmeidler (1978) on egalitarian-equivalence, with the Pareto-equity conflict due to Kolm (1972) and Feldman & Kirman (1974). The extended-sympathy tradition (Harsanyi 1955, Suppes 1966, Sen 1970, Hammond 1976, Arrow 1977) and Suzumura's (1981, 1983) and Tadenuma's (2002, 2005) work on lexicographic efficiency-equity composition form the immediate predecessors the paper generalizes.

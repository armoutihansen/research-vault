> [!abstract] Abstract
> Some recent studies have shown that machine learning can achieve higher predictive accuracy than logit models. However, existing studies rarely examine behavioral outputs (e.g., marginal effects and elasticities) that can be derived from machine-learning models and compare the results with those obtained from logit models. In other words, there has not been a comprehensive comparison between logit models and machine learning that covers both prediction and behavioral analysis, two equally important subjects in travel-behavior study. This paper addresses this gap by examining the key differences in model development, evaluation, and behavioral interpretation between logit and machine-learning models for mode-choice modeling. We empirically evaluate the two approaches using stated-preference survey data. Consistent with the literature, this paper finds that the best-performing machine-learning model, random forest, has significantly higher predictive accuracy than multinomial logit and mixed logit models. The random forest model and the two logit models largely agree on several aspects of the behavioral outputs, including variable importance and the direction of association between independent variables and mode choice. However, we find that the random forest model produces behaviorally unreasonable arc elasticities and marginal effects when these behavioral outputs are computed from a standard approach. After the introduction of some modifications that overcome the limitations of tree-based models, the results are improved to some extent. There appears to be a tradeoff between predictive accuracy and behavioral soundness when choosing between machine learning and logit models in mode-choice modeling.

## Summary

Zhao, Yan, Yu, and Van Hentenryck run a side-by-side horse race between the logit family (multinomial logit and mixed logit) and seven machine-learning classifiers on a stated-preference mode-choice dataset, and—crucially—extend the comparison beyond predictive accuracy to *behavioral outputs* (variable importance, direction of association, arc elasticities, marginal effects). Random forest predicts best, but the very flexibility that buys accuracy makes its elasticities and marginal effects behaviorally implausible under a naive computation. The paper documents a predictive-accuracy / behavioral-soundness tradeoff and proposes practical fixes for tree-based behavioral outputs.

## Research question

Does machine learning's well-documented predictive edge over logit models in travel-mode choice carry over to the *behavioral* quantities (elasticities, marginal effects, variable importance, direction of association) that travel-behavior analysts actually use for policy, or is there a tradeoff between prediction and behavioral interpretability?

## Method / identification

The paper is part conceptual taxonomy, part empirical bake-off. Conceptually, it contrasts the two families along model development, evaluation, and interpretation. Logit models rest on random utility maximization: mode $k$ has utility $U_k(X_k,\beta_k)=\beta_k^{T}X_k+\varepsilon_k$, and with i.i.d. Gumbel errors and fixed coefficients the MNL choice probability is

$$p_{ik}=\frac{\exp(\beta_k^{T}X_{ik})}{\sum_{p=1}^{K}\exp(\beta_p^{T}X_{ik})}$$

estimated by maximum likelihood. The mixed logit replaces fixed coefficients with individual-specific random coefficients (here on TT_PT, Parking_Cost, and the PT constant, all normal), giving a panel sequence probability $p_{ikj}=\int L_{ij}(\beta)g(\beta)\,d\beta$ with no closed form, estimated by simulated maximum likelihood (1,000 Halton draws, NLOGIT). Machine learning treats the same problem as classification $Y=f(Z,\theta)$, $Y\in\{1,\dots,K\}$, learning structure from data without an RUM assumption; the seven classifiers are Naive Bayes, CART, boosting (gbm), bagging, random forest, SVM (radial kernel), and a single-hidden-layer neural net (18 units). Hyperparameters tuned by grid search via `caret`.

Behavioral outputs from ML are obtained by sensitivity analysis. For feature $p$ of alternative $k$, the arc elasticity is

$$E_k(Z_p)=\frac{[Q_k(Z_{-p},Z_p\cdot(1+\delta))-Q_k(Z)]/Q_k(Z)}{\delta}$$

and the marginal effect is $M_k(Z_p)=Q_k(Z_{-p},Z_p+\delta)-Q_k(Z)$, with $Q_k$ the aggregate predicted share. The key methodological contribution is a *modified* approach for tree-based models: use a larger perturbation ($\delta=2$ min instead of 1 min, since small changes fail to cross split thresholds) and compute the metric on a subset that excludes "out-of-bound" instances (since trees cannot extrapolate beyond the observed range). Predictive accuracy is assessed by 10-fold cross-validation at the individual level and via an $L_1$-norm error for aggregate market-share recovery; direction of association is read off partial dependence plots, and variable importance from Gini index (RF) and weight-based methods (NN).

## Data

Stated-preference survey of University of Michigan (Ann Arbor) faculty, staff, and students. Respondents estimated trip attributes for walking, biking, driving, and bus, then chose among Car / Walk / Bike / PT in seven stated-choice scenarios featuring a hypothetical integrated mobility-on-demand transit system ("RITMO Transit", high-frequency buses plus on-demand shuttles). After cleaning, 8,141 observations from 1,163 individuals (panel structure). Features: mode travel times, PT wait time, parking cost, transfers, additional rideshare pickups, income, car-per-capita, gender, residential preferences (bike/walkability, PT access), and current/revealed mode. Variance inflation factors below 5 ruled out multicollinearity.

## Key findings

- **Prediction:** Random forest is the best individual-level classifier (mean accuracy 0.856) versus MNL 0.647 and mixed logit 0.631; most ML models beat both logit models. RF also has the lowest aggregate market-share $L_1$ error (0.0248 vs MNL 0.0399). Car (smallest market share, ~14.9%) is hardest to predict—a class-imbalance effect.
- **Mixed logit underperforms MNL out of sample** (0.631 vs 0.647) despite a much higher adjusted McFadden pseudo-$R^2$ (0.536 vs 0.365), interpreted as overfitting from the random parameters—consistent with Cherchi and Cirillo (2010).
- **Agreement:** RF, NN, and the logit models largely agree on variable importance rankings (travel-time variables dominate) and the *direction* of association; partial dependence plots from ML automatically reveal nonlinearities (e.g., flat PDP tails for very short/long TT_PT) that logit models need hand-curated interaction terms to capture.
- **Disagreement / the central result:** Under the standard computation, RF yields behaviorally unreasonable marginal effects and elasticities (e.g., a ~0.01% marginal effect for Wait_Time; implausibly small transfer/rideshare penalties). The proposed modified approach improves them substantially, but RF's value-of-time-style measures still diverge from the literature (transfer penalty usually >5 min of in-vehicle time). Hence a **tradeoff between predictive accuracy and behavioral soundness**.

## Contribution

One of the first studies to subject ML mode-choice models to a *behavioral* audit rather than a prediction-only comparison; to include the mixed logit (not just MNL) in such a comparison; to articulate the practical differences (input data structure, treatment of alternative-specific attributes, non-chosen-alternative handling, form of predicted output) between the two families; and to propose concrete fixes (larger perturbation, out-of-bound trimming) for computing tree-based elasticities and marginal effects. It reframes ML as an exploratory tool for discovering nonlinearities that can then inform better logit utility specifications.

## Limitations & open questions

- Behavioral analysis is "far from exhaustive"; value-of-time, willingness-to-pay, and welfare measures lack a behavioral foundation in ML and are not derived.
- The modified-approach perturbation ($\delta=2$ min) is chosen arbitrarily; how to set an appropriate perturbation per feature is open.
- It is unclear whether RF's divergent behavioral outputs reflect noise-fitting or are closer to ground truth (their TT_PT mixes in/out-of-vehicle time; the app-based MOD framing may genuinely lower transfer/wait penalties).
- Authors call for imposing **behavioral constraints on ML risk functions** to enforce sound outputs, and for identifying which ML models are best suited to behavioral analysis.
- Preference heterogeneity (the mixed-logit raison d'être) is not addressed by the ML models.
- **SP reporting bias:** its differential effect on logit vs ML is unknown, and ML analogues of joint RP–SP estimation (which corrects SP bias for logit) do not yet exist.
- Results rest on a single SP dataset; generalization to other datasets and feature sets is untested.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Current psychological theory and research affirm the positive affective and motivational consequences of having personal choice. These findings have led to the popular notion that the more choice, the better—that the human ability to manage, and the human desire for, choice is unlimited. Findings from 3 experimental studies starkly challenge this implicit assumption that having more choices is necessarily more intrinsically motivating than having fewer. These experiments, which were conducted in both field and laboratory settings, show that people are more likely to purchase gourmet jams or chocolates or to undertake optional class essay assignments when offered a limited array of 6 choices rather than a more extensive array of 24 or 30 choices. Moreover, participants actually reported greater subsequent satisfaction with their selections and wrote better essays when their original set of options had been limited. Implications for future research are discussed. (PsycInfo Database Record (c) 2020 APA, all rights reserved)

## Summary
This is the foundational "choice overload" paper. Across three experiments — two field studies and one lab study — Iyengar and Lepper show that offering people a *more extensive* set of options (24 or 30) rather than a *limited* set (6) increases initial attraction to the choice context but subsequently *reduces* the likelihood that people actually choose, lowers their satisfaction with what they chose, and even degrades the quality of work they produce. The result directly challenges both the social-psychological orthodoxy that choice always enhances intrinsic motivation and the economic intuition that more options weakly dominate fewer.

## Research question
Does the *number* of options offered have differential motivational consequences? Specifically, is a limited (psychologically manageable, ~6) choice set more intrinsically motivating than an extensive (psychologically excessive, 24-30) choice set — the "choice overload hypothesis" — even though extensive choice may seem initially more desirable?

## Method / identification
Three experiments, designed to rule out simple preference-matching and effortful information search (contexts chosen so that participants have no strong pre-existing preferences and "right/wrong" choices are subjective):

- **Study 1 (field, jam tasting).** At an upscale grocery store, a tasting booth displayed either 6 (limited) or 24 (extensive) flavors of Wilkin & Sons jam, rotated hourly and counterbalanced across two Saturdays. Common flavors (strawberry, raspberry) were stripped from the 28-variety brand to leave 24 exotic flavors; a preliminary survey of 30 undergraduates ensured the 6-flavor set was neither most nor least preferred. ~754 shoppers observed; outcomes were (i) initial attraction (stopping at booth) and (ii) subsequent purchase, tracked via coded $1-discount coupons. All buyers necessarily passed the full shelf regardless of condition.
- **Study 2 (field, essay, yoked design).** 197 intro social-psychology students were offered an extra-credit essay on "Twelve Angry Men" with either 6 or 30 topics. A yoked counterbalancing (five versions of the 6-topic sheet) ensured every extensive-choice topic also appeared in a limited-choice set. Outcomes: completion rate and blind-graded essay quality (content and form, two 10-point scales, two raters). Performance proxies intrinsic motivation since students were told essays would not be evaluated.
- **Study 3 (lab, chocolates, 3 conditions).** 134 Columbia students chose a Godiva chocolate from a display of 6 (limited) or 30 (extensive), plus a yoked **no-choice control** (sampled a chocolate chosen for them; half shown 6, half shown 30). Pre-screening removed chocolate-averse and Godiva-familiar participants. After choosing but *before* tasting, participants rated (on 1-7 Likert) enjoyment, difficulty, frustration, anticipated satisfaction, and satisficing vs. optimizing expectations; after tasting they rated satisfaction and regret. Choosing time was recorded, plus a manipulation check on perceived number of options. Final behavioral measure: choosing a box of Godiva (vs. $5 cash) as compensation, administered by a second blind experimenter. Sessions videotaped and rated for experimenter friendliness to rule out demand effects.

## Data
Original experimental data collected by the authors: ~754 observed shoppers (Study 1), 197 students / 193 analyzed (Study 2), and 134 lab participants (Study 3). No secondary or archival data.

## Key findings
- **Study 1.** Extensive choice was *more* initially attractive: 60% of passers-by stopped at the 24-jam booth vs. 40% at the 6-jam booth ($\chi^2(1)=19.89$, $p<.001$). But subsequent purchasing reversed sharply: ~30% of limited-choice consumers bought a jar vs. only 3% of extensive-choice consumers ($\chi^2(1)=32.34$, $p<.0001$). Number of jams sampled did not differ (~1.4-1.5).
- **Study 2.** 74% of limited-choice students completed the essay vs. 60% of extensive-choice ($\chi^2(1)=3.93$, $p<.05$). Limited-choice essays scored higher on content ($M=8.13$ vs. $7.79$), form ($M=8.04$ vs. $7.59$), and overall ($M=8.09$ vs. $7.69$, $F(1,124)=5.65$, $p<.02$). No difference in prior midterm performance by condition — so the effect is not selection on ability.
- **Study 3.** Manipulation check confirmed 30 chocolates felt "too many" ($M=4.88$) vs. "about right" for 6 ($M=3.61$). Extensive-choice participants found choosing simultaneously *more enjoyable* ($M=6.02$ vs. $4.72$) and *more difficult* ($M=4.45$ vs. $3.30$) and *more frustrating* ($M=3.10$ vs. $2.24$). Subsequent satisfaction was higher for limited choice ($M=6.28$ vs. $5.46$), and both choice groups beat the no-choice control ($M=4.92$). Behaviorally, 48% of limited-choice participants took chocolates as compensation vs. only 12% (extensive) and 10% (no-choice).
- **Mechanism nuance.** The hypothesized mediators were *not* supported: no condition differences in satisficing-vs-optimizing expectations, in feeling informed, or in default-choosing. So the demotivation is real and robust, but its psychological mechanism remained open.

## Contribution
First systematic experimental demonstration that more choice can be demotivating — coining the "choice overload" phenomenon. It unifies field external validity (real purchases, real coursework) with lab control (no-choice baseline, affect measures), and shows the effect on a *behavioral* outcome (purchase) rather than self-report alone. It is the canonical empirical anchor for behavioral-economics and marketing work on assortment size, and a counterexample to the revealed-preference axiom that adding options is weakly welfare-improving.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Mechanism unresolved.** Neither the satisficing/optimizing account nor the responsibility/regret account was empirically confirmed; the authors explicitly leave the mediating process open.
- **Boundary conditions.** The effect should attenuate or reverse in *preference-matching* contexts where choosers know what they want; the authors deliberately chose preference-uncertain domains, so the scope of choice overload is unspecified.
- **What counts as "too many"?** The threshold between manageable and excessive choice (somewhere between 6 and 24-30) is left for future calibration.
- **Generality.** Only consumer/educational hedonic goods studied; transferability to high-stakes decisions (careers, investments — invoked in the introduction) is conjectural.
- **Affect paradox.** Extensive choice is simultaneously more enjoyable and more frustrating; how these opposing affective signals net out, and over what time horizon, is unexplored.

## Connections
The paper builds on intrinsic-motivation theory in the self-determination tradition (Deci, 1975; Deci & Ryan, 1985) and the perceived-control literature (Langer & Rodin, 1976; Rotter, 1966). Its decision-theoretic backbone draws on bounded rationality and satisficing (Simon, 1955, 1956) and on accuracy–effort tradeoffs in choice (Payne, Bettman, & Johnson, 1988, 1993). The conflict-and-deferral results connect to [[@Shafir1993|Shafir, Simonson, and Tversky (1993)]], Shafir and Tversky (1992) on reason-based and deferred choice, and to Dhar (1997) on preference for the no-choice option. Information-overload framing relates to [[@Hauser1990|Hauser and Wernerfelt (1990)]]. It anticipates later "paradox of choice" treatments (Schwartz, 2004) and the large assortment-size literature in marketing. Within economics it is a stylized challenge to the menu-monotonicity / "more is better" presumption, relevant to models of choice from limited attention and to bounded-rationality choice procedures such as those in [[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti (2007)]] and rational-inattention accounts.

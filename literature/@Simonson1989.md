> [!abstract] Abstract
> Building on previous research, this article proposes that choice behavior under preference uncertainty may be easier to explain by assuming that consumers select the alternative supported by the best reasons. This approach provides an explanation for the so-called attraction effect and leads to the prediction of a compromise effect. Consistent with the hypotheses, the results indicate that (1) brands tend to gain share when they become compromise alternatives in a choice set; (2) attraction and compromise effects tend to be stronger among subjects who expect to justify their decisions to others; and (3) selections of dominating and compromise brands are associated with more elaborate and difficult decisions.

## Summary

Simonson reframes context-dependent choice as **choice based on reasons**: when consumers cannot pin down the utilities of alternatives (preference uncertainty), they pick the option supported by the *best justifications*, both to themselves and to anticipated evaluators. This lens explains the known **attraction (asymmetric dominance) effect** post hoc and, more impressively, predicts a novel **compromise effect** — a brand gains share simply by becoming the *middle* option in a set, with no dominance relationship involved. Across a pilot and three studies (choice tasks, a justifiability-rating study, and a think-aloud protocol study), both effects emerge robustly, strengthen when subjects expect to justify their choices to others, and are tied to more elaborate, difficult decisions. This is the founding paper of the compromise effect and a cornerstone of the behavioral literature on menu/context effects.

## Research question

Can individual choice under preference uncertainty be better understood as the selection of the alternative supported by the *best reasons* (rather than the highest expected utility)? Concretely: (i) does the reasons-based view account for the attraction effect; (ii) does it predict a new compromise effect; (iii) are both effects amplified when decision makers must justify choices to others; and (iv) are they associated with more elaborate/difficult decision processes?

## Method / identification

This is a behavioral / experimental paper, not a formal axiomatic one. The central theoretical claim is that relations *among* alternatives in a set (asymmetric dominance, compromise position) supply reasons that influence choice, even though most random-utility models (Luce, Luce & Suppes) treat such relations as irrelevant and impose **regularity** (adding an item cannot raise another item's choice probability) and **IIA**. The attraction effect violates regularity; the predicted compromise effect violates IIA in the *opposite* direction from the classic similarity/substitution effect.

Design (four pieces):
- **Pilot** (147 marketing students): preliminary test of the compromise effect and of a high-vs-low *justification* manipulation (choices evaluated/justified in class vs. confidential).
- **Study 1** (372 students, 3 universities): 12 choice sets across product categories (beer, car, color TV, apartment, calculator, mouthwash, calculator battery). Each alternative described on two attributes with the market-wide attribute range given. Three between-subject questionnaire versions rotate which alternative is dominating/compromise/extreme. Some sets use an *unavailable* described alternative to set compromise status while avoiding substitution-effect confounds. Importance weights elicited beforehand to net out subjects' own tastes. Estimation by **multinomial / conditional logit** (McFadden 1973) with a standardized weighted-additive utility $U$, a dominance dummy $\mathrm{DOM}$, a compromise dummy $\mathrm{COMP}$, and their interactions with the high-justification condition ($\mathrm{HDOM}$, $\mathrm{HCOMP}$). Including a set-dependent term operationalizes the deviation from random-utility theory.
- **Study 2** (100 students): direct test of the *mechanism* — subjects predict how others would evaluate each choice, rating likelihood of criticism and difficulty of justification; net effect of dominance/compromise isolated by re-rating alternatives in reduced sets with the relationship removed (regression on $\mathrm{DOM}$/$\mathrm{COMP}$ plus an attribute-based evaluation control $\mathrm{ATE}$).
- **Study 3** (23 graduate students): **think-aloud protocols** (Ericsson & Simon), double-coded (interjudge reliability 90%), measuring protocol length, mention of difficulty, consideration of both pros and cons, and consistency with prior attribute weights; choices classified as lexicographic vs. non-lexicographic.

## Data

Original experimental data: choice and rating responses from ~642 subjects total (147 + 372 + 100 + 23) — undergraduate and graduate business/psychology students at Southeastern U.S. universities — on hypothetical two-attribute product choice sets. No field/market data; stimuli are constructed sets with controlled dominance and compromise geometry.

## Key findings

- **Attraction effect replicated (H1a):** brands gain share when they asymmetrically dominate another option. Logit estimate $0.82\,U + 1.17\,\mathrm{DOM} + 0.44\,\mathrm{HDOM}$ — $\mathrm{DOM}$ strongly positive ($t=9.0$, $p<0.001$).
- **Justification amplifies attraction (H1b):** $\mathrm{HDOM}$ positive and significant ($t=2.3$, $p<0.05$); the dominating-brand share boost was ~17% larger in the high-justification condition.
- **Compromise effect (H2a) — the paper's headline contribution:** an alternative's share rises when it becomes the middle option. Logit estimate $1.40\,U + 0.63\,\mathrm{COMP} + 0.05\,\mathrm{HCOMP}$ — $\mathrm{COMP}$ positive ($t=7.9$, $p<0.001$); shares ~17.5% larger when an item is a compromise, significant in 9 of 11 cases. This runs *counter* to the similarity effect: the new entrant draws share from the more *distant* existing brand, not the adjacent one.
- **Justification and compromise (H2b):** predicted direction but *not* significant in Study 1 (significant only in the pilot and in the experimenter's home university) — a notable mixed result.
- **Mechanism (H1c, H2c):** dominating alternatives are seen as both easier to justify *and* less likely to be criticized; compromise alternatives are seen as less likely to be criticized but **not** easier to justify — justifiability and criticism-avoidance are distinct components. 65 of 100 subjects would criticize someone who *avoids* middle options.
- **Process (H1d, H2d):** non-lexicographic choices of dominating brands and choices of compromise brands have longer protocols, more explicit mention of difficulty, and more two-sided (pro/con) deliberation. Subjects explicitly verbalize the compromise rationale ("because it is a compromise," "I'll take the middle one").

## Contribution

(1) Introduces and empirically establishes the **compromise effect**, now one of the canonical context effects in choice. (2) Provides a unifying *reasons/justification* account that explains both attraction and compromise as consequences of seeking defensible choices under preference uncertainty. (3) Links context effects to **accountability**: anticipated evaluation by others reshapes which reasons carry weight, connecting consumer choice to social-psychological work on justification (Tetlock). (4) Demonstrates, via logit set-dependence terms and protocols, concrete violations of regularity and IIA, constraining the class of admissible choice models and motivating later context-dependent and reference-dependent models.

## Limitations & open questions

The paper states two explicit limitations, each a project-idea hook:
- **Scope of the reasons approach.** Only two relations (dominance, compromise) are studied. Much more work is needed to assess the advantages, limits, and implications of the reasons/justification view for predicting choice — and, critically, to identify *which reasons appear most compelling in which situations* (the paper concedes there is "only limited knowledge" of what makes an argument compelling a priori, so predictions remain partly post hoc).
- **Task realism.** Sets were limited to 2-3 alternatives on 2 attributes with verbal/numerical stimuli. Greater complexity and realism would make set relations less transparent and add choice error; future work should test whether attraction and compromise effects (and the reasons explanation) survive in **natural consumer environments**.

Additional open threads raised in discussion: reconciling the *weak/inconsistent* justification-by-compromise interaction (H2b); disentangling the reasons account from rival **salience** and **effort-accuracy/cost-benefit** explanations (the author argues against both but does not decisively rule them out); and the unresolved real-world existence debate around the attraction effect (Ratneshwar et al.).

## Connections

The intellectual lineage runs through **Montgomery (1983)**, **Slovic (1975)**, and **Slovic, Fischhoff & Lichtenstein** on justification-driven choice, and **Tversky (1972)** on elimination-by-aspects. The attraction effect originates with **Huber, Payne & Puto (1982)** and **Huber & Puto (1983)**; **Ratneshwar, Shocker & Stewart (1987)** challenged its robustness. The justification/accountability machinery draws on **Tetlock (1985)** ("preemptive self-criticism") and self-presentation work (Schlenker, Baumeister). The compromise effect is connected to **prospect theory** (Kahneman & Tversky 1979): switching from a middle reference point to an extreme incurs a salient loss. Downstream, this paper seeds the large literature on context/menu effects, reference-dependence, and rationalization-based choice models, and is a natural companion to work on social preferences and choice consistency where set-dependent justification matters.

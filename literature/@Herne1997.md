> [!abstract] Abstract
> A decoy is an option which causes preference reversals between two other options in a choice set. Introducing a decoy increases the popularity of a target option and decreases the popularity of a competitor. This phenomenon contradicts standard economic models of individual choice which assume preferences to be independent of irrelevant alternatives. Decoy effects include a set of phenomena in which particular types of decoys have been observed to influence choice. This paper focuses on two effects: the asymmetric domination and compromise effect. Earlier studies on decoy effects have concentrated on consumer choice. The aim of this paper is to investigate the possibility of observing the same effects in a political context. This question is studied by presenting choice problems over various policy issues to a group of respondents. The results show that the outcomes of a political decision making process can be affected by alternatives which should be irrelevant according to the standard approach.

## Summary
Herne ports two well-documented context effects from consumer-choice research — the *asymmetric domination effect* (ADE) and the *compromise effect* (CE) — into the domain of political/policy choice. Using a between-subjects survey of 219 Finnish students who faced hypothetical two-attribute policy options (economic unions, taxation, social benefits, environmental programs), she shows that adding a strategically placed but normatively "irrelevant" decoy systematically shifts choice shares toward a target option, violating the regularity and proportionality principles implied by the standard (IIA-based) model of individual choice. The upshot: political and opinion-poll outcomes can be manipulated by introducing options that should not matter.

## Research question
Do people choose in accordance with the standard model of individual choice when the alternatives are political/policy proposals rather than consumer products? Specifically, can adding an asymmetrically dominated decoy, or a decoy that makes a target a compromise, increase the target's (absolute and/or relative) choice share in a political-choice context — as it does in consumer choice?

## Method / identification
The conceptual backbone is the axiomatic apparatus of standard choice theory and its testable aggregate implications. Independence of irrelevant alternatives (IIA): for choice sets $R=\{x,y\}$ and $S=\{x,y,z\}$, if $C(R)=x$ then it cannot be that $C(S)=y$. At the aggregate level IIA implies **regularity**: with $P(x,R)$ the proportion choosing $x$ from $R$,
$$P(x,R)\ge P(x,S),\qquad x\in R\subset S,$$
i.e. enlarging the choice set cannot raise an option's share. A stronger candidate, **proportionality** (a new option draws from incumbents in proportion to their shares), is shown to be too strong — Herne gives a spatial-voting counterexample (parties $l\succ c\succ r$ for two voters, $r\succ c\succ l$ for three) where proportionality fails even though individual IIA holds, because the centre party $c$ loses votes to $r$ while $l$ does not.

The operative aggregate test is therefore Tversky and Simonson's (1993) **betweenness inequality**. With alternatives valued monotonically on each dimension $i$, $y$ lies between $x$ and $z$ if for every dimension either $x_i\le y_i\le z_i$ or $x_i\ge y_i\ge z_i$. Then if $y$ is between $x$ and $z$,
$$P(y,R)>\frac{P(y,S)}{P(y,S)+P(x,S)}$$
unless IIA or a **ranking condition** is violated, where the ranking condition requires $P(z\succ y\succ x)/P(z\succ x\succ y)>P(y,S)/P(x,S)$ — i.e. preference orders with the middle option between the extremes are relatively more common. A violation of betweenness inequality (an extreme option losing more share to another extreme than the middle option does) is read as evidence against IIA, conditional on the ranking condition's plausibility.

The two decoy geometries (Fig. 1, two-dimensional attribute space with a Target $T$ and Competitor $C$ neither dominating the other): an **asymmetrically dominated decoy** $D_1$ is dominated by $T$ on both dimensions but not by $C$; ADE predicts adding $D_1$ raises $T$'s share. A **compromise decoy** $D_3$ is placed so that $T$ becomes the middle (compromise) option between $C$ and $D$ on both dimensions (CE).

Empirical design: a between-subjects study (not a fully controlled lab experiment — information is uncontrolled since respondents may weigh unmentioned dimensions, and no monetary incentives are given, justified because induced preferences would defeat the hypothesis that preferences are unstable). Hypotheses: $H_0$ — the target's choice share (absolute or relative) is not increased by adding a decoy; $H_1$ — the target's share (absolute and/or relative) is larger with a dominating/compromise decoy than with the core alternatives alone. Subjects were randomly assigned (within each session) to a two-alternative core group $\{T,C\}$ or a three-alternative decoy group $\{T,C,D\}$. Significance assessed via $\chi^2$ tests on choice shares.

## Data
219 respondents in three sessions: University of Turku ($n=113$) and University of Tampere ($n=62$) in fall 1995, and the Turku School of Economics and Business Administration ($n=44$) in early 1996; mostly introductory political-science students (plus a methodology course), ages 18–40, 67% female. Each completed a booklet of 12 hypothetical policy choice tasks (~45 minutes); six tasks tested ADE and the rest CE. Options were defined on two familiar, meaningful attributes (e.g. inflation, unemployment, tax rates, pollution reduction, child benefits, in Finnmark units), avoiding ambiguous artificial scales; task order and within-task option order were randomized. No real payoffs (hypothetical choice).

## Key findings
- **ADE confirmed (regularity violated).** Pooling the six ADE tasks, $T$'s share *relative* to $C$ rose from 53% to 63% when the dominated decoy $D$ was added ($\chi^2=13.3$, $p<0.01$); the *absolute* share also rose, 53% to 59% ($\chi^2=4.9$, $p<0.05$). Dominated decoys were nonetheless chosen 6% of the time — attributed to subjects reading in unmentioned dimensions, so the "dominated" option was not perceived as dominated.
- **CE confirmed via relative shares (betweenness inequality violated).** When $D$ made $T$ a compromise, regularity was on average *not* violated — $T$ actually *lost* share in absolute terms (64% to 53% when decoy choices are counted with $C$). But relative to $C$ alone, $T$'s share rose from 64% to 75% ($\chi^2=14.4$, $p<0.01$), an ~11-point gain that violates the betweenness inequality condition. (Unlike dominated decoys, compromise decoys attract substantial direct support, so absolute regularity need not break.)
- **Real-world illustration.** A content analysis of 143 arguments (88 against, 55 for) in Finland's September 1993 parliamentary nuclear-power vote shows pro-nuclear advocates used *coal* as a dominated decoy: nuclear power dominates coal on environment while matching it on reliability, making nuclear look more attractive relative to the decentralized (alternative-energy) model. This motivates the lab study and links to the "phantom alternative" idea (a described-but-unavailable decoy, e.g. a "was/now" sale price).

## Contribution
First systematic demonstration that ADE and CE — established in consumer choice and gambles — operate on **policy choices** framed as political decisions (voter, MP, council member). It connects the behavioral context-dependence literature to formal social-choice theory: because decoys move individual choices, they move aggregate/social-choice outcomes and can be deployed strategically. Practically, it identifies decoy insertion as a manipulation channel for both voting agendas and opinion polls, and supplies a concrete real-politics case (the nuclear-power debate) of a decoy arising endogenously in elite argumentation.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Incentives unresolved.** No monetary stakes; the author flags the long-standing economist-vs-psychologist dispute over whether real payoffs would dampen the effect, noting prior decoy results survive real incentives (Simonson and Tversky 1992; Herne 1996a) but conceding payoffs are infeasible here.
- **Imperfect information control.** Respondents could import dimensions beyond the two stated attributes — directly evidenced by the 6% choice of "dominated" decoys — so "irrelevance" is only nominal; the *complexity of political issues* may itself drive part of the effect.
- **Hypothetical, non-strategic setting.** Choices are hypothetical and respondents had no information about others' preferences or the decision rule, so strategic voting was assumed absent; interaction of decoy effects with strategic behavior and agenda control is left open.
- **Sample.** Young, mostly-female student convenience sample; external validity to actual electorates and elite decision-makers is untested.
- **Mechanism unsettled.** The paper rehearses but does not adjudicate the two explanatory families — perceptual (decoys stretch/compress attribute dimensions, altering perceived distance) versus heuristic (rules of thumb like "pick the compromise") — leaving the cognitive process underlying the effects open.
- **Normative status.** Whether such "irrational" behavior warrants replacing the standard model is explicitly left unresolved; standard theory may remain the best available approximation while decoys still matter at the margin.

## Connections
The formal core is **Tversky and Simonson (1993)**, "Context-dependent preferences" (betweenness inequality, ranking condition) and the broader tradeoff-contrast / extremeness-aversion account of **Simonson and Tversky (1992)** and **Simonson (1989)** (choice based on reasons; the compromise effect). The ADE originates with **Huber, Payne and Puto (1982)** and **Huber and Puto (1983)** (asymmetric dominance, regularity violations, the similarity hypothesis). Gamble-based replications come from **Wedell (1991)** and **Herne (1996a)**; the phantom-alternative concept from **Farquhar and Pratkanis (1987)**. On the political side it builds on prior decoy demonstrations in candidate choice (**Pan, O'Curry and Pitts 1995**; **Highhouse 1996** for job candidates) and the rational-vs-psychological framing of political choice in **Quattrone and Tversky (1988)**. It situates itself against the rational-choice tradition in social choice — Condorcet, Black (1958), Plott (1967), McKelvey (1976), Schofield (1980), Downs (1957), and the strategic-voting/agenda results of Farquharson (1969), Gibbard (1973), Satterthwaite (1975), Plott and Levine (1978) — and the experimental-economics critique reviewed by **Camerer (1995)**, with the incentive debate framed via **Smith (1991)**.

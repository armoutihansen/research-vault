> [!abstract] Abstract
> Several decision models in marketing science and psychology assume that a consumer chooses by proceeding sequentially through a checklist of desirable properties. These models are contrasted to the utility maximization model of rationality in economics. We show on the contrary that the two approaches are nearly equivalent. Since the number of preference discriminations that an agent can make increases exponentially in the number of properties used, checklists provide a rapid procedural basis for utility maximization.

## Summary
Choosing by **checklist** — running through an ordered list of properties (subsets of alternatives) and, at each step, discarding every surviving alternative that lacks the current property *unless none has it* — looks like a crude, lexicographic, "non-compensatory" heuristic far removed from utility maximization. The paper shows the opposite: the benchmark (finitely terminating) checklist model is *nearly equivalent* to utility maximization, and because a length-$k$ checklist can sort the universe into up to $2^k$ indifference classes, checklists are a **fast and frugal way to maximize utility**. Against Simon, a utility function *aids* rather than burdens procedural decision-making.

## Research question
Is the "procedural rationality" of sequential property-elimination (Tversky's elimination-by-aspects, Gigerenzer's fast-and-frugal heuristics) genuinely distinct from — or even opposed to — the "substantive rationality" of utility/preference maximization? Specifically: which preference relations and which utility-maximizing choice functions can be generated by a checklist, how *quickly* (how many properties per discrimination) can a checklist run, and can the uncountably-many-indifference-class consumers of textbook theory choose by checklist at all?

## Method / identification
The primitives are a set of alternatives $X$, a domain $\mathcal{A}$ of choice sets, and a choice function $c$ with $\emptyset\neq c(A)\subseteq A$. A **property** is any $P(i)\subseteq X$; a **checklist** is a sequence $P=(P(i))_{i\in I}$ with index set $I=\{1,\dots,n\}$ or $\mathbb{N}$. Choice is defined by inductively shrinking **survivor sets**:
$$S_0(A)=A,\qquad S_i(A)=\begin{cases}S_{i-1}(A)\cap P(i)&\text{if }S_{i-1}(A)\cap P(i)\neq\emptyset,\\ S_{i-1}(A)&\text{otherwise.}\end{cases}$$
$P$ is a checklist for $c$ (Definition 1) if for every $A$ there is a stage $j$ after which survivors stop shrinking and $c(A)=S_j(A)$ — the **finite termination** requirement that makes the procedure practical. The empty-intersection rule guarantees a checklist never wipes out all of $A$.

The central device is an embedding of $X$ into a lexicographically ordered binary cube: identify each $x$ with $p_x\in\{0,1\}^I$ where $p_x(i)=1\iff x\in P(i)$, and order $\{0,1\}^I$ lexicographically. The induced relation $x\succeq y\iff p_x\succeq_L p_y$ is shown to be exactly the preference $c$ maximizes, and (for standard checklists) $u(x)=\sum_{i\in I}p_x(i)/3^{i}$ is a real-valued utility representation (base 3 sidesteps the dual-binary-expansion exceptions). Three model variants are studied: **standard** checklists ($I\leqslant\omega$), **extended** checklists (any well-ordered $I$, via transfinite induction with $S_i(A)=\bigcap_{k<i}S_k(A)\cap P(i)$ when nonempty), and **multivalued** checklists (each property replaced by a complete-transitive relation $R_i$, lexicographically aggregated). **Canonical checklists** (Proposition 1) show any choice function with a checklist also has one built solely from *upper-contour sets* $\{y:y\succeq x\}$, the workhorse for the characterization proofs. No data or estimation — this is pure decision theory.

## Data
None — the paper is purely theoretical/axiomatic. Illustrations use a stylized online car search and bundles of characteristics (Lancaster-style), e.g. **coordinate cutoffs** $\{(x_1,x_2):x_j\geqslant r\}$ and a **calorie cutoff** $\{(x_1,x_2):k_1x_1+k_2x_2\leqslant 800\}$.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1**: If a choice function has a (standard) checklist then it maximizes a *utility function*. Consequence: a checklist user can **never** behave like an agent with lexicographic preferences on $\mathbb{R}^n_+$ (which have no utility representation), even with infinitely many coordinate cutoffs. Apparent non-compensatory choosers are in fact classical maximizers.
- **Theorem 2**: A choice function has an *extended* checklist **iff** it maximizes a preference relation. Imposing no domain restriction, this is equivalent to Richter's strong-axiom characterization of preference maximization. The lexicographic-on-$\mathbb{R}^n_+$ cases are exactly those whose only checklists are extended (non-finitely-terminating, hence procedurally unwieldy) — Example 3 builds one from a dense set of $x_1$-cutoffs followed by a dense set of $x_2$-cutoffs.
- **Theorem 3** (the title result): A checklist can make $n$ discriminations with $k$ properties where $k$ is the least integer with $2^k\geqslant n$, and **no** checklist making $n$ discriminations can use fewer than $k$. So discriminative power is *exponential* in length: $2^{20}\geqslant 1{,}000{,}000$ gives "a million answers to twenty questions." Crucially a checklist performs the binary search **nonrecursively** — each property's content is fixed, independent of earlier eliminations, yet $P(i)$ effectively encodes $2^{i-1}$ conditional questions. With each $x$ equally likely to be the maximum, the optimal checklist matches the optimal yes/no question tree at $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ expected questions.
- **Theorem 4**: $c$ has a checklist **iff** it maximizes some utility $u$ such that $(\mathcal{A},\succeq)$ satisfies a **domain restriction** — the $\succeq$-maximal elements of "$\succeq$-top-dense" choice sets intersect at most countably many indifference classes (automatic for finite-set domains or countably many indifference classes). Thus even continuum-many-indifference-class consumers of textbook theory *can* choose by a finitely-terminating ("quick") checklist, e.g. via satisficing-threshold properties $P_r=\{x:u(x)\geqslant r\}$ over rational $r$. Example 4 (uniform on $[0,1]$ over closed sets) shows some violation of the restriction blocks any checklist.
- **Theorem 5**: A choice function maximizes a utility function **iff** it has an *approximate* checklist — one where $c(A)=\bigcap_{i\in I}S_i(A)$ (survivors converge set-theoretically to the choice, exact only in the limit). This removes the domain restriction and gives clean equivalence on arbitrary domains.
- **Section 8 / Theorem 2 extended**: multivalued checklists also characterize preference maximization. This recovers and refines **Chipman's** classical *lexicographic (real-valued) utility* theory; the two-valued (binary) restriction exposes structure invisible there — e.g. the exponential discrimination bound of Theorem 3 has no analogue when one $R_i$ can already make infinitely many distinctions. There is a trade-off between the number of indifference classes $n$ per property and the length $I$ needed to represent a given $\succeq$.

## Contribution
Bridges procedural/bounded-rationality decision models from psychology and marketing (elimination-by-aspects, fast-and-frugal heuristics, non-compensatory choice) with classical revealed-preference/utility theory, showing them to be near-equivalent rather than opposed. It reframes the Simon critique: checklists furnish a *tractable computational foundation* for utility maximization, and the exponential discrimination result (Theorem 3) gives a positive efficiency story. It also unifies the framework with Chipman's lexicographic-utility representation theory and introduces the standard/extended/approximate/multivalued checklist taxonomy.

## Limitations & open questions
- **No exact two-sided equivalence with a single notion**: standard checklists need a domain restriction (Theorem 4) and approximate ones are needed for clean equivalence (Theorem 5); the authors flag the practical termination problem when $I=\mathbb{N}$ — the agent may not *know* eliminations have ceased unless survivors form a singleton or one indifference class.
- For dense $\{x:x_j\geqslant r\}$ coordinate-cutoff checklists the authors **do not have an exact characterization** of the (strictly monotone, discontinuous) preferences generated — an explicit open problem. They also note coordinate cutoffs *rule out continuous preferences* however many are added.
- The model **cannot be used to compare decision-making speed** of preference-maximizers against other agent types, precisely because every checklist yields preference maximization; the authors defer this to Mandler (companion work, ref. [18]).
- **Other aggregation rules** (e.g. choose alternatives satisfying the most properties, or a weighted count) are only sketched as also generating rational behavior — a general theory of property-aggregation procedures is left open.
- Whether *natural* properties exist is context-dependent (summer/winter fruits vs. a disparate retirement-spending menu); the availability of natural properties, not just their logical existence, determines whether substantively rational behavior actually materializes — a behavioral question left unformalized.

## Connections
The procedural targets are Tversky's **elimination-by-aspects** (of which the deterministic checklist is a simplification) and the "fast and frugal"/non-compensatory heuristics of **Gigerenzer & Goldstein**, set against **Simon's** procedural-vs-substantive rationality. The representation results connect to **Chipman's** lexicographic utility and the ordinal-number constructions of Cuesta Dutari and Sierpinski; the characterization side relates to **Richter's** revealed-preference theorem and the strong axiom. Theorem 3's binary-search/twenty-questions framing links to **Huffman coding** and classic algorithmics. Within the bounded-rationality literature it is a companion to Manzini & Mariotti's work on shortlisting and consideration sets (see [[@Manzini2014|Manzini & Mariotti (2014)]] in this vault), and the alternative's representation as a bundle of attributes follows **Lancaster** characteristics theory.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Behavioral decision theories aim to explain human behavior. Can they help predict it? An open tournament for prediction of human choices in fundamental economic decision tasks is presented. The results suggest that integration of certain behavioral theories as features in machine learning systems provides the best predictions. Surprisingly, the most useful theories for prediction build on basic properties of human and animal learning and are very different from mainstream decision theories that focus on deviations from rational choice. Moreover, we find that theoretical features should be based not only on qualitative behavioral insights (e.g. loss aversion), but also on quantitative behavioral foresights generated by functional descriptive models (e.g. Prospect Theory). Our analysis prescribes a recipe for derivation of explainable, useful predictions of human decisions.

## Summary

Using the CPC18 choice-prediction tournament, the authors show that the best out-of-sample predictions of aggregate human choice between lotteries come not from pure machine learning nor from pure behavioral theory, but from hybrid models that feed a tree-based learner two distinct kinds of theory-derived features: *behavioral insights* (qualitative tendencies, e.g. loss aversion) and *behavioral foresights* (the quantitative point predictions of a fitted descriptive model). Both feature types are necessary; dropping either degrades accuracy. Strikingly, the foresight that works best (BEAST) builds on basic human/animal learning processes rather than on the classical "deviations-from-rationality" tradition (Expected Utility, (Cumulative) Prospect Theory). The paper distills a portable recipe for predicting human decisions under limited data.

## Research question

Can behavioral decision theories — built to *explain* behavior — actually improve *prediction* of human choice, and if so, in what form (qualitative insights vs. quantitative foresights) should theory be injected into a machine-learning predictor when training data are limited?

## Method / identification

The empirical engine is **CPC18**, an open choice-prediction competition. The prediction target (Track 1) is, for each of 60 held-out binary lottery problems, the aggregate choice rate of Option B in each of five time-blocks (5 trials each), i.e. 300 numbers ($60 \times 5$). Models receive only the 14 problem-defining parameters as input and must output five choice rates in $[0,1]$ per problem; submissions were blind to the test problems until after the deadline. Ranking is by mean squared error
$$\mathrm{MSE}=\frac{1}{300}\sum_{i}\left(\hat{y}_i-y_i\right)^2,$$
transformed for interpretability into the **equivalent number of observations (ENO)** — the number of agents one would have to sample before their empirical mean beats the model's prediction for a new agent. Bootstrap (2501 resamples of the 60 test problems) yields 95% CIs on MSE differences vs. the winner.

The conceptual contribution is the **insight/foresight decomposition recipe**: (1) start with a good quantitative descriptive model (a foresight); (2) decompose it into its decision-theoretic mechanisms (insights); (3) supply both the model's point predictions *and* the hand-crafted insight features to a learner (Random Forest / XGBoost), letting the data recover their best integration. Two baselines anchor the competition: **BEAST.sd**, a purely behavioral model (no statistical learning), and **Psychological Forest**, a Random Forest over 31 features (14 problem parameters + 16 insight features + BEAST's prediction as a foresight). BEAST itself evaluates each prospect as expected value + size-dependent estimation noise + the mean of a small mental sample drawn via four sampling tools (unbiased, equal-weighting, sign, contingent pessimism), capturing sensitivity to expected value and to the probability of experienced regret.

Post-competition ablations retrain RF and XGBoost dropping either feature type. A separate exercise restricts to the no-feedback, no-ambiguity subset (decisions under risk) and pits BEAST against Stochastic/Deterministic CPT, the Priority Heuristic, and Decision by Sampling, each used both on its own and as a foresight feature.

## Data

694,500 decisions by 926 participants across 270 binary choice problems from a 14-dimensional problem space (drawn at the Technion and Hebrew University; ~45-min sessions, incentivized). Each subject faced one cohort of 30 problems, 25 trials each (first 5 trials no feedback, trials 6-25 full feedback on obtained and forgone payoffs). 150 problems came from CPC15; Experiment 1 added 60 (public training), Experiment 2 added 60 (held-out test). The competition drew 20 valid Track-1 submissions from 46 registered teams. Raw data are public (Zenodo, doi:10.5281/zenodo.2571510).

## Key findings

- The winning model is an **XGBoost** over Psychological Forest's feature set (insights + BEAST foresight), achieving $\mathrm{ENO}=23.7$.
- **Both feature types are essential**: every hybrid submission used both, and ablating either insights or the foresight materially worsens both winner and baseline (Figure 1).
- **Theory is near-indispensable**: the only submission using no behavioral theory ranked 16th ($\mathrm{ENO}=15.2$), significantly worse than the winner (bootstrap CI on squared-error difference $[0.044, 0.483]$). The best pure-behavioral model ranked 2nd ($\mathrm{ENO}=22.7$), *not* significantly worse than the winner ($[-0.135, 0.175]$).
- **BEAST dominates classical models even on their home turf**: on the decisions-under-risk subset, BEAST-as-foresight gives $\mathrm{ENO}=16.75$ vs. $7.57$ for the next best (Stochastic CPT) — less than half the accuracy (Table 1). Using any model's predictions as a foresight feature beats using them raw.
- **What matters is resolution, not low error per se**: a foresight helps a tree learner if it discriminates well across inputs ($\hat{y}_i$ and $1-\hat{y}_i$ are equally useful).
- **Interpretability is retained**: the winner's predictions correlate 0.963 with BEAST, so the hybrid's behavior is explainable via BEAST's mechanisms.
- Robustness: the recipe replicates on two extensive-form game datasets, in one case setting a new state of the art.

## Contribution

Provides a reproducible, generalizable recipe for theory-augmented prediction of human choice under small data, and empirically establishes that (i) successful quantitative descriptive models make powerful features beyond their constituent qualitative insights, and (ii) models grounded in basic learning processes (BEAST) out-predict the dominant rationality-deviation tradition, even where the latter was specifically developed.

## Limitations & open questions

- The authors argue the assumption underlying classical decision research — that agents *understand and believe* described payoff distributions — should be reconsidered, since models relaxing it (BEAST) predict better even without feedback.
- They call for behavioral scientists to shift from isolating single mechanisms toward modeling **interactions and relative importance** of mechanisms, and to study situations that are *robust/common* rather than the *uncommon* situations that interest explanation-focused research.
- They flag that translating behavioral theory into ML features is hard ("easier said than done") and requires nuanced understanding; the best path forward is **behavioral-scientist/data-scientist collaboration**.
- Open ML challenges arise from the noisy, complex nature of human behavior; the paper focuses on aggregate Track-1 prediction and treats individual-level prediction (Track 2) only briefly.

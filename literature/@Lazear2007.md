> [!abstract] Abstract
> In this review of the personnel economics literature, we introduce key topics of personnel economics, focus on some relatively new findings that have emerged since prior reviews of some or all of the personnel economics literature, and suggest open questions in personnel economics where future research can make valuable contributions to the literature. We explore five aspects of the employment relationship - incentives, matching firms with workers, compensation, skill development, and the organization of work - reviewing the main theories, empirical tests of those theories, and the open questions in each area.

## Summary
A Handbook of Organizational Economics survey chapter that organizes personnel economics, the application of economic theory and econometrics to human resource management, around five facets of the employment relationship: incentives, matching firms with workers, compensation, skill development, and the organization of work. For each, Lazear and Oyer lay out the core theory, the (often mixed) empirical evidence, and the open questions. A recurring theme is the gap between elegant models (moral hazard, tournaments, signaling, matching) and their thin or contradictory empirical confirmation, with the authors arguing the highest future returns lie in studying selection, sorting, and matching, increasingly via single-firm field experiments and matched employer-employee data.

## Research question
What does economic analysis tell us about how firms design the employment relationship to maximize their objective (usually profit) subject to the constraint that worker behavior responds to those policies, and where are the most valuable unanswered questions? The chapter is a positive-and-normative stocktaking rather than a single hypothesis test.

## Method / identification
This is a narrative literature review (a handbook chapter), not an original empirical or theoretical study. Its "method" is the organizing taxonomy and the formal models it synthesizes. Key model objects surveyed:

- **Moral hazard / principal-agent.** Output is increasing in effort plus an unobservable shock; the risk-averse agent has utility decreasing-convex in effort and increasing-concave in income. The principal maximizes profit subject to incentive-compatibility and participation constraints, yielding the central **risk-incentive trade-off**: stronger pay-for-performance raises effort but loads risk onto the worker, so noisier environments should optimally use weaker incentives. The benchmark "sell the firm to the worker" (worker as residual claimant with a negative base wage) achieves first-best effort but fails for most jobs due to risk aversion, imperfect output measures, and limited commitment.
- **Distorted performance measures.** Gibbons (1987) ratchet effect; Baker (1992) on a contractible signal $P$ imperfectly correlated with the firm's true objective $V$; Holmstrom and Milgrom (1991) multitasking, where incentives on a measurable task distort effort allocation.
- **Subjective evaluation and implicit contracts.** Baker, Gibbons and Murphy (1994), Bull (1987), MacLeod and Malcomson (1989); career-concerns (Fama 1980; [[@Holmstrom1999|Holmstrom 1999]]; [[@Gibbons1992|Gibbons and Murphy 1992]]); influence activity (Milgrom and Roberts 1988); favoritism ([[@Prendergast1996|Prendergast and Topel 1996]]; MacLeod 2003).
- **Relative performance evaluation and tournaments.** [[@Lazear1981|Lazear and Rosen (1981)]]: prizes fixed in advance on relative rank; larger wage spreads motivate lower ranks; an optimal spread exists; noisier environments require larger spreads to offset reduced effort incentives. Extensions: Green and Stokey (1983), [[@Nalebuff1983|Nalebuff and Stiglitz (1983)]], sabotage/collusion (Dye 1984; Lazear 1989), insider handicaps (Chan 1995).
- **Matching with symmetric learning.** Output decomposed as
$$y_{ijt} = \alpha_i + \mu_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ijt}$$
where $\alpha_i$ is innate ability, $\mu_{ij}$ is match-specific productivity for worker $i$ at firm $j$, and $\varepsilon_{ijt}$ a shock, each mean-zero with variances $\sigma_\alpha,\sigma_\mu,\sigma_\varepsilon$. Selection matters only when $\sigma_\mu>0$ (Jovanovic 1979), predicting turnover falling and wages rising in tenure, predictions also generated by firm-specific human capital, creating an identification problem.
- **Asymmetric information / signaling.** Spence (1973) costly signals with cost inversely related to ability; self-selection (Salop and Salop 1976); Lazear (1986a) measurement-cost-driven sorting between salary and output-pay firms; Greenwald (1986) and [[@Akerlof1970|Akerlof (1970)]] adverse-selection "lemons" in the used-worker market causing inefficiently low mobility; Waldman (1984) strategic job assignment as a signal.

## Data
None of its own. The chapter cites empirical work using single-firm field experiments (e.g., Nagin et al. 2002 on telephone solicitors and monitoring; Bull, Schotter and Weigelt 1987 lab tournaments) and matched employer-employee datasets (Andersson et al. 2006; Woodcock 2006), and repeatedly notes the rising availability of such linked data as the key empirical enabler.

## Key findings
As a survey, the "findings" are synthesized regularities and tensions rather than new theorems. Principal takeaways: (1) the prediction that stronger incentives raise effort is broadly supported, but the **risk-incentive trade-off is empirically elusive/mixed**, motivating the distortion and multitasking extensions; (2) tournament theory's qualitative predictions (spreads affect effort, relative not absolute performance drives promotion) fit data reasonably well; (3) efficiency-wage predictions (monitoring-wage relationship) have weak empirical support; (4) matching and firm-specific human capital are observationally similar yet imply opposite managerial prescriptions (screen-and-select vs. train); (5) relative performance evaluation for CEOs shows surprisingly little filtering of common shocks; (6) the subjective-evaluation and asymmetric-information literatures are theoretically rich but under-tested empirically.

## Contribution
Provides the canonical mid-2000s map of personnel economics, codifying the field around five employment-relationship facets, updating earlier reviews (Gibbons and Waldman 1999; Prendergast 1999; Murphy 1999; Malcomson 1999), and explicitly distinguishing personnel economics (firm-as-optimizer, within-relationship welfare) from the broader labor economics in which it sits. It functions as both a research reference and a teaching scaffold for MBA-level personnel economics.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag these as the field's most fertile hooks: highest returns lie in studying selection, sorting, and matching, that is, how firms find and value the right workers for their context (Southwest, SAS, Whole Foods as motivating cases); how employees value non-cash fringe benefits and whether training is productivity, reward, or signal; why the risk-incentive trade-off lacks empirical support; whether US executives are overpaid and how one could even know; how firms manage reorganization as returns to skill rise; what happens if internal pay differentials grow too large; and how to extend single-firm experiments (lacking generalizability) and matched data to selection, the wage/benefit trade-off, subjective evaluation, and training.

## Connections
Builds directly on the tournament model of [[@Lazear1981|Lazear and Rosen (1981)]] and the moral-hazard tradition of Holmstrom (1979), Mirrlees (1974) and Shapiro and Stiglitz (1984). The performance-measurement strand connects Gibbons (1987), Baker (1992), and Holmstrom and Milgrom (1991); the subjective-contracts strand connects Baker, Gibbons and Murphy (1994), MacLeod and Malcomson (1989), and MacLeod (2003). On matching and information it draws on Jovanovic (1979), Spence (1973), [[@Akerlof1970|Akerlof (1970)]], Greenwald (1986), and Waldman (1984). It updates and complements the prior surveys by Prendergast (1999), Murphy (1999), Gibbons and Waldman (1999), and Malcomson (1999), and sits within the Handbook of Organizational Economics edited by Gibbons and Roberts.

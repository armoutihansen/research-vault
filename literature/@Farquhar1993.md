> [!abstract] Abstract
> A phantom alternative is an illusory choice option—it looks real but for some reason is unavailable at the time a decision is made. Phantoms can both help and hinder successful decision making. On the one hand, phantoms can provide useful information on the boundaries of a decision problem and thus help generate new options through a restructuring of the problem. But phantoms can also produce biases, deception, and suboptimal decisions. We argue that phantoms should be considered explicitly in decision structuring rather than being allowed to work their effects surreptitiously. We offer guidelines on recognizing unavailable alternatives, utilizing the information provided by phantoms, counteracting phantom biases, avoiding deception in decision structuring, and guarding against suboptimal decisions.

## Summary
A "phantom alternative" is an option that looks real but is unavailable when the decision is actually made (e.g., an out-of-stock item, a withdrawn job candidate, a banned product). Farquhar and Pratkanis argue that the prevailing prescriptive stance—delete unavailable options because they cannot be chosen—is wrong: phantoms carry information about the boundaries and constraints of a decision problem, but they also distort preferences (contrast effects, attribute-weight shifts, reactance) and enable deception (bait-and-switch, door-in-the-face). The paper synthesizes decision-analytic problem structuring with social-psychological evidence and offers operational guidelines for recognizing, exploiting, and neutralizing phantoms in applied decision analysis.

## Research question
How should phantom (unavailable) alternatives be treated during the *problem-structuring* phase of a decision analysis? When does deleting them harm the decision, when do they convey useful information, and how can the decision maker counteract the biases and manipulations they induce?

## Method / identification
This is a conceptual / prescriptive paper, not an empirical or formal-axiomatic one. The method is a structured synthesis that (1) builds a taxonomy of phantoms and (2) maps each stage of standard decision analysis to a phantom-induced failure mode, then (3) proposes guidelines.

The taxonomy distinguishes **known phantoms** (the decision maker correctly assumes from the outset an option is unavailable) from **unrecognized phantoms** (the decision maker incorrectly assumes an option is available). Three reasons phantoms go unrecognized are catalogued: *benign neglect* (immateriality of a remote unavailability), *unintentional mistakes* (e.g., "spurious awareness" of nonexistent products), and *deliberate deception*. The authors then walk through five elements of decision structuring—decision objectives, option generation, uncertainty assessment, preference elicitation, and decision strategies—showing how phantoms perturb each. A worked tire-selection example (Table 1, 22 disguised radial-tire brands graded on treadwear, traction, temperature) illustrates dominance screening and motivates a *k-dominance* generalization: an option is "$k$-dominated" if exactly $k$ available options dominate it; standard screening keeps only the $0$-dominated (nondominated) set, but the authors propose retaining $0$- and $1$-dominated options (and adding $2$-dominated ones as unavailability risk rises) as a hedge.

## Data
None in the empirical sense. The paper uses illustrative cases (airline overbooking, poultry stock selection, academic "best athlete" hiring, pari-mutuel betting, Tris flame-retardant) and one stylized labeled-tire table for exposition; it cites prior experimental work (Wright & Barbour 1977; Farquhar & Pratkanis 1987) rather than reporting new experiments.

## Key findings
- **Phantoms violate regularity.** A known phantom preferred on one ("focal") attribute (i) produces a *contrast effect* lowering the attractiveness of available options on that attribute—stronger for options nearer the phantom—and (ii) raises the *weight* placed on the focal attribute. Net preference reversals among available options can result, violating Luce's (1959) regularity principle of classical choice.
- **Phantoms carry contextual information** about the boundaries and constraints of the problem; deleting them per Luce's choice axiom or Hammond's consequentialism also discards this information and can degrade option generation.
- **Screening with unrecognized phantoms causes suboptimization.** Because people focus on options surviving a screen and rarely reconsider eliminated ones (Wright & Barbour 1977), a dominant phantom can knock out the truly best *available* option; opportunity loss equals the value gap between best-available and chosen.
- **Guidelines** are offered in four clusters: recognize phantoms early (contingency planning, e.g., the overbooking case), see through deceptive phantoms (interrogate who structured the option set and why), counteract biases via *complementary phantoms* (introduce a second phantom dominant on a different attribute to equalize contrast/weight shifts) and "structural sensitivity analysis," and guard against suboptimal decisions via $k$-dominance retention of "close contenders."

## Contribution
Reframes unavailable alternatives from a nuisance to be pruned into a first-class object of decision structuring, and bridges normative decision analysis with the social psychology of persuasion and reactance. It supplies a vocabulary (phantom, known vs. unrecognized, focal attribute, complementary phantom, $k$-dominance) and a debiasing toolkit for applied decision analysts and a theoretical anchor for later behavioral work on availability and choice-set effects.

## Limitations & open questions
- The authors pose as an explicit open problem: **"to determine under what conditions a particular choice of $k$ is optimal"** for the $k$-dominance screening hedge.
- How to *resolve the motives* (self-consistency vs. reactance) aroused by a surprise phantom "depends on the meaning of a phantom and the structure of the immediate decision environment"—left unformalized.
- Complementary phantoms have a noted drawback: adding another phantom reinforces overall contrast, making *all* available options seem worse, so the selection should be made before continuing—conditions for net benefit are not characterized.
- The cognitive processes integrating availability probability with desirability (which mutually influence each other, e.g., in dating choices) are flagged as "exceedingly complicated" and not analyzed.
- No formal model or empirical test is provided for the proposed guidelines.

## Connections
The phantom-induced contrast and attribute-weight shifts are an instance of context-dependent / menu-dependent choice that violates regularity, connecting to the attraction (asymmetric-dominance) effect of [[@Huber1982|Huber, Payne & Puto (1982)]] and to the broader literature on choice-set effects later modeled by [[@Tversky1993|Tversky & Simonson (1993)]]. The taxonomy of unavailable options relates to limited-attention and consideration-set models in which not all options are evaluated, anticipating formal treatments such as [[@Manzini2007|Manzini & Mariotti (2007)]] and the stochastic choice-set-generation approaches of Manski (1977) and Swait & Ben-Akiva (1987) cited in the text. The regularity-violation framing builds directly on Luce (1959) and on Hammond's (1986) consequentialism, which the authors critique. The reactance and "forbidden fruit" mechanisms draw on Brehm (1966) and commodity theory (Brock 1968; Cialdini 1984), while the imaginability and debiasing discussion connects to [[@Tversky1974|Tversky & Kahneman (1974)]]. The companion theoretical paper Farquhar & Pratkanis (1993) and the review Pratkanis & Farquhar (1992) develop the experimental side.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Two core meanings of "utility" are distinguished. "Decision utility" is the weight of an outcome in a decision. "Experienced utility" is hedonic quality, as in Bentham's usage. Experienced utility can be reported in real time (instant utility), or in retrospective evaluations of past episodes (remembered utility). Psychological research has documented systematic errors in retrospective evaluations, which can induce a preference for dominated options. We propose a formal normative theory of the total experienced utility of temporally extended outcomes. Measuring the experienced utility of outcomes permits tests of utility maximization and opens other lines of empirical research.

## Summary
Kahneman, Wakker, and Sarin revive Bentham's hedonic ("experienced") notion of utility and sharply separate it from the modern revealed-preference ("decision") notion. They argue experienced utility is measurable and empirically distinct from decision utility, document that retrospective evaluation of episodes obeys a Peak-End rule with duration neglect (so choices can violate dominance), and supply an axiomatic normative theory under which the total utility of a temporally extended outcome is the time-integral of a transformation of instant utility. Admitting experienced utility, they argue, turns "people maximize utility" from a maintained tautology into a testable empirical claim.

## Research question
Can hedonic experienced utility be rehabilitated as a measurable, normatively governed quantity distinct from decision utility, and if so, how should the total experienced utility of an outcome extended over time be defined and evaluated? Subsidiary: what determines the remembered utility of an episode, and do biases in remembered utility distort subsequent choice?

## Method / identification
Three-pronged: conceptual taxonomy, empirical synthesis, and an axiomatic representation theorem. The taxonomy distinguishes instant utility (hedonic value of a moment), remembered utility (retrospective evaluation of a past temporally extended outcome, TEO), total utility (the normative construct), decision utility (inferred from choice), and predicted utility (beliefs about future experienced utility).

The formal core is a normative theory of total utility over utility profiles. A utility profile $f$ of duration $x$ assigns an instant-utility level to each time point; profiles concatenate, with $f$ of duration $x$ and $g$ of duration $y$ yielding a profile of duration $x+y$. Three axioms are imposed: (1) concatenation with a hedonically neutral profile leaves total utility unchanged; (2) monotonicity in instant utility (local increases in instant utility never decrease total utility); (3) monotonicity in total utility under concatenation, which delivers separability of disjoint time intervals — instant-utility ratings must be "sufficient statistics" carrying all order and interaction effects. A fourth axiom characterizes cardinal measurability of instant utility (invariance of the total-utility ordering when instant utility is shifted by a common constant over an equal-length period). Proof technique combines a Hölder-type argument with Gorman's (1968) representation of separable preferences. Measurement is grounded in psychophysics: real-time "instant utility" is recorded with sliding-knob / discomfort-meter devices, anchored by a distinctive hedonic zero ("neither pleasant nor unpleasant").

## Data
Secondary synthesis of the authors' own experiments rather than one fresh dataset. Sources include: Fredrickson and Kahneman (1993) film-clip study (sixteen short pleasant/aversive clips, short ~45s vs long ~120s versions, continuous instant-utility traces); Redelmeier and Kahneman (1996a) colonoscopy study (continuous pain reports every 60s; mean duration 23 min, SD 13 min) and (1996b) randomized prolongation trial ($N=682$); the cold-water (cold-pressor) experiments of Kahneman et al. (1993, $N=32$ plus a 37-subject replication); and the Schreiber and Kahneman (1996) aversive-sound study. Reported correlations: Peak-End average with remembered utility .77 (pleasant) / .69 (aversive) for films and .67 for colonoscopy; duration with global evaluation only ~.03–.06.

## Key findings
- **Peak-End evaluation (empirical):** remembered utility of a homogeneous episode is well predicted by the average of the Peak (most intense) instant (dis)utility and the End instant (dis)utility.
- **Duration neglect:** episode duration has little or no independent effect on remembered utility once Peak-End is controlled.
- **Violations of temporal monotonicity / dominance:** adding an extra stretch of milder discomfort can *improve* remembered (dis)utility; in the cold-water and sound experiments most subjects chose to repeat the objectively longer, dominated aversive trial (~65–69% preferred the Long trial). Hence decisions fail to maximize experienced utility while plausibly maximizing remembered utility.
- **Theorem 1 (ordinal):** Axioms 1–3 hold iff there exists a nondecreasing value transformation $v$ of instant utility, with $v(0)=0$, such that total utility orders profiles by the integral of $v$ of instant utility over time, $$U(f)=\int v\big(u(t)\big)\,dt,$$ unique up to a positive scale factor (a ratio scale).
- **Corollary 1:** total utility depends only on the time distribution over instant utility (time spent in each hedonic region) — encoding time neutrality; profile reordering does not change total utility.
- **Theorem 2 (cardinal):** Axioms 1, 2, 4 hold iff total utility orders profiles by the plain integral of instant utility over time, $$U(f)=\int u(t)\,dt,$$ no value transformation required.
- **Corollary 2 (time monotonicity):** concatenating a wholly positive profile cannot lower, and a wholly negative profile cannot raise, total utility — the principle the experiments empirically violate.

## Contribution
Establishes the now-standard conceptual map of utility types (instant, remembered, total, decision, predicted) and Figure I's research agenda relating them. It provides the first axiomatic normative foundation for temporal integration of hedonic experience, exploiting an *undated* concatenation operation that distinguishes it from the time-preference/discounting literature. By showing that utility maximization can be *proved false* once experienced utility is measured, it converts a maintained hypothesis into a testable proposition and launches the empirical "hedonic measurement" program (well-being, the day-reconstruction/experience-sampling agenda, and Peak-End in applied settings).

## Limitations & open questions
- Findings come from a narrow class: short, hedonically homogeneous episodes with passive subjects; generalization to long, mixed-affect, active outcomes is open.
- Separability is reasonable only if instant utility truly absorbs all order effects and inter-outcome interactions; whether such a measure can be constructed in practice is unresolved.
- Cross-situation calibration of the instant-utility scale is acknowledged to fail when situations are "radically different" (e.g., baby vs adult), bounding interpersonal and intertemporal comparison.
- Time neutrality (no discounting) is defended normatively but conflicts with the prevailing view that discounting is rational — a live normative dispute.
- Predicted utility is poorly accurate (people mispredict future tastes), opening the welfare question of which utility should guide policy when decision, experienced, and remembered utilities diverge — and the resulting challenge to consumer sovereignty.
- Measurement is framed as "a difficult technical problem, not a hopeless quest," explicitly inviting better real-time and physiological instruments.

## Connections
The decision-vs-experienced split builds directly on Bentham (1789) and the marginalist hedonics of Jevons and Edgeworth (1881), whose "hedonimeter" the integration theorem formalizes. The separability machinery draws on Gorman (1968) and parallels Stigler and Becker (1977), who secured separability by redefining outcomes as conjunctions of present and past consumption; non-separable alternatives are noted in Gilboa (1989) and Constantinides (1990), and habit/rational-addiction models in Becker (1996). The skeptical stance on predicted utility connects to Scitovsky (1976), Kahneman and Snell (1992), Loewenstein and Adler (1995), and Simonson (1990). The reference-dependence and framing themes invoke Tversky and Kahneman (1986, 1991) and Kahneman (1994); intertemporal-choice normative debates reference [[@Loewenstein1992|Loewenstein (1992)]] and Broome (1991). Empirical anchors include Brickman, Coates, and Janoff-Bulman (1978) on hedonic adaptation and Redelmeier and Kahneman (1996) on the colonoscopy trials. This paper is the conceptual seed for later well-being and experience-sampling work (e.g., the Day Reconstruction Method) and for Kahneman's distinction between the experiencing and remembering selves.

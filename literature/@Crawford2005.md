> [!abstract] Abstract
> Exploiting a rich panel data set on anti-ulcer drug prescriptions, we measure the effects of uncertainty and learning in the demand for pharmaceutical drugs. We estimate a dynamic matching model of demand under uncertainty in which patients learn from prescription experience about the effectiveness of alternative drugs. Unlike previous models, we allow drugs to have distinct symptomatic and curative effects, and endogenize treatment length by allowing drug choices to affect patients' underlying probability of recovery. We find that drugs' rankings along these dimensions differ, with high symptomatic effects for drugs with the highest market shares and high curative effects for drugs with the greatest medical efficacy. Our results also indicate that while there is substantial heterogeneity in drug efficacy across patients, learning enables patients and their doctors to dramatically reduce the costs of uncertainty in pharmaceutical markets.

## Summary
Crawford and Shum estimate a structural dynamic discrete-choice model of pharmaceutical demand in which patients (acting through their doctors) are uncertain about how well each drug works for them and learn about it via Bayesian updating from prescription experience. The innovation is a *two-dimensional* matching model: each drug has both a **symptomatic** match value (affecting per-period utility) and a separate **curative** match value (affecting the probability of recovery), and treatment length is endogenized through that recovery probability. Using Italian anti-ulcer prescription panel data, they find substantial cross-patient heterogeneity in efficacy, rapid learning (two-thirds of initial uncertainty resolved after one prescription), risk-averse patients, and—via counterfactuals—that learning and experimentation let patients reach utility levels close to a complete-information first best.

## Research question
How large are the effects of uncertainty and learning on prescription choices and treatment outcomes in a real pharmaceutical market? Specifically, can one separately identify and quantify (i) heterogeneity in how well a drug *matches* a given patient versus (ii) the uncertainty/learning that surrounds that match, and what are the welfare costs of uncertainty once forward-looking experimentation and diagnostic matching by doctors are accounted for?

## Method / identification
The paper builds a **dynamic Bayesian learning / matching model** of drug choice estimated by smoothed simulated maximum likelihood. A doctor chooses a sequence of drugs to maximize a patient's present-discounted expected utility, $\max_{D}\,E_D\sum_{t=1}^{\infty}\beta^t d_{jnt}\,u_{jnt}(1-w_{j,t-1})$, with $\beta$ fixed at $0.95$ and $w_{jt}$ the recovery indicator.

Two features distinguish it from prior learning models (Erdem & Keane 1996; Ackerberg 2003). First, each drug $n$ carries **two** time-invariant patient-specific match values: a symptomatic value $\mu_{jn}$ and a curative value $\nu_{jn}$. Each prescription delivers two independent noisy normal signals, $x_{jnt}$ centered on $\mu_{jn}$ and $y_{jnt}$ centered on $\nu_{jn}$. To generate persistence, per-period utility is CARA (exponential) rather than linear: $u(x_{jnt},p_n,\epsilon_{jnt})=-\exp(-r\,x_{jnt})-\alpha\,p_n+\epsilon_{jnt}$, with $r>0$ the risk-aversion parameter and $\epsilon_{jnt}$ i.i.d. Type-I extreme value (yielding logit choice probabilities). Second, treatment length is **endogenous**: the recovery probability evolves via a logistic-style recursion in the curative signals, $h_{jt}=\frac{h_{jt-1}/(1-h_{jt-1})+d_{jnt}y_{jnt}}{1+h_{jt-1}/(1-h_{jt-1})+d_{jnt}y_{jnt}}$, seeded by an unobserved initial illness-severity $h_{0j}=\theta_k$.

Doctors hold multivariate-normal priors over $(\mu_{jn},\nu_{jn})$ with means $(\bar\mu_{nk},\bar\nu_{nk})$ and dispersions $(\bar\sigma_n,\bar\tau_n)$, and update conjugately as signals arrive; posterior means/variances follow standard normal-learning recursions (DeGroot 1970). The state vector $S_t$ collects posterior means $\mu_{jn}^t,\nu_{jn}^t$, use counts $l_{jn}^t$, recovery probability $h_{jt}$, and the logit shocks. The value function solves a Bellman equation; the CARA form makes the expected per-period utility $-\exp(-r\mu_{jn}^t+\tfrac12 r^2(\sigma_n^2+V_{jn}^t))-\alpha p_n+\epsilon_{jnt}$, so the term $\tfrac12 r^2(\sigma_n^2+V_{jn}^t)$ acts as a **risk premium / endogenous switching cost** that falls with experience. Unobserved illness severity is a $K=4$-point discrete distribution (Heckman–Singer style), integrated out in the likelihood. The value function is computed with a Keane–Wolpin (1994) approximation variant; standard errors use the outer-product-of-the-gradient form valid under simulation asymptotics (Lee 1992; Pakes & Pollard 1989).

**Identification (Section 4.1):** the exclusion that symptomatic effects enter only utility while curative effects enter only recovery is the key restriction. Initial-prescription market shares pin down prior symptomatic means $\bar\mu_n$; the change in choice probabilities early vs. late in treatment identifies the cross-patient dispersion $\bar\sigma_n^2$; the *overall* incidence of switching identifies risk aversion $r$, while how switching declines with use count $l$ identifies signal noise $\sigma_n$. Curative parameters and illness heterogeneity are jointly identified from recovery frequencies conditional on drug histories. The exponential (not linear) utility is what separately identifies $\bar\mu_n$ from the price coefficient $\alpha$, since $\mu_{jn}^t$ enters interacted with the time-varying variance $V_{jn}^t$. Rational-expectations priors are relaxed only for omeprazole, the new entrant, whose prior means are allowed to drift over six-month intervals to capture doctor-level learning.

## Data
A unique Italian panel: all anti-ulcer prescriptions for a 10% sample of patients aged 15–85 in the Rome metropolitan area, monthly over 36 months (Jan 1990–Dec 1992), ~310,000 prescription observations for >55,000 patients. After filters (excluding within-month multi-drug months and left-censored patients first seen before June 1990), the estimation sample is 34,972 patients. Five "drugs" are modeled: ranitidine (Glaxo, ~64% share), omeprazole (Astra), famotidine (Merck), nizatidine (Lilly), and a composite "drug 5" of 19 smaller molecules. National Health System coverage means uniform insurance and regulated, roughly time-constant prices—features that mitigate agency/insurance confounds. Patients average 2.8 prescriptions over ~6 months; 54% receive only one prescription; repeat-purchase probabilities range 74.8%–94.1%.

## Key findings
Strong illness heterogeneity: Type 1 ("not-so-sick," 59.3%) start with a 43.3% recovery chance, Type 2 ("sick," 33.5%) only 12.7%. Estimated risk aversion $r\approx0.99$ is significant, generating persistence even after a single prescription. Symptomatic and curative drug rankings **differ**, and both differ from the market-share ranking: high-share drugs (ranitidine) have high symptomatic effects, while medically efficacious drugs (omeprazole, the PPI entrant) score high on the curative dimension despite lower share and higher price. Match-value dispersion ($\hat\sigma\approx1.58$) is large relative to across-drug mean differences, implying drugs are strongly *horizontally* differentiated across patients. Omeprazole's prior means rise over time, evidencing doctor-level learning. Counterfactuals: **complete information** raises average discounted utility only ~9% (and notably *lengthens* treatment and de-concentrates the market); **no experimentation** (forced to keep the first drug) lowers utility ~6%; **no diagnostic matching** (doctors cannot read patient type) is the costliest, lowering utility ~11% and raising treatment length/cost 30–40%. Thus diagnostic matching by doctors is at least as valuable as the idiosyncratic experiential learning, and it raises market concentration (Herfindahl 4242 baseline vs. 3333 without diagnostic matching).

## Contribution
First structural estimates of the magnitude of uncertainty and learning in a real pharmaceutical market, extending Bayesian-learning dynamic discrete-choice demand (Erdem–Keane, Ackerberg) along two substantively new dimensions—separating symptomatic from curative effects (a two-signal *outcome*-bearing matching problem) and endogenizing treatment length through recovery. It quantifies the welfare cost of uncertainty, the risk-aversion-induced switching cost arising after even one prescription, and the relative importance of diagnostic vs. experiential matching, with implications for promotion, market concentration, and information policy.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit gaps. (i) **Agency:** doctors are assumed perfect agents for patients, ruling out conditional "doctor effects"; data lack the doctor-level prescription histories and marketing-exposure information needed to estimate agency/detailing effects—left as "an important topic for further research." (ii) **Doctor learning across patients** is only partially accommodated (for omeprazole) because patient-level data cannot directly identify it. (iii) Computational cost forces assuming **independent** symptomatic/curative signals and priors (no correlation). (iv) Results apply only to ulcer sufferers who actually obtain and fill a prescription—incidence of illness and the doctor-visit decision are outside the data. (v) The authors plan future work on **information-diffusion policy** and promotion (especially direct-to-consumer advertising) given the switching costs found. They also suggest the framework extends to other "expert" markets (legal, business services) with measurable ex-post performance.

## Connections
The matching structure follows the job-matching literature (Jovanovic 1979; Miller 1984; survey in Eckstein & Wolpin 1987). The empirical learning model builds directly on Bayesian-learning dynamic discrete choice in demand—Eckstein, Horsky & Raban (1988), Erdem & Keane (1996), and Ackerberg (2003)—with the latter two also using two signals, though only for current utility. Within pharmaceutical-demand learning it relates to Currie & Park (1999) and Ching (2002), differing by assuming forward-looking patients; the time-varying-prior treatment of omeprazole mirrors Ching (2002), and doctor-level learning links to Coscelli & Shum (2004) and Ferreyra (1999). Endogenizing health outcomes parallels the health literature (Gilleskie 1998; Blau & Gilleskie 2000) and the learning-based fertility model of Mira (2000). Static pharmaceutical demand and intra- vs. intermolecular substitution work includes Stern (1996) and Ellison, Cockburn, Griliches & Hausman (1997); the same Italian data are used by Coscelli (2000). Theoretical strategic-learning/pricing antecedents include [[@Nelson1970|Nelson (1970)]], Shapiro (1983), Kihlstrom & Riordan (1984), [[@Milgrom1986|Milgrom & Roberts (1986)]], and especially Bergemann & Välimäki (2004). Methodologically it draws on Keane & Wolpin (1994) for value-function approximation, DeGroot (1970) for conjugate normal learning, and Pakes & Pollard (1989) and Lee (1992) for the simulation-estimation asymptotics.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> There is strong evidence that people exploit their bargaining power in competitive markets but not in bilateral bargaining situations. There is also strong evidence that people exploit free-riding opportunities in voluntary cooperation games. Yet, when they are given the opportunity to punish free riders, stable cooperation is maintained, although punishment is costly for those who punish. This paper asks whether there is a simple common principle that can explain this puzzling evidence. We show that if some people care about equity the puzzles can be resolved. It turns out that the economic environment determines whether the fair types or the selfish types dominate equilibrium behavior.

## Summary
Fehr and Schmidt propose that, alongside purely self-interested agents, a fraction of the population is *inequity averse*: people dislike unequal payoff distributions and trade off own material payoff against equity, suffering more from being behind than from being ahead. A single one-parameter-pair utility function, embedded in standard game theory, reconciles seemingly contradictory experimental facts: "fair" outcomes in ultimatum and gift-exchange games, "unfair" competitive outcomes in market games, free-riding in public-good games without punishment, and stable cooperation once costly punishment is available. The central methodological insight is that the *economic environment* determines whether fair or selfish types are decisive in equilibrium.

## Research question
Is there a single, simple, fully rational behavioral model that can simultaneously explain why people split surplus roughly equally in bilateral bargaining yet accept extremely unequal outcomes in competitive markets, and why they free-ride absent punishment yet cooperate when punishment is feasible?

## Method / identification
The paper is theoretical: a utility model analyzed through standard (subgame-perfect) equilibrium across several game forms. For a set of $n$ players with monetary payoff vector $x=(x_1,\dots,x_n)$, player $i$'s utility is
$$U_i(x)=x_i-\frac{\alpha_i}{n-1}\sum_{j\neq i}\max\{x_j-x_i,0\}-\frac{\beta_i}{n-1}\sum_{j\neq i}\max\{x_i-x_j,0\},$$
with the maintained restrictions $\beta_i\leq\alpha_i$ and $0\leq\beta_i<1$. The second term is the disutility from *disadvantageous* inequality (being behind), the third from *advantageous* inequality (being ahead). The reference point is the egalitarian outcome; comparison is self-centered (player $i$ does not care about inequality *among* others). The restriction $\alpha_i\geq\beta_i$ encodes loss aversion in social comparisons; $\beta_i<1$ rules out implausibly costly money-burning to reduce one's own advantage; $\alpha_i$ is unbounded above. The model is then solved game by game (ultimatum, proposer-competition market, responder-competition market, public goods with and without punishment, dictator, gift exchange), with proofs in an appendix.

## Data
None of its own. The paper is calibrated against and tested on published experimental results: ultimatum-game meta-evidence (Roth 1995; Camerer and Thaler 1995; Cameron 1995; Hoffman, McCabe, and Smith 1996; Slonim and Roth 1997), market games (Roth, Prasnikar, Okuno-Fujiwara, and Zamir 1991; Guth, Marchand, and Rulliere 1997), and public-good games with punishment (Fehr and Gachter 1996). Section V backs out a plausible single distribution of $(\alpha,\beta)$ pairs across the subject population consistent with all of these games at once.

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 (ultimatum game):** the responder accepts any $s\geq 0.5$, rejects $s$ below the threshold $s'(\alpha_2)\equiv\alpha_2/(1+2\alpha_2)<0.5$, and accepts above it; the proposer offers $0.5$ if $\beta_1>0.5$ and $s'(\alpha_2)$ if $\beta_1<0.5$. This generates the stylized facts: no offers above $0.5$, modal offers in $[0.4,0.5]$, and rejection of low offers.
- **Proposition 2 (market with proposer competition):** for *any* preference parameters there is a unique subgame-perfect equilibrium outcome where at least two proposers offer $s=1$ and the responder reaps all surplus. Competition makes the distribution of preferences irrelevant — a single selfish player drives the outcome.
- **Proposition 3 (market with responder competition):** an equilibrium with $s=0$ (proposer takes everything) always exists if $\beta_1<(n-1)/n$; the highest sustainable offer depends only on the *least* inequity-averse responder and converges to zero as $n\to\infty$.
- **Proposition 4 (public good, no punishment):** if $a+\beta_i<1$, contributing nothing is a dominant strategy for $i$; if enough such players exist, full free-riding is the unique equilibrium; positive-contribution equilibria require sufficiently many players with $a+\beta_i>1$ who are not too averse to the disadvantageous inequality created by free riders.
- **Proposition 5 (public good with punishment):** a group of "conditionally cooperative enforcers" with $\alpha+\beta_i\geq 1$ can credibly threaten to punish defectors, sustaining cooperation even among otherwise-selfish players — explaining the roughly 80% full cooperation observed once punishment is available.
- A single calibrated preference distribution reproduces the majority of individual choices across all these games.

## Contribution
It introduced the canonical *Fehr–Schmidt inequity-aversion* utility function, now one of the two reference models of distributional social preferences (alongside Bolton and Ockenfels). Its central conceptual contribution is showing that heterogeneous preferences interact with the strategic environment, so the *same* population yields "fair" or "selfish" aggregate behavior depending on institutions — competition can render fairness preferences behaviorally irrelevant while punishment opportunities make them decisive. By keeping the model outcome-based and within standard game theory (no psychological games), it remains tractable for $n$-person and market games that intention-based models cannot handle.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Intentions are not modeled.** The authors concede intentions matter (citing Rabin) and that their outcome-based formulation captures them only implicitly; explicit modeling would require psychological game theory they deliberately avoid for tractability.
- **Linearity** of the utility in inequality is assumed for simplicity; the authors note dictator-game evidence suggests a nonnegligible fraction exhibit *nonlinear* advantageous-inequality aversion.
- **$\beta_i<0$ types** (status-seekers who like being ahead) are assumed away because they have "virtually no impact" in the games studied — but their existence and influence in other environments is left open.
- **Reference group and reference outcome** are taken as the egalitarian split among co-players; their determination "is ultimately an empirical question" left unresolved.
- The **calibrated parameter distribution** is inferred ex post rather than independently estimated; out-of-sample identification of $(\alpha_i,\beta_i)$ is an open empirical agenda.
- Whether the relevant comparison is the average payoff or pairwise comparison is flagged as unresolved by the available evidence.

## Connections
The principal rival outcome-based model is [[@Bolton2000|Bolton and Ockenfels (2000)]], which uses relative payoff shares rather than pairwise differences. [[@Rabin1993|Rabin (1993)]] is the canonical *intention-based* alternative (kindness and reciprocity in psychological games), extended to extensive form by Dufwenberg and Kirchsteiger and by Falk and Fischbacher. The structural estimation of individual social-preference parameters that this model invites was later pursued by Bruhin, Fehr, and Schunk and by mixture-model approaches; [[@Charness2002|Charness and Rabin (2002)]] propose a competing model combining distributional concern with reciprocity. The empirical anchors include Roth, Prasnikar, Okuno-Fujiwara, and Zamir (1991) for cross-country ultimatum and market games, Fehr and Gachter (1996, 2000) for public goods with punishment, and Loewenstein, Thompson, and Bazerman (1989) for the asymmetric ($\alpha\geq\beta$) inequality aversion the model assumes. The loss-aversion analogy draws on Tversky and Kahneman (1991).

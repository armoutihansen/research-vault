## Summary
The paper exploits the Dutch Postcode Lottery (PCL) — which each week awards a "Street Prize" of €12,500/ticket plus one BMW to participants in a randomly drawn six-digit postcode — as a natural experiment delivering a large (~8 months of income), unexpected, temporary income shock that is exogenous both to the winner and to the winner's residential neighbors. Using a partial-population design and survey data on 419 codegroups (2,011 households), the authors test the permanent-income/life-cycle hypothesis, theories of in-kind transfers, and relative-income ("keeping up with the Joneses") and habituation models of happiness. Winners' consumption responds only in durables — mainly cars; happiness is unaffected by own or neighbors' winnings; and there are robust, highly localized social effects on neighbors' car consumption.

## Research question
What are the causal effects of a large, unexpected, temporary income shock (a) on the winning household's own consumption and happiness, and (b) on the consumption and happiness of the winner's non-winning neighbors? Specifically, do households treat in-kind prizes (a BMW) as cash, does the life-cycle model hold for durables vs. nondurables, and do relative-income or habituation models of happiness survive genuinely exogenous shocks to own and neighbors' income?

## Method / identification
The core is a **partial-population design**: postcodes (peer groups averaging 19 households) are randomly selected for treatment, and within winning postcodes only PCL participants ("ticket holders") actually win. Crucially, the takeup (participation) decision is made *before* group randomization, so the authors observe which households in non-winning codes *would have been* treated, enabling separate effect estimates for participants and nonparticipants and a direct within-group test for social effects. A winning postcode plus its nearby non-winning "control" codes form a *codegroup* $CG(i)$, with postcode $PC(i)\subseteq CG(i)$ and assumed social comparison group $N(i)$ (default $N(i)=PC(i)\setminus\{i\}$; alternatives: two/four immediate neighbors, all addresses within 25 m).

Two estimands are defined via potential outcomes. Observed consumption is $C_i = C_{0i} + W_i(C_{1i}-C_{0i})$. Under **Assumption 1** ($E(C_{0i}\mid W_i=1,P_i=1)=E(C_{0i}\mid W_i=0,P_i=1)$) the average treatment effect on participants is identified by the simple winning-vs-nonwinning code difference:
$$\text{ATE}=E[C_i\mid W_i=1,P_i=1]-E[C_i\mid W_i=0,P_i=1].$$
Under **Assumption 2** (analogous, for nonparticipants) the average indirect (social) effect is
$$\text{ITE}=E[C_i\mid W_i=1,P_i=0]-E[C_i\mid W_i=0,P_i=0],$$
interpretable as a lower bound on the true ITE if spillovers also reach nonparticipants in nearby non-winning codes. The ATE bundles own and social effects.

Section VI adds structure with a linear consumption regression:
$$C_i=\alpha+\beta\,WIN_i+\gamma\,WIN_{N(i)}+\mu_i,$$
where $\beta$ is the pure own effect and $\gamma$ the social effect; the ITE is proportional to $\gamma$ and the ATE a linear combination of $\beta$ and $\gamma$. Because individual ticket holdings $T_i$ may correlate with unobserved tastes, all regressions include a quadratic in tickets plus a participation fixed effect; codegroup fixed effects absorb spatial unobservables (and neighbors' unobserved ticket holdings, which are not survey-observed). Standard errors are clustered on postcodes. Robustness includes dropping codegroup fixed effects, restricting to codes with ≥16 addresses, and within-household time-differencing.

## Data
Mailed surveys (Sept 2003–July 2006) sent six months after each prize to all addresses in PCL-winning postcodes and to matched neighboring non-winning postcodes; 419 postcodes, final sample 2,011 households (510 by telephone), response rate ~32.7%. Surveys cover demographics, education, labor supply, happiness (1–10), car ownership, large expenditures, income, and lottery participation — with some retrospective (pre-lottery) measures. The PCL administration supplied winners' street addresses and winnings; geographic $(x,y)$ coordinates from land registries permit Euclidean-distance neighbor definitions. Cadastre house-sale data confirm no differential out-migration from winning addresses.

## Key findings
- **Own effects on winners (life cycle / in-kind transfers):** No detectable effects on food at home, transportation, or total monthly outlays. Effects concentrate in durables — winning €10,000 lowers main-car age by ~0.4 years and raises noncar durables spending by ~€310; consistent with timing-of-durables smoothing (Browning & Crossley) or mental-accounting borrowing constraints (Shefrin & Thaler).
- **In-kind transfer:** Six months out, BMW winners are statistically no more likely to own a BMW; only 4 of 25 (16%) BMW winners still owned one — they converted the in-kind prize to cash/other goods despite tax penalties, as in-kind-transfer theory (Moffitt 1984, 1989) predicts.
- **Social effects on neighbors:** Robust, large, and *localized* to immediate neighbors and visible durables. An immediate neighbor's win raises a household's probability of buying a car within six months by ~7 pp and cuts main-car age by ~half a year (~7%) — comparable to the own effect of winning €10,000. Effects appear for all four car indicators; Euclidean-distance measures fail (81% live in multiunit dwellings).
- **Happiness:** Neither own winnings nor living in a winning postcode affects happiness (bounded within ±0.07–0.11 SD for neighbors), challenging relative-income (Luttmer) and habituation models. Cross-sectional income, by contrast, is strongly associated with happiness.

## Contribution
First study (with Angelucci & De Giorgi) of social consumption effects via a partial-population design, and the first using genuinely exogenous, randomized shocks to *neighbors'* incomes in a developed economy. Provides clean natural-experimental tests of in-kind-transfer theory and of relative-income happiness models, and documents a social multiplier in durables consumption with implications for "cash-for-clunkers"-type stimulus.

## Limitations & open questions
- The design does *not* yield an experimental estimate of neighbors' winnings on *participants* (only on nonparticipants); $\beta$ for participants requires the added assumption that $\beta,\gamma$ are common across participants/nonparticipants.
- Estimates cannot distinguish **endogenous** social effects (imitation: "I buy a car because you buy one") from **exogenous** effects of neighbors' income on consumption.
- Mechanism behind car spillovers is unresolved: conspicuous-consumption ("keeping up with the van den Bergs") vs. used-car resale to neighbors vs. intrafamily income transfers vs. information sharing about car models — the paper cannot fully rule these out.
- The null happiness result is ambiguous on timing: six months may be too long (transitory boost faded, per Easterlin/Kahneman) or too short (Gardner & Oswald find delayed effects).
- Survey-derived neighbor/participation indicators are incomplete; retrospective consumption measures risk recall bias; Euclidean measures cannot discriminate within multiunit dwellings.

## Connections
Builds directly on lottery-winner and income-shock studies — Imbens, Rubin & Sacerdote (2001) and Agarwal, Liu & Souleles (2007) on own-income effects — and on the partial-population/social-effects literature: Angelucci & De Giorgi (2009) is the closest analog (PROGRESA, with pre-program income playing PCL participation's role), alongside Duflo & Saez (2003), Miguel & Kremer (2004), Bobonis & Finan (2009), and Lalive & Cattaneo (2009); Moffitt (2001) frames partial-population vs. group-changing designs. The happiness results engage Easterlin (1974), Luttmer (2005), Stevenson & Wolfers (2008), Kahneman et al. (2006), Clark, Frijters & Shields (2008), and Gardner & Oswald (2007). Theoretical anchors include the permanent-income/life-cycle model, Browning & Crossley (2009), Shefrin & Thaler's (1988) mental accounting, Moffitt (1984, 1989) on in-kind transfers, and Veblen/conspicuous-consumption ideas; social multipliers connect to Glaeser, Sacerdote & Scheinkman (2003), and the model-information channel to Grinblatt, Keloharju & Ikäheimo (2008). The regret motive draws on Zeelenberg & Pieters (2004).

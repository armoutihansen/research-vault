## Summary
Fisman, Kariv and Markovits turn the dictator game into a graphical budget-set decision task: subjects repeatedly point-and-click an allocation on a budget line (or step-shaped frontier) trading off their own payoff against others'. The design yields ~50 choices per subject, enough to test and estimate preferences one person at a time, with no pooling or homogeneity assumption. They find that the large majority of subjects choose almost exactly as if maximizing a well-behaved (continuous, increasing, concave) utility function, recover individual CES preferences for giving, and document that those preferences are extremely heterogeneous yet systematically tilted toward raising aggregate ("social-welfare") payoffs rather than equalizing them. A three-person extension separates preferences for giving (self vs. others) from social preferences (other vs. other) and finds a strong within-subject correlation between the two efficiency–equity attitudes. A step-shaped extension exposes a minority whose self-damaging choices cannot be rationalized by any canonical distributional model.

## Research question
What do individuals' other-regarding (distributional) preferences actually look like at the individual level? Specifically: (i) Are giving choices consistent with utility maximization at all? (ii) If so, what is the shape of each person's utility over own vs. others' payoffs, and how heterogeneous is it? (iii) Are "preferences for giving" (self vs. others) distinct from "social preferences" (allocating among others), and how are they related within a person? (iv) Can one detect the kind of not-well-behaved (e.g. difference-averse) preferences that prominent theories posit, given that linear-budget data inherently cannot?

## Method / identification
The core methodological move is a graphical modified dictator game (after [[@Andreoni2002|Andreoni and Miller 2002)]]. Persons are $s$ (self) and $o$ (other); monetary payoffs are $\pi_s,\pi_o$ at prices $p_s,p_o$, with budget $p_s\pi_s+p_o\pi_o\le m$, so $p_o/p_s$ is the *relative price of giving*. Each subject faces 50 randomly drawn budget sets and clicks an allocation.

Three identification layers:
1. **Revealed-preference test.** Consistency with utility maximization is tested via the Generalized Axiom of Revealed Preference (GARP). By **Afriat's Theorem**, the following are equivalent: (a) the data satisfy GARP; (b) some nonsatiated utility rationalizes them; (c) a *continuous, increasing, concave, nonsatiated* utility rationalizes them. Crucially, (b)$\Leftrightarrow$(c) means linear-budget data can *never* reject well-behavedness — a well-behaved rationalization always exists if any does. Goodness-of-fit is summarized by Afriat's Critical Cost Efficiency Index (CCEI; Varian 1982/1991), bounded in $[0,1]$, the fraction of wasted budget from inconsistent choices. A Bronars (1987) random-chooser benchmark (25,000 simulated uniform subjects) calibrates how far real subjects beat chance.
2. **Structural estimation.** For subjects with high CCEI, preferences are estimated as CES: $$U_s=\big[\alpha\,\pi_s^{\rho}+(1-\alpha)\,\pi_o^{\rho}\big]^{1/\rho},$$ where $\alpha$ is the weight on self and $\rho$ governs curvature, with elasticity of substitution $\sigma=1/(\rho-1)$. The induced CES demand for the giving share is estimated by nonlinear tobit MLE (with normal errors), recovering $(\alpha_n,\rho_n)$ per subject $n$. Interpretation: $\rho>0$ ($\sigma<-1$) = preferences tilted toward *increasing aggregate payoffs* (efficiency); $\rho<0$ ($-1<\sigma<0$) = tilted toward *reducing differences* (equity); $\rho\to1$ perfect-substitutes/utilitarian, $\rho\to-\infty$ Leontief/Rawlsian, $\rho\to0$ Cobb–Douglas.
3. **Three-person & step-shaped elaborations.** With two others $o=\{A,B\}$ and prices $p_o=(p_A,p_B)$, an *independence/separability* assumption lets $U_s=v_s(\pi_s,w_s(\pi_o))$ decompose into a macro function $v_s$ (preferences for giving) and a subutility $w_s(\pi_A,\pi_B)$ (social preferences); both are taken CES and estimated by a two-stage procedure. The **step-shaped set** has a nonconvex, sharply nonlinear frontier with only two socially optimal corners $\pi^s$ (max self) and $\pi^o$ (max other), forcing every interior move to be self- or other-damaging. This "spans" prototypical types — competitive ($u$ rising in $\pi_s-\pi_o$), narrowly selfish, difference-averse, social-welfare, and lexself (lexicographic self-over-other) — that linear budgets cannot separate, providing a sharp test of difference aversion and a way to detect not-well-behaved choices.

## Data
Original lab experiment at UC Berkeley's X-Lab (undergraduates + staff). 199 subjects across three treatments, each making 50 independent decisions: two-person budget sets (IDs 1–76, $n=76$), step-shaped sets (IDs 77–134, $n=58$), and three-person budget sets (IDs 135–199, $n=65$). One round per subject was randomly paid; matching ensured no pair was used twice in both self–other roles. Screens removed perfectly-selfish subjects (avg $p_s\pi_s/m>0.95$) and low-CCEI subjects (below 0.80) before estimation. Aggregate giving (~19% of tokens, ~21% of expenditure two-person; ~26%/~25% three-person) closely matches prior dictator studies (Camerer 2003), but the individual distributions are far smoother and richer.

## Key findings
- **Near-optimization is the norm.** Half the two-person subjects have *zero* GARP violations; CCEI scores cluster near 1 and dominate the Bronars random benchmark, so violations are "minor enough to ignore." A well-behaved rationalizing preference order is confirmed for the large majority.
- **Vast individual heterogeneity.** Estimated CES preferences for giving span the full range from perfect substitutes to Leontief; the self-weight $\alpha_n$ and curvature $\rho_n$ vary dramatically across people — preferences are *not* monolithic.
- **Efficiency leaning ("social-welfare" tilt).** A pronounced (though not universal) majority have $\rho>0$ ($\sigma<-1$), i.e. they sacrifice equality to raise total payoffs rather than the reverse — both in the two- and three-person data.
- **Giving vs. social preferences are conceptually distinct but correlated within person.** In three-person data, $\alpha'\approx1/2$ for almost all subjects (no A-vs-B bias — others treated symmetrically, a consequence of anonymity). Across subjects, ~63.6% have *positive* $\rho$ in **both** preferences for giving and social preferences; the efficiency–equity attitude $\rho$ is strongly associated within subjects, suggesting people apply "unified distributive principles" to self-vs-others and among others alike.
- **Detecting the not-well-behaved minority (step-shaped).** Of 53 cleanly classifiable subjects: ~49% **lexself** (always choose $\pi^s$ — the single most common type), ~13% selfish, ~13% **social welfare** (validated via relative-surplus regressions significant at 1%), ~5.7% **competitive**, and ~17% display a *blend of selfishness and difference aversion* producing self-damaging choices on the inequality-reducing segment $H_2$ that the [[@Charness2002|Charness–Rabin (2002)]] canonical model **cannot** accommodate. These behaviors are invisible on linear budgets (per Afriat) — the step-shaped design is what surfaces them.

## Contribution
Methodologically, the paper introduces the graphical budget-set / point-and-click design as a high-power instrument for collecting enough within-subject data to test rationality and estimate preferences *one individual at a time*, dispensing with population pooling. Substantively it (i) shows distributional choice is overwhelmingly consistent with utility maximization while being deeply heterogeneous; (ii) cleanly separates "preferences for giving" from "social preferences" and documents their within-person link; and (iii) uses Afriat's theorem as both an enabling result and a caution, then designs step-shaped sets precisely to escape its limits and detect not-well-behaved preferences. The graphical-budget methodology seeded a large subsequent literature (e.g. Choi, Fisman, Gale, Kariv on choice under uncertainty).

## Limitations & open questions
- **Linear budgets cannot test well-behavedness.** By Afriat's theorem, consistency with a concave utility is a mechanical consequence of the linear constraint, not evidence about the true utility's shape — this is why the step-shaped experiment is needed, and it remains the binding identification caveat.
- **Competitive preferences are under-identified.** The authors state the step-shaped sets "are not ideally suited to identifying competitive preferences." Their proposed fix — an *upward-sloping* subset $H'$ of the constraint — would, however, confound the selfish-vs-lexself distinction; they "leave this extension for future work."
- **Unclassifiable interior choosers.** Five step-shaped subjects make many strictly interior (both-damaging) allocations with no discernible rule, resembling random allocators; no taxonomy classifies them, and they are dropped.
- **A residual is genuinely anomalous.** The ~17% blend of selfishness + difference aversion, and one subject mixing selfish/difference-averse/social-welfare choices, fall outside canonical distributional models — an open modeling challenge.
- **External validity.** A student/staff convenience sample making small-stakes, fully anonymous, abstract token allocations; whether the recovered $(\alpha,\rho)$ generalize to real charitable giving or policy contexts is left open.

## Connections
The design directly extends **[[@Andreoni2002|Andreoni and Miller (2002)]]** ("Giving According to GARP"), enriching the budget-set menu for higher-power consistency tests. The preference taxonomy and the not-well-behaved cases are framed against **[[@Charness2002|Charness and Rabin (2002]], 2005)**, whose model nests several canonical distributional types as special cases and serves as the benchmark the step-shaped data ultimately strain. The revealed-preference toolkit rests on **Afriat (1967)**, **Varian (1982, 1991)**, **Bronars (1987)**, and **Houtman and Maks (1985)**. The substantive debate over efficiency vs. inequality-aversion engages **[[@Fehr1999|Fehr and Schmidt (1999)]]**, **Bolton (1991)** and **[[@Bolton2000|Bolton and Ockenfels (2000)]]** (difference-aversion / ERC models that posit not-well-behaved utilities), as well as **Engelmann and Strobel (2004)** on efficiency vs. maximin. The graphical methodology is shared with **Choi, Fisman, Gale and Kariv (2007)** on individual behavior under uncertainty, and the broader context is surveyed in **Camerer (2003)**.

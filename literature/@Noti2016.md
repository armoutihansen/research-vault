> [!abstract] Abstract
> A large body of work in behavioral fields attempts to develop models that describe the way people, as opposed to rational agents, make decisions. A recent Choice Prediction Competition (2015) challenged researchers to suggest a model that captures 14 classic choice biases and can predict human decisions under risk and ambiguity. The competition focused on simple decision problems, in which human subjects were asked to repeatedly choose between two gamble options. In this paper we present our approach for predicting human decision behavior: we suggest to use machine learning algorithms with features that are based on well-established behavioral theories. The basic idea is that these psychological features are essential for the representation of the data and are important for the success of the learning process. We implement a vanilla model in which we train SVM models using behavioral features that rely on the psychological properties underlying the competition baseline model. We show that this basic model captures the 14 choice biases and outperforms all the other learning-based models in the competition. The preliminary results suggest that such hybrid models can significantly improve the prediction of human decision making, and are a promising direction for future research.

## Summary
This short paper proposes a *hybrid* approach to predicting human choice under risk and ambiguity: rather than choosing between hand-tuned behavioral models and generic machine learning, it feeds features derived from established behavioral theory (prospect theory valuations, expected-value gaps, entropy/variance differences) into a Support Vector Machine. Entered into the Choice Prediction Competition (CPC) 2015, the model passed all 14 choice-anomaly tests and beat every other pure learning-based entry, while still finishing behind the theory-based baseline model and its variants. The take-away is that learning algorithms alone underperform on small samples of human-decision data, but behaviorally-informed feature engineering substantially closes the gap.

## Research question
Can machine learning predict human decisions under risk and ambiguity across a broad problem space if, instead of using raw problem parameters, one supplies the learner with features computed from well-established behavioral theories? More pointedly: are psychological/behavioral features essential to the success of a learning-based predictor of choice?

## Method / identification
The setting is CPC 2015 (Erev, Ert & Plonsky 2015). The organizers define an 11-dimensional problem space
$$PS = LA \times HA \times pHA \times LB \times HB \times pHB \times LotNum \times LotShape \times Corr \times Amb \times FB,$$
where each problem is a repeated binary choice between gamble $A$ (high outcome $HA$ w.p. $pHA$, else $LA$) and gamble $B$ (a lottery), with ambiguity dimension $Amb\in\{0,1\}$ and within-problem feedback. Subjects face each problem for 25 trials grouped into five blocks; the target is the block-level B-rate (share choosing $B$). Models are scored by mean squared deviation
$$MSD(OBS, B) = 100 \cdot \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j}(OBS_j - B_j)^2,$$
averaged over the five blocks.

The hybrid model has three parts. **(1) Feature representation.** For block $i$, probabilities are first mapped to an "effective" form capturing underweighting of rare events as experience accumulates:
$$\text{effective}(p,i) = \begin{cases} p & p \le 0.02 \\ p + (1-\tfrac{1}{i})(1-p) & p \ge 0.98 \\ p & \text{otherwise.}\end{cases}$$
From the effective problem $p^{eff}_i$, 16 behavior-based features are computed, largely from the baseline model: indicators `IsGain`/`IsLoss`, expected value `EvA`, a best-estimate `BevB` (pessimistic and shrunk toward `EvA` for ambiguous first-round problems), prospect-theory valuations `BevA_pt`/`BevB_pt` using $V(o)=o^{\alpha}$ for gains and $-\lambda|o|^{\beta}$ for losses with weighting $\pi(p)=p^{\gamma}/(p^{\gamma}+(1-p)^{\gamma})^{1/\gamma}$ (parameters $\alpha=0.77,\beta=0.9,\gamma=0.79,\delta=0.87,\lambda=1.0023$), several EV-difference variants (`BevBminA`, uniform, sign, prospect-theory), `BisBetter` (probability $B$ yields a higher outcome), the Stochastic Cumulative Prospect Theory prediction `SCPT`, and variance/entropy differences `VarBminA`, `EntBestMinA` with $entropy(p)=-\sum_i p_i\log p_i$. The final block-$i$ vector concatenates 8 raw parameters, the 16 features, and the $i-1$ previously predicted B-rates; the first 24 entries are standardized against 100,000 randomly generated problems. **(2) Learning.** A separate SVM (polynomial kernel of degree 3, chosen by 10-fold CV over the 90 estimation problems) is trained per block. **(3) Prediction.** B-rates are predicted sequentially $B_1,\dots,B_5$, each fed forward, with two post-hoc adjustments: averaging toward the trivial choice when one option dominates, and pushing $B_1$ toward `SCPT` ($B_1 = 0.7 B_1 + 0.3\,\text{SCPT}$) when the first-block prediction is near-indifferent.

## Data
CPC 2015 experimental data: 90 estimation problems (30 hand-tailored to reproduce the 14 anomalies + 60 randomly generated) with observed block-level B-rates, plus a held-out test set of 60 newly generated problems used for final scoring. Each problem's B-rate is averaged over human subjects facing 25 trials (first 5 without feedback, next 20 with full feedback).

## Key findings
- The hybrid model **passed all 14 anomaly tests**, confirming it reproduces the classic biases (the four prospect-theory biases plus 10 documented with/without feedback, including the experience-description gap on rare events).
- It **outperformed every other learning-based / statistical entry** in the competition: of 25 submissions, ranks 1-13 were all baseline-model variants and the hybrid model placed 14th overall, i.e., best among non-baseline approaches.
- Quantitatively, estimation-set MSD was 0.74 (baseline 0.71; field mean 0.94, sd 0.74); test-set MSD was 1.24 (baseline 0.98; field mean 1.47, sd 0.89).
- Interpretation: pure learning methods are insufficient for this small-sample human-choice prediction problem, but behaviorally-grounded feature engineering and adjustments make a learner competitive.

## Contribution
Demonstrates, in a controlled prediction competition, that combining behavioral-theory-derived features with off-the-shelf machine learning (SVM) yields markedly better human-choice prediction than learning on raw parameters alone. It is an early, concrete proof-of-concept that "feature engineering from psychology" is the lever, positioning hybrid behavioral-ML models as a research direction later expanded by Plonsky, Erev and co-authors.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit that this is a "vanilla" model still beaten by the baseline and its variants, and that the integration "should be further studied and improved." Their stated open problems: (i) exploit the full SVM hyperparameter range and try alternative learning algorithms; (ii) train on a much larger dataset, e.g., via large-scale online experiments on Amazon Mechanical Turk; (iii) more generally, deepen the study of hybrid behavioral-ML models for human decision prediction. Implicit limitations include the tiny training set (90 problems), reliance on hand-crafted adjustments, and scope restricted to the CPC problem space of binary gambles.

## Connections
The work sits directly atop the Choice Prediction Competition framework of Erev, Ert & Plonsky (2015) and uses its baseline model and Stochastic Cumulative Prospect Theory from Erev, Roth, Slonim & Barron (2002). Its theoretical features draw on [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky (1979)]] prospect theory and the description-experience gap of Hertwig & Erev (2009); the SVM tool is Cortes & Vapnik (1995). The hybrid feature-engineering theme connects to subsequent psychological-forest and theory-augmented ML work by Plonsky, Erev and colleagues, and more broadly to the behavioral-economics-meets-machine-learning prediction literature exemplified by Camerer and by Fudenberg and Liang on predicting and benchmarking human choice with learned models.

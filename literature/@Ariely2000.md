> [!abstract] Abstract
> (no abstract in Zotero) — The published abstract reads: Many individual decisions take place in a group context wherein group members voice their choices sequentially. This article examines the impact of this dynamic decision process on individuals' choices and satisfaction with their outcomes. The authors propose that choices reflect a balancing of two classes of goals: goals that are strictly individual and goals that are triggered by the existence of the group. The latter sometimes results in choices that undermine personal satisfaction and increase regret. Support for goal balancing is found in three studies tracking consumers' orders of dishes and drinks (Lunch, Beer, Wine).

## Summary
When people order food and drinks aloud and in sequence at a table, they systematically pick options others have not yet chosen — "taking the road less traveled." Ariely and Levav frame this as goal balancing: each diner trades off an individual-alone goal (satisfy own taste) against individual-group goals (minimize regret, gather information, present a unique self). Across three field/lab studies the group context reliably raises group-level variety, and crucially this variety comes at the cost of personal satisfaction and increased regret — most sharply for those who order after the first person. Information-gathering and need-for-uniqueness motives, not informational social influence or conformity, drive the effect.

## Research question
Do individuals' choices differ when made sequentially in a group context relative to choices made alone or independently, and if so, what goals are being traded off and at what hedonic cost? The motivating anecdote: a diner switches from pancakes to French toast simply because another party member ordered pancakes first.

## Method / identification
Three studies, each using a common dependent variable, the Variety Index $V$ for a table, defined as
$$V = \frac{(\text{number of different options chosen within a table}) - 1}{(\text{table size}) - 1}.$$
By construction $V=0$ when all diners pick the identical dish and $V=1$ when all pick different dishes.

- Study 1 (Lunch, field, one-cell): 814 lunch order slips (2,202 diners) from a Chinese restaurant, individually portioned (no sharing). Because dining-alone customers risk a selection confound, the benchmark is a Monte Carlo of nominal groups: the 2,202 individual choices are randomly reassigned to tables preserving the original table-size distribution, repeated 100 times, and the real-table $V$ is compared to this simulated reference distribution.

- Study 2 (Beer, lab-in-the-field experiment): 100 tables (mean size 2.7) approached by the authors posing as waiters offering free 4-oz samples of four house beers. Tables randomized between a Collective condition (order aloud one-by-one in a randomly predetermined sequence) and an Independent condition (mark choice privately, "special rule," no consulting). Post-consumption survey measures liking (0–10), regret (yes/no), and (irrelevant) planned visits. The randomized speaking order enables the "sequential-order" test: the first speaker faces no group contingency, later speakers do.

- Study 3 (Wine, lab experiment): 45 groups of Duke business students (mean size 4.3), randomized to Information vs. No-Information. Both first sample four wines, then choose one for a full glass. In the Information condition the second-phase wines are the already-tasted set (no new information to acquire); in No-Information the second phase uses a fresh, counterbalanced set (learning/sharing still possible). Measures: $V$ (vs. nominal-group simulation), liking, regret, wine knowledge, and the Snyder–Fromkin Need-for-Uniqueness (NFU) scale. Because $V$ is group-level while NFU is individual, the authors build an individual Sequential Variety dummy (1 if a subject picks a not-yet-chosen option, 0 otherwise; first speaker dropped) and run logistic regressions of it on NFU, sequence position, and condition.

## Data
Field and experimental consumer-choice records: 814 restaurant order slips / 2,202 diners (Lunch); 100 tables of beer drinkers (Beer); 45 student groups tasting wine (Wine). All choices are voiced sequentially and consumed individually (no sharing), isolating the group contingency from physical sharing.

## Key findings
- Group-level variety seeking. Lunch: real-table $V=.952$ exceeded every one of the 100 simulated nominal-group sets (simulated $M=.925$; $t=4.46$, $p<.001$); the gap held within four of five table sizes. Beer: Collective $V=.843$ vs. Independent $V=.46$ ($t_{98}=5.69$, $p<.001$). Wine: real $M=.658$ vs. simulated $M=.554$ ($t_{44}=5.38$, $p<.001$).
- Variety is hedonically costly. Beer: satisfaction higher in Independent ($M=7.75$) than Collective ($M=6.80$), $t_{98}=3.12$, $p<.005$; regret 38.4% vs. 12.4%. This rules out personal taste satisfaction as the driver, since collective choosers were less satisfied.
- Sequential-order effect. Beer: the first speaker (effectively unconstrained) was as satisfied ($M=7.26$) as Independent choosers ($M=7.75$, n.s.), whereas later speakers were far less satisfied ($M=6.37$ vs. $7.75$, $t_{49}=4.15$, $p<.001$); first-speaker regret 24% vs. 48% for later members. Wine replicates: first-person satisfaction $6.69$ vs. $5.6$ for the rest ($p<.01$). This also rules out the positive-affect / inclusive-categorization (Kahn & Isen) alternative.
- Mechanism. Information matters: Wine $V$ higher in No-Information ($M=.734$) than Information ($M=.645$), $t_{43}=1.89$, $p=.066$ (directional). Uniqueness matters: group-mean NFU positively predicts $V$ (full sample $\beta=.396$, $R^2=.22$, $p<.005$). At the individual level, the Sequential Variety logit shows NFU significant ($\chi^2=4.67$, $p=.03$) and sequence position significant ($\chi^2=12.56$, $p<.001$; variety falls as more options are taken), while the Information manipulation is not ($\chi^2=.49$, $p=.82$) — hinting NFU has the stronger pull and a possible compensatory relation between uniqueness and information motives.

## Contribution
Introduces the "group contingency" as a distinct source of preference construction: unlike task framing or individual traits, it is an interpersonal property of the social context, arising purely from sequential public choice with no confederates or manipulated norms. It documents that ordinary sequential group choice produces a robust, replicable variety-seeking pattern that lowers individual welfare, and it decomposes the driver into information-gathering and uniqueness-signaling rather than conformity or informational social influence. The goal-balancing framework (four goal types across two classes) yields directional predictions for uniformity vs. variety and a clean welfare prediction tied to sequence position.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems and boundary conditions, each a research hook:
- Entitativity (Campbell 1958; Hamilton & Sherman 1996): does a stronger sense of the group as a single entity raise group-level variety, and will low entitativity make individuals ignore others' choices?
- Time discounting / preference misprediction: the studies impose a lag between ordering and consumption; group-dependent goals are immediate while taste payoff is delayed. Future work could model the influence of others across decision → consumption → post-consumption, predicting a weaker group effect when there is no ordering-consumption lag.
- Table size as a boundary: all tables were small/medium; larger tables may split into subgroups, or variety there may be mere natural heterogeneity rather than active seeking.
- Preference ambiguity / product familiarity: information gathering should dominate as uncertainty rises, so familiarity may govern which choice pattern emerges.
- Domain of uniqueness: variety seeking may not generalize to all attributes — diners avoid extreme disparities in price or dish type, so variety in one dimension is coupled with uniformity in another; the observed effect may even be a variety-seeking norm whose antecedents are information and self-presentation.
- Prescriptive tension: "always order first" maximizes taste utility but pits taste satisfaction against the need to be polite — left for future research.

## Connections
The paper bridges social-influence research (Asch 1955; Deutsch & Gerard's 1955 normative vs. informational influence; Kaplan & Miller 1987) with the consumer variety-seeking literature (McAlister 1982 attribute-satiation; McAlister & Pessemier 1982; Simonson 1990 on purchase quantity/timing; Kahn 1995). The welfare-reducing variety motive connects to Ratner, Kahn & Kahneman (1999) "Choosing Less-Preferred Experiences for the Sake of Variety" and to naive diversification / diversification bias (Read & Loewenstein 1995; Thaler 1998). Loss aversion and regret derive from Kahneman & Tversky (1979); the construction-of-preference backdrop is Slovic (1995) and Payne, Bettman & Johnson (1993). The individual/group-goal scaffold is Mackie & Goethals (1987), here made dynamic. The uniqueness mechanism rests on Snyder & Fromkin (1977, 1980) and Hsee (1999). Within social-preference and sequential-choice work the result is notable for showing endogenous, welfare-reducing differentiation that arises without any imposed payoff externality — purely from the act of choosing in observed sequence.

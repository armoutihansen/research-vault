> [!abstract] Abstract
> Hierarchical regressions using the responses of 138 subordinates about 55 managers show that, as predicted, Bass' (1985b) transformational leader model adds to initiation and consideration in explaining the variance of subordinates' satisfaction and ratings of leader effectiveness. Further analysis, using leader ratings by one subordinate and outcome ratings by a second subordinate, largely confirms this augmentation effect. There was disconfirmation for the effect on reported extra effort, suggesting that the relation of transformational leadership to subordinate's extra effort is a dyadic rather than a group effect.

## Summary
Seltzer and Bass test whether the three transformational-leadership dimensions (charisma, individualized consideration, intellectual stimulation) explain subordinate outcomes *beyond* the two classic leadership behaviors — initiation of structure and consideration — that have dominated leadership measurement since the 1960s. Using subordinate ratings of MBA-student managers, they find that transformational scales add 8–28% to the variance explained in leader effectiveness, subordinate satisfaction, and extra effort. A conservative "criss-cross" design, in which one subordinate rates the leader and a *different* subordinate rates outcomes, confirms the augmentation for satisfaction and effectiveness but not for extra effort — implying that effort is driven at the dyadic (leader–individual) level rather than the group level.

## Research question
Does transformational leadership (charisma, individualized consideration, intellectual stimulation) add explanatory power to the traditional initiation-and-consideration model in accounting for subordinates' ratings of leader effectiveness, their satisfaction, and their reported extra effort? This extends Bass's (1985a) "augmentation hypothesis" — previously tested against transactional leadership — to the older Ohio State initiation/consideration framework.

## Method / identification
The core design is a set of **hierarchical (sequential) regressions** (Cohen & Cohen, 1975), run separately for each of three outcome measures. Step 1 enters initiation and consideration; Step 2 adds the three transformational scales. The test of interest is the incremental $\Delta R^2$ at Step 2 and its $F$-test — i.e., whether transformational scales explain variance over and above the LBDQ behaviors. The sequential ordering is justified by the prior theoretical claim that transformational leadership *builds on* (augments) the more basic behaviors.

To rule out **single-source / common-method variance** — the worry that correlations are inflated because the same subordinate rates both the leader's behavior and the outcomes — the authors deploy a **criss-cross design** (Bass, Avolio & Yammarino, 1988). For each manager with multiple responding subordinates, the leadership scores from subordinate S1 are matched with the *outcome* scores from subordinate S2 (and cycled across three subordinates). This isolates leader-*group* effects (variance shared across subordinates) and provides a conservative estimate that strips out leader–individual idiosyncrasy. They also run a reverse-order regression (transformational first, then LBDQ) to confirm augmentation is directional, and partial correlations controlling for charisma and individualized consideration to probe intellectual stimulation.

Measures: transformational behavior via three 10-item MLQ Form 5 scales (charisma, individualized consideration, intellectual stimulation); initiation (10 items) and consideration (10 items) from LBDQ Form 12 (Stogdill, 1963); leader effectiveness (4 items), extra effort (3 items), and satisfaction (2 items) from Bass et al. (1975). Scale alphas are reported as satisfactory.

## Data
Survey field data. 98 full-time managers who were also part-time advanced-MBA students each distributed MLQ Form 5 to up to three subordinates, who returned questionnaires anonymously. After eliminating managers with no/one subordinate, the usable sample is **138 subordinate questionnaires describing 55 managers** (27 managers with two responding subordinates, 28 with three); the response rate was 55% of 250 distributed. The criss-cross analyses use $n=55$ managers.

## Key findings
- **Augmentation confirmed (full sample, $n=138$).** With initiation and consideration entered first, they explain 22–50% of outcome variance; the transformational scales add a significant $\Delta R^2$ of **.12 for effectiveness, .02 (8%) for extra effort, and .08 for satisfaction** — F-tests significant at $p<.001$. Charisma is positively related to all three outcomes; intellectual stimulation is positively related to extra effort.
- **Reverse-order check.** Entering transformational scales first and LBDQ second, initiation/consideration add little or nothing (e.g., $.00$ n.s. for effort), so augmentation runs in the predicted direction and "the reverse was not found."
- **Criss-cross design ($n=55$).** Augmentation survives the conservative design for **effectiveness ($\Delta R^2=.06$) and satisfaction ($\Delta R^2=.08$)** but **not for extra effort ($\Delta R^2=.00$, n.s.)** — extra effort appears to be a leader–individual (dyadic) effect, not a leader–group effect.
- **Level-of-analysis split.** Charisma and initiation operate primarily as *leader–individual* effects (correlations collapse under criss-cross; interrater reliability of charisma items is lower than for the full set, $Z=4.78$, $p<.01$), whereas consideration, individualized consideration, and intellectual stimulation retain *leader–group* effects.
- **Intellectual stimulation paradox.** Controlling for charisma and individualized consideration, intellectual stimulation shows an *inverse* partial correlation with effectiveness ($-.09$) and satisfaction ($-.26$, $p<.05$). The authors attribute this to a second component of intellectual stimulation (pressing subordinates to use reasoning/evidence rather than unsupported opinion; Seltzer, Numerof & Bass, 1989) that can reduce satisfaction.

## Contribution
Demonstrates empirically that transformational leadership augments not only *transactional* leadership (the prior literature) but also the long-dominant **initiation/consideration** behaviors — closing a gap the authors note had never been tested. Methodologically, the criss-cross design separates leader–individual from leader–group effects, foreshadowing the multilevel turn in leadership research (Yammarino & Bass). It pushes leadership measurement "beyond initiation and consideration," supporting the case that the MLQ captures variance those scales miss.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit:
- **Common-method/perceptual bias** is mitigated but not eliminated even by criss-cross, since subjects still rate both behaviors and outcomes on the same instrument.
- **Range restriction**: managers self-selected which (up to three) subordinates received the instrument.
- **Social-desirability / honesty**: despite anonymity, subordinates may have withheld honest, especially negative, responses; need for "hard-data" outcomes.
- **Modest variance** explained in the criss-cross design.
- **Open puzzle — extra effort at the group level**: effort showed leader–individual but not leader–group effects; "future research might more directly investigate this finding and its relationship to group productivity."
- **Charisma's level of operation**: charisma operating at the leader–individual rather than leader–group level "contradicts the more general view that charisma draws the attention and support of many followers" — flagged as a "fertile topic for future study."
- The **intellectual-stimulation two-component structure** (positive vs. satisfaction-reducing) warrants further work.

## Connections
This paper sits at the center of Bass's transformational/transactional research program. It directly extends **Bass (1985a, 1985b)** *Leadership and Performance Beyond Expectations* and the augmentation hypothesis of **Waldman & Bass (1985)**, applying it to the **Ohio State initiation/consideration** tradition (Hemphill 1960; Stogdill 1963 LBDQ). The transformational/transactional distinction originates with **Burns (1978)**, whom the authors revise from a bipolar continuum to Bass's independent dimensions. The **MLQ** instrument (Bass & Avolio, 1989) and its empirical track record across naval officers (Yammarino & Bass 1990; Hater & Bass 1988), clergy (Onnen 1987), and simulations (Avolio, Waldman & Einstein 1988) supply the validating evidence. The criss-cross / levels-of-analysis methodology connects to **Bass, Avolio & Yammarino (1988)** and **Yammarino & Bass (in press)**. The intellectual-stimulation paradox links to the authors' companion burnout study (**Seltzer, Numerof & Bass, 1989**).

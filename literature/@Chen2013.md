> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper develops a model of reference-dependent assessment of subjective beliefs in which loss-averse people optimally choose the expectation as the reference point to balance the current felicity from the optimistic anticipation and the future disappointment from the realisation. The choice of over-optimism or over-pessimism depends on the real chance of success and optimistic decision makers prefer receiving early information. In the portfolio choice problem, pessimistic investors tend to trade conservatively, however, they might trade aggressively if they are sophisticated enough to recognise the biases since low expectation can reduce their fear of loss.

## Summary
Chen builds an "optimal beliefs" model that endogenizes whether a decision maker is over-optimistic or over-pessimistic. Agents pick subjective probabilities to maximize the sum of anticipatory utility (felicity from a rosy outlook) and a future, reference-dependent gain-loss utility, where the self-chosen expectation is the Köszegi-Rabin reference point. Because loss aversion makes disappointment hurt more than optimism pleases, the model can generate over-pessimism rather than the uniform over-optimism of Brunnermeier-Parker. The sign of the bias hinges on whether the true probability of beating one's expectation exceeds a cutoff determined by loss aversion $\lambda$ and the future-utility weight $\eta$. Applications cover information-timing preferences, risk attitudes (naive vs. sophisticated agents), and portfolio choice/asset pricing.

## Research question
When agents can manipulate their own subjective beliefs but are also loss averse over realized outcomes relative to those beliefs, when do they choose to be over-optimistic versus over-pessimistic, and what does this imply for information acquisition, risk-taking, and asset prices?

## Method / identification
A two-period, single-agent, multi-state decision-theoretic model. States $S=\{1,\dots,S\}$ ($S\ge 2$) carry ordered payoffs $0\le Z_1\le\dots\le Z_S$ with objective probabilities $p_s$. Following Köszegi & [[@Koszegi2009|Rabin (2009)]], utility is separable into reference-independent consumption utility $u(Z_s)$ (with $u'>0$, $u''\le 0$, $u(0)=0$, abbreviated $u_s$) and a "universal gain-loss utility" $\mu(x)=x$ for $x\ge 0$ and $\mu(x)=\lambda x$ ($\lambda>1$) for $x<0$. The agent has no control over the outcome and chooses only her subjective beliefs $\{q_s\}$, which are fixed across both periods. Anticipatory utility is reference-independent and equals the subjective expectation $\sum_s q_s u_s$; the prospective gain-loss term compares realized $u_s$ to that anticipation. The agent solves

$$\max_{\{q_s\}}\ U=\sum_{s\in S} q_s u_s + \eta\Big\{\sum_{s\in S} p_s\,\mu\Big(u_s-\sum_{s\in S} q_s u_s\Big)\Big\}\quad\text{s.t.}\ \sum_{s\in S}q_s=1,$$

with $0<\eta\le 1$ the weight on gain-loss utility (interpretable as a future discount factor; the upper bound enforces a revealed-preference monotonicity requirement). The key object is the cutoff probability

$$P^*=\frac{\eta\lambda-1}{\eta(\lambda-1)},$$

derived from the first-order condition $\partial U/\partial q_k = u_k\{1-\eta P_+ -\eta\lambda(1-P_+)\}$, where $P_+=\sum_{s\in A}p_s$ and $A=\{s\in S: u_s-\sum_s q_s u_s\ge 0\}$ is the "gain" region. Appendix B relaxes linearity of $\mu$ to $\mu'(-x)=\lambda(x)\mu'(x)$ with $\lambda(x)>1$, $\lambda'(x)\le 0$, recovering the same qualitative results. Applications: an information-timing comparison ($U_{\text{early}}$ vs. $U_{\text{wait}}$) following Köszegi-[[@Koszegi2009|Rabin (2009)]], and a portfolio problem $\max_\alpha \int u(R_f+\alpha R)\,dF(R)$ distinguishing naive agents (who act on biased beliefs) from sophisticated agents (who recognize the bias).

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 (Biased beliefs preferred to rational):** if $\lambda\ge 1/\eta$, then for some state $q_s\neq p_s$ with $U_{BS}\ge U_{RE}$ — an agent caring about both anticipation and prospective gain-loss never holds rational beliefs.
- **Proposition 2 (Belief tradeoff across states):** shifting a small mass $\varepsilon$ toward higher (lower) states raises utility when $P_+>P^*$ ($P_+<P^*$); utility is increasing in $q_k$ iff $P_+>P^*$.
- **Proposition 3 (Optimism vs. pessimism):** the agent is on average over-optimistic ($\sum q_s u_s>\sum p_s u_s$) iff $P_+^0>P^*$ and over-pessimistic iff $P_+^0<P^*$. The cutoff $P^*$ is increasing in both $\lambda$ and $\eta$ — more loss-averse / more future-oriented agents are less likely to be over-optimistic. Optimal beliefs are not unique: any $\{q_s\}$ delivering the required $\sum q_s u_s$ achieve the same utility.
- **Proposition 4 (Information timing):** early information is strictly preferred iff $P_+^0>P^*$ (optimists want early news); pessimists ($P_+<P^*$) prefer to wait, while rational agents are indifferent.
- **Propositions 5-6:** between two lotteries, a naive agent strictly prefers the one she is optimistic about; this drives preferences for skewness/spreading.
- **Propositions 7-9 (portfolio/risk):** naive optimists trade aggressively ($\alpha_{OP}>\alpha_{RE}$) and naive pessimists conservatively. Sophisticated agents behave near-rationally but overweight low-ranking outcomes; a sophisticated pessimist can bear *more* risk than an optimist because low anticipation numbs the fear of loss. In equilibrium with short-sale constraints, the risky asset's price falls with pessimism in a naive market but is U-shaped in a sophisticated market (moderate confidence gives the highest equity premium).

## Contribution
The paper extends the Brunnermeier-Parker optimal-expectations program by adding Köszegi-Rabin reference dependence and loss aversion. Whereas earlier optimal-belief models (BP 2005; [[@Brunnermeier2007|Brunnermeier-Gollier-Parker 2007]]; Bernheim-Thomadsen 2005) generate only over-optimism, embedding loss aversion supplies a first-order counterforce so that over-pessimism emerges endogenously, with the optimism/pessimism switch governed by a single cutoff in $(\lambda,\eta)$. Beliefs are derived by inverting the utility-maximization problem rather than posited via an ad hoc attitude parameter (contrasting with Mayraz 2011a). It also lets agents choose beliefs directly (not just actions), so they exhibit nontrivial information-source preferences that BP's action-centric model cannot.

## Limitations & open questions
The author flags these explicitly:
- **IIA violation (Spiegler 2008):** because beliefs are payoff-dependent, the model fails the revealed-preference IIA test; a fix would require choice-set-dependent preferences treating the same outcome in different bundles as distinct.
- **Non-uniqueness:** the optimal belief set $\{q_s\}$ is not unique.
- **Curse of knowledge:** the model cannot explain why well-informed agents with high success chances might turn pessimistic; a possible remedy is reference-dependent (rather than reference-independent) anticipation, valid when agents have fresh recent experience.
- **Information under uncertainty:** modeling preferences over noisy/uncertain information is open, but Bayes' rule "can hardly hold" when beliefs are utility-serving, so a new utility-consistent updating rule is needed.
- **Incomplete equilibrium:** the asset-pricing equilibrium is only sketched under homogeneous beliefs; a general equilibrium with heterogeneous traders is left for future work.

## Connections
The model's two pillars are Köszegi & [[@Koszegi2006b|Rabin]] (2006, 2009) reference-dependent preferences and the optimal-expectations framework of [[@Brunnermeier2005|Brunnermeier & Parker (2005)]] and [[@Brunnermeier2007|Brunnermeier, Gollier & Parker (2007)]]; it directly contrasts its pessimism result with their uniform optimism, as well as with Bernheim & Thomadsen (2005). The gain-loss formulation rests on [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky (1979)]] prospect theory and [[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Kahneman (1992)]] cumulative prospect theory, and the asset-pricing application connects to Barberis & Huang (2008) on skewness and probability weighting. Anticipatory-utility roots trace to [[@Loewenstein1987|Loewenstein (1987)]] and [[@Caplin2001|Caplin & Leahy (2001)]], with self-signalling and cognitive-dissonance variants in Bénabou & Tirole (2002, 2004), Rabin (1994), and Epstein & Kopylov (2006). Mayraz (2011a, 2011b) provides both the closest axiomatic alternative ("priors and desires") and experimental evidence that biases are independent of material accuracy costs; Macera (2011) studies the dynamic time-path of optimism. Methodological critiques are drawn from Spiegler (2008) and Eliaz & Spiegler (2006), and the rational-expectations benchmark being departed from is Muth (1961).

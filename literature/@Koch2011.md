> [!abstract] Abstract
> Goals are an important motivator. But little is known about why and how people set them. We address this issue in a model based on two stylized facts. First, goals serve as reference points for performance. Second, present-biased preferences create self-control problems. We show the power and limits of self-regulation through goals. Goals increase an individual's motivation—but only up to a certain point. Furthermore, they are painful self-disciplining devices. Thus, greater self-control problems may result in tougher goals; but for a severe present bias goals either lack a motivating force, or are too painful to be accepted.

## Summary
Koch and Nafziger build a theory of *endogenous* goal setting: a sophisticated, present-biased individual sets an internal goal that acts as a loss-averse reference point for a future task outcome. A higher goal raises the psychological pain of falling short, which disciplines the future "self 1" into working hard. But goals have two limits — their motivating force is bounded, and they are intrinsically painful — so the optimal goal is *non-monotonic* in the severity of the present bias, and sufficiently biased people rationally abstain from goal setting altogether.

## Research question
Why and how do people set goals for themselves? Psychology treats goals as exogenously assigned and studies how given goals affect performance; the paper instead asks how a self-aware agent with a self-control problem *chooses* a goal to regulate his own future behavior, and where the limits of such self-regulation lie.

## Method / identification
A three-date theoretical model with $(\beta,\delta)$ quasi-hyperbolic preferences ($\delta=1$, present-bias parameter $\beta\in[0,1)$), solved by backward induction across two intrapersonal selves.

- **Task:** At date 1 the individual picks effort $e\in\{\underline{e},\bar{e}\}$; high effort costs $c$ immediately, low effort is costless. At date 2 a stochastic outcome $y\in[\underline{y},\bar{y}]$ realizes, with $F(y\mid\bar e)<F(y\mid\underline e)$ (high effort first-order stochastically dominates, Assumption 1).
- **Goal as reference point:** At date 0 the agent fixes a goal $a\ge\underline{y}$ that anchors as a reference standard. Date-2 utility is consumption utility plus a Kahneman–Tversky gain–loss value function with *linear loss aversion*:
$$u_2(y,a)=y+\eta\big(\max\{y-a,0\}-\mu\cdot\max\{a-y,0\}\big),$$
with loss-aversion coefficient $\mu>1$ and gain–loss weight $\eta>0$. The expected date-2 value is
$$g(e,a)=(1+\eta)\,E(y\mid e)-\eta a-\eta(\mu-1)\int_{\underline{y}}^{a}F(y\mid e)\,dy.$$
- **Self-control problem:** Self 0 and self 1 evaluate $U_0=\beta\{-c(e)+g(e,a)\}$ and $U_1=-c(e)+\beta g(e,a)$. Assumption 2 imposes a genuine conflict — without a goal ($a=\underline{y}$), self 0 strictly prefers high effort but self 1 shirks, i.e. $\beta<\beta^{SC}\equiv c/\big(g(\bar e,\underline{y})-g(\underline e,\underline{y})\big)$.
- The model is deliberately stripped down (linear consumption utility, sophistication, no external commitment) to isolate the internal reference-point channel.

## Data
None — this is a pure theory paper. It motivates and disciplines its assumptions with stylized facts from the psychology of goal setting (Locke and Latham) and behavioral economics, but estimates nothing.

## Key findings
- **Lemma 1 (motivation and its bound):** A higher goal raises self 1's incentive to exert effort, $\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\big(\beta\{g(\bar e,a)-g(\underline e,a)\}-c\big)>0$ on $(\underline{y},\bar{y})$ — difficult goals motivate more than easy ones. But the motivating force is *bounded*: $\lim_{a\to\infty}\{g(\bar e,a)-g(\underline e,a)\}=g(\bar e,\bar{y})-g(\underline e,\bar{y})<\infty$, because unattainable goals add no incentive.
- **Lemma 2 (feasibility and toughness):** Self-regulation is feasible iff $\beta\ge\beta^{IC}\equiv c/\big(g(\bar e,\bar{y})-g(\underline e,\bar{y})\big)$, with $\beta^{IC}<\beta^{SC}$. The motivating goal $\hat a$ solves $\beta(g(\bar e,\hat a)-g(\underline e,\hat a))=c$, and a more severe bias requires a tougher goal: $\frac{\partial \hat a}{\partial\beta}<0$. For $\beta<\beta^{IC}$ no goal can solve the problem.
- **Lemma 3 (goals are painful):** Holding effort fixed, self 0's expected utility is *decreasing* in the goal level, $\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\beta\{g(e,a)-c(e)\}<0$, because larger goals raise the loss-weighted chance of falling short.
- **Proposition 1 (acceptance and non-monotonicity):** If the instrumental-utility gain is large ($g(\bar e,\bar{y})-c\ge g(\underline e,\underline{y})$), feasibility alone governs: self 0 sets a painful goal whenever $\beta\ge\beta^{IC}$. If the gain is small, there is a cutoff $\beta^{IC}<\beta^0<\beta^{SC}$ such that goals are *accepted* only for $\beta\in[\beta^0,\beta^{SC})$; for $\beta\in[\beta^{IC},\beta^0)$ self-regulation is feasible but the required goal is too painful, so the agent rationally sets no goal. Hence the optimal goal is non-monotone in the present bias, and severe present-bias agents behave like agents with no self-control problem (no goal) — but shirk rather than work.
- **Implication:** Adding instrumental incentives (monetary stakes, public commitment, reputation — e.g. WeightWatchers, StickK.com commitment contracts) enhances goal *acceptance* by raising the gain term, explaining why such external props complement internal goals.

## Contribution
Provides one of the first economic models of *self-chosen* goals as continuously adjustable, loss-averse reference points, deriving both the power (motivation through fear of loss) and the limits (bounded force; intrinsic pain) of self-regulation. It connects the psychology of goal setting to behavioral economics of present bias, and rationalizes the empirical finding that goal acceptance moderates the goal-difficulty/performance relationship.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Single task only:** the model treats one task; the authors flag multi-task extensions to study *goal bracketing* (their companion Koch and Nafziger 2009; Hsiaw 2010) as the natural next step.
- **Sophistication assumed:** the agent fully anticipates his bias; naïve or partially-naïve agents are not analyzed.
- **Goal commitment taken as primitive:** the capacity to set self-binding goals that "remain meaningful over time" is assumed (grounded in Gollwitzer 1999), not micro-founded — how/why internal goals stay credible is left open.
- **Linear loss aversion and linear consumption utility** are adopted for tractability; curvature could alter the comparative statics on $\hat a$.
- The interaction between internal goals and *external* commitment technologies, and the empirical separation of the motivation bound ($\beta^{IC}$) from the acceptance bound ($\beta^0$), are left for future work.

## Connections
The reference-point machinery follows Kahneman and Tversky's (1979) Prospect Theory value function and the expectations-based reference-dependence of Köszegi and [[@Koszegi2006b|Rabin (2006)]]; the linear loss-aversion specification is Tversky and Kahneman (1991). Present bias is modeled à la Strotz (1955), Phelps and Pollak (1968), Laibson (1997), and O'Donoghue and Rabin (1999a). The paper situates itself against parallel goals-as-self-fulfilling-rational-expectations models by Suvorov and van de Ven (2008) and Hsiaw (2009), contrasting its continuously chosen goals with their equilibrium-restricted reference points. It builds on the self-control and commitment literature surveyed by Brocas et al. (2004) — including Diamond and Köszegi (2003), DellaVigna and Malmendier (2004), Carrillo (2005), and especially Carrillo and Dewatripont (2008) on promises — and on intrapersonal-control models of Bénabou and Tirole (2004), Benhabib and Bisin (2005), and Herweg and Müller (2010). Empirically it connects to Heath, Larrick, and Wu (1999), Fehr and Götte (2007), Abeler et al. (2010), and Gächter, Johnson, and Herrmann (2007) on measured loss aversion, and to Falk and Knell (2004) on social-comparison motives for goal choice, alongside the psychology surveys of Locke and Latham (1990, 2002).

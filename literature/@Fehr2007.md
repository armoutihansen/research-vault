> [!abstract] Abstract
> We show experimentally that fairness concerns may have a decisive impact on the actual and optimal choice of contracts in a moral hazard context. Bonus contracts that offer a voluntary and unenforceable bonus for satisfactory performance provide powerful incentives and are superior to explicit incentive contracts when there are some fair-minded players, but trust contracts that pay a generous wage up front are less efficient than incentive contracts. The principals understand this and predominantly choose the bonus contracts. These results are consistent with recently developed theories of fairness, which offer important new insights into the interaction of contract choices, fairness, and incentives.

## Summary
In a one-shot moral-hazard principal–agent setting, principals choose among an explicit incentive contract (verifiable fine for shirking), a trust contract (generous up-front wage), and an implicit bonus contract (low base wage plus a non-binding promised bonus). Standard self-interest theory predicts the incentive contract dominates both alternatives, which are "doomed to fail" as cheap talk. Experimentally, the bonus contract is vastly superior to the incentive contract and is chosen almost universally, whereas the trust contract performs poorly and is abandoned. A two-type inequity-aversion model ([[@Fehr1999|Fehr–Schmidt 1999]]) rationalizes the full pattern: the party with the lower cost of trusting should trust first, which is why the bonus contract (agent trusts by exerting effort) beats the trust contract (principal trusts by paying a high wage).

## Research question
How do concerns for fairness affect the actual and the optimal choice of contracts when effort is subject to moral hazard? Specifically, when principals can freely choose the contract form, which contract prevails, and can a tractable fairness model explain why implicit bonus contracts dominate both explicit incentive contracts and trust contracts?

## Method / identification
A laboratory gift-exchange / contract-choice experiment combined with a game-theoretic inequity-aversion model. The principal–agent stage game: effort $e\in\{1,\dots,10\}$ produces gross profit $v(e)=10e$ at private cost $c(e)$ (convex, $c''>0$); first-best is $e=10$ with surplus $v(e)-c(e)=80$. Three contract technologies:
- Incentive Contract (IC): principal pays verification cost $k=10$; with probability $p=1/3$ courts verify $e<e^{*}$ and a fine $f\le\bar f=13$ is imposed. The highest incentive-compatible effort satisfies the no-shirking condition $pf\ge c(e^{*})$, giving $\hat e=4$. Payoffs $M^{A}=w-c(e)-pf\mathbf{1}[e<e^{*}]$, $M^{P}=v(e)-w-k+pf\mathbf{1}[e<e^{*}]$.
- Trust Contract (TC): unconditional wage $w$, requested $e^{*}$, no enforcement; $M^{A}=w-c(e)$, $M^{P}=v(e)-w$.
- Bonus Contract (BC): base wage $w$, requested $e^{*}$, announced non-binding bonus $b^{*}$; principal chooses actual $b\ge0$ after observing $e$. $M^{A}=w-c(e)+b$, $M^{P}=v(e)-w-b$.

Design: 6 sessions, 20–24 subjects each, random anonymous re-matching over 10 periods (no two interactions with same partner), no reputation/observation of others, ensuring within-subject behavior across contracts and ruling out self-selection of preference types. Treatments: Trust–Incentive (TI, sessions S1–S2), Bonus–Incentive (BI, S3–S4), and control sessions S5–S6 (framing checks). Identification of fairness effects comes from randomization (controls self-selection) plus the known cost function and one-shot structure.

The theory uses the two-player inequity-aversion utility
$$U_i(x)=x_i-\alpha_i\max\{x_j-x_i,0\}-\beta_i\max\{x_i-x_j,0\},$$
with a simplified population of 60% selfish types ($\alpha=\beta=0$) and 40% fair types ($\alpha=2$, $\beta=0.6$, so $q=0.4$). The model is solved as a (signaling) game; the BC induces a signaling game because the principal moves twice.

## Data
Experimental data only. 195 contract offers in the TI treatment (183 effort choices after rejections) and 230 offers in the BI treatment (224 accepted). Subjects were students at the University of Munich and TU Munich; token payoffs converted at 1 token = €0.1; average earnings ≈ €22.5. No field/observational data.

## Key findings
- Result 1 (TI): The incentive contract dominates the trust contract — 69% of TI offers are ICs, rising toward 80% late; average effort and principal payoff are higher under IC.
- Result 2 (TI): Most ICs violate the no-shirking condition (58.5% demand $e^{*}>4$ despite using the maximal fine $\approx12.3$); non-incentive-compatible ICs induce full shirking ($e=1$ in 62% of cases) and average losses of $-7.6$, while incentive-compatible ICs earn $+8.6$. The share of incentive-compatible ICs rises strongly over time as principals learn.
- Result 3 (TI): Raising the wage to elicit non-incentive-compatible effort lowers principal payoff (OLS: $e=1.08+0.04w$; a 10-token wage rise raises effort only ~0.4).
- Result 4 (BI): Adding the bonus reverses everything — bonus contracts are chosen ~87% in period 1 rising to ~96%; the trust contract is never chosen. Average BC effort ($>5$) far exceeds IC effort (2–3); BC yields average profit 26.8 vs. an average IC loss of 8.9.
- Result 5 (BI): Principals pay substantial bonuses (~40% of compensation, average $b\approx10.4$) that rise steeply with effort (≈0 for $e\le2$, ≈30 for high effort), making high effort profitable for agents.
- Proposition 1 (TI): Higher trust-contract wages raise effort too little to be profitable; in the optimal IC both types demand $\hat e=4$ with maximal fine, fair principals pay a fair wage and selfish principals minimize the wage. Hence both prefer IC over TC.
- Proposition 2 (BC): No separating equilibrium exists; a unique pooling equilibrium has both principal types offer $w=15$, selfish agents choose $e=7$ (rewarded with $b=25$ by fair principals), fair agents choose $e=3$, selfish principals pay no bonus — expected effort $\approx5.4$, close to the data.
- Proposition 3 (general): For all $q\in(0,1)$ the optimal bonus contract induces (weakly) higher expected effort than the optimal trust contract, independent of functional forms. The general principle: the party with the lower cost of trusting should trust first. The agent's risk under BC is $c(e)$, smaller than the principal's risky wage investment $w(e)=c(e)+[v(e)-c(e)]/2$ under TC.

## Contribution
First experiment to let principals freely choose the contract form, isolating how fairness shapes which contracts prevail rather than just how agents respond to fixed incentives. It shows that the standard "efficiency principle" survives — principals converge to the most efficient available contract — but the efficiency ranking is reordered by fairness: implicit bonus contracts that fail under pure self-interest become superior. It supplies a tractable inequity-aversion model that not only rationalizes the qualitative reversal but delivers accurate quantitative average predictions, and reconciles the poor trust-contract performance with the favorable gift-exchange results of [[@Fehr1993|Fehr, Kirchsteiger, and Riedl (1993)]] via differences in the principal payoff function. The paper positions itself as a step toward "behavioral contract theory."

## Limitations & open questions
- The inequity-aversion model fits average ("as if") behavior but does poorly at the individual level; many principals pay intermediate, inconsistent bonuses.
- Convergence to the most efficient contract is shown only for this simple environment; whether it extends to "more complicated environments" is explicitly left open.
- The presence of fair types is not a panacea and can exacerbate contracting problems: fair agents respond less to bonus incentives (a "fairness compatibility" constraint adds to incentive compatibility), and fairness inhibits trusting behavior.
- The authors do not attempt to test inequity aversion against intention-based reciprocity models ([[@Rabin1993|Rabin 1993]]; [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger 2004]]; [[@Falk2006|Falk & Fischbacher 2006]]), nor to explain the dynamic time path of behavior.
- External validity beyond one-shot anonymous student interactions is suggestive (citing field/legal evidence) but not directly tested.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Economists often estimate models using data from a particular setting, e.g. estimating risk preferences in a specific subject pool. Whether a model's predictions extrapolate well across settings depends on whether the estimated model has captured generalizable structure. We provide a tractable formulation for this "out-of-domain" prediction problem, and define the transfer error of a model to be its performance on data from a new domain. We derive finite-sample forecast intervals that are guaranteed to cover realized transfer errors with a user-selected probability when domains are iid, and use these intervals to compare the transferability of economic models and black box algorithms for predicting certainty equivalents. We find that in this application, black box algorithms outperform the economic models when estimated and tested on different data from the same domain, but models motivated by economic theory generalize across domains better than the black-box algorithms do.

## Summary
The paper formalizes *out-of-domain* (transfer) prediction: instead of asking how a fitted model predicts on held-out data from the *same* distribution, it asks how it predicts on a *new domain* (e.g. a new subject pool). The key device is to treat each domain's data-generating distribution as itself an i.i.d. draw from a meta-distribution $\mu$, so that domains play the role observations play in conformal inference. Under this assumption the authors derive distribution-free, finite-sample *forecast intervals* that cover the realized transfer error of any given decision rule with a user-selected probability. Applying this to certainty-equivalent prediction for binary lotteries across 44 subject pools, they find a striking reversal: black-box methods (random forest, kernel ridge) beat expected-utility (EU) and cumulative prospect theory (CPT) *within* domain, yet the economic models *transfer* substantially better, because the black boxes fail to extrapolate when the set of sampled lotteries (the feature marginal) shifts across domains.

## Research question
Do structured economic models generalize across domains better than flexible black-box machine-learning algorithms, and how can an analyst quantify and put valid confidence bounds on a model's cross-domain transfer performance from a finite meta-dataset of already-observed domains?

## Method / identification
The statistical object is a prediction rule $\sigma:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{Y}$ scored by a loss $\ell$, with per-sample error $e(\sigma,S)=\frac{1}{|S|}\sum_{(x,y)\in S}\ell(\sigma(x),y)$. The departure from standard out-of-sample analysis: the analyst observes samples $S_d$ from domains $d\in\{1,\dots,n\}$, where each $(P_d,m_d)\sim\mu$ is drawn i.i.d. from a meta-distribution over distributions $P\equiv\Delta(\mathcal{X}\times\mathcal{Y})$ and sample sizes, and then $m_d$ observations are drawn i.i.d. from $P_d$. A *decision rule* $\rho$ maps the (training) meta-data $M_T$ to a chosen prediction rule; the two leading classes are empirical-risk-minimization over an economic model class $\Sigma^*$ and training a black-box algorithm.

Three transferability measures are defined for a target sample $S_{n+1}\sim\mu$: (i) **transfer error** $e(\rho(M_T),S_{n+1})$; (ii) **normalized transfer error** $\frac{e(\rho(M_T),S_{n+1})}{\min_{\tilde\rho\in R} e(\tilde\rho(S_{n+1}),S_{n+1})}$, dividing by the best in-sample error achievable on the target by a rule in a fixed set $R$ (a transfer-adapted "completeness" measure); and (iii) **transfer deterioration** $\frac{e(\rho(M_T),S_{n+1})}{e(\rho(S_{n+1}),S_{n+1})}$, the ratio of transferred error to the error from re-estimating on the target, capturing the value of retraining.

The identification of valid intervals rests on *exchangeability across domains*. Split the $n$ domains into $n_T$ training domains $T$ and test domains; let $e_{T,(r)}$ be the $r$-th smallest transfer error over test domains. Because ranks are uniform under i.i.d. domain sampling, a one-sided interval $(-\infty,e_{T,(r)}]$ with $r=\lceil\tau(n-n_T)\rceil$ satisfies $P(e_{T,n+1}>e_{T,(r)})\le 1-\frac{n-n_T}{n-n_T+1}\tau$ (Claim 1), with a two-sided analog (Claim 2) — these are split-conformal intervals with domains in place of observations. To remove the arbitrary choice of training set, **Proposition 1** averages over a random training set $\tilde T\sim\varphi(n_T,[n])$ and pools transfer errors; using $\tau$-th quantiles $\bar e_\tau,\underline e_\tau$ of the pooled errors, $[\underline e_\tau,\bar e_\tau]$ is a level-$\left(4\frac{n-n_T}{n-n_T+1}\tau-3\right)$ forecast interval. The proof builds a $U[0,1]$ statistic per training set and applies Meng (1994) / Rüschendorf (1982) results on averages second-order stochastically dominated by uniform. Crucially the guarantee needs no further knowledge of $\mu$. The framework also nests *partial transfer* (some parameters $\theta\in\Theta$ transferred, domain-specific $\lambda\in\Lambda$ re-estimated), ratios of two rules' errors, and (Appendix A) domain cross-validation, distributionally robust optimization, and hierarchical Bayes as decision rules.

Two extensions move from realized to population targets: **Proposition 2** + **Corollary 1** give finite-sample confidence intervals for *quantiles* $q_\beta$ of the transfer-error distribution (whose width shrinks with more domains, unlike forecast intervals) by counting domain-pairs with $e_{T_j,t_j}\le q$ via a $\mathrm{Bin}(J,\beta)$ argument plus the same averaging trick, solvable by simulation. **Proposition 3** introduces a *transfer Rademacher complexity* $R(\Sigma^*,M)$ that upper-bounds expected transfer error $E_{S\sim\mu}[e(\sigma,S)]\le e(\sigma,M)+2R(\Sigma^*,M)+4c\sqrt{2\ln(4/\delta)/n}$.

## Data
A purpose-built meta-dataset of reported certainty equivalents for binary lotteries from 44 subject pools (domains), drawn from 14 experimental-economics papers; one cross-country study contributes 30 of the samples. Samples range 72 to 8906 observations (mean 2752.7); prizes converted to dollars via PPP. Each observation is $(\underline z,\bar z,p;y)$ with feature space $\mathcal{X}=\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}\times[0,1]$, outcome $y\in\mathbb{R}$, squared-error loss reported as RMSE. Robustness: re-running with each *paper* as a domain, and with the 30 same-lottery country samples, gives qualitatively similar conclusions.

## Key findings
- **Within-domain reversal.** Tenfold cross-validated, random forest and kernel regression beat EU and CPT in 38 of 44 domains; on average the CPT error is 1.06x and EU 1.21x the random-forest error (Table 1) — black boxes are slightly better in-domain.
- **Transfer reversal (main result).** Across all three measures the black-box forecast intervals are *wider* with much *higher* upper bounds. The 71% forecast interval (5th–95th pooled percentiles, $\tau=0.95$, $n_T=1$) for CPT normalized transfer error is [1.02, 2.62] versus [1.02, 6.42] for random forest; transfer-error upper bounds for black boxes are roughly twice the economic models'.
- **Mechanism.** Poor black-box transfer is *not* merely "too flexible": on the 30 country samples sharing the same 27 lotteries, the intervals are nearly identical across methods. The decisive factor is whether the shift across domains is in the *feature marginal* (covariate shift — which lotteries appear) versus the *conditional* $P_{Y\mid X}$ (model shift). Black boxes match economic models under conditional shift but transfer far worse under marginal shift, because they do not learn global structure to extrapolate to unseen lotteries (illustrated by a three-domain degenerate-lottery example where a decision tree's MSE is 64 vs EU's ≈0.05).
- **Predictability of the gap.** Features of the train/test lottery sets predict the RF/CPT transfer-error ratio: a random forest cuts MSE to 30% of the constant benchmark; a one-split tree on $\mathrm{stddev}(\bar z)>0$ predicts a ratio of 5.47 when training prizes are held constant vs 1.43 otherwise.
- **Population-quantile CIs** (Proposition 2) yield the same ranking — RF lower and upper quantile bounds exceed CPT's at every level — while the Rademacher bound (Proposition 3) is too loose to be informative in this application.

## Contribution
Provides the first tractable, assumption-light formalization of out-of-domain prediction that delivers *distribution-free, finite-sample* coverage guarantees for transfer performance, recasting conformal inference with exchangeability across *domains* rather than observations. It supplies a menu of interpretable transferability measures usable to rank models and to value retraining, and offers among the first empirical validations of the long-standing intuition that theory-based economic models generalize across settings better than black boxes — while pinpointing *covariate shift in the feature marginal* as the operative reason, a mechanism with no analog in classical out-of-sample testing.

## Limitations & open questions
- **The i.i.d.-domains assumption is restrictive** and explicitly fails under serial correlation (time-series domains) or site-selection bias; the authors note only a partial robustness result (coverage degrades by at most the total-variation distance to the nearest i.i.d. distribution).
- **Forecast intervals do not shrink** with more domains and routinely *overlap* across methods even under first-order stochastic dominance — the quantile CIs of Section 5.1 are the proposed remedy but still overlap given only 44 domains.
- **Estimating a "best" transfer rule is left open.** The paper deliberately evaluates existing procedures and flags cross-domain cross-validation, hierarchical Bayes, and distributionally robust optimization as decision rules worth developing for better transfer — an explicit invitation.
- **Single application / homogeneous domain generalization.** Results are for risk-preference certainty equivalents with a constant outcome set; generality of the black-box-vs-theory verdict and the role of feature-marginal variation in other settings is conjectural.
- **The Rademacher bound is uninformative here** because the loss bound $c$ is large; sharper expected-transfer-error bounds are open.

## Connections
The forecast intervals are explicitly conformal inference (split conformal as in Lei et al. 2018) with domain-level rather than observation-level exchangeability; the de-randomization over training domains uses Rüschendorf (1982), Meng (1994), and Vovk and Wang (2020). The work sits against the meta-analysis literature (DellaVigna and Pope 2019; Meager 2019), which studies parameter variation across samples rather than predictive transfer — the paper carefully distinguishes parameter stability from transfer error. It connects to computer-science *domain generalization* (Blanchard et al. 2011; Muandet et al. 2013; survey Zhou et al. 2021), but inverts the goal: rather than building algorithms that generalize, it provides intervals for the generalization error of a *given* rule, enabling cross-model comparison. It extends the structured-models-vs-black-box prediction agenda of [[@Fudenberg2019a|Fudenberg and Liang (2019)]], Plonsky et al. (2017, 2019), Camerer et al. (2019), and Ke et al. (2020), and the normalized-error measure adapts the "completeness" notion of Fudenberg et al. (2022). The CPT specification draws on Goldstein and Einhorn (1987), Lattimore et al. (1992), Karmarkar (1978), and the one-parameter $\gamma$ form of Fudenberg et al. (2021). Külpmann and Kuzmics (2022) is cited as the closest prior out-of-domain test of economic models (estimating on $2\times 2$ and testing on $3\times 3$ games).

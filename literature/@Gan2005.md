## Summary

A Lincoln University discussion paper that horse-races three binary-choice predictors on a single empirical task: predicting whether a New Zealand consumer is an electronic-banking user. It pits a standard binary logit against two artificial neural networks - a multi-layer feed-forward network (MLFN) and a probabilistic neural network (PNN). On both in-sample and out-of-sample classification accuracy, both neural nets beat the logit, and the PNN dominates all models (near-perfect in-sample, ~99% out-of-sample correct). The methodological contrast is the familiar one: the logit yields interpretable coefficients and marginal effects, while the ANNs trade interpretability for predictive accuracy. The paper is essentially a predictive-modeling exercise rather than a structural or behavioral one.

## Research question

Which model class predicts a consumer's discrete banking-channel choice (electronic vs. non-electronic banking) most accurately: conventional logistic regression, an MLFN, or a probabilistic neural network? A secondary question is which consumer and demographic factors most strongly drive the choice.

## Method / identification

Three estimators are compared on the same feature set (19 regressors). The economic setup is a standard random-utility discrete-choice model: consumer $i$ chooses electronic banking if $U_i(y_i=1, w_i-c, z_i) \geq U_i(y_i=0, w_i, z_i)$, where $y_i$ is the binary choice, $w_i$ wealth, $z_i$ characteristics, and $c$ the average cost. With latent utility written as $y_i = f(x_i) + \varepsilon_i$ and $x_i=(w_i,z_i)$, a logistic error gives the binary logit (estimated by maximum likelihood in LIMDEP).

The MLFN is a feed-forward net with one hidden layer. Each node applies a transfer function $F_j$ to a weighted sum of inputs, $U_j = F_j\left(\sum_i W_{ij} X_i\right)$, and the full network output is
$$Z = F\left(\sum_{j=1}^{J} W_j^{(2)} F_j\left(\sum_{i=1}^{I} W_{ij}^{(1)} X_i\right)\right)$$
trained by back-propagation to minimize mean squared error. The chosen architecture is 19-19-1 with logistic activations, learning rate 0.1, momentum 0.1.

The PNN (Specht, 1990) is a four-layer Bayesian classifier: input, pattern, summation, and output layers. It classifies $X$ into class $A$ over $B$ when $h_A c_A f_A(X) > h_B c_B f_B(X)$, where $h$ are priors and $c$ misclassification costs. Class densities are estimated nonparametrically via Parzen (1962) windows:
$$f_A(X) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{p/2} \sigma^p n_A} \sum_{j=1}^{n_A} \exp\left(\frac{-(X-Y_{Aj})'(X-Y_{Aj})}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$
with smoothing parameter $\sigma$ (here 0.5186). The PNN configuration is 19-527-2-1. ANNs are built in NeuroShell2. Predictive power is assessed by out-of-sample forecasting: the sample is split into a 422-observation training set and a 105-observation forecast set, models re-estimated on training data, and classification rates compared.

## Data

A random mail survey (Dillman Total Design Method) sent to 1,960 households in the Canterbury Region, New Zealand; ~27% response yields 527 usable observations (384 electronic-banking users, 143 non-users). Predictors are six 5-point Likert constructs - service quality, perceived risk, user input factors, price factors, service product characteristics, individual factors - plus demographic dummies (age, gender, marital status, ethnicity, education, employment, income, residence).

## Key findings

- The logit fits well: McFadden $R^2 = 0.6777$, chi-squared 417.6 (df 19) rejecting no-explanatory-power, and 92.0% overall correct in-sample. Significant predictors are service quality (+), perceived risk (-), user input factors (+), old age (-), high-school education (-), rural residence (+), high income (+), white-collar and casual employment (+).
- By marginal effect, perceived risk (rank 1) and user input factors (rank 2) are the dominant drivers; this ranking is consistent across all three models.
- Both ANNs beat the logit on overall accuracy. In-sample overall correct: logit 92.0%, MLFN 94.9%, PNN 99.8%. The MLFN predicts the electronic-banking group well but the non-user group poorly; the PNN predicts both groups near-perfectly.
- Out-of-sample (n=105) the ranking holds: logit 91.4% correct, MLFN 92.4%, PNN 99.05%. The PNN has the smallest error rates (Type I 0.70%, Type II 0.00% in-sample).

## Contribution

The paper's stated novelty is applying the probabilistic neural network - rather than only the commonly used MLFN - to a consumer-choice classification problem, and showing it outperforms both the MLFN and the logit on banking-channel prediction. It positions the PNN as a promising tool for marketing research where predictive accuracy is the objective.

## Limitations & open questions

The authors are explicit about ANN drawbacks: (1) neural networks lack theoretical/explanatory capability - connection weights cannot be interpreted as structural relationships between dependent and independent variables; (2) there are no formal hypothesis tests for the relevance of inputs nor a principled variable-selection procedure under nonlinear methods; (3) training can be computationally slow. They also note there is no theoretical framework for choosing the number of hidden nodes/layers, making MLFN design a trial-and-error process. As future work they propose extending the predictive comparison beyond binary choice to multiple-level (multinomial) classification problems. A reader should also note the strikingly high PNN in-sample accuracy is partly mechanical (the pattern layer memorizes every training sample), so the out-of-sample figures are the meaningful ones.

## Connections

The discrete-choice utility setup follows Ben-Akiva & Lerman (1985) and the logit/probit treatment in Maddala (1993) and Greene (1990). The PNN is Specht (1990), built on nonparametric density estimation by Parzen (1962) and Cacoullos (1966). The paper's core comparison of neural networks against statistical choice models extends West, Brockett & Golden (1997) in Marketing Science, and follows earlier ANN-vs-conventional comparisons by Dasgupta, Dispensa & Ghose (1994), Fish, Barnes & Aiken (1995), Hu, Shanker & Hung (1999), and Vellido, Lisboa & Vaughan (1999). The consumer decision-making framing draws on Dewey (1910), Engel, Kollat & Blackwell (1973), and Zeithaml & Bitner (2003). This sits within the broader machine-learning-vs-econometrics theme in choice prediction relevant to comparing flexible predictors against structural discrete-choice models.

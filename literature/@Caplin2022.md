> [!abstract] Abstract
> What do machines learn, and why? To answer these questions we import models of human cognition into machine learning. We propose two ways of modeling machine learners based on this join: feasibility-based and cost-based machine learning. We evaluate and estimate our models using a deep learning convolutional neural network that predicts pneumonia from chest X-rays. We find these predictions are consistent with our model of cost-based machine learning, and we recover the algorithm's implied costs of learning.

## Summary
The paper imports the economic theory of rational inattention / costly information acquisition into machine learning, treating an opaque ML algorithm as an *as if* Bayesian decision maker that (i) optimally chooses a signal structure and (ii) makes loss-minimizing predictions given the resulting posteriors. It develops two nested models — feasibility-based and cost-based machine learning — and a hierarchy of revealed-preference tests (loss calibration, strong loss-adaptedness, loss-adaptedness). Applying these to CheXNeXt, a CNN predicting pneumonia from chest X-rays trained under three class-weighted cross-entropy losses, the authors find the algorithm is loss-calibrated and consistent with cost-based but *not* feasibility-based learning, and they recover sharp bounds on the algorithm's implied learning costs.

## Research question
What do machine learning algorithms learn, and why? More precisely: does there exist a parsimonious *as if* representation of an opaque (black-box) machine learner — analogous to how economics models human cognition — that reasonably approximates its behavior, can be tested on observable prediction data, and lets the analyst recover the algorithm's implicit costs/constraints on learning?

## Method / identification
The formal backbone is the Blackwell (1953) model of experimentation, signal processing, and choice, recast as loss minimization. There is a finite instance set $X$, outcome set $Y$, ground truth $f:X\to Y$, prediction set $A$, and loss function $L:A\times Y\to\mathbb{R}$ (the running case is $\beta$-weighted cross-entropy $L_\beta(a,y)=-\beta y\log a-(1-\beta)(1-y)\log(1-a)$). Training with $L$ yields a model $g^L:X\to A$ summarized by performance data $P^L(a,y)$, the joint distribution of predictions and outcomes on a test set.

**Signal-based representation (SBR).** $P^L$ has an SBR if there is a prior $\mu\in\Delta(Y)$, a Bayes-consistent distribution of posteriors $Q\in\mathcal{Q}$ (where $\mathcal{Q}\equiv\{Q\mid\sum_{\gamma}\gamma Q(\gamma)=\mu\}$), and a prediction function $q:\operatorname{supp}(Q)\to\Delta(A)$ such that the prior is correct, predictions are optimal at every posterior ($q\in\hat q(L,Q)$), and predictions are generated by the model. The SBR is characterized by a single testable condition, **loss calibration**: a wholesale switch from any used prediction $a$ to any alternative $a'$ never strictly lowers loss, i.e. $a\in\arg\min_{a'}\sum_y P^L(a,y)L(a',y)$. Under weighted cross-entropy the loss-calibrated score has closed form $a_\beta(\gamma)=\frac{\beta\gamma}{1-\beta-\gamma+2\beta\gamma}$, collapsing to $a_{0.5}(\gamma)=\gamma$ (truthful belief revelation) for the proper unweighted loss.

**Two learning models (how the signal structure is chosen).**
- *Feasibility-based:* the algorithm picks $(Q,q)$ to minimize loss subject to $Q\in\mathcal{Q}^*$, a loss-function-independent feasible set. Consistent iff there exists $\mathcal{Q}^*$ with $P^L\in\tilde P(L,\mathcal{Q}^*)$ for all $L$.
- *Cost-based:* the algorithm minimizes resource-adjusted loss $\hat L((Q,q)\mid L,K)=\sum_\gamma Q(\gamma)\sum_a q(a\mid\gamma)\sum_y\gamma(y)L(a,y)+K(Q)$ for a learning-cost function $K:\mathcal{Q}\to\mathbb{R}$ (intrinsic difficulty of learning, *not* monetary/compute cost). Cost-based nests feasibility-based (set $K=0$ on $\mathcal{Q}^*$, $\infty$ otherwise).

**Tests (revealed-preference, via cross-loss matrix).** With $M$ loss functions, the cross-loss matrix $G^{mn}=\sum_{a}\min_{a'}\sum_y L^m(a',y)P^n(a,y)$ records minimized loss when judging $P^n$ under $L^m$. Define direct loss differences $D_0^{mn}=G^{mn}-G^{mm}$. Feasibility-based learning $\Leftrightarrow$ **strongly loss-adapted**: $D_0^{mn}\ge 0$ for all $m,n$ (from Caplin, Martin & Marx 2022). Cost-based learning $\Leftrightarrow$ **loss-adapted**: the indirect (path-minimized) loss-difference matrix $D^{mn}=\min_{\vec h\in H(m,n)}\sum_j D_0^{h(j)h(j+1)}$ has non-negative diagonal $D^{mm}\ge 0$ (no cycle of switches lowers loss; from Caplin & Dean 2015). $D$ is computable in polynomial time via the Floyd-Warshall algorithm on the weighted directed graph with edge weights $D_0^{mn}$.

**Cost recovery.** All qualifying costs satisfy $D_0^{mm}+K^m\le D_0^{mn}+K^n$; normalizing $K^M=0$ they form a convex polyhedron in $\mathbb{R}^{M-1}$ whose extreme points come from the sign-inverted $M$-th row/column of $D$. The average $\bar K$ over normalizations serves as a "representative" learning cost.

## Data
ChestX-ray14 (Wang et al. 2017): 112,120 frontal chest X-ray images, outcome $Y=\{0,1\}$ for pneumonia (only 1.3% positive). The algorithm is CheXNeXt / CheXNet (DenseNet-style CNN, ~6.97M learnable parameters), retrained on the isolated pneumonia task across class weights $\beta\in\{0.7,0.9,0.99\}$, with early stopping and ensemble (model-averaging) over nested cross-validation to separate substantive learning from stochastic training noise. The $9\times 9$ covariance of $G$ is estimated by 10,000 bootstrap samples.

## Key findings
- **Loss calibration holds empirically (SBR is viable).** Decile-binned calibration curves match the closed-form loss-calibrated scores $a_\beta(\gamma)$ across all $\beta$, and the algorithm is approximately (unconditionally) calibrated at the proper loss $\beta=0.5$ — consistent with regularization (weight decay) and deep ensembling reducing overconfidence.
- **Feasibility-based learning is rejected.** The estimated $G=0.01\begin{bmatrix}3.750&3.752&3.762\\3.349&3.352&3.362\\1.365&1.366&1.370\end{bmatrix}$ implies a *common preference* for the performance data trained with lower $\beta$, so $D_0^{mn}\ge 0$ fails. A Bonferroni-corrected multivariate one-sided Wald test rejects strong loss-adaptedness at $p=0.0014$.
- **Cost-based learning is not rejected.** The indirect loss-difference matrix $D$ has a zero diagonal, so loss-adaptedness holds; the null is not rejected at any significance level (pointwise consistency in 53% of bootstrap samples), and the authors note the test was powerful — any reordering of chosen signal structures would have produced a pointwise rejection.
- **Implied learning costs recovered.** Normalizing $K^{0.99}=0$, the cost polyhedron is nearly point-identified with $K^{0.99}\le K^{0.9}\le K^{0.7}$: placing relatively lower weight on (rare) pneumonia instances entails higher learning costs, sensible because it shifts weight onto the far more common non-pneumonia class.

## Contribution
The paper forges a "join" between three literatures — information economics (information design/Bayesian persuasion), bounded rationality (rational inattention, costly information acquisition), and machine learning — by treating an algorithm as an economic decision maker. It adapts revealed-Bayesian-learning characterizations (Caplin & Dean 2015; Caplin, Martin & Marx 2022) into testable, estimable models for ML, introduces *loss calibration* as a Bayesian-rationality condition apparently new to ML (and trivially enforceable with one line of code), and demonstrates empirically that an opaque state-of-the-art CNN admits a parsimonious cost-based *as if* representation whose structural learning costs can be recovered. It argues machines are in some ways *better* subjects for these models than humans: the loss function is a known, externally manipulable primitive (vs. an inferred utility function), machines naturally generate state-dependent stochastic choice data, and they may emulate the rational paradigm more closely (no self-serving belief distortions).

## Limitations & open questions
- **Ground truth assumed correct.** The map $f$ is taken as known/correct; the authors note the framework "could be extended to include uncertainty about the ground truth."
- **Restriction to SBR/loss-calibrated data.** The model tests are characterized only for performance data with an SBR representation; behavior off that restriction is not addressed.
- **Cost function only set-identified.** Learning costs are recovered as a polyhedron of qualifying functions, not a point; the "representative" average cost is a pragmatic, not uniquely justified, selection. Whether "not learning is free" (Denti 2022's minimal-cost normalization, natural for humans) is appropriate for algorithms is flagged as unclear.
- **Scope of the empirical test.** Evidence rests on one CNN, one binary task, three class weights ($M=3$); generality across architectures, tasks, multi-class outcomes, and other loss families is open.
- **Mechanism of cost variation is interpretive.** Why resource use varies with the loss function (hyperparameter optimization, number of epochs / "ReduceLROnPlateau", etc.) is offered as plausible interpretation rather than identified mechanism.
- More broadly, the authors stress how *little* is known about algorithms-as-decision-makers and position the work as opening the door to applying economic tools to understand modern ML.

## Connections
The foundation follows Blackwell (1953) and the information-design objects of Kamenica & Gentzkow (2011), Bergemann & Morris (2016, 2019), and Kamenica (2019). Feasibility-based learning applies the capacity-constrained learning of Caplin, Martin & Marx (2022) ("Revealed Bayesian Learning"), generalizing fixed-capacity rational inattention (Sims 2003) and noisy cognition (Woodford 2014). Cost-based learning applies the general rational-inattention characterization of Caplin & Dean (2015), which nests the Shannon-entropy version of Matejka & McKay (2015) and Caplin, Dean & Leahy (2017); related costly-learning theory includes Gentzkow & Kamenica (2014), De Oliveira et al. (2017), Hébert & Woodford (2017), Denti (2022), and Lipnowski & Ravid (2022). Loss calibration draws on Caplin & Martin (2015) and Bergemann & Morris (2016). The ML side builds on CheXNeXt/CheXNet (Rajpurkar et al. 2017, 2018) and ChestX-ray14 (Wang et al. 2017), and engages the calibration literature (Guo et al. 2017; Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana 2005; Lakshminarayanan et al. 2017; Minderer et al. 2021) and the bias-variance / double-descent discussion (Hastie et al. 2009; Belkin et al. 2019). It contrasts with Pattanayak & Krishnamurthy (2021), who treat the algorithm's objective as an unobservable utility, and connects to ML-in-economics work (Kleinberg et al. 2015; Athey 2017; [[@Fudenberg2019a|Fudenberg & Liang 2019]]; [[@Fudenberg2022|Fudenberg, Kleinberg, Liang & Mullainathan 2022)]] and to fairness-vs-accuracy information design ([[@Andrews2022|Liang, Lu & Mu 2022)]].

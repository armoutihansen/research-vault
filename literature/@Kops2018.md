> [!abstract] Abstract
> Standard rational choice relies on the assumption that a decision maker is certain about her preferences. The psychology literature, on the other hand, provides well-established evidence that consumers are often uncertain about the true value of alternatives. This is particularly so when alternatives have several attributes and focusing on different attributes shifts a decision maker's ranking of alternatives. In this paper, we propose and behaviorally characterize a new model of boundedly rational choice that formalizes these insights from psychology into a choice procedure. Simply put, our approach introduces menu dependence into the idea of lexicographic preferences. We study some of its properties and highlight how this procedure exacerbates any welfare judgments.

## Summary
Kops proposes the **flexicographic shortlist method (FSM)**, a boundedly rational choice procedure that generalizes Manzini & Mariotti's (2007) rational shortlist method by letting the *order* in which two rationales are applied vary across menus. A decision maker (DM) screens a menu in two rounds, eliminating alternatives that are not maximal under each rationale, but the sequence of the two rationales may flip from one choice problem to another. This single relaxation lets the model accommodate binary choice cycles, the attraction effect, and the compromise effect simultaneously. The paper gives a complete behavioral characterization via one axiom, *reversal consistency*, a three-alternative extension of weak WARP, and discusses public-choice and welfare implications.

## Research question
Can a parsimonious, testable choice procedure capture the empirically robust violations of rational choice (binary cycles, attraction effect, compromise effect) by formalizing the psychological idea that *which attribute is salient depends on the menu*, i.e., that the order of lexicographic comparison is menu-dependent? And what is the minimal consistency such a procedure imposes on observable choice data?

## Method / identification
The setup is abstract single-valued choice. Let $X$ be a finite set of alternatives with $|X|>2$ and $\mathcal{P}(X)$ the non-empty subsets. A choice function $\gamma:\mathcal{P}(X)\to X$ selects $\gamma(S)\in S$. For an asymmetric binary relation (a *rationale*) $P$, the $P$-maximal set is $\max(S;P)=\{x\in S\mid \neg\exists y\in S\text{ with }(y,x)\in P\}$.

**Definition (FSM).** $\gamma$ is an FSM if there exist two rationales $P,P'\subseteq X\times X$ such that
$$\{\gamma(S)\}=\max\big(\max(S;P_1(S));P_2(S)\big),\quad \forall S\in\mathcal{P}(X)$$
where $P_1(S),P_2(S)\in\{P,P'\}$ and $P_1(S)\neq P_2(S)$. The two rationales are fixed across all menus, but their *order* may be either $(P,P')$ or $(P',P)$ depending on $S$. The RSM of [[@Manzini2007|Manzini & Mariotti (2007)]] is the special case where the order is invariant. The FSM is the non-probabilistic counterpart of Tversky's (1972) elimination by aspects.

A key construct for the characterization is **feasibility**: an alternative is feasible from $S$ (written $x\in\Lambda_\gamma(S)$) if there is *some* order of the rationales under which it would be selected, even if not actually chosen. Because a choice function is single-valued, the construction implicitly bounds the number of feasible alternatives per menu at two. Feasibility must be *inferred* from other choices via an explicit recursive definition involving "$x$ trails $z$" ($z=\gamma(x,z)$) and $x$-covers.

The central axiom: **Reversal Consistency.** For all $R,S,T$ with $\{x,y,z\}\subseteq R\subseteq S\subseteq T$,
$$[x\in\Lambda_\gamma(x,y)\cap\Lambda_\gamma(S)\ \text{and}\ z\in\Lambda_\gamma(x,y,z)\cap\Lambda_\gamma(T)]\Rightarrow \gamma(R)\neq y.$$
This is a three-alternative generalization of weak WARP (which states $\{x,y\}\subset S\subset T$, $x=\gamma(x,y)=\gamma(T)\Rightarrow\gamma(S)\neq y$), capturing a "confirmed revealed preference" idea.

## Data
None of the author's own. The paper is purely theoretical but reinterprets three published experimental datasets: [[@May1954|May (1954)]] marriage-partner binary cycles (27% cyclic subjects); [[@Kivetz2004|Kivetz, Netzer & Srinivasan (2004)]] Economist-subscription attraction effect; and [[@Simonson1992|Simonson & Tversky (1992)]] microwave compromise effect. Each is shown to be matched by an FSM with explicitly constructed rationales.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1 (characterization).** For any (not necessarily finite) $X$, a choice function $\gamma$ is an FSM if and only if it satisfies reversal consistency. This shows the FSM is non-vacuous and falsifiable, and identifies the minimal behavioral postulate it requires.
- **Proposition 1.** If $x$ is feasible from $S$ then $x\in\{\max(\max(S;P);P'),\max(\max(S;P');P)\}$, i.e., feasibility correctly captures choosability under some order.
- The FSM can violate both weaker-than-WARP conditions — *always chosen* (AC) and *no binary cycles* (NBC) — demonstrated by explicit three-element examples; hence it spans all three subclasses of WARP violations identified by Manzini & Mariotti.
- It is the first axiomatic sequential-elimination model to make the *order* of stages flexible, and the only one in the surveyed set compatible with cycles, attraction, and compromise effects simultaneously (Figure 1 maps RSM, FSM, CTC, RM, CLA against these phenomena).
- **Welfare.** The FSM "exacerbates" welfare judgments: an example (Table 6) reproduces Kivetz et al.'s attraction-effect data and shows the [[@Bernheim2009|Bernheim & Rangel (2009)]] unambiguous-choice welfare criterion can be deceived, because a decoy silently determines the order of rationales.
- **Extensions.** A public-choice reading casts the FSM as a dual to Arrow's aggregation problem (least consistency retained when aggregating two possibly incomplete/intransitive criteria). A choice-correspondence version (Definition 6) handles indecisive choice, characterized by Reversal Consistency* plus a Partial Expansion axiom.

## Contribution
Two-fold by the author's account: (i) it provides a behavioral foundation for a non-probabilistic elimination-by-aspects procedure, making Tversky's (1972) idea testable via simple choice experiments; and (ii) it extends the [[@Manzini2007|Manzini & Mariotti (2007)]] axiomatic sequential-choice program by being the first such model with menu-dependent ordering of elimination stages, tying the formal machinery tightly to the psychological notion of constructive, salience-driven preferences (Dhar et al. 2000; [[@Simonson1992|Simonson & Tversky 1992]]; [[@Shafir1993|Shafir et al. 1993]]).

## Limitations & open questions
- The number of rationales is **fixed at two**; extending flexible ordering to $n>2$ rationales (à la [[@Manzini2012|Manzini & Mariotti 2012]] lexicographic semiorders) is left open.
- The model is silent on *what determines* the order of rationales in a given menu — the "frame" driving the flip is treated as a free, unmodeled parameter, raising the worry (acknowledged via the Sen apple-banana example) of rendering the theory hard to refute if alternatives are re-individuated.
- Welfare analysis is shown to be problematic under the FSM (decoy-driven reversals confound the Bernheim–Rangel criterion), but no positive welfare procedure is offered — an explicit hook for future work.
- The probabilistic structure of elimination by aspects is deliberately discarded; reconciling the FSM with stochastic choice data is not addressed.

## Connections
The paper sits squarely in the sequentially-rationalizable choice literature launched by [[@Manzini2007|Manzini & Mariotti (2007)]], generalizing their rational shortlist method and relating to their categorize-then-choose ([[@Manzini2012a|Manzini & Mariotti 2012a]]) and lexicographic-semiorder ([[@Manzini2012a|Manzini & Mariotti 2012b]]) models — every CTC is shown to be an FSM. It builds directly on Tversky's (1969, 1972) intransitivity and elimination-by-aspects work and on the lexicographic-semiorder tradition. It contrasts with the limited-attention / revealed-attention models of [[@Masatlioglu2012|Masatlioglu, Nakajima & Ozbay (2012)]] and Lleras, Masatlioglu, Nakajima & Ozbay (2014), and with the rationalization model of [[@Cherepanov2013|Cherepanov, Feddersen & Sandroni (2013)]], giving explicit examples separating FSM from CLA. The empirical anchors are [[@May1954|May (1954)]], [[@Huber1982|Huber, Payne & Puto (1982)]], [[@Simonson1989|Simonson (1989)]], [[@Simonson1992|Simonson & Tversky (1992)]], and [[@Kivetz2004|Kivetz, Netzer & Srinivasan (2004)]]. The welfare discussion engages [[@Bernheim2009|Bernheim & Rangel (2009)]] and the framing literature of [[@Salant2008|Salant & Rubinstein (2008)]] and [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky (1979)]]; the public-choice remark connects to Arrow's impossibility theorem and [[@Sen1993|Sen (1993)]] on internal consistency of choice.

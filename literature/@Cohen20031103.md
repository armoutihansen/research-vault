## Summary
Across four experiments, Cohen shows that politically involved undergraduates' attitudes toward a welfare policy are determined almost entirely by the stated position of their own party, overwhelming both the policy's objective content and their prior ideology. Liberals endorsed a stringent program when told Democrats backed it; conservatives endorsed a lavish program when told Republicans backed it. This group effect was not the product of lazy heuristic processing: participants read carefully, generated as many thoughts, and recalled as much detail as controls. Rather, group information redefined the very object being evaluated, shifting its assumed factual qualities and moral connotations. Strikingly, participants denied being influenced by their party while readily attributing such influence to others, especially ideological adversaries.

## Research question
Does reference-group (political party) information dominate objective policy content and personal ideology in shaping political attitudes, even under effortful processing? And are people aware of this group influence on themselves relative to others?

## Method / identification
Four between-subjects laboratory experiments using a fabricated newspaper report about a state welfare policy, with self-identified liberal and conservative students selected via mass pre-testing.
- **Study 1:** A 2 (participant ideology: liberal/conservative) x 2 (policy content: generous/stringent) x 2 (reference group: Democrats favor/Republicans favor) ANOVA. Attitude measured 1-7; perceived causal sources of one's own attitude rated on four factors (policy details, philosophy of government, what the typical Democrat/Republican believes, personal experience).
- **Study 2:** Adds a no-group-information control condition (liberals only) and tests prior welfare knowledge (median split) as a moderator, via a 2 (content) x 2 (group info absent/available) x 2 (knowledge) ANOVA plus a 4 (condition) x 2 (knowledge) test. A follow-up replication strips out party rhetoric (percentile cue only) and uses sealed envelopes to rule out social-desirability/demand explanations of denied influence.
- **Study 3:** Conservatives only; presents generous and stringent policies side-by-side to hold scale "perspective" (Ostrom & Upshaw, 1968) constant, ruling out an anchoring confound. Adds process measures: self-reported reading effort, a thought-listing (cognitive response) task coded for number/valence/referent of thoughts, and open-ended recall, to test heuristic vs. biased-systematic processing accounts.
- **Study 4:** Liberals only; a job-training program, no-group-info vs. "Democrats oppose" conditions, alternative held constant. Adds: a social-meaning rating (liberal-conservative scale), coding of thought content (generous vs. stringent factual qualities) and moral connotations (individualism vs. humanitarianism), a consequential behavioral measure (signed editorial sent to a policy institute), and attributions for others' attitudes plus self/other objectivity ratings. Mediation assessed via the Baron & Kenny (1986) / Sobel procedure.

## Data
None external. Original experimental data from college student samples (e.g., 48 liberals + 31 conservatives in Study 1; ~54 in Study 2; 31 conservatives in Study 3; ~49 in Study 4), recruited from introductory psychology pools over several terms.

## Key findings
- **Party dominates content (Studies 1-3).** In Study 1 the ideology x group-information interaction was highly significant, $F(1,64)=120.81$, $p<.001$; liberals and conservatives alike adopted their party's stated position regardless of whether the policy was generous or stringent, while policy content had no direct effect ($F<1$).
- **Reversal under group info (Study 2).** Without group cues, liberals favored the generous policy ($M=4.75$) over the stringent ($M=3.31$); with group cues this reversed, favoring the Democrat-backed stringent policy ($M=5.00$) over the Republican-backed generous one ($M=3.20$).
- **Knowledge does not moderate (Study 2).** High- and low-knowledge participants were equally swayed (all $Fs<2$).
- **Not a perspective/anchoring artifact, not heuristic processing (Study 3).** With policies side-by-side the policy x group-info interaction held, $F(1,26)=10.43$, $p<.005$. Reading effort, number of thoughts, and recall did not differ by condition, and thought-attitude correlations were comparable, indicating systematic processing in both conditions.
- **Mechanism: redefinition of the object, plus moral reframing (Study 4).** Group info shifted attitudes ($F(1,45)=12.52$) and behavior (76% wrote pro editorials in control vs. 71% anti in the Democrats-oppose condition, $\chi^2=10.78$, $p<.005$), and shifted perceived social meaning ($F(1,45)=15.27$). Biased systematic processing accounted for roughly half the effect (Sobel $Z=-2.34$, $p<.02$), but the larger story is that group info changed the assumed factual qualities of the program (generous-minus-stringent thought index dropped) and its moral framing (humanitarianism emphasized over individualism when the party opposed it).
- **Blindness to influence.** Across studies participants rated "what the typical Democrat/Republican believes" as the least influential factor on their own attitude, and rated it no higher in group-info than in no-group-info conditions, yet they readily attributed group influence to others, especially adversaries. This is interpreted as a failure to apply a valid social theory to oneself.

## Contribution
Demonstrates experimentally that group identity does not merely bias persuasion at the margin but defines the social meaning of attitude objects, dominating objective content and prior ideology even under high-effort processing. The paper integrates dual-process models of persuasion (heuristic-systematic; elaboration likelihood) with social-identity and naive-realism / bias-blind-spot research, showing biased systematic processing and object-redefinition as distinct mechanisms, and links the asymmetric self/other attribution to self-deception.

## Limitations & open questions
The author explicitly flags: (1) the manipulation confounds ingroup endorsement with outgroup position, so conformity to the ingroup vs. reactive devaluation of the outgroup cannot be separated; (2) the studies cover only political decision-making, where attitudes may be unusually symbolic and thus especially malleable; (3) reliance on college undergraduates whose beliefs/loyalties may be more malleable or less crystallized than older adults', limiting generality; (4) whether an issue-irrelevant reference group (e.g., one's classmates) produces as much attitude change as an issue-relevant one (the party) remains unresolved; and (5) group influence is not claimed to always dominate content - sufficiently extreme or salient content could plausibly resist it.

## Connections
Builds directly on Asch (1948, 1952) on social definition of objects and on Sherif, Sherif & Nebergall (1965) and Turner (1991) on group-based values. The persuasion framework draws on the heuristic-systematic model (Chaiken & Maheswaran, 1994; Chen, Duckworth & Chaiken, 1999) and the elaboration likelihood model (Petty & Cacioppo, 1986; Eagly & Chaiken, 1993; Fleming & Petty, 2000), with biased-assimilation antecedents in Lord, Ross & Lepper (1979). The blindness-to-influence thread connects to the bias blind spot (Pronin, Lin & Ross, 2002), naive realism (Ross & Ward, 1995; Robinson et al., 1995), the illusion of objectivity (Armor, 1999), the third-person effect (Davison, 1983), introspective limits (Nisbett & Wilson, 1977), and "holier than thou" self/other asymmetries (Epley & Dunning, 2000). The moral-reframing analysis relates to Kristiansen & Zanna (1994), Tetlock (1986), Kahan (1999), and Haidt (2001). The scale-perspective alternative tested and ruled out is Ostrom & Upshaw (1968); attitude attribution by party draws on Brady & Sniderman (1985); mediation analysis follows Baron & Kenny (1986).

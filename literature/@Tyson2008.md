> [!abstract] Abstract
> A theory of decision making is proposed that offers an axiomatic basis for the notion of "satisficing" postulated by Herbert Simon. The theory relaxes the standard assumption that the decision maker always fully perceives his preferences among the available alternatives, requiring instead that his ability to perceive any given preference be decreasing with respect to the complexity of the choice problem at hand. When complexity is aligned with set inclusion, this exercise is shown to be equivalent to abandoning the contraction consistency axiom of classical choice theory.

## Summary
Tyson provides an axiomatic foundation for Simon's satisficing by reinterpreting the failure of classical rationality as a *perception* problem: an agent has genuine preferences but, because cognitive resources are scarce, may fail to perceive a given preference once the choice problem becomes too complex. Formalizing "complexity = set inclusion" yields a *nestedness* hypothesis on the menu-indexed family of perceived preferences, and the paper's central message is that imposing nestedness (while keeping the standard ordering properties) is exactly equivalent to dropping the contraction consistency axiom $\alpha$ from classical revealed-preference theory. A graded series of theorems then maps ordering assumptions (acyclic, partial, weak, linear) onto expansion-consistency axioms, and a finiteness restriction delivers a characterization of the satisficing (threshold) representation.

## Research question
Can Simon's satisficing—choosing any alternative that meets a threshold rather than the maximum—be given a revealed-preference axiomatization grounded in *cognitive constraints* and *environmental complexity*, and how does such a relaxation relate formally to the classical consistency axioms of choice theory?

## Method / identification
Pure axiomatic choice theory; no data. The primitives are a finite-or-infinite universe $X$, the set of menus $\mathcal{A}=\{A\subseteq X: A\neq\varnothing\}$, and a choice function $C:\mathcal{A}\to\mathcal{A}$ with $C(A)\subseteq A$. The key innovation is the *preference system* $\mathbf{R}=\langle R_A\rangle_{A\in\mathcal{A}}$, a vector of binary relations indexed by menus, where $R_A\subseteq R\cap[A\times A]$ collects the preferences the agent actually *perceives* on menu $A$. The system *generates* $C$ when $C(A)=\{x\in A:\forall y\in A,\ \neg[yR_A x]\}$.

The substantive restriction is **nestedness**: for $x,y\in A\subseteq B$, $xR_B y$ only if $xR_A y$ (a perceived preference survives when the menu shrinks). A relation $R$ is a *foundation* for $\mathbf{R}$ if $\bigcup_A R_A\subseteq R$, exact if equality holds; the **base relation** $P_b$ is defined by $xP_b y\iff y\notin C(xy)$. The classical benchmark is Arrow's Weak Axiom, decomposed (Bordes–Sen) into Contraction (axiom $\alpha$) plus Strong Expansion. Numerical representation: a **satisficing representation** is a pair $\langle f,\theta\rangle$ with utility $f:X\to\mathbb{R}$ and threshold $\theta:\mathcal{A}\to\mathbb{R}$ such that $C(A)=\{x\in A: f(x)\geq\theta(A)\}$, generalizing the maximizing criterion $f(x)\geq\max f[A]$. Proofs proceed by constructing revealed-preference objects (a global relation $P_g$ and a nested local relation system $P_l$) and showing they exhibit the required ordering properties under each axiom (Appendix A).

## Data
None — this is a theoretical paper. The argument is entirely axiomatic, illustrated by worked choice-function examples over $X=wxyz$.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 2.** [A] $C$ is generated by a single weak order iff it satisfies Contraction and Strong Expansion. [B] $C$ is generated by a *nested system of weak orders* iff it satisfies Strong Expansion alone. Hence deleting Contraction = replacing full perception with nestedness while transferring ordering properties from $R$ to $\mathbf{R}$.
- **Proposition 2.** If a nested system generates $C$, the base relation $P_b$ provides an exact foundation for it.
- **Theorem 3.** $C$ is generated by a nested system of weak orders admitting a *weak-order foundation* iff it satisfies **Base Acyclicity** ($P_b$ acyclic) and Strong Expansion. Szpilrajn's embedding theorem restores full ordering properties to the underlying $R$.
- **Theorem 5** (finite $X$). $C$ admits an **expansive satisficing representation** iff it satisfies Base Acyclicity and Strong Expansion, where *expansiveness* requires: $\forall A\subseteq B$, $\max f[A]\geq\theta(B)\Rightarrow\theta(A)\geq\theta(B)$. This is the threshold-representation analogue of Strong Expansion.
- **Theorems 6–8** (robustness across ordering assumptions). Weak Expansion characterizes nested systems of *acyclic* orders; Weak + Auxiliary Expansion ("Aizerman's Axiom") characterize nested *partial* orders; for *linear* orders (Univalence) full perception and cognitive constraint coincide—nestedness plus weak connectedness collapses back to a single menu-independent linear order.
- The unifying pattern (Table 1): deleting Contraction from any classical characterization yields its cognitively-constrained counterpart.

## Contribution
The paper supplies the first revealed-preference axiomatization of satisficing motivated explicitly by Simon's twin themes of bounded cognition and environmental complexity, and—more strikingly—identifies the well-known failure of contraction consistency as *equivalent* to a perception story rather than a defect. It reframes menu-dependence not as preference instability but as variation in what preferences are *perceivable*, and shows that the entire ladder of classical ordering axioms has cognitively-constrained twins obtained by deleting one axiom.

## Limitations & open questions
- The author explicitly *declines* further reductionism: nestedness is taken as primitive and the model offers no underlying cognitive budget constraint. He flags the open questions: *Where is the agent's budget constraint? What are the nature and origin of the perceptual limitations? Why is one preference constructible and another not?* These are deliberately left unanswered, since answering them would break the parallel with classical theory.
- **Idempotence** is explicitly rejected: the model does not require $x\in C(A)\Rightarrow x\in C(C(A))$ (the "waiter has already reappeared" argument), leaving open how to model multi-stage or deliberative refinement of choice.
- Nestedness fails in important behavioral settings—Sen's tea/heroin epistemic-importance example and Simonson–Tversky extremeness aversion / asymmetric dominance—so the theory is best suited to pairwise (not holistic) evaluation. Extending it to menu-holistic choice is open.
- Behavior may rest on cyclic underlying preferences (Example 1) unless Base Acyclicity is imposed.

## Connections
The classical scaffolding is Samuelson's and Arrow's Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference and Sen's and Bordes's expansion/contraction conditions; the contraction axiom itself traces to Chernoff and Nash. Aleskerov & Monjardet (1995) characterize the satisficing representation per se via a Separation Acyclicity condition, absent the perception motivation; Duggan's unpublished note reaches very similar results from social choice and tournaments. The work connects directly to the bounded-rationality choice-function literature: Manzini & Mariotti's (2007) lexicographic "shortlisting" two-stage model falls under Theorem 6B (Weak Expansion), and Kalai, Rubinstein & Spiegler's "rationalization by multiple rationales" can be translated into nested relation systems. Conceptual antecedents on consistency criteria include Herzberger, Anand, and (on median choice) Gaertner & Xu and Baigent & Gaertner. The deliberate refusal to micro-found cognition contrasts with the optimization-under-constraints program criticized by Conlisk and Elster.

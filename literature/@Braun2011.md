> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper introduces collective bargaining at the firm and at the sector level into the heterogeneous firm model of Melitz and Ottaviano (Melitz, M. J., Ottaviano, G. I. P., 2008. Market size, trade, and productivity. Review of Economic Studies 75 (1), 295–316). It then analyses how the two bargaining regimes change aggregate industry productivity and firm performance relative to a competitive labour market. While sector-level bargaining forces the least productive firms to exit and thus increases average productivity relative to the competitive benchmark, firm-level bargaining allows less productive firms to stay in the market and thus reduces average productivity. Sector-level bargaining also results in higher average output and profit levels than either firm-level bargaining or a competitive labour market. The paper also shows that the choice between sector- and firm-level bargaining can involve a trade-off between product variety and product prices: Not only the average price level but also product variety tends to be lower under sector-level bargaining than under firm-level bargaining.

## Summary

Braun grafts collective bargaining onto the [[@Melitz2008|Melitz–Ottaviano (2008)]] model of monopolistic competition with heterogeneous firms and endogenous mark-ups, and asks how the *level* of wage centralisation reshapes the equilibrium productivity distribution rather than just one firm's productivity. The central message is that the bargaining regime endogenously determines which firms survive: sector-level (centralised) bargaining imposes a uniform wage floor that prices the least productive firms out, raising average productivity, output and profits; firm-level (decentralised) bargaining sets productivity-increasing wages that shield weak firms, lowering average productivity. Both regimes soften competition relative to the flexible-wage benchmark, and both reduce consumer surplus — though the centralised-vs-decentralised ranking of consumer surplus is ambiguous and hinges on how high the negotiated wage floor is.

## Research question

How do *unionisation structures* — specifically the degree of wage-setting centralisation (firm-level vs sector-level bargaining) — affect aggregate industry productivity, firm performance (output, profits, mark-ups), product variety, and consumer surplus, once one accounts for the fact that unionisation changes the *number and characteristics* of firms that survive in equilibrium, not just outcomes for a fixed set of firms?

## Method / identification

A two-sector general-equilibrium-flavoured (actually partial-equilibrium, since quasi-linear utility sweeps income effects into the numeraire) model. The representative consumer has quasi-linear preferences over a continuum of differentiated varieties and a numeraire, giving linear inverse demand $p_i=\alpha-\gamma q_i-\eta Q^c$ and a price elasticity that rises with the toughness of competition — summarised by an upper price bound $p_{\max}=\frac{1}{\eta N+\gamma}(\gamma\alpha+\eta N\bar p)$. Firms pay sunk entry cost $f_E$, then draw a cost level $c$ from a Pareto-implied distribution $G(c)=(c/c_M)^k$ (shape $k$), produce under constant returns and monopolistic competition, and exit if they cannot cover marginal cost. The cost cut-off is $c_{\max}=p_{\max}/w(c_{\max})$ and free entry drives expected profits to zero: $\int_0^{c_{\max}}\pi(c)\,dG(c)=f_E$.

Three wage-determination regimes $\rho\in\{P,U,D\}$ are compared: competitive ($w^P=1$); centralised/sector-level, a uniform binding floor $w^U=\theta>1$; and decentralised/firm-level, where $N$ firm-specific unions maximise rents $[w(c)-w^P]E(c)$ under right-to-manage. The firm-level wage solves $w^D(c)=\frac{\varepsilon_w(c)}{\varepsilon_w(c)-1}w^P$ and works out to $w^D(c)=\frac{c+p_{\max}}{2c}$, decreasing in $c$ (higher wages for more productive firms, because their labour demand is less elastic). The analysis first fixes $p_{\max}$ to isolate a **selection effect** (centralised bargaining lowers the cut-off) and a **direct profit effect** (both regimes cut per-firm profit), then endogenises $p_{\max}$ via free entry to obtain an **anti-competitive effect** that raises the price bound under both bargaining regimes. Results are stated as a sequence of orderings (Propositions 1–5).

## Data

None — this is a purely theoretical contribution. The paper cites empirical regularities for motivation and calibration plausibility (inter-firm wage differentials falling with centralisation: Teulings & Hartog 1998; Hibbs & Locking 2000; Pareto productivity with $k\approx2$: Del Gatto et al. 2006; Swedish solidarity-bargaining productivity evidence: Hibbs & Locking 2000), but estimates nothing.

## Key findings

- **Proposition 1 (cut-offs and competition):** equilibrium cost cut-offs order as $c^D_{\max}>c^P_{\max}>c^U_{\max}$, so selection is *tightest under centralised* bargaining and *loosest under firm-level* bargaining; the upper price bound is *lowest (competition toughest) in the competitive market*, $p^P_{\max}<\min[p^U_{\max},p^D_{\max}]$, with $p^U_{\max}\gtrless p^D_{\max}$ as $\theta^k\gtrless4$.
- **Proposition 2 (performance):** average cost orders $\bar c^D>\bar c^P>\bar c^U$ (so average *productivity* is highest under sector-level bargaining), and both average output $\bar q$ and average profit $\bar\pi$ order $U>P>D$. Centralised bargaining raises average productivity, output and profits relative to *both* other regimes; firm-level bargaining lowers all three.
- **Within-firm distribution:** bargaining creates winners and losers. Under centralised bargaining, high-productivity firms gain (higher mark-ups outweigh higher wages) while low-productivity firms lose; under firm-level bargaining the reverse holds, since firm-specific wages rise with productivity.
- **Proposition 3 (prices):** the *average price level* is higher under both bargaining regimes than in the benchmark and highest under decentralised bargaining for a given $p_{\max}$; the *variance of prices* is highest under centralised and lowest under firm-level bargaining (wage compression compresses prices).
- **Proposition 4 (variety):** $N^P>N^U$ always; $N^D$ can exceed or fall short of $N^P$, with high demand $\alpha$ favouring more variety under decentralisation.
- **Proposition 5 (consumer surplus):** $CS^P>\min[CS^U,CS^D]$ — both bargaining regimes reduce consumer surplus relative to the competitive benchmark. The centralised-vs-decentralised ranking flips at a threshold $\theta'>4$: $CS^U>CS^D$ iff $\theta^k<\theta'$, i.e., centralised bargaining can be better for consumers when the wage floor is close to the competitive wage.

## Contribution

It is the first to embed both firm-level and sector-level wage bargaining into a Melitz–Ottaviano heterogeneous-firm model with endogenous mark-ups, and to show that *the bargaining regime selects the surviving firm population*. This reframes the long-standing "what do unions do to productivity" literature: rather than estimating the effect of union status on a fixed firm, the paper argues the sample of surviving firms is itself an equilibrium object of the bargaining regime, so standard cross-firm comparisons can be biased. It identifies and names three mechanisms — selection, direct-profit, and anti-competitive effects — and delivers sharp, sign-definite predictions for productivity, profits, variety and welfare that can guide empirical work exploiting variation in wage centralisation across countries/industries and over time (e.g., the 1980s Swedish and Danish decentralisations).

## Limitations & open questions

- **Partial-equilibrium income channel:** quasi-linear utility shuts down income effects, so $p_{\max}$ depends only on $N$ and $\bar p$, not income. Braun explicitly notes that in general equilibrium unionisation could additionally affect the cut-off through disposable income, and that a GE model with variable demand elasticities (building on Behrens & Murata 2007; Behrens et al. 2009) would yield further insights — flagged as beyond the paper's scope.
- **Exogenous productivity:** firms cannot influence their cost draw; the model abstracts entirely from innovation/effort responses to unionisation, which is exactly where much of the hold-up and innovation-incentive literature lives.
- **Exogenous, reduced-form centralised wage:** the uniform floor $\theta$ is imposed rather than derived; a closed-form monopoly-union derivation requires assuming the industry union ignores its effect on $p_{\max}$.
- **Single occupation / homogeneous labour:** all workers do the same job, so the model cannot speak to within-plant inter-occupational wage differentials (and the offsetting Hibbs–Locking finding there).
- **No empirical test:** the predictions are offered as hypotheses; the natural next step is to bring them to data on changing wage-centralisation regimes.

## Connections

The framework is a direct extension of [[@Melitz2008|Melitz & Ottaviano (2008)]], itself building on Melitz (2003), and inherits the Pareto-productivity assumption validated by Del Gatto et al. (2006). The core "wage compression aids productivity through tougher selection" result is closest to Moene & Wallerstein (1997), who formalise Swedish solidaristic bargaining (Rehn 1952) in a vintage-capital model with exogenous output prices — Braun's value added is endogenous competition intensity and the price/variety trade-off. On unionisation and innovation incentives in fixed market structures, the paper contrasts with Haucap & Wey (2004), and with the hold-up tradition of Grout (1984) and Van der Ploeg (1987) and the strategic-investment counterpoints of Tauman & Weiss (1987) and Ulph & Ulph (1994, 2001). The productivity-increasing firm-level wage echoes fair-wage heterogeneous-firm models of Egger & Kreickemeier (2009) and contrasts with Eckel & Egger (2009), where CES demand yields a common wage; the labour-demand-elasticity logic draws on Dowrick & Spencer (1994) and Lommerud et al. (2006). The "voice" view of unions traces to Freeman & Medoff (1984), and the survey evidence on the inconclusive net effect to Doucouliagos & Laroche (2003), Metcalf (2003) and Hirsch (2004). Empirical anchors on wage compression and centralisation come from Teulings & Hartog (1998) and Hibbs & Locking (2000).

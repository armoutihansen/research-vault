> [!abstract] Abstract
> Numerous experimental studies indicate that people tend to reciprocate favors and punish unfair behavior. It is hypothesized that these behavioral responses contribute to the enforcement of contracts and, hence, increase gains from trade. It turns out that if only one side of the market has opportunities for reciprocal responses, the impact of reciprocity on contract enforcement depends on the details of the pecuniary incentive system. If both sides of the market have opportunities for reciprocal responses, robust and powerful reciprocity effects occur. In particular, reciprocal behavior causes a substantial increase in the set of enforceable actions and, hence, large efficiency gains.

## Summary

Fehr, Gächter, and Kirchsteiger run a series of laboratory labor-market experiments to test whether reciprocity (in the sense of [[@Rabin1993|Rabin 1993]]) eases the incentive-compatibility constraints that limit contract enforcement under moral hazard. They contrast a no-reciprocity control, a one-sided ("weak") reciprocity treatment in which only workers can reciprocate, and a two-sided ("strong") reciprocity treatment that adds a costly firm punishment/reward stage. One-sided reciprocity raises wages and effort above the selfish prediction but its strength is sensitive to the pecuniary details; two-sided reciprocity produces robust, powerful effects, expanding the set of enforceable effort levels and yielding a Pareto improvement for both firms and workers.

## Research question

Standard contract theory derives incentive-compatibility constraints assuming fully rational, purely selfish agents, which caps the set of enforceable contracts and pushes outcomes away from first-best. Can reciprocal motivations enlarge the set of enforceable contracts and generate efficiency gains beyond what selfish incentive-compatibility allows, and does this depend on whether one or both sides of a trade can reciprocate? Whether reciprocity is strong enough to overcome enforcement problems is treated as an empirical question.

## Method / identification

The design is a competitive labor market with $N<L$ (here typically 6 firms, 8 workers), so there is exogenous excess supply giving firms the power to extract zero worker rents. Three treatments operationalize the comparative statics:

- **NRT (no reciprocity, control):** effort is exogenously fixed at $e=0.2$; firms cannot elicit reciprocity.
- **WRT (weak / two-stage):** firms simultaneously post contracts $(w,e,f)$ — wage, demanded effort, fine. A worker accepts, then chooses actual effort $e$. With probability $s$ shirking ($e<\bar e$) is verifiable and the worker pays fine $f$. Worker utility is $u^{ns}=w-c(e)$ if not shirking and $u^{s}=(1-s)[w-c(e)]+s[w-f-c(e)]$ if shirking; firm profit is $\pi=(q-w)e$. A worker provides $\bar e$ iff $sf\ge c(\bar e)$. The fine is capped at an exogenous $f^0$, which caps the enforceable effort $e^0$ via $sf^0=c(e^0)$. The selfish/risk-neutral subgame-perfect prediction is zero job rent and minimal effort: $(r^*,e^*,f^*)=(0,0.1,10)$, where job rent is $r=w-c(\bar e)$.
- **SRT (strong / three-stage):** identical to WRT, plus a third stage in which the firm, after observing effort, picks $p\in[0,2]$ that multiplies the worker's stage-1–2 gain at cost $k(p)$ to the firm ($k$ decreasing on $(0,1)$, increasing on $(1,2)$, $k(0)=0$). Worker payoff becomes $u^{ns}=[w-c(e)]\,p$; firm payoff $\pi=(q-w)e-k(p)$. Under common-knowledge selfishness the firm always sets $p=1$, so the third stage is payoff-irrelevant and predictions collapse to the WRT.

Identification of reciprocity is by treatment contrast: NRT vs. WRT isolates firms' attempt to elicit worker reciprocity (since reciprocity is impossible under fixed effort), and WRT vs. SRT isolates the added effect of firm-side reciprocity. Anonymity (separate rooms, telephone messaging, no identity revelation) rules out reputation/repeated-game channels, and full common knowledge of parameters and payoffs lets each party compute the other's monetary gains (subjects pre-solved hypothetical examples). Reciprocity predictions are qualitative (signs of comparative statics), following Rabin's "crude reference point." Estimation uses OLS of rent on effort $r_{ti}=a_1+a_2 e_{ti}+\varepsilon_{ti}$ and probit models of the non-shirking probability $O_{ti}=\beta_1+\beta_2(u^{ns}_{ti}-u^{s}_{ti})+\beta_3 r_{ti}+\beta_4\sum_{j} u_{ji}+\varepsilon_{ti}$, plus nonparametric tests (Wilcoxon, Spearman, Kolmogorov–Smirnov, chi-square, robust rank-order).

## Data

Original experimental data from eighteen sessions: S1–S4 (NRT), S5–S10 (WRT), S11–S12 (SRT), plus robustness sessions S13–S16 (firms can make losses; pure lump-sum wages; risk preferences neutralized) and S17–S18 (backward-induction test via scaled $k(p)$ costs). Subjects were mainly engineering/computer-science students from the Universities of Technology in Vienna and Zurich. Main sessions yielded 353 actual trades in the NRT, 509 in the WRT, and 144 in the SRT.

## Key findings

- **R1:** Firms persistently demanded effort above the selfish level $e^0=0.1$, but high shirking kept actual average effort near $e^0$ in the WRT.
- **R2:** Firms paid higher job rents when demanding higher effort ($a_2>0$, highly significant pooled; positive Spearman $r$–$e$ correlation for 29 of 33 firms) — contradicting the standard prediction of zero rents.
- **R3:** Workers on average reciprocate — raising $r$ raises the probability of non-shirking ($\beta_3>0$, significant pooled), even controlling for the larger pecuniary incentive to shirk that accompanies higher effort.
- **R4:** Average rents are higher in the WRT than NRT; rents above 20 are essentially absent in the NRT but ~30% (over 40% in the last three periods) in the WRT.
- **R5:** High-effort/high-rent strategies ($e=0.2,0.3$) yield higher average firm profits than low-effort/low-rent ones, confirmed by chi-square and rank correlations and by expected-profit maximization on the probit estimates.
- **R6–R10 (SRT):** Firms demand and enforce far higher effort; firms do punish shirkers and reward compliant workers; workers anticipate this and shirk much less (shirking falls from ~65% in WRT to ~21% in SRT); and the outcome is a Pareto improvement — average gains per trade rise from 17 to 24 ATS for workers and from 6 to 42 ATS for firms.

Robustness (S13–S16) with losses and pure-transfer wages reproduces the qualitative patterns; reciprocity is even stronger in the modified WRT, equally strong in the modified SRT. The backward-induction sessions (S17–S18) show behavior responds systematically to scaled reciprocity costs, ruling out "failure to backward-induct" as the explanation.

## Contribution

Provides early, clean experimental evidence that reciprocity functions as an endogenous contract-enforcement device, enlarging the set of enforceable actions and generating efficiency gains beyond the selfish incentive-compatible frontier. It demonstrates that two-sided reciprocity is far more robust than one-sided reciprocity, and argues that principal-agent theory that neglects reciprocity may be seriously incomplete — both in predictive power and in normative (optimal-contract) conclusions. This paper is foundational for the gift-exchange / fairness-in-labor-markets literature and for later models of social preferences.

## Limitations & open questions

- The authors emphasize there is **no general theory locating reference standards** and **no empirical method for the exact determination of reference points**, so predictions are necessarily qualitative (signs only), not quantitative. Building such a theory/measurement is left open.
- The reference standard is assumed (job rent for workers; acceptance of $\bar e$ as the firm's reference), not derived; heterogeneity in workers' reference standards is acknowledged but not modeled.
- The third stage is explicitly **not** intended as a literal map of real-world institutions; how reward/punishment opportunities actually arise in long-term, incompletely contracted relationships is left informal.
- In the original WRT, reciprocity strength is sensitive to pecuniary details (e.g., whether firms can make losses, random verification and risk); pinning down which features drive this sensitivity is open.
- One-shot, anonymous, student-subject lab design; external validity to real labor markets and the interaction of reciprocity with reputation in repeated settings are not resolved here.

## Connections

The reciprocity model is taken directly from [[@Rabin1993|Rabin (1993)]], whose fairness equilibrium underlies the qualitative predictions. The empirical motivation builds on the authors' own gift-exchange experiments ([[@Fehr1993|Fehr, Kirchsteiger, and Riedl 1993]], 1996; Fehr, Kirchler, Weichbold, and Gächter 1994) and on Berg, Dickhaut, and McCabe (1995, trust game) for positive reciprocity, and on ultimatum-game evidence (Güth and Tietz 1990; Roth 1995; [[@Benartzi1995|Camerer and Thaler 1995]]) for negative reciprocity / punishment of unfairness. It situates itself against standard contract/mechanism theory (Laffont and Maskin 1982; Grossman and Hart 1983; Hart and Holmström 1987; Milgrom and Roberts 1992) and uses the third stage to sidestep folk-theorem multiplicity (Fudenberg and Maskin 1986). It is a direct precursor to outcome-based social-preference models ([[@Fehr1999|Fehr and Schmidt 1999]]; [[@Bolton2000|Bolton and Ockenfels 2000]]) and to the broader strong-reciprocity literature.

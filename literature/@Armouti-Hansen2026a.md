> [!abstract] Abstract
> We show that choosing a non-centralized distribution channel—that is, a retailer or an intermediary (e.g., sales representative, social media influencer)—instead of a centralized channel for selling goods with uncertain consumption quality can be optimal for a manufacturer as a self-commitment device. By this choice, the manufacturer can save costs from interacting with consumers (e.g., costs for sales activities). We derive conditions under which this self-commitment argument holds. In addition, we show that decentralized selling through a retailer can be even optimal if the manufacturer has to reimburse the retailer for the anticipated costs from consumer interaction, and if the manufacturer and the retailer simultaneously exert sales activities, which eliminates the self-commitment property of a retailer. In a second step, we discuss our self-commitment result under product competition, consumer naivety, product innovation, and product quality improvement.

## Summary
This theory paper (Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel, dated April 2026) asks when a manufacturer of a good with *uncertain consumption quality* (experience/trust goods: cars, smartphones, drugs, financial products) should sell through a centralized channel (sell directly, manage interaction) versus a non-centralized channel — a retailer (decentralization) or an intermediary that interacts with consumers while the manufacturer keeps pricing/ownership (hybrid selling). The novel mechanism is that non-centralization functions as a *self-commitment device* not to invest in costly, ultimately futile sales activities. Coverage is full: abstract, introduction, related literature, basic model, all of Section 4 (centralized, decentralized, comparison, three corollaries), all five Section-5 extensions, and the conclusion. Proofs live in the online supplement (`IJIO_Supplement.pdf`).

## Research question
Under what conditions does a manufacturer of a good with common (two-sided) quality uncertainty strictly prefer to delegate consumer interaction — to a retailer or an intermediary — rather than sell centrally, and how is that preference reshaped by competition, consumer naivety, product innovation, and quality improvement?

## Method / identification
This is a pure game-theoretic IO paper; no data. The core friction is *signal jamming* in the Holmström (1999) tradition. Quality $q\sim N(\bar q,\sigma^2)$ is common knowledge in distribution but its realization is unknown to both sides. Consumers observe one additive signal $s=q+\theta+a$, with noise $\theta\sim N(0,\sigma_\theta^2)$ and endogenous, demand-exaggerating sales activity $a\in[0,\bar a]$ at convex cost $c(a)=\tfrac{\kappa}{2}a^2$. Bayesian updating gives posterior $E[q\mid s]=\bar q+\tfrac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2+\sigma_\theta^2}(s-\bar q-\hat a)$, where $\hat a$ is consumers' equilibrium belief about $a$. Demand is linear, $D(p)=1+d-\beta p$, with a quality-driven inelastic component $d\in\{d_L,d_H\}$; the high state arrives with probability $P(E[q\mid s]\ge\hat q)$ for an exogenous reference point $\hat q$ (interpreted as inverse brand loyalty: $\hat q<\bar q$ = high loyalty). Expected demand becomes $E[D(p)]=1+d_L+\Delta d\,[1-F(\Psi+\hat a-a)]-\beta p$ with $\Delta d:=d_H-d_L$, $\Psi:=(\hat q-\bar q)\tfrac{\sigma^2+\sigma_\theta^2}{\sigma^2}+\bar q$, and $F$ the cdf of $q+\theta$. The solution concept is pure-strategy Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium, solved by backward induction; a sufficiently large $\kappa$ guarantees concavity (the technical lemma conditions). Under centralization the manufacturer jointly picks $(a,p)$; under decentralization the manufacturer sets wholesale $w$, then the retailer picks $(a,p)$ — introducing double marginalization; under hybrid selling the manufacturer sets $p$ and offers an intermediary a contract $(S,r)$ (fixed pay + commission rate), a moral-hazard problem.

## Data
None — this is a theory paper. The only empirical material is an illustrative Table 1 of Apple/Samsung centralized-vs-non-centralized sales-channel shares (2014–2024) from annual reports, used purely as motivating anecdote.

## Key findings
The *equilibrium trap*: in PBE, consumers anticipate $\hat a=a^*$, so exaggeration cancels and $a$ yields no demand gain — yet the selling party must still incur $c(a^*)$, since cutting $a$ below $a^*$ would bias beliefs downward. Lemma 1 (centralized) and Lemma 2 (decentralized) give closed-form $p^*,a^*,w^*$ and profits; notably $p^*_{Dec}=\tfrac32 w^*$. **Proposition 1**: $p^*_{Cen}<p^*_{Dec}$ and $a^*_{Cen}>a^*_{Dec}$, and the manufacturer prefers decentralization iff $\tfrac{\Delta d^2}{\beta\kappa}f(\Psi)^2>1$ — i.e. when returns to sales activity ($\Delta d$) are large and marginal sales cost ($\kappa$) and price elasticity ($\beta$) are small. Decentralization is a costly commitment: it saves $c(a^*_{Cen})$ but incurs double marginalization. **Corollary 1**: even when the self-commitment property is destroyed (manufacturer and retailer *both* exert $a$), decentralization can still win iff $\tfrac{\Delta d^2}{\beta\kappa}f(\Psi)^2>\tfrac{1+\sqrt5}{2}$ — here double marginalization *helps* by lowering the retailer's sales-effort incentive. **Corollary 2**: if the manufacturer reimburses the retailer's anticipated $c(a^*_{Dec})$, decentralization wins iff $\tfrac{\Delta d^2}{\beta\kappa}f(\Psi)^2>\tfrac43$. **Corollary 3**: with an unconstrained-wealth retailer, an optimal two-part tariff makes decentralization *strictly* dominate centralization, but with a strictly positive $w^*>0$ (not $w=0$) to balance double marginalization against the equilibrium trap. Extensions: **Prop. 2** — manufacturer competition (substitutability $\alpha\in[-1,1]$) further favors decentralization regardless of substitutes/complements, via the wholesale-price channel. **Prop. 3** — consumer naivety ($\hat a<a^*$) raises demand under both modes; it can give centralization a *relative* edge (double marginalization blocks full demand capture), but decentralization still wins on absolute profit when $\kappa$ is small. **Prop. 4 / Lemma 3** — product innovation (higher $\sigma^2$) raises sales only under low brand loyalty ($\hat q\ge\bar q$) and then favors decentralization iff $(\hat q-\bar q)^2>\tfrac{\sigma^6}{(2\sigma_\theta^2+\sigma^2)(\sigma_\theta^2+\sigma^2)}$. **Prop. 5** — a quality improvement (higher $\bar q$) favors decentralization iff $\hat q>\bar q$. **Lemma 5 / Prop. 6** — hybrid selling: optimal contract is $(S^*,r^*)=(\bar u,0)$ (zero/low-powered incentives kill the equilibrium trap); hybrid beats centralization when the intermediary's outside option $\bar u$ is low, and decentralization beats hybrid when $\bar u$ is high. **Prop. 7** maps each extension onto hybrid selling (e.g. hybrid is *dominated* by centralization under consumer naivety unless the intermediary has a cost advantage).

## Contribution
Two-fold. (1) To the centralization-vs-decentralization channel literature (Spengler's double marginalization; Mathewson & Winter; Moorthy; Tirole) it adds a genuinely new rationale for decentralization: a retailer/intermediary as a *self-commitment device* against wasteful signal-jamming, distinct from the standard vertical-restraint, manufacturer-competition, or fairness-concern arguments. (2) To the signal-jamming / quality-exaggeration literature (Holmström 1999; Dellarocas 2006; Grunewald & Kräkel 2017, 2022; Aköz et al. 2020) it adds the channel-choice dimension and the self-commitment effect, which that literature had not considered. It also extends strategic-delegation logic (Vickers; Fershtman & Judd; Sklivas) with a new commitment motive: delegating to escape the equilibrium trap rather than to alter market aggressiveness.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag the *static* nature of the model as the headline limitation: brand loyalty (the reference point $\hat q$) is exogenous, and a dynamic model endogenizing it as a function of past quality realizations is left open — this is the explicit future-research hook. Further open problems implicit in the structure: the single, exogenously-additive signal and quadratic cost are stylized; the knife-edge $(S^*,r^*)=(\bar u,0)$ contract is acknowledged as too extreme relative to real low-powered-but-positive incentive schemes; wealth-unconstrained retailers (needed for Corollary 3's strict dominance) are admittedly unrealistic; consumers are assumed to observe wholesale price / intermediary compensation exactly (a partial-observability relaxation is only gestured at in a footnote); intermediaries are assumed to share the manufacturer's cost function except where noted, so the empirically central case of a *specialized* intermediary (a social media influencer with a flatter $c(a)$) is left largely unmodeled. An empirical/structural test of the channel-choice prediction against data like Table 1 is also absent.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> We show that choosing a non-centralized distribution channel—that is, a retailer or an intermediary (e.g., sales representative, social media influencer)—instead of a centralized channel for selling goods with uncertain consumption quality can be optimal for a manufacturer as a self-commitment device. By this choice, the manufacturer can save costs from interacting with consumers (e.g., costs for sales activities). We derive conditions under which this self-commitment argument holds. In addition, we show that decentralized selling through a retailer can be even optimal if the manufacturer has to reimburse the retailer for the anticipated costs from consumer interaction, and if the manufacturer and the retailer simultaneously exert sales activities, which eliminates the self-commitment property of a retailer. In a second step, we discuss our self-commitment result under product competition, consumer naivety, product innovation, and product quality improvement.

## Summary
This theory paper (Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel, dated April 2026, 42 pp.) asks when a manufacturer of a good with *uncertain consumption quality* (experience/trust goods: cars, smartphones, drugs, financial products) should sell through a centralized channel (sell directly, manage consumer interaction) versus a non-centralized channel — a retailer (decentralization) or an intermediary who interacts with consumers while the manufacturer keeps pricing and ownership (hybrid selling). The novel mechanism is that non-centralization functions as a *self-commitment device* not to invest in costly, ultimately futile sales activities aimed at exaggerating product quality. ## Research question
Under what conditions does a manufacturer of a good with common (two-sided) quality uncertainty strictly prefer to delegate consumer interaction — to a retailer or to an intermediary — rather than sell centrally, and how is that preference reshaped by manufacturer competition, consumer naivety, product innovation, and product quality improvement?

## Method / identification
A pure game-theoretic IO paper; no data. The core friction is *signal jamming* in the Holmström (1999) tradition. The good's quality $q\sim N(\bar q,\sigma^2)$ has a commonly known distribution but an unknown realization (a two-sided matching problem: the manufacturer knows features, consumers know their own tastes). Consumers observe a single additive signal $s=q+\theta+a$, with exogenous noise $\theta\sim N(0,\sigma_\theta^2)$ and endogenous, quality-exaggerating sales activity $a\in[0,\bar a]$ at convex cost $c(a)=\tfrac{\kappa}{2}a^2$. Bayesian updating gives the posterior $E[q\mid s]=\bar q+\tfrac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2+\sigma_\theta^2}(s-\bar q-\hat a)$, where $\hat a$ is consumers' equilibrium belief about $a$. Demand is linear, $D(p)=1+d-\beta p$, with a quality-driven price-inelastic component $d\in\{d_L,d_H\}$ ($d_H>d_L>0$); the high state arrives with probability $P(E[q\mid s]\ge\hat q)$ for an exogenous reference point $\hat q$, interpreted as inverse brand loyalty ($\hat q<\bar q$ = high loyalty; $\hat q\ge\bar q$ = low loyalty). Expected demand becomes $E[D(p)]=1+d_L+\Delta d\,[1-F(\Psi+\hat a-a)]-\beta p$ with $\Delta d:=d_H-d_L$, $\Psi:=(\hat q-\bar q)\tfrac{\sigma^2+\sigma_\theta^2}{\sigma^2}+\bar q$, and $F$ the cdf of the composite $q+\theta\sim N(\bar q,\sigma^2+\sigma_\theta^2)$. The solution concept is pure-strategy Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium, solved by backward induction; a sufficiently large $\kappa$ guarantees concavity of the objective (the technical lemma conditions, analogous to the cdf-driven objectives in rank-order-tournament theory). The three modes differ in who chooses $(a,p)$: under centralization the manufacturer jointly picks $(a,p)$; under decentralization the manufacturer sets a wholesale price $w$, then the retailer (the new owner) picks $(a,p)$, introducing double marginalization; under hybrid selling the manufacturer sets $p$ and offers an intermediary an incentive contract $(S,r)$ (fixed pay $S$ plus commission rate $r$ on realized sales) — a moral-hazard problem with the intermediary's $a$ as a hidden action.

## Key findings
The *equilibrium trap* is the engine: in PBE, consumers correctly anticipate $\hat a=a^*$, so the exaggeration cancels out and $a$ yields no demand gain — yet the selling party must still incur $c(a^*)$, because cutting $a$ below $a^*$ would bias the market's beliefs downward (Holmström's career-concerns logic). Lemma 1 (centralized) and Lemma 2 (decentralized) deliver closed-form $p^*,a^*,w^*$ and profits; notably $p^*_{Dec}=\tfrac32 w^*$ and the manufacturer's decentralized profit is twice the retailer's. **Proposition 1**: $p^*_{Cen}<p^*_{Dec}$ and $a^*_{Cen}>a^*_{Dec}$, and the manufacturer prefers decentralization iff $\tfrac{\Delta d^2}{\beta\kappa}f(\Psi)^2>1$ — i.e. when returns to sales activity ($\Delta d$) are large and marginal sales cost ($\kappa$) and price elasticity ($\beta$) are small. Decentralization is a costly commitment: it saves $c(a^*_{Cen})$ but imposes double marginalization. **Corollary 1**: even when the self-commitment property is destroyed (manufacturer and retailer *both* exert $a$), decentralization can still win iff $\tfrac{\Delta d^2}{\beta\kappa}f(\Psi)^2>\tfrac{1+\sqrt5}{2}$ — here double marginalization *helps*, by lowering the retailer's sales-effort incentive. **Corollary 2**: if the manufacturer reimburses the retailer's anticipated $c(a^*_{Dec})$, decentralization wins iff $\tfrac{\Delta d^2}{\beta\kappa}f(\Psi)^2>\tfrac43$. **Corollary 3**: with a wealth-unconstrained retailer, an optimal two-part tariff makes decentralization *strictly* dominate centralization — but via a strictly positive $w^*>0$ (not $w=0$), which fine-tunes the retailer's effort to balance double marginalization against the equilibrium trap. Extensions: **Prop. 2** — manufacturer competition (substitutability $\alpha\in[-1,1]\setminus\{0\}$) further favors decentralization regardless of whether goods are substitutes or complements, through the wholesale-price channel. **Prop. 3** — consumer naivety ($\hat a<a^*$) raises demand under both modes and can give centralization a *relative* edge (double marginalization blocks full capture of the naivety-driven demand, and now $w^*<p^*_{Cen}$), yet decentralization still wins on absolute profit when $\kappa$ is small. **Lemma 3 / Prop. 4** — a product innovation (higher $\sigma^2$) raises expected sales only under low brand loyalty ($\hat q\ge\bar q$) and then additionally favors decentralization iff $(\hat q-\bar q)^2>\tfrac{\sigma^6}{(2\sigma_\theta^2+\sigma^2)(\sigma_\theta^2+\sigma^2)}$. **Lemma 4 / Prop. 5** — a quality improvement (higher $\bar q$) additionally favors decentralization iff $\hat q>\bar q$. **Lemma 5 / Prop. 6** — hybrid selling: the optimal contract is $(S^*,r^*)=(\bar u,0)$ with zero (i.e. very low-powered) incentives that undermine the equilibrium trap; hybrid beats centralization when the intermediary's outside option $\bar u$ is low, and decentralization beats hybrid when $\bar u$ is high (the double-marginalization loss is then worth bearing to avoid contracting costs). **Prop. 7** maps each extension onto hybrid selling — e.g. hybrid is *strictly dominated* by centralization under consumer naivety unless the intermediary has a cost advantage, while both product innovation and quality improvement favor hybrid over decentralization (the manufacturer's expected sales under hybrid are twice those under decentralization). ## Contribution
Two-fold. (1) To the centralization-vs-decentralization channel literature (Spengler's double marginalization; Mathewson & Winter; Moorthy; Tirole; recent work on fairness concerns and agency-vs-reselling) it adds a genuinely new rationale for decentralization: a retailer or intermediary serving as a *self-commitment device* against wasteful signal jamming, distinct from the standard vertical-restraint, manufacturer-competition, or fairness-concern arguments. (2) To the signal-jamming / quality-exaggeration literature (Holmström 1999; Dellarocas 2006; Grunewald & Kräkel 2017, 2022; Aköz et al. 2020) it adds the channel-choice dimension and the self-commitment effect, which that strand had not considered. It also extends strategic-delegation logic (Vickers; Fershtman & Judd; Sklivas) with a new commitment motive — delegating to escape the equilibrium trap rather than to make the agent behave more or less aggressively in the market.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag the *static* nature of the model as the headline limitation: brand loyalty (the reference point $\hat q$) is exogenous, and a dynamic model endogenizing it as a function of past quality realizations is the explicit future-research hook (it would also re-test decentralization/intermediaries as commitment devices). Further open problems implicit in the structure: the single additive signal and quadratic cost are stylized; the knife-edge contract $(S^*,r^*)=(\bar u,0)$ is acknowledged as too extreme relative to real low-powered-but-positive incentive schemes; the wealth-unconstrained retailer needed for Corollary 3's strict dominance is admittedly unrealistic; consumers are assumed to observe the wholesale price / intermediary compensation exactly (a partial-observability relaxation is only gestured at in footnotes); and intermediaries are assumed to share the manufacturer's cost function except where noted, so the empirically central case of a *specialized* intermediary (a social-media influencer with a flatter $c(a)$) is left largely unmodeled. An empirical or structural test of the channel-choice prediction against data like Table 1 is also absent.

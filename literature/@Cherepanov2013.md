> [!abstract] Abstract
> In 1908, the Welsh neurologist and psychoanalyst Ernest Jones described human beings as rationalizers whose behavior is governed by "the necessity of providing an explanation." We construct a formal and testable model of rationalization in which a decision maker selects her preferred alternative from among those that she can rationalize.

## Summary
The paper builds a decision-theoretic model of *rationalization*: a decision maker (Dee) has stable preferences but faces possibly unobservable psychological constraints, and she chooses her most-preferred option among those she can *justify* to herself. To rationalize an option is to find at least one subjectively acceptable "story" (a binary relation, called a rationale) under which that option is best. This makes choice a constrained optimization where the constraint set is the set of rationalizable options. The model can accommodate cyclic choices and WARP violations while preserving a single stable preference order, yet it remains testable (characterized by Weak WARP, or by No Binary Chain Cycles under ordered preferences). The authors then introduce a *minimum constraint* refinement that, combined with permissibility (nonchoice) assumptions, uniquely identifies preferences and constraints across a range of anomalous-but-acyclic choice patterns, coinciding with standard revealed-preference theory whenever behavior is non-anomalous.

## Research question
Can the informal psychological notion of rationalization (Jones 1908, Freud) be turned into a formal, *testable* theory of choice? Specifically: if the inability to rationalize imposes constraints on behavior, what structure does the need-to-rationalize impose on those constraints, what is the empirical content of the resulting theory, and when can an analyst recover ("identify") the agent's underlying preferences and psychological constraints from observed (and possibly nonchoice) data?

## Method / identification
Pure decision theory; no data or experiments of their own. Let $A$ be a finite alternative set, an *issue* a nonempty $B\subseteq A$, and $C:\mathcal{B}\to A$ a choice function with $C(B)\in B$. A *preference* $P$ is an asymmetric binary relation (an *order* if also complete and transitive; the focus is on orders $P^o$). A *psychological constraint function* $\psi$ maps each issue $B$ to a nonempty $\psi(B)\subseteq B$ of *permissible* options. A *model of behavior* is a pair $(P,\psi)$ that *underlies* $C$ if for every $B$, $C(B)\in\psi(B)$ and $C(B)\mathbin{P}y$ for all $y\in\psi(B)\setminus\{C(B)\}$ — i.e. Dee picks her $P$-best option among the permissible ones.

The behavioral content comes from *rationales*: a rationale is any binary relation $R$ (need not be complete, transitive, or asymmetric), and $x$ is *rationalized* by $R$ in issue $B$ iff $x\mathbin{R}y$ for all $y\in B\setminus\{x\}$. Given a set of rationales $\mathcal{R}$, the induced constraint $\psi_{\mathcal{R}}(B)$ is the set of options rationalizable by *some* $R\in\mathcal{R}$. The *basic theory of rationalization* is $P\times\bar{\Psi}$ and *order rationalization* is $P^o\times\bar{\Psi}$, where $\bar{\Psi}$ are the constraint functions generated by some $\mathcal{R}$.

The key structural lemma: $\psi\in\bar{\Psi}$ iff it satisfies the *expansion/contraction* condition

$$B\subseteq B^{*}\ \Rightarrow\ \psi(B^{*})\cap B\subseteq\psi(B).$$

Intuitively, an option that can be justified in a larger menu can also be justified in any sub-menu (justifying $x$ gets harder as alternatives are added). There is no loss in restricting rationales to be transitive and asymmetric.

For identification, *permissibility assumptions* $A=\{(y_i,B_i)\}$ stipulate $y_i\in\psi(B_i)$ (nonchoice data: Dee *can* rationalize $y_i$ in $B_i$). A preference order $\dot P$ is *identifiable* if some permissibility assumptions make order $A$-rationalization pin it down uniquely. A model $(P,\psi)$ is *dominated* by $(P',\psi')$ if $P'$ is an order and $\psi(B)\subseteq\psi'(B)$ for all $B$ with strict inclusion somewhere; the *minimum constraint theory* keeps only undominated models (those using no more constraints than needed).

## Data
None — this is a theory paper. It revisits published experimental and field evidence as motivating illustrations: Snyder et al. (1979) on handicapped avoidance (movie alone vs. with a person in a wheelchair), the Thomas Schelling encyclopedia anecdote ([[@Shafir1993|Shafir et al. 1993]]) and the [[@Iyengar2000|Iyengar–Lepper (2000)]] jam study as "difficult choice" anomalies, and the Berger–Smith (1997) donation field study as an attraction-effect case used to show when rationalization should be *rejected*.

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 (empirical content).** $C$ is consistent with basic rationalization iff it satisfies *Weak WARP*; $C$ is consistent with *order* rationalization iff it satisfies *No Binary Chain Cycles*. Hence order rationalization accommodates any choices over three alternatives and permits cyclic choice with a stable underlying order, yet remains falsifiable.
- **Proposition 2 (revealed preference).** Define $x\mathbin{Rev}y$ when anomalous choices give $x=C(B)$, $y=C(B^{*})$ with $B\subseteq B^{*}$. Under basic rationalization $x$ is revealed preferred to $y$ iff $x\mathbin{Rev}y$; under order rationalization, iff there is a $Rev$-chain from $x$ to $y$.
- **Theorem 1 (identification = minimum constraint).** A preference order $P$ is identifiable (via some permissibility assumptions) iff $(P,\psi)$ lies in the minimum constraint theory for some $\psi$. So only undominated, minimally-constrained models rest on identifiable preferences.
- **Propositions 3–4.** If $C$ is acyclic, minimum constraint theory reveals the order given by binary choices ($C(\{x,y\})=x\Rightarrow x$ revealed preferred to $y$), and the undominated constraint function is *unique* — so both preferences *and* constraints are identified. This recovers standard revealed preference under WARP and extends it to acyclic anomalies (e.g., the difficult-choice/Schelling pattern reveals $e_1\,P\,e_2\,P\,n$ with the only binding constraint in $\{e_1,e_2,n\}$).
- **Differentiation of theories.** The rational short list ([[@Manzini2007|Manzini–Mariotti 2007]]) is a strict special case of rationalization; *categorize-then-choose* ([[@Manzini2012a|Manzini–Mariotti 2012a]]) coincides empirically with basic rationalization and is strictly subsumed by order rationalization. Attention filters ([[@Masatlioglu2012|Masatlioglu et al. 2012]]) are neither nested in nor nesting rationalization, whereas *limited consideration* (Lleras et al. 2010) shares the *same formal structure* (condition (1)) despite a different interpretation; the two can still be separated using *interpretation-specific* permissibility/nonchoice assumptions (e.g., evidence that Dee "considered" vs. "can rationalize" an option).

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit about open problems, all flagged in Section 6.1 ("Future work") and the Conclusion:
- **Beyond Weak WARP.** A referee's four-alternative example (add "movie 1 with a favorite person", $w$) produces choices that violate Weak WARP and therefore cannot be accommodated. They call for a *more flexible yet still testable* theory that retains some identification of core elements.
- **Game-theoretic rationalization.** Rationales may be partly *social constructs*, so moral speech could change rationales and hence behavior; the paper notes (footnote) that rationalizers can generate reciprocity-like behavior and that examples are available on request, but a full strategic theory is left open.
- **Welfare analysis.** When preferences and choices clash (e.g., a moral prohibition blocks a life-saving procedure Dee actually prefers), should a planner respect preferences or choices? Rationalization identifies *when* they clash but the welfare implications are explicitly "unresolved."
- The literature review/comparison is acknowledged as non-comprehensive (several related models left uncommented).

## Connections
This sits in the boundedly-rational / consideration-set program in decision theory. It generalizes the *rational short list* ([[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti 2007]]) and its *categorize-then-choose* successor (2012a), and is formally isomorphic to *choice with limited consideration* (Lleras et al. 2010) while differing from *choice with limited attention* ([[@Masatlioglu2012|Masatlioglu, Nakajima, Ozbay 2012]]). The expansion condition and Weak WARP link it to Manzini–Mariotti and to [[@Ehlers2008|Ehlers and Sprumont (2008)]]. It is distinct from game-theoretic "rationalizability" (Bernheim 1984; Pearce 1984) and from cognitive-dissonance "rationalization" (Chen 2008), and connects to motivated-cognition work (Bénabou and Tirole 2002; [[@Akerlof1982|Akerlof and Dickens 1982]]; Rabin 1995), to multiple-selves / dual-self models ([[@Kalai2002|Kalai, Rubinstein, Spiegler 2002]]; Fudenberg and Levine 2006; [[@Gul2005|Gul and Pesendorfer 2005]]), to Spiegler's (2002, 2004) justification games, and to the nonchoice-data debate (Kreps 1990; [[@Dekel2010|Dekel and Lipman 2010]]) and the behavioral-welfare debate ([[@Bernheim2009|Bernheim and Rangel 2009]]; [[@Rubinstein2012|Rubinstein and Salant 2012]]; [[@Thaler2003|Thaler and Sunstein 2003]]).

## Summary
Fahn and Hakenes develop a relational-contracting model in which agents with present-biased preferences (quasi-hyperbolic, $\beta$-$\delta$ discounting) voluntarily form teams in order to commit their future selves to higher effort. Because exit from a team destroys a valuable stream of future cooperation, the threat of losing a partner's goodwill substitutes for the self-control the agent lacks on his own. The striking implication is that self-control problems are a *precondition* for productive teamwork: agents without time inconsistency cannot sustain any positive effort in a team, while moderate present bias can push effort toward the first best. The attached file is the paper's **Online Appendix only** (7 pages), which extends the benchmark model to naive agents, asymmetric teams, imperfect monitoring, and general cost functions; the main-text results are reconstructed from these extensions and the standard published version.

## Research question
Why do people work in teams even when individual production is technologically feasible and free-riding is a concern? Specifically: can the disciplining force of a relational (self-enforcing) team contract serve as a *commitment device* for agents who suffer from self-control problems, and under what conditions on the discount structure is cooperative team effort sustainable?

## Method / identification
The paper is a theoretical, infinite-horizon repeated-game / relational-contract model. Agents have quasi-hyperbolic preferences à la O'Donoghue and Rabin: a long-run discount factor $\delta$ and a present-bias parameter $\beta\le 1$, so future utility is weighted by $\beta\delta$. Each period an agent can exert effort $e$ at cost $c e^2/2$ (or, in the appendix, a general convex cost $C(e)$ with $C'>0$, $C''>0$, $C(0)=0$, $\lim_{e\to1}C(e)=\infty$); expected output is $eV$, realized next period.

Working alone, the present-biased agent chooses $e^I=\beta\delta V/c$ (or $\beta\delta V - C'(e^I)=0$), strictly below the first best $e^{FB}=\delta V/c$ (i.e. $\delta V - C'(e^{FB})=0$). In a team, mutual monitoring lets partners condition continued cooperation on observed effort: any deviation triggers a permanent breakdown to individual production. Sustainability is governed by an incentive/dynamic-enforcement (IC/DE) constraint,
$$\beta\delta\!\left(e^T V-\tfrac{c(e^T)^2}{2}\right)-\beta\delta\!\left(e^I V-\tfrac{c(e^I)^2}{2}\right)+\frac{\beta\delta}{1-\delta}\!\left[\left(\delta e^T V-\tfrac{c(e^T)^2}{2}\right)-\left(\delta e^I V-\tfrac{c(e^I)^2}{2}\right)\right]\ge 0,$$
where the second (continuation) line is positive precisely because $e^I<e^T$ for present-biased agents. The appendix extends identification to (i) naive and partially naive agents with a believed bias $\hat\beta\in[\beta,1]$, (ii) asymmetric pairs $(\beta_1,\beta_2)$, (iii) imperfect public monitoring where an effort-revealing signal arrives with probability $p$, and (iv) general cost functions, re-proving the core lemmas and propositions.

## Key findings
- **Lemma 1 / B1 (no bias, no team):** For $\beta=1$, no positive team effort can be enforced. With $e^I=e^{FB}$ the continuation surplus from staying in the team is non-positive, so the breakdown threat has no bite — agents without self-control problems gain nothing from teamwork.
- **Lemma 2 / B2:** No team effort $e^T\le e^I$ can be enforced; teams only make sense when they raise effort above the solo level.
- **Proposition 1 / B1:** For every $\beta<1$ and any $e^T\in(e^I,e^{FB}]$, that effort is enforceable when $\delta$ is close to 1. Hence sufficiently patient present-biased agents can attain first-best effort through teamwork.
- **Proposition B2 (interior optimum of bias):** There exists a unique $\beta^{max}\in(0,1)$ that maximizes the scope for cooperation. Lowering $\beta$ has two opposing effects — it makes the future less valuable (tightening enforcement) but also lowers the off-path outside option $e^I$ (loosening it); near $\beta=1$ the second effect dominates, so *more severe* self-control problems can *improve* team performance.
- **Naiveté (Appendix §1):** Fully naive agents ($\hat\beta=1$) cannot sustain any team effort — they perceive a breakdown as costless — and greater naiveté generally tightens the (IC) constraint.
- **Asymmetry (Appendix §2):** An agent with no self-control problem ($\beta_1=1$) can be productive *only* when matched with a biased partner; the "diligent" agent is effectively the lazy one, working hard to retain the other's goodwill, and earns a rent for serving as a commitment device.
- **Imperfect monitoring (Appendix §3):** With detection probability $p$, the first-best threshold is $\delta^{FB}=\frac{1-\beta(1-\beta)}{1-\beta((1-p)-\beta^2 p)(2-\beta)-1}$-type expression with $\partial\delta^{FB}/\partial p<0$; teamwork survives but requires more patience, and $\delta^{FB}=1$ at $p=0$.

## Contribution
The paper reframes teams not as solutions to technological complementarities or risk-sharing but as **endogenous commitment devices** for self-control. It unifies the relational-contracting literature (self-enforcing agreements sustained by future surplus) with the behavioral literature on present bias, showing that the two interact non-monotonically: time inconsistency, usually a friction, becomes the very glue that holds a productive team together. This inverts the standard intuition that disciplined, time-consistent workers make the best teammates.

## Limitations & open questions
- The model assumes **sophisticated** agents in the main text; the appendix shows naiveté undermines cooperation but does not fully characterize partially naive equilibria.
- **Symmetric agents** are the baseline; only a single asymmetric example is worked out, leaving the general matching/sorting problem (who pairs with whom, and equilibrium team composition) open.
- Under **imperfect monitoring** the authors only sketch a perfect-public-equilibrium condition; they explicitly leave a full surplus-maximizing, possibly non-stationary characterization (including using output as an extra signal) "beyond the scope of this paper."
- $\beta$ and $\hat\beta$ are held **constant with no learning** — dynamics of belief updating about one's own bias are unmodeled.
- Team size is effectively two; scaling to larger teams and the free-rider/monitoring trade-off there is not analyzed.

## Connections
The relational-contract machinery builds on Bull (1987), MacLeod and Malcomson (1989), Baker, Gibbons and Murphy (1994, 2002), and Levin (2003). The behavioral foundation — quasi-hyperbolic discounting — comes from Laibson (1997) and O'Donoghue and Rabin (1999, 2001), the latter cited directly for the naive/partially-naive taxonomy. The idea of self-imposed commitment devices connects to Thaler and Shefrin (1981), Strotz (1955–56), and Bénabou and Tirole (2004) on self-control and personal rules; the team free-riding contrast is to Holmström (1982) and Alchian and Demsetz (1972). The non-monotone "bias helps" result echoes themes in the strategic-delegation and commitment literature (e.g. Schelling 1960).

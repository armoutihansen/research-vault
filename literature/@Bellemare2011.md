> [!abstract] Abstract
> We specify and estimate an econometric model which separately identifies the effects of distributional preferences and penalizing unfair proposer behavior ("perceived intentions") on responder decisions in the ultimatum game. We allow the effects of perceived intentions to depend, among other things, on the subjective probabilities responders attach to the possible offers. The latter allows expectation violations to be a driving force for responder behavior. We estimate the model on a large representative sample from the Dutch population. We find that the relative importance of distributional preferences and perceived intentions depends significantly on the socio-economic characteristics of responders. Strong inequity aversion to the other player's disadvantage is found for lower educated and older respondents. Responders tend to punish more unequal offers made by proposers if they expect that unequal proposals are made less often.

## Summary
The paper structurally disentangles two competing explanations for ultimatum-game rejections — outcome-based inequity aversion versus reactions to the proposer's intentions — by comparing a "normal" ultimatum game (offers chosen by a human proposer) against a "random" ultimatum game (offers drawn by a computer, so intentions are absent). Distributional preferences operate in both treatments; intention-driven penalties operate only in the normal treatment, so the gap between treatments identifies the role of intentions without eliciting hard-to-measure beliefs. A second innovation lets the intention effect depend on each responder's elicited subjective probability distribution over offers, operationalizing the expectancy-violation hypothesis. Estimated on a large representative Dutch sample, both forces matter; inequity aversion dominates on average (especially for low offers), but intentions matter more for the young and highly educated, and responders punish unfair offers harder when they consider them unlikely.

## Research question
Can the effect of distributional (outcome-based) preferences be separated from the effect of penalizing perceived proposer intentions in driving responder behavior in the ultimatum game, and does the intention effect depend on how much the realized offer violates the responder's expectations? A third question is whether behavioral patterns (preferences and intention reactions) differ systematically across socio-economic groups in a representative population versus the student-like subgroup that populates most lab experiments.

## Method / identification
The identification engine is a two-treatment design (after Blount, 1995; Falk et al., 2008). In the **random** treatment intentions are absent, so responder utility is the Fehr–Schmidt (1999) form over own payoff $y^{resp}$ and the other's payoff $y^{prop}$:
$$u_i(y^{resp}, y^{prop}) = y^{resp} - \alpha_i \max\{y^{prop}-y^{resp},0\} - \beta_i \max\{y^{resp}-y^{prop},0\}$$
where $\alpha_i$ is aversion to disadvantageous inequality and $\beta_i$ to advantageous inequality. The kink at the equal split lets the model rationalize "plateau" behavior (rejecting both stingy and overly generous offers); systematic rejection of offers above the equal split requires $\beta_i>1$, which the authors permit despite Fehr–Schmidt deeming it implausible. In the **normal** treatment the preference parameters are augmented by intention parameters $\theta_i$ (for disadvantageous offers) and $\delta_i$ (for advantageous offers):
$$u_i(y^{resp}, y^{prop}) = y^{resp} - (\alpha_i+\theta_i)\max\{y^{prop}-y^{resp},0\} - (\beta_i+\delta_i)\max\{y^{resp}-y^{prop},0\}$$
Because $\theta_i,\delta_i$ enter only the normal treatment while $(\alpha_i,\beta_i)$ enter both, the across-treatment difference identifies intention attribution with no belief data. Each parameter is a linear index in observables $x_i$ plus a normally distributed unobserved-heterogeneity term, e.g. $\alpha_i = x_i'\gamma^{\alpha} + \nu_i^{\alpha}$; the pairs $(\nu_i^{\alpha},\nu_i^{\beta})$ and $(\nu_i^{\theta},\nu_i^{\delta})$ are bivariate normal with estimated correlations $\rho_{\alpha\beta}$, $\rho_{\theta\delta}$. The **general** model makes the intention effect a function of beliefs: $\theta_i = x_i'\gamma^{\theta} + \lambda_i\,Q_i(O\le y^{resp}) + \nu_i^{\theta}$ and $\delta_i = x_i'\gamma^{\delta} + \mu_i\,Q_i(O\ge y^{resp}) + \nu_i^{\delta}$, where $Q_i(\cdot)$ is the responder's elicited subjective probability of an offer at least as extreme; $\lambda_i,\mu_i$ are themselves indices in $x_i$. A smaller $Q_i(O\le y^{resp})$ (a low offer seen as more surprising) is expected to raise the penalty, so $\lambda_i<0$ is predicted. Suboptimal play is handled by adding logistic errors to the utility of accepting, scaled by noise-to-signal parameters $\sigma_N$ (normal) and $\sigma_R$ (random), yielding binary-logit acceptance probabilities $1/(1+e^{-u^A_{ij}/\sigma})$ for each of the eight offers. Estimation is by **maximum simulated likelihood**: conditional on the heterogeneity draws the likelihood is a product of eight logits, integrated over the unobservables via $R=100$ Halton draws (asymptotically equivalent to exact ML). Appendix A builds an extended joint model that lets the elicited beliefs $Q_{ij}$ be endogenous (a censored/Tobit beliefs equation correlated with the preference unobservables through $\alpha_i+\theta_i$); the key $\lambda,\mu$ parameters remain identified without exclusion restrictions because endogeneity enters only through individual-level heterogeneity that does not vary across the eight choice problems — the same logic as fixed-effects panel identification.

## Data
A large-scale, incentivized experiment run in March 2004 with the CentERpanel, an internet survey representative of the Dutch adult population (ages 16+). Of 1304 participants, 626 were responders (355 normal, 271 random); after dropping inconsistent/incomplete records the estimation sample is 311 responders in the normal and 246 in the random treatment. Proposers split D 10 (1000 CentERpoints) into one of eight allocations from (1000,0) to (0,1000). Responder decisions were collected by the **strategy method** (accept/reject for all eight offers), enabling detection of plateau behavior. After deciding, responders stated subjective probabilities (elicited as natural frequencies summing to 100) over the eight possible offers — these first-order beliefs drive the general model. Background covariates: gender, three age bands, three education levels, three income groups, and work status.

## Key findings
Both forces are significant. Aggregate acceptance rates are markedly higher in the random than the normal treatment for offers below the equal split (e.g. +13.5 points for a zero offer), directly evidencing intention-based punishment. Likelihood-ratio tests order the models cleanly: the no-difference model (LL −1842.8) is beaten by the baseline two-treatment model (LL −1433.0), which is beaten by the belief-augmented general model (LL −1392.4), all at p<0.001. Average predicted parameters (general model): $\bar\alpha=0.796$ (most people are inequity-averse to their own disadvantage), $\bar\beta=0.183$ with large dispersion, $\bar\theta=0.554$ (intentionally stingy offers draw negative reactions), $\bar\delta=-0.246$ (generous offers signal good intent on average, but with wide heterogeneity, many subjects penalize over-generosity). **Distributional concerns dominate intentions on average, especially for low offers.** Heterogeneity: older and lower/middle-educated responders have stronger aversion to the other's disadvantage ($\beta$) and a stronger tendency to reject very generous offers (higher $\delta$, possibly "pride"/suspicion); young, highly educated subjects have lower inequity aversion (predicted $\beta=-1.267$, i.e. competitive preferences), weigh intentions more, and accept far more often. **Expectation violations matter for low offers:** the estimated $\lambda$ for the benchmark group is negative and significant — responders punish low offers more when they regard them as unlikely — and this effect is significantly stronger for the young. No symmetric effect is found for very favorable offers. Scenario simulations confirm that if responders expect disadvantageous offers (EDO scenario), even zero offers are frequently accepted. The endogeneity test (Appendix A) fails to reject exogeneity of beliefs (p=0.211), and the unobserved-heterogeneity components of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are positively correlated ($\rho_{\alpha\beta}\approx0.74$).

## Contribution
Three stated innovations: (1) a structural econometric model that separately identifies distributional preferences from intention reactions and ties both to observed and unobserved heterogeneity, without needing second-order beliefs or commitment to a specific intention-based model (e.g. Dufwenberg–Kirchsteiger); (2) making the intention reaction depend on elicited first-order beliefs, giving an economic test of social-psychology's expectancy-violation theory inside a game; and (3) using a large representative population rather than a student convenience sample, showing that student-like subgroups give a distorted picture of both preferences and intention reactions.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several. Belief questions were not incentivized. The advantageous-offer intention parameter $\delta$ is poorly identified because its unobserved-heterogeneity variance $\sigma^2_{\delta}$ is small and insignificant. The expectancy-violation effect is robust for disadvantageous offers but absent (insignificantly signed) for favorable offers — leaving open why surprise asymmetrically affects only the punishment of stinginess. Subjective probabilities were not elicited from random-treatment responders (to avoid confusion), which prevents separate identification of the auxiliary covariance parameters $\gamma^{\alpha j}$ and $\gamma^{\theta j}$ in the endogeneity extension. The "student proxy" (young + high education) is indirect, since the panel does not record student status and finer age cutoffs (25, 30) yield too few observations. More broadly, the design abstracts from indirect reciprocity (the uniform random distribution was chosen deliberately to avoid it), so the interaction of expectations with reciprocal histories is unexplored.

## Connections
The outcome-based pillar is Fehr & Schmidt (1999) and Bolton & Ockenfels (2000); the intention-based pillar is Rabin (1993) and Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger (2004), with Falk & Fischbacher (2006) on second-order beliefs. The normal-vs-random identification strategy originates with Blount (1995) and is used by Falk, Fehr & Fischbacher (2008), Offerman (2002) and Cox & Deck (2005). The belief-elicitation methodology draws on Manski (2002, 2004) and Hoffrage et al. (2000). The paper is a direct extension of Bellemare, Kröger & van Soest (2008), which estimated distributional preferences from proposer and responder data but did *not* separate intentions; it reuses that normal-treatment responder data and adds new random-treatment data plus responder (rather than proposer) beliefs. Representativeness concerns connect to Bellemare & Kröger (2007), Henrich et al. (2004), and Carpenter et al. (2005). The expectancy-violation hypothesis is imported from social psychology (Jussim et al., 1987; Jackson et al., 1993; Bettencourt et al., 1997).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> We present a laboratory experiment that measures the effects of induced group identity on social preferences. We find that when participants are matched with an ingroup member, they show a 47 percent increase in charity concerns and a 93 percent decrease in envy. Likewise, participants are 19 percent more likely to reward an ingroup match for good behavior, but 13 percent less likely to punish an ingroup match for misbehavior. Furthermore, participants are significantly more likely to choose social-welfare-maximizing actions when matched with an ingroup member. All results are consistent with the hypothesis that participants are more altruistic toward an ingroup match.

## Summary
Chen and Li run a laboratory experiment that induces artificial group identity (via the classic Klee/Kandinsky painting-preference paradigm and, in robustness treatments, pure random assignment) and then measures how that identity shifts social preferences across a battery of 24 two-person sequential games drawn from [[@Charness2002|Charness and Rabin (2002)]]. They graft a group-identity term onto the Charness-Rabin distributional-preference model and estimate it by maximum likelihood. Matching with an ingroup member sharply raises charity when one is ahead, nearly eliminates envy when one is behind, increases the probability of rewarding good behavior, reduces the probability of punishing misbehavior, and raises the rate of social-welfare-maximizing (SWM) choices, thereby raising expected earnings. A second set of treatment comparisons dissects *what* creates the group effect, concluding that pure categorization alone is sufficient, while a problem-solving chat boosts self-reported attachment without much further behavioral change.

## Research question
Three substantive questions plus one methodological. (1) Are people more difference-averse (more charitable, less envious) toward ingroup than outgroup members, and by how much? (2) Are people more likely to positively reciprocate good intentions, and more forgiving of bad intentions, from ingroup members? (3) Are people more likely to choose social-welfare-maximizing actions with an ingroup match? (4) Methodologically, what *generates* group effects — pure categorization (Tajfel-Turner) versus expectations of generalized reciprocity (Yamagishi-Kiyonari) — and which design components (assignment rule, chat, other-other allocation, within- vs between-subject) matter?

## Method / identification
The core identification is a structural extension of the Charness-Rabin two-person social-preference model, estimated on player B's binary choices. In the baseline model, B's utility is a payoff-comparison-weighted average of her own and her match's monetary payoffs:
$$u_B(\pi_A,\pi_B) = w_A\,\pi_A + (1-w_A)\,\pi_B, \qquad w_A = \rho\,r + \sigma\,s,$$
where $r=1$ if $\pi_B>\pi_A$ (B ahead) and $s=1$ if $\pi_B<\pi_A$ (B behind). Here $\rho$ is the charity weight when ahead and $\sigma$ the (negative) envy weight when behind. Chen-Li redefine the weight to embed group identity:
$$w_A' = \rho\,(1+\mathbb{I}\,a)\,r + \sigma\,(1+\mathbb{I}\,b)\,s,$$
where $\mathbb{I}=1$ iff A and B are in the same group. The parameters $a$ and $b$ capture the *additional* ingroup effect on charity and envy; $\rho(1+a)$ is ingroup charity, $\rho$ is outgroup charity, and the difference identifies $a$. Choice probabilities use a logit link,
$$P_B(\text{action}_1) = \frac{e^{\gamma\,u(\text{action}_1)}}{e^{\gamma\,u(\text{action}_1)}+e^{\gamma\,u(\text{action}_2)}},$$
where $\gamma$ governs choice sensitivity to utility differences ($\gamma=0$ is random choice). Reciprocity is analyzed separately via logit regressions on positive- and negative-reciprocity games, with regressors including the ingroup dummy, B's cost to reward/punish, A's benefit/damage, and B's payoff gap, plus their interactions with the ingroup dummy (coefficients reported as probability derivatives, SEs clustered at the individual level). The experiment has four stages: (1) group assignment (painting preferences or random), (2) an online chat problem-solving task to strengthen identity, (3) an other-other token-allocation stage (strategy method, five rounds, 200-400 tokens) replicating the minimal-group paradigm, and (4) the 24 self-other sequential games (5 dictator + 16 response games + 3 added variants of Berk31). Identification of *what creates* group effects comes from pairwise treatment contrasts: Original vs NoChat isolates chat; NoChat vs NoHelp isolates other-other allocation; Original vs RandomWithin isolates true-preference vs random assignment; RandomWithin vs RandomBetween isolates the salience of the outgroup / experimenter-demand.

## Data
Original laboratory data: 36 independent computerized sessions (z-Tree) at Michigan's RCGD lab, Jan-Jul 2005 plus May-Jun 2007. 566 subjects recruited, 562 used in analysis, mostly Michigan students. Six conditions: Control (no identity, stage 4 only), Original, NoChat, NoHelp, RandomWithin, RandomBetween. Exchange rate 100 tokens = $1, plus $5 show-up; average earnings $18.85 (treatment) and $14.40 (control). A post-experiment survey collected demographics, past giving, strategy reports, group affiliation, and prior knowledge of the artists.

## Key findings
**Result 1 (Distribution preferences).** Participants exhibit charity when ahead and envy when behind; both shift toward the ingroup. Estimated charity rises from $\rho_0=0.323$ (outgroup) to $\rho_0(1+a)=0.474$ (ingroup), an extra-ingroup effect $a=0.467$ ($p<0.01$), i.e., the headline 47 percent increase in charity. Envy moves from $\sigma_0=-0.112$ (outgroup) to $\sigma_0(1+b)=-0.008$ (ingroup, not distinguishable from zero), with $b=-0.931$ ($p<0.01$), the 93 percent decrease in envy — ingroup behavior when behind is statistically equivalent to own-payoff maximization. Both are consistent with putting more weight on an ingroup match's payoff and, crucially, depend on the payoff comparison, unlike payoff-independent altruism models (e.g., Basu 2006).

**Result 2 (Reciprocity).** Reciprocity differs significantly by group. An ingroup match raises B's likelihood of rewarding good behavior by 18.6 percent ($p<0.01$); B is significantly more forgiving of (less likely to punish) ingroup misbehavior. Reward also rises with A's benefit (8.9 percent per 100 tokens) and falls with B's payoff lag (envy restrains positive reciprocity, $-7.8$ percent per 100 tokens). Interaction terms show these channels are group-contingent (e.g., (Benefit to A)$\times$Ingroup $=0.076$, $p<0.01$; (B behind A)$\times$Ingroup $=-0.160$, $p<0.01$).

**Result 3 (Social welfare maximization).** Both players A and B are more likely to choose SWM actions with an ingroup match (McNemar tests, $p<0.01$), and more SWM than control with ingroup but less with outgroup. Consequently, ingroup matching yields significantly higher expected earnings.

**Other-other allocation.** With real money and true-preference groups, the minimal-group ingroup-favoritism result replicates: when allocating between one ingroup and one outgroup member, the ingroup share exceeds the outgroup share by 32.2-38.4 percent of endowment ($p<0.01$, all five rounds).

**What creates group effects.** Random assignment is as effective as painting-based assignment (no behavioral or attitudinal difference across the 24 games). Within- and between-subject designs induce the same group effects (no experimenter-demand artifact, no role for outgroup salience). The online chat significantly raises self-reported attachment but changes behavior in essentially only one game (Resp 2a). The other-other allocation stage has no significant effect on behavior or attachment and may even *crowd out* later costly group-contingent choices. There is suggestive evidence that categorization-induced group effects deteriorate over time (about a 1 percentage-point drop in group-contingent choices per allocation game, $p<0.02$). Bottom line: pure categorization alone suffices to generate group effects.

## Contribution
Two contributions. (1) An empirical foundation for embedding identity in economic models: rather than treating identity merely as a substitute for monetary rewards / cost-saving device (the Akerlof-Kranton framing), the dominant measured effect is increased SWM behavior and positive reciprocity. The calibrated transformed utility functions can make out-of-sample forecasts across new games, reconciling disparate prior findings. (2) Practical implications for organization and mechanism design: deliberately cultivating group identity (the Nike "swoosh" tattoo example) can raise cooperation and joint payoffs, especially where monetary incentives are limited (e.g., online communities). Relative to social psychology, the study uses a far larger and more varied game set spanning self-interest conflicts, real incentives, no deception, and a systematic component-by-component decomposition of group-effect formation.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems and design fixes. (i) Mechanism ambiguity: does identity work by changing *beliefs/expectations* about fellow ingroup members or by changing *preferences*, and which functional forms/axioms best fit — average-payoff maximization, intergroup-difference maximization, or the metacontrast principle? (ii) The deterioration-over-time confound: an improved design should hold constant the elapsed time between group assignment and the sequential games by inserting an individually completed filler task of equal duration. (iii) The conjectured crowding-out effect of costless other-other allocation on later costly choices deserves direct test. (iv) On theory: a formal model of group identity applied to organization design, optimal contracts, and organizational hierarchies. (v) On empirics: study identity in practical mechanism design in both lab and field. They note that field experiments inducing identity are scarce (Goette-Huffman-Meier being the main one) and recommend random assignment when feasible.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> We propose a new decision criterion under risk in which individuals extract both utility from anticipatory feelings ex ante and disutility from disappointment ex post. The decision maker chooses his degree of optimism, given that more optimism raises both the utility of ex ante feelings and the risk of disappointment ex post. We characterize the optimal beliefs and the preferences under risk generated by this mental process and apply this criterion to a simple portfolio choice/insurance problem. We show that these preferences are compatible with first-degree and second-degree stochastic dominance and yield a preference for early resolution of uncertainty. Furthermore, they are consistent with observed violations of the independence axiom, such as the preference reversal in the Allais paradox, and predict that the decision maker takes on less risk compared to an expected utility maximizer. Our decision criterion can thus help explain the equity premium puzzle and the preference for low deductibles in insurance contracts.

## Summary
Gollier and Muermann propose a decision criterion under risk that merges Akerlof–Dickens endogenous (chosen) beliefs with Bell's disappointment theory. Between deciding (Date 1) and learning the outcome (Date 2), the agent picks subjective beliefs that trade off the pleasure of *savoring* an optimistic dream against the pain of *ex post disappointment*. Optimism feels good now but raises a reference point (the anticipated payoff $y$) against which the realized payoff is judged, so excess optimism breeds disappointment. The optimal $y$ is a scalar sufficient statistic; the induced preference functional respects first- and second-degree stochastic dominance, violates independence (rationalizing Allais), prefers early resolution of uncertainty, and makes agents *more* risk-averse than expected utility (EU), unlike prior optimal-beliefs models.

## Research question
Can a single, internally rational model of belief formation—where agents actively choose how optimistic to be—simultaneously (i) explain why people are not systematically optimistic (countering "optimal expectations" models), (ii) generate a well-behaved decision criterion consistent with stochastic dominance, and (iii) match anomalies such as the Allais paradox, the equity premium puzzle, and the demand for low insurance deductibles?

## Method / identification
A purely theoretical (axiomatic/decision-theoretic) model. Two dates. Over a fixed support $c_1<c_2<\dots<c_S$, the agent faces objective lottery $Q=(q_1,\dots,q_S)$ but forms subjective beliefs $P=(p_1,\dots,p_S)$. A bivariate utility $U(c,y)$ gives ex post felicity from payoff $c$ given anticipated payoff (reference point) $y$, with $U_c>0$, $U_{cc}\le 0$ (risk aversion), and $U_y\le 0$ (disappointment: a higher reference point lowers ex post utility). The anticipated payoff equals the *subjective* certainty equivalent:
$$U(y,y)=\sum_{s=1}^{S} p_s\, U(c_s,y).$$
Intertemporal welfare is a $k$-weighted sum of ex ante savoring (subjective expected utility) and ex post objective expected utility:
$$W(Q)=\max_{P\in\Delta,\,y}\ k\sum_s p_s U(c_s,y)+\sum_s q_s U(c_s,y)\quad\text{s.t.}\quad U(y,y)=\sum_s p_s U(c_s,y).$$
Substituting the constraint collapses the program to a scalar problem in $y$ alone:
$$W(Q)=\max_{c_1\le y\le c_S} F(y,Q)=k\,v(y)+\sum_s q_s U(c_s,y),\qquad v(y)\equiv U(y,y).$$
$P$ drops out: $y$ is a sufficient statistic, so the model pins down the optimal anticipated payoff $y^*(Q)$ but not a unique $P$. Crucially $y^*(Q)$ and $W(Q)$ are independent of the menu of alternative lotteries—distinguishing it from [[@Brunnermeier2005|Brunnermeier–Parker (2005)]] and Gollier (2005), where welfare can fall as the choice set grows. The functional is shown to be a special case of Kreps–Porteus / Machina induced preferences over temporal risk, with $M(c,y)=k\,v(y)+U(c,y)$ playing the role of Machina's local utility function.

## Data
None — this is a theory paper. The portfolio/insurance application is a worked analytical example (CRRA/additive-habit specifications); the only quantitative content is a numerical Allais example (Table 1) and computed risk premia, not empirical data.

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1:** $y^*$ weakly increases in the intensity $k$ of anticipatory feelings; since $k$ proxies the time until resolution, agents lower expectations as feedback nears (consistent with Shepperd et al. 1996, Van Dijk et al. 2003).
- **Proposition 2:** An FSD deterioration in $Q$ weakly lowers $y^*$ iff $U_y$ is increasing in $c$ ($U_{cy}>0$), interpreted as *disappointment aversion* (correlation loving in Eeckhoudt et al. 2007).
- **Proposition 3:** A Rothschild–Stiglitz increase in risk lowers $y^*$ iff $U_y$ is concave in $c$ ($U_{ccy}\le 0$).
- **Proposition 4:** $W$ is an EU functional iff $U(c,y)=u(c)f(y)+g(y)$, i.e. iff absolute risk aversion $-U_{cc}/U_c$ is independent of $y$ (CARA-additive or CRRA-multiplicative habit cases).
- **Proposition 5:** $W$ respects second-degree stochastic dominance (hence FSD and Rothschild–Stiglitz)—optimizing beliefs can never offset an objectively worse risk.
- **Value of information:** $W$ is convex in $Q$, so information has positive value *even when it cannot change the decision*, implying a preference for early resolution of uncertainty.
- **Proposition 6 (Allais):** Under disappointment aversion, $W$ "fans out" and explains the Allais paradox iff absolute risk aversion is increasing in $y$, $\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(-U_{cc}/U_c\right)\ge 0$. A numerical example ($U(c,y)=(1+c-y/2)^{1-\gamma}/(1-\gamma)$, $\gamma=4$, $k=1$) reproduces the common-consequence preference reversal: the less favorable second menu lowers $y^*$ from $\approx 1$ to $\approx 0$, cutting risk aversion.
- **Portfolio/insurance (Props. 7–11):** Agents take *less* risk than EU; the risky-asset share decreases in $k$ (Prop. 9), and full coverage is optimal under fair insurance, partial under unfair (Prop. 10). Under RRA > 1, insurance demand rises in $k$ and in disappointment intensity $\phi$ (Prop. 11). This speaks to the equity premium puzzle and the preference for low deductibles—and, unlike optimal-expectations models, applies to optimists and pessimists alike.

## Contribution
The paper repairs the central defect of Bell's (1985) disappointment theory (where everyone would choose the most pessimistic prior to avoid disappointment) by endogenizing the reference point through a savoring–disappointment trade-off rather than rational-expectations consistency. It thereby delivers a tractable, menu-independent decision criterion that—unlike [[@Brunnermeier2005|Brunnermeier–Parker (2005)]] and Gollier (2005)—predicts heightened risk aversion and so helps, rather than worsens, the equity premium puzzle, while nesting EU as a limiting case and connecting endogenous beliefs to Machina-style non-EU preferences.

## Limitations & open questions
- The model is static (two dates) and does not pin down a unique subjective distribution $P$, only the scalar $y^*$.
- The authors explicitly flag developing a **dynamic version** to study the *dynamics of optimal subjective beliefs* over time.
- They propose **contrasting a dynamic version with the long-run-risks asset-pricing model** of Bansal–Yaron (2004) and Epstein–Zin recursive preferences, asking which better matches asset returns with low effective risk aversion.
- Several predictions (e.g. correlation between optimism and risk aversion, deductible choices) invite further **empirical/experimental testing** beyond the cited Ben Mansour et al. (2008).

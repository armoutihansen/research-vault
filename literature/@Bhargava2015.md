> [!abstract] Abstract
> Policymakers have recently embraced Behavioral Economics as an alternative approach which recognizes the limits and consequences of human decision-making.  Early applications of BE ("nudges") produced notable successes and helped to set the stage for more aggressive applications aimed at the deeper causes of policy problems.  We contend that policies that aspire to simplify products and incentives, rather than choice environments, aggressively protect consumers from behavioral exploitation, and leverage BE to enhance the design and implementation of traditional policy instruments offer solutions commensurate with contemporary challenges. Case studies in health insurance, privacy, and climate change illustrate the application of these ideas.

## Summary
This is a short agenda-setting essay (an AEA Papers & Proceedings piece aimed at students of policy and their instructors) arguing that behavioral economics (BE) should move "beyond nudging." Bhargava and Loewenstein grant that early BE applications - automatic-enrollment defaults, informational nudges, simplified disclosures - were genuine successes, but contend that "nano-sized" interventions cannot address the structural, deep causes of contemporary policy problems. They propose three guiding principles for a more ambitious behavioral public policy and illustrate them with three deliberately disparate case studies: health insurance, privacy, and climate change.

## Research question
How should behavioral economics inform public policy going forward? Specifically: given that nudges have succeeded at the margin but leave the underlying causes of major policy problems (obesity, financial complexity, climate inaction, privacy erosion) untouched, what broader and more aggressive set of BE-informed policy tools should "students of behavioral public policy" learn to deploy?

## Method / identification
None in the empirical sense - this is a conceptual/perspective essay, not a study with a model or estimator. Its "method" is a normative framework built around three guiding principles plus illustrative case studies. The framework rests on the concept of "internalities" (or "within-person externalities"), coined by Herrnstein et al. (1993): an internality is produced when an individual fails to consider the full impact of a behavior on her own present/future utility. Whereas traditional economics justifies intervention to correct externalities and informational asymmetries, BE additionally licenses intervention to correct internalities. The three principles are:
1. **Simplify products and incentives, not just choice environments.** Move beyond simplifying the *choice architecture* (the nudge paradigm) toward simplifying or standardizing the underlying products and incentive structures themselves.
2. **Aggressively protect consumers from behavioral exploitation.** Because firms facing unsophisticated, inattentive consumers may settle into a steady state in which they exploit biases via product complexity and marketing (citing Heidhues and Koszegi 2014; Gabaix-Laibson-style logic), the conventional asymmetric-information rationale for consumer protection should be broadened to justify taxation, menu restriction, or explicit regulation of choice.
3. **Leverage BE to improve traditional policy instruments.** Since behavior responds to *perceived* rather than *actual* incentives, the salience, complexity, nominal incidence, and framing of a tax (not just demand elasticity) shape responses; loss aversion implies tiered unemployment benefits may outperform flat insurance (DellaVigna et al. 2014). BE can also mobilize citizens through education and persuasion rather than mere nudging.

## Data
None - this is an essay. It marshals others' evidence rather than presenting original data, drawing on a study of 55,000 European retail financial products (Celerier and Vallee 2014) and the authors' own companion empirical work on the health-plan choices of over 50,000 employees (Bhargava, Loewenstein, and Sydnor 2015).

## Key findings
There are no theorems; the contribution is a set of arguments illustrated by three case studies.
- **Health insurance.** In the authors' companion study, a large US firm offered a standardized menu in which nearly all low-deductible plans were financially *dominated* by otherwise-equivalent high-deductible plans, yet a majority of employees - especially low-income ones - chose dominated plans, generating unnecessary spending equal on average to 42 percent of annual premiums. Targeted education had only moderate success; the ACA's metal-tier labels slightly *worsened* choices in an ongoing project. The proposed remedy is not better disclosure but mandating dramatically simplified, standardized products (e.g., a feasible copay-only plan, per Loewenstein et al. 2013) or restricting menus to reasonable options.
- **Privacy.** Disclosure-based protection fails because limited attention and motivated reasoning lead consumers to ignore privacy disclosures or to wrongly infer that their presence implies protection (Acquisti, Brandimarte, and Loewenstein 2015). Even simplified disclosures are insufficient; the recommended approach is regulation restricting firms' use of personal information to purposes consistent with reasonable consumer expectations (as in the EU).
- **Climate change.** Standard theory diagnoses the problem (free-riding, externalities; remedies are carbon taxes or cap-and-trade), yet progress has stalled because of psychological factors - motivated disbelief, the ostrich effect, confirmation bias, present bias, adaptation, intangibility (drawing on Marshall 2014). BE should be used to build informed public support for the best long-term economic policies, not merely to nudge individual energy use.
- **Synthesizing example.** Tobacco control is held up as a successful merger of traditional levers (taxes, bans) with psychologically motivated measures (vivid warning labels, public-health campaigns, substitute products), one of the most successful US policy initiatives in history.

## Contribution
The essay reframes the BE-and-policy agenda. Against the dominant "libertarian paternalism / nudge" framing ([[@Thaler2003|Thaler and Sunstein 2003]], 2008; Camerer et al. 2003), it argues that BE need not confine itself to choice-preserving, low-burden interventions and that more aggressive, structurally targeted policies "need not be more controversial" than the original nudge proposals. It supplies a compact vocabulary (internalities, behavioral exploitation, perceived incentives) and a three-principle scaffold that has been widely cited as the manifesto for a "second generation" of behavioral public policy. It is explicitly pedagogical, positioning these tools - taxes, subsidies, regulations, nudges, educational campaigns - as a full complement that students of policy should learn.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors themselves flag that stronger policy interventions "should not be taken lightly, given concerns about overreach and unexpected outcomes." Several open problems are hooks for further work:
- **Welfare standard.** Acting on internalities requires a normative benchmark for an individual's "true" interests; the essay does not resolve how policymakers should identify it without paternalistic overreach.
- **Scope of behavioral exploitation regulation.** When exactly is firm complexity "exploitation" warranting taxation or menu restriction versus legitimate product differentiation? The line is asserted, not formalized.
- **Why metal-tier labels backfired.** The ongoing finding that standardized labels *worsened* insurance choices is reported without a mechanism - an open empirical question.
- **Generalizability and political economy.** The case studies are "intentionally disparate"; whether the principles transfer cleanly across domains, and how concentrated interests (energy, tobacco) shape what is politically feasible, is left open.

## Connections
The piece sits explicitly atop the foundational nudge literature - Thaler and Sunstein's "Libertarian Paternalism" (2003) and *Nudge* (2008), and Camerer et al.'s "asymmetric paternalism" (2003) - which it both credits and pushes past; it can be read as the sequel to Madrian's (2014) survey of applying BE to policy design. The internalities concept derives from Herrnstein, Loewenstein, Prelec, and Vaughan (1993). The consumer-protection argument leans on the behavioral-IO literature on exploiting naive consumers (Heidhues and Koszegi 2014; Celerier and Vallee 2014). The "perceived incentives" thread connects to Congdon, Kling, and Mullainathan's *Policy and Choice* (2011) and to reference-dependent labor-supply work (DellaVigna et al. 2014). On scarcity and the disproportionate burden of complexity on the poor it cites Mullainathan and Shafir (2013); on the limits of disclosure, Loewenstein, Sunstein, and Golman (2014); on climate psychology, Marshall (2014). The health-insurance case is the authors' own companion paper, Bhargava, Loewenstein, and Sydnor (2015) on dominated health-plan choices.

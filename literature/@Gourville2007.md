> [!abstract] Abstract
> Decision researchers have long been interested in behaviors that deviate from rational choice. Of these, the compromise effect has received considerable attention, with it repeatedly shown that the probability of choosing an item increases when that item is a middling, as opposed to extreme, alternative in a choice set. The term extremeness avoidance has been used to describe the reason underlying this phenomenon. In this research, we argue that extremeness avoidance behavior depends on assortment type, with consumers displaying extremeness avoidance for alignable assortments, but systematically and predictably displaying extremeness seeking for non-alignable assortments. Across three studies, we show the extremeness seeking effect, contrast it with extremeness avoidance, and explore its underlying cause.

## Summary
Gourville and Soman argue that the much-studied compromise effect (extremeness *avoidance*) is not universal but is moderated by the *type* of assortment a consumer faces. When alternatives differ along a single continuous/compensatory dimension (an **alignable** assortment, e.g. processor speed), adding intermediate options pulls share toward the middle (classic extremeness avoidance). But when alternatives differ along multiple discrete, non-compensatory dimensions (a **non-alignable** assortment, e.g. distinct feature bundles), adding intermediate options instead pushes share toward the two extremes — a phenomenon they name **extremeness seeking**. Three survey-experiments document the effect, contrast it with extremeness avoidance, and trace both to the same underlying driver: preference uncertainty and the need to justify one's choice.

## Research question
How does the number and *type* of alternatives in a within-brand choice set affect which alternative a consumer chooses? Specifically: does the addition of intermediate alternatives always reduce the probability of choosing an extreme option (as the compromise effect and the regularity axiom predict), or can it systematically *increase* it, and if so, when and why?

## Method / identification
The paper is a set of three between/within-subjects survey-based choice experiments. Each presents respondents with a single brand's product line (Toshiba laptops, then four diverse categories) and varies the choice set; the dependent variable is the probability of choosing one of the two *extreme* alternatives (basic or fully-loaded). Inference is by $\chi^2$ tests on choice shares and by logistic regressions of the log-odds of extreme choice on contrast-coded treatment factors.

- **Study 1** (N = 280, mall intercept): one factor, **assortment size** $\in\{3,4,5\}$, built by adding one, two, or three non-alignable intermediate laptops (each adding a single distinct feature) between a fixed basic and fully-loaded model. Tests H1 (extremeness seeking rises with non-alignable assortment size).
- **Study 2** (N = 540, airport): a $3\times2$ full-factorial design crossing **assortment size** $\{3,4,5\}$ with **assortment type** {non-alignable (distinct features) vs alignable (varying processor speed)}, 90 per cell. Logistic regression with contrast codes for size, type, and their interaction. Tests H1 and H2 (extremeness *aversion* with alignable assortments) jointly, isolating non-alignability as the driver.
- **Study 3** (N = 280, undergraduates): a $4\times2\times2$ design — **product category** (cable TV, digital cameras, wireless service, Italian vacations; within-subjects) $\times$ **assortment size** $\{3,5\}$ $\times$ **justification** (told vs not told they might be asked to explain their choice). Justification is the key process manipulation, borrowed from [[@Simonson1989|Simonson (1989)]]: if preference uncertainty / accountability drives extremeness seeking, expecting to justify a choice should magnify the effect.

The design is framed against two normative benchmarks: extremeness aversion ([[@Simonson1992|Simonson and Tversky 1992]]) and the **regularity** axiom (Luce 1959), which forbids the choice probability of an existing alternative from rising when a new alternative is added. Extremeness seeking violates regularity.

## Data
None beyond the three original survey-experiments. Respondents are convenience samples (shopping-mall adults, airport travelers, business undergraduates); all choices are hypothetical purchase intentions, not incentivized purchases. Prices/features were drawn from contemporaneous (1998, 2001) manufacturer and trade-press literature.

## Key findings
- **H1 — Extremeness seeking (Study 1).** As the non-alignable assortment grew from 3 to 4 to 5 alternatives, the combined share of the two extremes rose monotonically: 40.9% → 61.7% → 82.5%, each step significant. The migration was symmetric — *both* the basic (19.2% → 37.5%) and the fully-loaded (21.7% → 45.0%) share roughly doubled — i.e. genuine attraction to both poles, not polarization toward one.
- **H1 vs H2 — type moderates direction (Study 2).** Logistic regression found no main effect of assortment size, a significant main effect of assortment *type*, and a significant **size $\times$ type interaction** ($\chi^2(2)=20.41$, $p<.001$). For non-alignable assortments extreme share rose (48.9% → 66.7%); for alignable assortments it fell (54.4% → 26.7%). Same brand, same number of options — opposite behavior depending on whether attributes are alignable.
- **Robustness + mechanism (Study 3).** Extremeness seeking replicated across all four product categories: pooled extreme choice rose from 34.3% (size 3) to 52.0% (size 5) in the no-justification condition. The **justification** manipulation magnified it: a significant main effect of justification and a significant size $\times$ justification interaction — extreme choice rose ~18 points without justification but ~32 points when subjects expected to explain their choice. This supports preference uncertainty / accountability as the driver.

## Contribution
The paper introduces and names **extremeness seeking** and reframes the compromise effect as a special case of a broader contingency keyed on **attribute alignability**. It shows that the entire extant compromise-effect literature has (in retrospect) used almost exclusively alignable assortments, so the "robust" extremeness-avoidance finding is in fact assortment-type-specific. Crucially, it unifies the two opposing effects under one mechanism — preference uncertainty and the desire to justify choices — rather than positing a separate force for each. It thus extends Simonson's (1989) reasons-based account from middle options to extreme options.

## Limitations & open questions
- All studies use **hypothetical, unincentivized** survey choices; the authors do not test whether extremeness seeking survives real purchases with money at stake.
- The mechanism evidence is indirect: justification *magnifies* the effect, but the paper does not directly measure preference uncertainty or the content of the justifications subjects would give.
- The boundary between "alignable" and "non-alignable" is treated as a clean dichotomy; the authors leave open how partially-alignable assortments behave and how consumers classify ambiguous attributes.
- Generalizability beyond within-brand product lines (the framing throughout) to across-brand or service choices is asserted via the four categories but not formally bounded.
- The work does not model *which* extreme a consumer migrates to, nor individual heterogeneity in the basic-vs-fully-loaded split — a natural hook for a structural choice model.

## Connections
The paper is built directly on [[@Simonson1989|Simonson (1989)]] and [[@Simonson1992|Simonson and Tversky (1992)]], who introduced the compromise effect and extremeness aversion, and on [[@Tversky1993|Tversky and Simonson (1993)]] and Wernerfelt (1995). The alignability construct comes from the authors' own prior work, Gourville and Soman (2005). The regularity benchmark it violates is Luce (1959). The substantive concern — choice probabilities that respond to the composition of the menu rather than just the chosen item's own value — connects to context-dependent and menu-dependent choice models more broadly, including the attraction/asymmetric-dominance effect literature ([[@Huber1982|Huber, Payne, and Puto 1982]]) and reason-based choice ([[@Shafir1993|Shafir, Simonson, and Tversky 1993]]). It also speaks to the assortment-size and choice-overload literature ([[@Iyengar2000|Iyengar and Lepper 2000]]). For an economist's audience the violation of regularity links naturally to bounded-rationality choice procedures such as those in [[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti (2007)]] and to random-utility/context-effect models that attempt to rationalize compromise and attraction effects.

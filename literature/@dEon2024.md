> [!abstract] Abstract
> Researchers building behavioral models, such as behavioral game theorists, use experimental data to evaluate predictive models of human behavior. However, there is little agreement about which loss function should be used in evaluations, with error rate, negative log-likelihood, cross-entropy, Brier score, and squared L2 error all being common choices. We attempt to offer a principled answer to the question of which loss functions should be used for this task, formalizing axioms that we argue loss functions should satisfy. We construct a family of loss functions, which we dub "diagonal bounded Bregman divergences", that satisfy all of these axioms. These rule out many loss functions used in practice, but notably include squared L2 error; we thus recommend its use for evaluating behavioral models.

## Summary
A first-principles, axiomatic treatment of *which loss function* should be used to evaluate (not train) interpretable behavioral models. The authors propose five axioms — two "alignment" axioms governing how loss varies with the prediction and three "interpretability" axioms governing how it varies with the data — and show that the only losses satisfying all five form the family of *diagonal bounded Bregman divergences* (DBBDs). Among losses common in behavioral game theory, only squared $L_2$ error qualifies; error rate, MAE, NLL, cross-entropy, KL, and Brier score each violate at least one axiom, and the entire class of scoring rules fails the interpretability axioms. The recommendation: train with whatever is convenient, but evaluate/report with a DBBD, with squared $L_2$ error as the natural default.

## Research question
Given experimental data and a class of *interpretable* (low-parameter, generally misspecified) predictive models of human behavior, which loss function should a researcher use to evaluate and report model quality? The likelihood principle answers this only for well-specified model classes; the scoring-rule literature answers it for forecasting but leaves an entire class of admissible losses. Neither addresses the misspecified, multiply-sampled, discrete-outcome setting characteristic of behavioral game theory.

## Method / identification
Pure axiomatic theory. The setup: a fixed action set $A=\{1,\dots,d\}$, the simplex $\Delta(A)$ of distributions over actions, an unknown true distribution $p\in\Delta(A)$, and a dataset $y\sim p^n$ of $n$ i.i.d. draws with empirical distribution $\bar p(y)$. A loss is $L:\Delta(A)\times D(A)\to\mathbb{R}$, encoding preferences over predictions. The argument rests on four characteristics of the field (a mapping from settings to finite discrete distributions; multiple samples per setting; the goal of out-of-sample prediction; interpretability/misspecification), so it transfers to any domain sharing them.

Five axioms are imposed. The two **alignment** axioms strengthen standard propriety using *Pareto improvement* ($q\succ_r p$ means $q$ is weakly closer to $r$ than $p$ in every coordinate and strictly closer in one): **Distributional Pareto-Alignment (DPA)** — $f\succ_p g\Rightarrow \mathbb{E}_{y\sim p^n}L(f,y)<\mathbb{E}_{y\sim p^n}L(g,y)$ — and **Sample Pareto-Alignment (SPA)** — $f\succ_{\bar p(y)}g\Rightarrow L(f,y)<L(g,y)$. These imply the weaker Distributional Propriety and Sample Propriety. The three **interpretability** axioms are **Empirical Distribution Sufficiency (EDS)** ($\bar p(y)=\bar p(y')\Rightarrow L(f,y)=L(f,y')$, i.e. invariance to count/order), **Counterfactual Pareto-Regularity (CPR)** (holding $f$ fixed and $n$ fixed, $\bar p(y)\succ_f \bar p(y')\Rightarrow L(f,y)<L(f,y')$), and **Zero-Minimum (ZM)** ($L(\bar p(y),y)=0$).

The characterization builds on Abernethy & Frongillo (2012): any loss satisfying Distributional Propriety is essentially a Bregman divergence $\nabla_{(B,dB)}(\rho(y),f)+c(y)$. Adding SP, ZM, and EDS pins down $c$, $\rho=\bar p$, and removes $n$-dependence, leaving a Bregman divergence between the empirical distribution and the prediction. The qualifying subfamily is the **DBBD**: for a continuously differentiable convex $b:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}$ with bounded derivative, set $B_b(x)=\sum_i b(x_i)$ and $L(f,y)=\nabla_{(B_b,\nabla B_b)}(\bar p(y),f)$.

## Data
None for the main results — this is a theory paper. The body uses small worked numerical examples (e.g. a two-action defect/cooperate game with $p=(2/3,1/3)$) to exhibit each axiom violation; the appendix additionally demonstrates several violations on real behavioral datasets.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 4.4 (informal):** under mild conditions, $L$ satisfies SP and DP iff it is a Bregman divergence $\nabla_{(B(n),dB(n))}(\bar p(y),f)+c(y)$; adding ZM and EDS forces $c\equiv 0$ and removes the $n$-dependence — so DP+SP+ZM+EDS essentially characterize Bregman divergences between empirical distribution and prediction.
- **Theorem 4.6:** every DBBD satisfies all five axioms (SPA, DPA, EDS, CPR, ZM).
- **Evaluation of existing losses (Table 1):** squared $L_2$ error is a DBBD (take $b(x)=x^2$) and satisfies everything. Error rate violates all but EDS; MAE fails DPA/DP (its expected loss elicits the median, not $p$); NLL uniquely violates EDS (it scales with $n$); cross-entropy and Brier score violate CPR and ZM; KL violates SPA/DPA/CPR via infinite boundary losses (any prediction assigning zero probability to an observed action is scored $\infty$).
- **Proposition 5.6:** every scoring rule satisfying SPA violates ZM, and *no* scoring rule satisfies CPR. So the entire class of proper scoring rules — despite its celebrated alignment properties — is unsuitable for *reporting* model performance.
- **Theorem 5.7 / corollary:** every Bregman divergence has a corresponding scoring rule giving identical loss *differences* between predictions (e.g. Brier score ↔ squared $L_2$). Scoring rules are cheaper to minimize (no need to materialize $\bar p(y)$), so they remain fine for *training*; just convert to the matching DBBD for evaluation.

## Contribution
Reframes the loss-choice question in behavioral game theory as an axiomatic problem and resolves it: it gives the first characterization tailored to *misspecified, interpretable* models evaluated on *multiply-sampled discrete* data, cleanly separating the training role (alignment suffices) from the reporting role (interpretability also required). It supplies a concrete, large admissible family (DBBDs) and an actionable incumbent recommendation (squared $L_2$ error), while explaining precisely why each popular alternative misbehaves. It complements [[@Fudenberg2022|Fudenberg et al. (2022)]]: apply their completeness measure to a loss satisfying these alignment axioms.

## Limitations & open questions
- All four field characteristics are load-bearing: DP/DPA are inappropriate for purely descriptive (in-sample-fit) work; DPA/SPA are unnecessary for high-capacity uninterpretable models (deep nets) where propriety alone suffices.
- The interpretability axioms are not well motivated for **continuous** distributions (energy, climate) or single-sample settings (precipitation-type forecasting); extending the axioms by lifting the discreteness requirement or aggregating similar observations is left open.
- Whether a *single* best loss can be argued for is open: it requires identifying further axioms (e.g. agnosticism to action identities in rock-paper-scissors), and it is unknown whether any loss survives such additional constraints.
- ZM is acknowledged as the most subjective axiom (one might instead anchor to a uniform-random baseline).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Society's demands for individual and corporate social responsibility as alternative responses to market and distributive failures are becoming increasingly prominent. We draw on recent developments in the psychology and economics of prosocial behaviour to shed light on this trend and the underlying mix of motivations. We then link individual concerns to corporate social responsibility, contrasting three possible understandings of the term: firms' adoption of a more long-term perspective, the delegated exercise of prosocial behaviour on behalf of stakeholders, and insider-initiated corporate philanthropy. We discuss the benefits, costs and limits of socially responsible behaviour as a means to further societal goals.

## Summary
This is the published version of Tirole's 2009 Coase Lecture: a synthetic, largely non-technical essay that organizes the economics of individual and corporate social responsibility (SRB/CSR) around the psychology of prosocial behaviour. Bénabou and Tirole argue that giving, green consumption, socially responsible investment (SRI) and corporate philanthropy arise as private responses to market and government failure, and are driven by a mix of intrinsic altruism, material incentives, and social- and self-image concerns. They expose the "dark side" of image-based incentives (over-justification, distorted signal targets, the zero-sum positional nature of esteem, and moral licensing), derive a modification of the Pigovian tax/subsidy rule, and then map the individual analysis onto three distinct visions of CSR.

## Research question
Why do citizens and corporations increasingly substitute for the state in correcting market failures and inequality through "socially responsible behaviour", what motivations drive this, and what are the benefits, costs and limits of SRB and CSR as instruments for achieving societal goals? A secondary question is what "CSR" actually means analytically and whether existing empirical work can discriminate among competing interpretations.

## Method / identification
The paper is a theory-led survey/essay rather than an original model or empirical study; it draws results from the authors' formal companion papers (especially Bénabou and [[@Benabou2006|Tirole 2006a]], "Incentives and Prosocial Behavior", and 2010, "Laws and Norms") and reviews experimental and field evidence. The underlying behavioural model has utility of the form
$$U = (v_a + v_y\, y)\,a + \mu_a\, \mathbb{E}[v_a \mid a, y] - C(a),$$
where $a$ is the level of prosocial contribution, $v_a$ parametrizes altruism, $v_y$ the marginal utility of income, $y$ the per-unit material incentive, $C(a)$ a (quadratic) cost, $\mu_a$ the intensity of image concern, and $\mathbb{E}[v_a\mid a,y]$ the observer's posterior on the agent's altruism — the reputational/self-signal payoff. With $(v_a, v_y)$ jointly Gaussian, the signal-extraction problem over the two-dimensional type generates the over-justification effect: as $y$ rises, contribution can flatten or fall before eventually recovering. From this they derive a "corrected Pigou" prescription: the optimal subsidy subtracts from the standard externality value the image rent the agent already captures, and varies non-monotonically with the prevalence of the behaviour (admirable, modal, respectable acts). For CSR they use a taxonomy/typology method, defining three visions and asking which observable correlations each predicts.

## Data
None original. The argument leans on cited experiments and field studies — Glazer and Konrad (1996) on anonymous donations, Lacetera and Macis (2008) on blood-donation medals, Funk (2008) on Swiss mail ballots, DellaVigna, List and Malmendier (2009) on door-to-door solicitation, Carpenter and Myers (2007) on volunteer firemen, Dana, Kuang and Weber (2007) on moral wriggle room, Mazar et al. (2008) on honour-code reminders, [[@Ariely2009|Ariely, Bracha and Meier (2009)]] on image motivation, and Monin et al. on moral-rebel backlash — plus CSR-profitability meta-analyses (Orlitzky et al. 2003; Margolis and Elfenbein 2007) and Andries (2008) on SRI asset returns.

## Key findings
- Image concerns are first-order for prosocial behaviour: anonymous donations are rare, rankings work only when published, and lowering the cost of unobserved good acts (mail voting) can reduce them.
- The "moral wriggle room" results (Dana et al.) show much apparent altruism is self-signalling: people avoid information or delegate "dirty work" to preserve a good self-image.
- Four dark sides of leveraging image: (a) scaling up publicity triggers over-justification / crowding out; (b) giving is distorted toward visible, "glamorous" targets (hybrid cars over efficient furnaces, elite universities over schools); (c) esteem is a positional, zero-sum good with a "reputation-stealing" externality and backlash against do-gooders; (d) moral licensing/credentializing — good acts in one domain justify worse behaviour in another.
- Corrected-Pigou rule: optimal material incentives should be below the standard externality level by the image value bought, and should be strongest for "modal" acts, weak for "admirable" and "respectable" ones.
- Three visions of CSR: (1) win-win / long-term perspective (correcting short-termism — profit-maximizing); (2) delegated philanthropy (firm channels stakeholders' willingness to sacrifice money — also profit-consistent, lower transaction costs than individual giving); (3) insider-initiated philanthropy (management's own tastes — not profit-maximizing, raises governance/entrenchment issues per Cespa and Cestone 2007). Visions 1 and 2 predict positive CSR-profit correlation; vision 3 predicts negative.
- Empirically, CSR-profitability correlation is roughly zero or slightly positive, but is hard to interpret because of mixed channels, endogeneity (CSR and profits jointly determined), the operating-vs-stock-return choice, differing risk premia, and ongoing "slow repricing" of ESG factors.

## Contribution
Provides one of the first organizing economic frameworks for individual and corporate social responsibility, integrating image-motivation theory with public economics (a behaviourally amended Pigovian rule) and with corporate governance/finance (the three-vision taxonomy). It reframes CSR debates by separating profit-consistent from profit-sacrificing interpretations and articulates why empirical CSR-performance studies are inconclusive.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors explicitly flag the work as early-stage and "modest in scope". Open problems they name as project hooks: the downsides of shaming punishments are left for future research; the role of default options and opt-in/opt-out architecture for carbon offsets, payroll/401(k) charitable giving; whether prosocial behaviour is habit-forming (rationale for "compulsory volunteering"); the central institutional question of who — state, stakeholders, or firms — is best placed to address market failure and inequality, and whether their actions are complements or substitutes (public-private partnerships); how free-riding in SRI/fair-trade evolves as the movement scales and decreasing returns depress yields; how rating agencies should aggregate heterogeneous CSR dimensions and whether performance should be absolute or relative (Landier and Nair 2008); and how to keep agencies and CEOs from pandering to constituents' biases.

## Connections
The paper is the survey companion to Bénabou and Tirole's formal work — "Incentives and Prosocial Behavior" (Bénabou and Tirole, 2006a), "Belief in a Just World and Redistributive Politics" (2006b), "Identity, Morals and Taboos: Beliefs as Assets" (2007), and "Laws and Norms" (2010) — and connects to their self-signalling/willpower line. The image-motivation experiments echo [[@Ariely2009|Ariely, Bracha and Meier (2009)]] and the signalling theory of charity in Glazer and Konrad (1996). On prosocial preferences and motivated agents it links to [[@Besley2005|Besley and Ghatak]] (2005, 2007) and Brekke and Nyborg (2008). The moral-wriggle-room and self-deception evidence connects to Dana, Kuang and Weber (2007) and Hamman, Weber and Loewenstein (2009). On the CSR-finance side it engages Baron (2001) on strategic CSR, Friedman (1970) on shareholder primacy, Cespa and Cestone (2007) and Dewatripont, Jewitt and Tirole (1999) on missions and entrenchment, and Hong and Kacperczyk (2009) on "sin stocks". The Pigovian-correction theme extends Pigou (1920). The crowding-out logic relates to the broader motivation-crowding literature (e.g. Frey and Oberholzer-Gee) and to Gneezy and Rustichini on incentives backfiring.

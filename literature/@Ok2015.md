> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper develops axiomatically a revealed preference theory of reference-dependent choice behavior. Instead of taking the reference for an agent as exogenously given in the description of a choice problem, we suitably relax the Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference to obtain, endogenously, the existence of reference alternatives as well as the structure of choice behavior conditional on those alternatives. We show how this model captures some well-known choice patterns such as the attraction effect.

## Summary
Ok, Ortoleva, and Riella build a revealed-preference theory in which an agent's *reference point* is not a primitive given in the choice problem but is inferred endogenously from choice data. They weaken the Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference (WARP) into four behavioral postulates and prove a representation theorem: choice is "as if" the agent either maximizes a reference-free utility $u$ over the whole feasible set, or — when she singles out a reference alternative $r(S)$ — restricts attention to the options that dominate $r(S)$ on every subjective attribute in a set $\mathcal{U}$ and maximizes $u$ over that "attraction region." The model rationalizes the attraction (asymmetric-dominance) effect without assuming alternatives carry exogenous attributes, and derives both the reference points and the relevant attributes from behavior alone.

## Research question
Can the attraction effect — and reference-dependent choice more generally — be captured within the revealed-preference paradigm when (i) reference points are *not* exogenously labeled (no status quo, default, or framing marker), and (ii) alternatives carry *no* exogenously given attribute structure, so the relevant attributes may be private to the decision maker? Equivalently: what minimal, behaviorally testable weakening of WARP characterizes endogenous reference-dependent choice that still retains predictive discipline?

## Method / identification
Pure axiomatic revealed-preference theory. The primitive is a continuous choice correspondence $c$ on $\mathcal{X}$, the nonempty compact subsets of a separable metric space $X$; $\emptyset\neq c(S)\subseteq S$. The authors weaken WARP via four logically independent axioms:

- **No-Cycle**: pairwise choices are acyclic — if $x\in c\{x,y\}$ and $y\in c\{y,z\}$ then $x\in c\{x,z\}$ (rules out cyclic doubleton choice; references cannot act in two-element sets).
- **Rationality of Indifference (RI)**: if $\{x,y\}\subseteq c(S)$ for some $S$, then $\{x,y\}=c\{x,y\}$ (revealed indifference in any menu must already show up pairwise).
- **Reference Acyclicity (RA)**: chaining revealed references is disciplined — if $x_i$ is a revealed $c$-reference for $x_{i+1}$ along a chain, then $x_1$ is at least a *potential* $c$-reference for $x_n$.
- **Reference Consistency (RCon)**: for any choice problem $S$ and any $c$-cover $\mathcal{T}$ of $S$ containing a doubleton, WARP holds on at least one member $T'$ of the cover: $c(T')=c(S)\cap T'$. This confines WARP violations to reference effects.

Two behavioral definitions drive the construction. $z$ is a **revealed reference** for $x$ if some $y$ exhibits a WARP-violating "rescue" of $x$ by $z$ (e.g. $y=c\{x,y\}$ but $\{x,y\}\cap c\{x,y,z\}=\{x\}$). $z$ is a **potential reference** for $x$ if adding $z$ never helps a rival $y$ against $x$ nor removes an already-chosen $x$ (unless $z$ is the unique choice). The target representation is a triplet $(u,r,\mathcal{U})$ with $u:X\to\mathbb{R}$ continuous, reference map $r:\mathcal{X}\to X\cup\{\diamond\}$, and a set $\mathcal{U}$ of real "attribute" maps; the attraction region is $\mathcal{U}^{\uparrow}(x)=\{y:U(y)>U(x)\ \forall U\in\mathcal{U}\}$. Proofs use Debreu's representation theorem (via No-Cycle giving a complete continuous preorder) plus a combinatorial "$c$-awkward set" argument.

## Data
None — this is a theory paper. There is no empirical estimation. The authors motivate the model with documented attraction-effect evidence from psychology and marketing (questionnaire and incentivized lab studies, plus the Doyle et al. grocery field experiment) and note that RCon, restated for finite $X$ with three-set covers (Remark 1), is "relatively easy to test in experiments."

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1 (representation).** A continuous $c$ satisfies No-Cycle, RI, RA, and RCon if and only if there exists a reference-dependent choice model $(u,r,\mathcal{U})$ such that: if $r(S)=\diamond$, $c(S)=\arg\max u(S)$; if $r(S)\neq\diamond$, $c(S)=\arg\max u\big(S\cap \mathcal{U}^{\uparrow}(r(S))\big)$; and a nestedness condition (iii) ties references across subsets. The reference $r(S)$ is itself feasible but never chosen; standard rationality is the special case $r\equiv\diamond$.
- **Proposition 1.** Whenever $c(S)\neq\arg\max u(S)$, the model's reference $r(S)$ is a *revealed* $c$-reference for every $x\in c(S)$ — linking the representation back to behavior.
- **Proposition 2.** Revealed reference $\Rightarrow$ attribute dominance ($U(x)>U(z)\ \forall U\in\mathcal{U}$); attribute dominance $\Rightarrow$ potential reference. (Remark 2: under No-Cycle, RI, and "every revealed reference is potential" replacing RA, one gets a more general constraint-set version $c(S)=\arg\max u(S\cap Q(r(S)))$ but loses the attribute interpretation.)
- **Uniqueness (Proposition 3, Corollary 1).** Two equivalent models share an ordinally unique $u$, the same revealed-reference correspondence, and the same *reference correspondence* $R$; the reference map $r$ is only non-unique in a behaviorally inconsequential, "arbitrary" way (Example 1), and redundancy in $\mathcal{U}$ is confined to attributes not induced by revealed references.

## Contribution
First revealed-preference (axiomatic) model to derive *both* reference points and the relevant (possibly subjective) attributes endogenously from choice, with no exogenous attribute or reference labeling, while deviating from rational choice in a parsimonious, testable way. It explains the attraction effect in arbitrary choice domains — including ones where attributes are private (e.g. voting over candidates) — and identifies the exact behavioral content separating reference-dependence from other menu effects.

## Limitations & open questions
- "Being a reference" is modeled as **all-or-nothing**, not graded; the authors explicitly note RI could be weakened to allow graded references but do not pursue it (a clear extension hook).
- The model addresses **deterministic** choice only; it does not cover stochastic reference dependence (contrast Natenzon 2014) nor risk/uncertainty-driven references (contrast Köszegi–Rabin).
- The **reference map is non-unique** (Example 1); only the reference *correspondence* is pinned down — leaving open richer identification of *which* alternative is "the" reference.
- The authors leave **applications** to future/companion work: optimal exploitation of the attraction effect in monopoly screening and market segmentation, and reference effects in political platform choice and portfolio presentation.
- RA could be replaced by the weaker "revealed-implies-potential" axiom (Remark 2), yielding a more general but less interpretable model — characterizing the trade-off is open.

## Connections
Closest revealed-preference neighbors are the limited-attention / attention-filter model of [[@Masatlioglu2012|Masatlioglu, Nakajima, and Ozbay (2012)]] (their finite-$X$ choice-function case nests this model's finite specialization, but is otherwise non-nested), the rationalization-by-multiple-rationales model of [[@Cherepanov2013|Cherepanov, Feddersen, and Sandroni (2013)]] (related but non-nested), and the sequential two-stage procedures of [[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti (2007)]] and [[@Rubinstein2006|Rubinstein and Salant (2006)]], whose WARP violations are complementary — their intersection with this class is just classical rationality under No-Cycle. The only other endogenous-reference revealed-preference work cited is Köszegi and [[@Koszegi2006b|Rabin]] (2006, 2007), where the reference is a belief about outcomes rather than a feasible (undesirable) choice item. Other attraction-effect models include de [[@deClippel2012|Clippel and Eliaz (2012)]] (bargaining-solution rationalization), [[@Lombardi2009|Lombardi (2009)]] (uncovered-set elimination), [[@Kamenica2008|Kamenica (2008)]] (equilibrium with informed/uninformed consumers), and Natenzon (2014) (gradual-learning stochastic choice). The exogenous-reference / status-quo tradition includes Tversky and Kahneman (1991), Bossert and Sprumont (2003), Masatlioglu and Ok (2005, 2014), Sagi (2006), and Apesteguia and Ballester. The empirical attraction-effect basis traces to [[@Huber1982|Huber, Payne, and Puto (1982)]], [[@Simonson1992|Simonson and Tversky (1992)]], and Doyle et al. (1999); foundational revealed preference is Arrow (1959) and Debreu's utility representation theorem. Manzini and Mariotti (2010) is cited for evidence that menu effects, not pairwise inconsistency, drive most WARP violations.

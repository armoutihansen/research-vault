> [!abstract] Abstract
> Recently developed models of fairness can explain a wide variety of seemingly contradictory facts. One of the most controversial and yet unresolved issues in the modeling of fairness preferences concerns the behavioral relevance of fairness intentions. Intuitively, fairness intentions seem to play an important role in economic relations, political struggles, and legal disputes but there is surprisingly little direct evidence for its behavioral importance. We provide experimental evidence for the behavioral relevance of fairness intentions in this paper. Our main result indicates that the attribution of fairness intentions is important in both the domains of negatively and positively reciprocal behavior. This means that equity models exclusively based on preferences over the distribution of material payoffs cannot capture reciprocal behavior. Models that take players' fairness intentions and distributional preferences into account are consistent with our data, while models that focus exclusively on intentions or on the distribution of material payoffs are not.

## Summary
Falk, Fehr & Fischbacher provide clean experimental evidence that **fairness intentions**, not just material outcomes, drive reciprocal behavior. Using a moonlighting game in which the first mover's action is either chosen ($I$-treatment) or determined by a salient random device that mimics the human choice distribution ($NI$-treatment), they show that both positive and negative reciprocity are substantially weaker when intentions cannot be attributed. This rejects pure outcome-based inequity-aversion models (Fehr–Schmidt; Bolton–Ockenfels). But because some reciprocity survives the $NI$-treatment, pure-intention models (Rabin; Dufwenberg–Kirchsteiger) are also rejected. Models combining both, notably [[@Falk2006|Falk & Fischbacher (2006)]], fit best.

## Research question
Do fair-minded people respond to fair or unfair **intentions**, or solely to fair or unfair **outcomes**? At stake is not only the correct modeling of fairness preferences, but the consequentialism of standard utility theory, which holds that an action's utility depends only on its consequences, not on the intention behind it.

## Method / identification
The design is a one-shot **moonlighting game** (Abbink et al., 2000). Both players are endowed with 12 points. Player $A$ chooses $a \in \{-6,\dots,6\}$: if $a \geq 0$, $A$ gives and $B$ receives the tripled amount $3a$; if $a < 0$, $A$ takes $|a|$, gaining $|a|$ while $B$ loses $|a|$. Player $B$ then chooses $b \in \{-6,\dots,18\}$, where $b \geq 0$ is a reward transferring $b$ from $B$ to $A$, and $b < 0$ is a sanction costing $B$ exactly $|b|$ but reducing $A$'s income by $3|b|$. The game permits both positive and negative reciprocity.

The **strategy method** elicits $B$'s response to every feasible $a$, enabling within-subject measurement of both positive and negative reciprocity. The treatment variable is whether $A$'s choice is intentional. In the $I$-treatment, $A$ chooses $a$ himself (full control, signals intent). In the $NI$-treatment, a salient random device (two ten-sided dice rolled in front of $B$) determines $a$ per a fixed table. Crucially, the random distribution **mimics the empirical human choice distribution** from Abbink et al. (2000), and $B$ subjects in the $I$-treatment were told that distribution, so beliefs about $A$'s choice distribution are held constant across treatments. This isolates intention attribution from confounds: efficiency motives (the design rules out total-payoff increases via reciprocity), repetition (one-shot), weak manipulation (salient give/take around a common reference point), and unequal-probability "unfair device" anger (matched distributions). Identification of treatment differences uses Mann–Whitney $U$-tests per action and an OLS regression of $B$'s payoff impact on $a$, a treatment dummy $I$, and an interaction $a \times I$ (robust SEs clustered on subject).

## Data
112 subjects (66 in $I$, 46 in $NI$), non-economics students at the University of Zurich / ETH Zurich, run June 1998 using z-Tree (Fischbacher, 2007). 1 point = 1 CHF; average earnings CHF 22.20 ($I$) and CHF 24.10 ($NI$) including a CHF 10 show-up fee.

## Key findings
- **Treatment effect on aggregate reciprocity.** In the $I$-treatment, rewards rise in $a$ and sanctions rise in $-a$, contradicting the self-interest prediction ($b=0,\forall a$). In the $NI$-treatment reciprocity is much weaker: the entire distribution of $B$'s responses shifts toward zero, and median behavior coincides exactly with the selfish prediction. Mann–Whitney tests reject equality for almost all $a \neq 0$ at the 5% level. The regression interaction $a \times I$ is highly significant ($0.907$, $p<.01$) while the $I$ dummy is insignificant, so treatments differ only away from $a=0$.
- **Rejects pure inequity aversion.** Since payoff consequences are identical across treatments, Fehr–Schmidt and Bolton–Ockenfels predict identical $B$ behavior; the data reject this.
- **Rejects pure-intention models.** Reciprocity is not fully eradicated in $NI$: the coefficient on $a$ is positive and weakly significant, and Spearman correlations between mean $b$ and $a$ are $0.872$ ($NI$) versus $0.995$ ($I$), both $p<.001$. Thus outcomes matter too, contradicting Dufwenberg–Kirchsteiger's $b=0,\forall a$ prediction for $NI$.
- **Individual-level.** Selfish behavior ($b=0,\forall a$) rises from 0% ($I$) to 30% ($NI$); any reciprocity drops from 76% to 39%. Among $I$-subjects, 40% are both positively and negatively reciprocal, but 21% reward without punishing and 15% punish without rewarding, contradicting single-parameter reciprocity and the Fehr–Schmidt assumption that rewarders must also punish.

## Contribution
Provides the first clean, confound-controlled demonstration that intention attribution matters for **both** positive and negative reciprocity, at aggregate and individual levels. It adjudicates among major fairness models, showing combined intention-plus-distribution models (Falk & Fischbacher, 2006) dominate, and casts doubt on consequentialism in standard utility theory. It is also the first to measure correlation of positive and negative reciprocity within individuals.

## Limitations & open questions
- The Dufwenberg–Kirchsteiger model has **multiple equilibria** and depends on higher-order beliefs; a proper test requires eliciting those beliefs, which the authors did not do. Higher $a$ need not signal friendlier intentions in equilibrium.
- The strategy method could bias the estimated intention effect upward if subjects respond emotionally to an actually experienced unfair offer but not to a hypothetical one (raised by a referee).
- Whether **incompetence** is punished like unfair intent is left open: punishment should depend on whether a bad outcome reflects laziness (unkindness) versus genuine incapacity.
- The "other patterns" subjects (non-monotone, e.g. reward low and punish higher transfers) are unexplained; they might reflect non-monotone beliefs in a Dufwenberg–Kirchsteiger spirit.

## Connections
The study tests four families of fairness models. Outcome-based inequity aversion comes from [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] and [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]]; intention-based reciprocity from [[@Rabin1993|Rabin (1993)]] and its sequential extension [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger (2004)]]; the combined model is [[@Falk2006|Falk & Fischbacher (2006)]]; type-based kindness models are [[@Levine1998|Levine (1998)]] and [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]]. The game builds on Abbink, Irlenbusch & Renner (2000). Prior, less conclusive evidence on intentions includes Blount (1995) and Offerman (2002) (negative reciprocity), and null results in Charness (2004), Bolton, Brandts & Ockenfels (1998), and [[@Cox2004|Cox (2004)]] (positive reciprocity). Choice-set effects on fairness perception appear in Falk, Fehr & Fischbacher (2003) and Brandts & Solà (2001). Efficiency-motive confounds draw on [[@Andreoni2002|Andreoni & Miller (2002)]], Engelmann & Strobel (2004), and [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]]. The norm of reciprocity traces to Gouldner (1960); related procedural-fairness lottery evidence to Bolton, Brandts & Ockenfels (2005). Software is z-Tree (Fischbacher, 2007).

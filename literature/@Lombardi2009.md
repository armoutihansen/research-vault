> [!abstract] Abstract
> A reason-based choice correspondence rationalizes choice behaviour in terms of a two-stage choice procedure. Given a feasible set S, the individual eliminates from it all of the dominated alternatives according to her fixed (not necessarily complete) strict preference relation, in the first step. In the second step, first she constructs for each maximal alternative identified in the first step its lower contour set (i.e. the set of alternatives which are dominated by it in S), and then she eliminates from the maximal set all of those alternatives so that the following justification holds: there exists another maximal alternative whose lower contour set strictly contains that of another maximal alternative. This procedural model captures the basic idea behind the experimental finding known as ``attraction effect''. We study the rationalizability of reason-based choice correspondences axiomatically. We relate our choice-consistency conditions to standard consistency proprieties. Our characterization result offers testable restrictions on this 'choice anomaly' for large (but finite) set of alternatives.

## Summary
Lombardi proposes a two-stage procedural choice model that gives a revealed-preference foundation for the *attraction effect* (asymmetric dominance). An agent holds a fixed but possibly incomplete strict preference $\succ$. From any finite feasible set she first discards dominated alternatives, keeping the $\succ$-maximal ones; then she breaks ties among maximals by a set-inclusion criterion on lower contour sets, keeping a maximal $x$ only if no other maximal $y$ strictly dominates *more* alternatives than $x$ does. The paper's contribution is an axiomatic characterization of exactly the choice correspondences generated by this procedure, using six choice-consistency conditions that are weakenings of standard ones (Property $\alpha$, Property $\beta$, WARP, WARNI, BDC) plus a novel "Reason-Based Bias" axiom encoding the attraction effect.

## Research question
Can the systematic, context-dependent "attraction effect" — where adding a relatively inferior decoy makes a dominating alternative more attractive — be reconciled with the revealed-preference methodology, and which testable choice-consistency restrictions exactly characterize such reason-based behaviour over large (finite) menus?

## Method / identification
The setup is abstract revealed-preference theory. $X$ is a fixed universal set of alternatives, $\mathcal{S}$ the collection of nonempty finite subsets, and a choice correspondence $C$ assigns a nonempty $C(S)\subseteq S$ to each $S$. The primitive preference $\succ\subseteq X\times X$ is a strict, **acyclic** (hence asymmetric), and not-necessarily-complete relation. For $S$, the lower contour set is $\prec(x,S)=\{y\in S\mid x\in S\setminus\{y\},\, x\succ y\}$ and the maximal set is $M(S,\succ)=\{x\in S\mid y\not\succ x \text{ for all } y\in S\setminus\{x\}\}$.

A **reason-based choice correspondence** is defined by
$$C(S)=\{x\in M(S,\succ)\mid \prec(x,S)\subset\prec(y,S)\text{ for no }y\in M(S,\succ)\setminus\{x\}\}.$$
That is, after taking maximals, eliminate any maximal whose lower contour set is *strictly contained* in that of another maximal (the rival dominates strictly more, giving a "reason" to drop the weaker one).

The identification strategy is axiomatic. Lombardi introduces a key auxiliary notion: two alternatives $x,y\notin S$ are **indistinguishable** on $S$ if they behave identically in all pairwise comparisons with each $z\in S$. He then states six axioms: Property $\alpha 2$ ($x\in C(S)\Rightarrow x\in C(\{x,y\})$ for all $y\in S$); **Weak Property $\beta$** (Sen's $\beta$ restricted to indistinguishable pairs); **Weak WARP** (Samuelson's WARP restricted to a uniquely chosen $x$ and indistinguishable $x,y$); **Strong BDC**, a strengthening of Binary Dominance Consistency / Condorcet-winner principle (if $\{x\}=C(S)$ and $x$ beats every element of $T$ in pairs, then $\{x\}=C(S\cup T)$); **Weak WARNI**, a weakening of Eliaz–Ok's Weak Axiom of Revealed Non-Inferiority; and **Reason-Based Bias (RBB)**: for distinct $x,y,z$, if $\{x\}=C(\{x,y\})$, $x\in C(\{x,z\})$, and $y\in C(\{y,z\})$, then $\{x\}=C(\{x,y,z\})$ — directly encoding the decoy/attraction pattern.

## Data
None — this is a pure axiomatic/theoretical paper. Motivation draws on experimental evidence on the attraction effect (cited via Rieskamp, Busemeyer & Mellers 2006; [[@Simonson1989|Simonson 1989]]; [[@Tversky1993|Tversky & Simonson 1993]]), illustrated with running examples (a digital-camera choice and a lunch-voucher cycle across three days), but no original data are analysed.

## Key findings
- **Lemma 1**: the six axioms ($\alpha 2$, W$\beta$, WWARP, SBDC, WWARNI, RBB) are mutually independent, each shown via an explicit small counterexample.
- **Theorem 1 (characterization)**: a choice correspondence $C$ on $\mathcal{S}$ is a reason-based choice correspondence **if and only if** it satisfies all six axioms simultaneously. The proof (Appendix B) constructs the revealed relation by $x\succ y\Leftrightarrow C(\{x,y\})=\{x\}$, proves it acyclic, and shows both inclusions of the defining equation.
- The model **violates** Sen's (1977) Property $\gamma 2$ and Sen's (1971) Property $\gamma$, and need not satisfy "binariness": an explicit example gives $y\in C(\{x,y\})$, $y\in C(\{x',y\})$, yet $\{x\}=C(\{x,x',y\})$. Consequently $\alpha 2$ is **not** equivalent to Property $\alpha$ here, so Sen's Proposition 10 fails in this framework. Reason-based choice can violate Property $\alpha$ and WARP, yet never selects a dominated alternative.

## Contribution
The paper supplies the first full axiomatic, revealed-preference characterization of a procedural model whose distinctive second stage uses information from the *entire* offered set — specifically the strict set-inclusion order on lower contour sets — to break ties among incomparable maximals. It thereby renders the attraction effect a testable choice "anomaly" with sharp restrictions, while keeping the agent normatively defensible (she never chooses a dominated alternative). The framework refines Ok's (2004) two-stage rationalization and contrasts with sequentially-rationalizable shortlisting models by replacing a second fixed relation with one constructed endogenously from the menu.

## Limitations & open questions
- The model admits **framing manipulations** (the lunch-voucher example produces a cycle of choices across menus that violate Property $\alpha$); the author flags these as hard to explain from pure preference maximization and as a tension to investigate further.
- The diversity of possible "reasons" guiding decisions is acknowledged as open: the set-inclusion tie-break is one of many self-imposed rules (cf. Baigent & Gaertner's "never choose the uniquely largest").
- Results are confined to **finite** menus; extension to infinite/continuous choice sets is left open.
- The relationship to reference-dependent and history-dependent procedures (Houy; Masatlioglu & Ok) is contrasted but not unified — a candidate for a more general procedural theory.

## Connections
The work builds directly on the revealed-preference tradition of [[@Samuelson1938|Samuelson (1938)]] and Richter (1966), and on Sen's (1971, 1977, 1993, 1997) analysis of choice-consistency conditions (Properties $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$). The second-stage idea generalizes Ok (2004) on independence of irrelevant alternatives. It sits alongside two-stage boundedly rational procedures: Manzini & Mariotti (2006, 2007) on sequentially rationalizable choice, [[@Rubinstein2006|Rubinstein & Salant]] (2006, 2008) on the principle of revealed preference, and Houy (2006) on choice with states of mind. The novel axioms adapt [[@Eliaz2006|Eliaz & Ok (2006)]] (WARNI; incomplete preferences) and [[@Ehlers2008|Ehlers & Sprumont (2008)]] (Binary Dominance Consistency, weakened WARP and top-cycle rules), with related cyclic-revelation results in Lombardi (2008). The empirical and conceptual motivation comes from [[@Simonson1989|Simonson (1989)]], [[@Tversky1993|Tversky & Simonson (1993)]], [[@Shafir1993|Shafir, Simonson & Tversky (1993)]] on reason-based choice, and Rieskamp, Busemeyer & Mellers (2006); [[@Kalai2002|Spiegler (2002)]] formalizes reason-based choice in games. Masatlioglu & Ok (2006) provide the contrasting reference-dependent model, and Mariotti (2008) and Suzumura (1983) the broader revealed-preference context.

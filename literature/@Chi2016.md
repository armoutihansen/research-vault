> [!abstract] Abstract
> The k-means algorithm is often used in clustering applications but its usage requires a complete data matrix. Missing data, however, are common in many applications. Mainstream approaches to clustering missing data reduce the missing data problem to a complete data formulation through either deletion or imputation but these solutions may incur significant costs. Our k-POD method presents a simple extension of k-means clustering for missing data that works even when the missingness mechanism is unknown, when external information is unavailable, and when there is significant missingness in the data.

## Summary

k-POD ("k-means on Partially Observed Data") extends Lloyd's k-means to incomplete data matrices without deletion or imputation. It recasts k-means as a low-rank matrix factorization, defines the loss as the squared residual over the *observed* entries only, and minimizes this missing-data loss with a majorization-minimization (MM) algorithm whose minimization step is just an ordinary k-means run on a matrix whose unobserved entries are filled in with the current centroid estimates. The method is tuning-free, requires no assumptions on the missingness mechanism, runs in $O(knp)$ per iteration, and remains accurate and fast even at very high missingness (up to 75%), where mainstream multiple-imputation pipelines (Amelia, mi, mice) either fail to complete or take orders of magnitude longer.

## Research question

How can the simple, fast, and ubiquitous k-means clustering algorithm be extended to cluster partially observed data directly — without resorting to deletion (which discards information) or probabilistic imputation (which is costly and relies on possibly-wrong distributional assumptions about the missingness) — in a way that still works when the missingness mechanism is unknown and missingness is severe?

## Method / identification

The authors first restate the k-means problem as a matrix factorization. With data $Y\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$, a membership matrix $A$ in the set $H=\{A\in\{0,1\}^{n\times k}:A\mathbf{1}=\mathbf{1}\}$ (one-hot cluster assignments), and a centroid matrix $B\in\mathbb{R}^{k\times p}$, classic k-means minimizes the sum of squared residuals, equivalently
$$\min_{A\in H,\,B}\ \lVert Y-AB\rVert_F^2.$$

For missing data, let $\Omega\subseteq\{1,\dots,n\}\times\{1,\dots,p\}$ index the observed entries and let $P_\Omega$ project onto $\Omega$. The missing-data k-means problem is
$$\min_{A\in H,\,B}\ \lVert P_\Omega(Y)-P_\Omega(AB)\rVert_F^2,$$
i.e. the fit is scored only over observed entries (the same residual-over-$\Omega$ used in matrix completion, e.g. Candès & [[@Candes2009|Recht 2009]]), but the goal here is clustering, not imputation.

This is solved by an MM algorithm. A surrogate $g(A,B\mid\tilde A,\tilde B)$ is built by adding the nonnegative term $\lVert P_{\Omega^c}(AB)-P_{\Omega^c}(\tilde A\tilde B)\rVert_F^2$ to the objective, which majorizes the loss (it satisfies domination $g\ge f$ everywhere and tangency $g=f$ at the anchor). The surrogate collapses to $\lVert\hat Y-AB\rVert_F^2$ where $\hat Y=P_\Omega(Y)+P_{\Omega^c}(\tilde A\tilde B)$ — that is, observed entries kept, unobserved entries filled from the current factorization. Minimizing the surrogate is therefore exactly a complete-data k-means run on $\hat Y$. The resulting Algorithm 1 iterates: (1) fill in $Y^{(m)}=P_\Omega(Y)+P_{\Omega^c}(A^{(m)}B^{(m)})$; (2) $(A^{(m+1)},B^{(m+1)})\leftarrow\text{k-means}(Y^{(m)})$; repeat to convergence. Because k-means itself is monotone and $g$ majorizes the loss, the MM iterates drive the missing-data objective monotonically downhill — the descent guarantee holds even though the inner k-means only inexactly minimizes the surrogate. Initialization uses column means to fill in, then k-means++ (Arthur & Vassilvitskii 2007) for the starting partition, inheriting its $O(\log k)$ approximation guarantee.

## Data

One real dataset — the UCI "wine" data (178 observations, 13 chemical variables plus a class label, 3 clusters) — and two simulated datasets drawn as mixtures of $k=10$ and $k=25$ isotropic multivariate normals (500 observations, 100 variables, covariance $\Sigma=10I$, means iid $N(0,10^2)$). Missingness was synthetically imposed under MCAR (entries removed at random), MAR (entries removed only in selected columns), and NMAR (entries removed by within-variable quantile thresholds, mimicking income-style nonresponse), at overall missingness up to 45% (wine) and 75% (simulated). 100 trials per cell; clustering quality measured by the (adjusted) Rand index against true labels; experiments capped at 55 hours.

## Key findings

- **Descent guarantee:** k-POD provably drives the incomplete k-means objective monotonically downhill, $\lVert P_\Omega(Y)-P_\Omega(A^{(m)}B^{(m)})\rVert_F^2\ge\lVert P_\Omega(Y)-P_\Omega(A^{(m+1)}B^{(m+1)})\rVert_F^2$, via the MM majorization combined with k-means monotonicity.
- **Accuracy at high missingness:** at low missingness all methods do well, but as missingness grows imputation pipelines fail to complete (Amelia fails from ~16–36%, mice from ~19% under MAR). k-POD completes every scenario and is the most accurate at 50–75% missingness in the simulated MCAR/NMAR experiments; elsewhere it matches mice's Rand scores.
- **Speed:** each iteration is $O(knp)$, linear in the data; the fill-in step is just copying centroid entries. k-POD is roughly as fast as Amelia, much faster than mice, and dramatically faster than mi (e.g. mi ~70–140s vs k-POD <1s on wine). On the simulated data Amelia and mi failed to finish within 55 hours.

## Contribution

To the authors' knowledge this is the first method to extend k-means clustering to missing data by minimizing fit over observed entries only, bypassing the complete-data reformulation entirely. The conceptual move is to fuse a matrix-completion-style observed-entries loss with the MM framework so that the inner solver is unchanged off-the-shelf k-means — yielding a "works out of the box" meta-algorithm that is simple, tuning-free, distribution-assumption-free, and provably convergent. Software ships as the kpodclustr R package on CRAN.

## Limitations & open questions

- k-POD is a meta-algorithm wrapped around k-means and inherits all of k-means' weaknesses: it struggles when clusters overlap or when within-cluster scatter varies greatly across clusters. In such cases (even with complete data) EM/mixture-model clustering can be superior, and the analogous missing-data mixture-model extensions may be required.
- The precise relationship between k-POD and the EM algorithms for missing-data mixture models (Ghahramani & Jordan 1994; Hunt & Jorgensen 2003; Lin, Lee & Ho 2006) is left open — "beyond the scope of this article" — given that k-means is itself an approximation to EM clustering (Kulis & Jordan 2012).
- Convergence speed degrades when there are many clusters and high MCAR missingness simultaneously (k-POD takes longer to converge for large $k$ such as $k=25$).
- The objective is non-convex (k-means is NP-hard), so only monotone descent to a local optimum is guaranteed, not global optimality.

## Connections

The core reformulation borrows the observed-entries residual loss $\lVert P_\Omega(Y)-P_\Omega(AB)\rVert_F^2$ from the matrix-completion literature — Candès & [[@Candes2009|Recht (2009)]], Cai, Candès & Shen (2010), and Mazumder, Hastie & Tibshirani (2010) — but redirects it toward clustering rather than imputation. The MM machinery follows Becker, Yang & Lange (1997) and Lange, Hunter & Yang (2000). The base clustering algorithm and its seeding are Lloyd (1982) / MacQueen (1967) / Hartigan & Wong (1979) and k-means++ of Arthur & Vassilvitskii (2007); NP-hardness is from Aloise et al. (2009). The missingness taxonomy (MCAR/MAR/NMAR) and the multiple-imputation baselines come from Little & Rubin (2002) and Rubin (1987, 1996), implemented via Amelia (Honaker, King & Blackwell 2011), mice (van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn 2011), and mi (Su et al. 2011). The augmented-deletion alternative for astronomy is Wagstaff & Laidler (2005); the connection of k-means to Bayesian-nonparametric EM is Kulis & Jordan (2012); missing-data mixture models are Ghahramani & Jordan (1994), Hunt & Jorgensen (2003), and Lin, Lee & Ho (2006). The Rand index for evaluation is Rand (1971), and the high-missingness motivation cites the Netflix Prize (Bell, Koren & Volinsky 2010).

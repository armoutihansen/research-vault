> [!abstract] Abstract
> We develop a model of reference-dependent preferences and loss aversion where "gain-loss utility" is derived from standard "consumption utility" and the reference point is determined endogenously by the economic environment. We assume that a person's reference point is her rational expectations held in the recent past about outcomes, which are determined in a personal equilibrium by the requirement that they must be consistent with optimal behavior given expectations. In deterministic environments, choices maximize consumption utility, but gain-loss utility influences behavior when there is uncertainty. Applying the model to consumer behavior, we show that willingness to pay for a good is increasing in the expected probability of purchase and in the expected prices conditional on purchase. In within-day labor-supply decisions, a worker is less likely to continue work if income earned thus far is unexpectedly high, but more likely to show up as well as continue work if expected income is high.

## Summary
Kőszegi and Rabin propose a portable model of reference-dependent preferences in which the reference point is not the status quo but a person's recent rational expectations about outcomes, pinned down endogenously through a fixed-point "personal equilibrium." Total utility combines classical consumption utility with gain-loss utility built directly from changes in consumption utility, so any reference-independent model can be mechanically translated into its reference-dependent counterpart. In deterministic settings the model reduces to standard utility maximization, but under uncertainty it generates novel, testable predictions for consumer demand (attachment and comparison effects) and labor supply (target-income behavior).

## Research question
What is the reference point, and how is it determined endogenously? Existing reference-dependence theories (notably prospect theory) specify how people react to departures from a posited reference point but leave the reference point itself exogenous and usually identified with the status quo. The paper asks how to (i) construct gain-loss utility disciplined by consumption utility, and (ii) endogenize the reference point as rational expectations, so as to obtain a generally applicable, near-"zero-degrees-of-freedom" theory.

## Method / identification
The framework is a formal decision-theoretic model. A person consuming a $K$-dimensional bundle $c\in\mathbb{R}^K$ relative to reference bundle $r$ has utility $u(c\mid r)\equiv m(c)+n(c\mid r)$, where consumption utility $m(c)\equiv\sum_k m_k(c_k)$ and gain-loss utility $n(c\mid r)\equiv\sum_k n_k(c_k\mid r_k)$ are both additively separable across dimensions. Gain-loss utility is tied to consumption utility through a universal gain-loss function $\mu$: $n_k(c_k\mid r_k)\equiv\mu(m_k(c_k)-m_k(r_k))$. The function $\mu$ satisfies Kahneman-Tversky value-function properties (A0-A4): $\mu(0)=0$, strictly increasing, loss aversion in the large ($\mu(y)+\mu(-y)<\mu(x)+\mu(-x)$ for $y>x>0$), diminishing sensitivity ($\mu''\le 0$ for gains, $\ge 0$ for losses), and small-stakes loss aversion $\lambda\equiv\mu'_-(0)/\mu'_+(0)>1$. A linear-gain-loss variant A3' ($\mu''=0$) isolates loss aversion from diminishing sensitivity.

Both outcomes and the reference point may be stochastic: a lottery $F$ evaluated against a stochastic reference lottery $G$ yields $U(F\mid G)=\int\int u(c\mid r)\,dG(r)\,dF(c)$, so each outcome is compared against every point in the support of the reference lottery.

The reference point is then endogenized as recent rational expectations via two solution concepts. A selection of plans $\{F_l\in D_l\}$ over possible choice sets (drawn by belief $Q$) is a **personal equilibrium (PE)** if planned behavior in each contingency is optimal given the reference point generated by those very plans, $\int F_l\,dQ(l)$. A **preferred personal equilibrium (PPE)** is the PE with highest ex ante expected utility. Existence follows from Kőszegi (2005).

## Data
None - this is a theory paper. It is motivated by, and tested against, prior empirical work (lab endowment-effect studies; New York taxi-driver labor-supply data from Camerer et al. 1997 and Farber 2004, 2005; stadium-vendor and bicycle-messenger studies), but presents no original data.

## Key findings
**Proposition 1** establishes basic comparative statics: fixing outcomes, a (FOSD-)lower reference point makes a person happier, and preferences exhibit status-quo bias (if one would abandon reference $r$ for $c$, then $c$ is strictly preferred when $c$ itself is the reference). **Proposition 2** shows that when $m$ is linear, $u(c\mid r)-u(r\mid r)=\sum_k v_k(c_k-r_k)$ inherits the A0-A4 properties, recovering prospect-theory shape locally. **Proposition 3** shows that in deterministic environments PPE coincides with maximizing consumption utility $\arg\max_{c}m(c)$, so loss aversion affects neither behavior nor welfare without uncertainty.

In the **shopping** application ($m(c)=c_1+c_2$, shoes worth \$1, $\mu(x)=\eta x$ for gains and $\eta\lambda x$ for losses), willingness to pay rises with the expected probability of purchase (the **attachment effect**, eq. 6: bound increasing in $q_L$) and with expected prices conditional on purchase (the **comparison effect**, eq. 7: bound increasing in $p_L$), the latter producing a violation of the law of demand. The Becker-DeGroot-Marschak elicitation mechanism is shown to fail in theory, not just in practice, because elicited value is expectations-dependent.

In the **driving / labor-supply** application (Proposition 4), the afternoon reservation wage is increasing in the morning-wage *surprise*: drivers have negative labor-supply elasticity with respect to unexpected morning income (rationalizing "target income"), yet higher *expected* wages raise both participation and afternoon driving, avoiding the unrealistic prediction that generically higher wages lower effort.

## Contribution
The paper supplies the first widely adopted formalization of an expectations-based, endogenous reference point with an explicit equilibrium rule (PE/PPE), and disciplines gain-loss utility by deriving it from consumption utility. This yields a portable "translation algorithm" from any classical model to its reference-dependent analogue, reconciling endowment-effect evidence (including its absence among experienced traders or budgeted buyers/sellers) and unifying consumer-demand and labor-supply anomalies under one mechanism.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors explicitly flag several gaps as hooks for further work: (1) the set of hedonic consumption dimensions over which gain-loss utility separates is taken as given and may require judgment (the Tropicana vs. Florida's Natural example); (2) the "moment of first focus" that fixes the relevant expectations $Q$ is underspecified and interpretational; (3) the model assumes preferences are linear in probabilities despite evidence of probability weighting; (4) the exact treatment of stochastic reference points (whether losses-relative-to-a-possible-outcome dominate relief) lacks decisive evidence; (5) the model does not cover settings where long delays separate decision and consumption, where alternative models using the same principles would apply. They also note many unexplored applications: auctions (revenue non-equivalence), intertemporal choice mimicking present bias, principal-agent contracting, and bargaining/posturing.

## Connections
The gain-loss value function builds directly on [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky (1979)]] prospect theory and the A0-A4 axioms of Bowman, Minehart & Rabin (1999). The personal-equilibrium fixed point applies the framework of Kőszegi (2005) and is closely related to Shalev's (2000) loss-aversion equilibrium and to the psychological-game equilibrium of Geanakoplos, Pearce & Stacchetti (1989). The utility specification is most similar to Sugden (2003), differing in comparing each outcome to the whole support of the reference lottery rather than state-by-state; related axiomatic reference-dependent theories include [[@Gul1991|Gul (1991)]], Masatlioglu & Ok (2005), and Sagi (2005), with the consumption-utility link anticipated by Köbberling & Wakker (2005). The labor-supply application engages Camerer et al. (1997), Farber (2004, 2005), Oettinger (1999), and Fehr & Goette on target income. Downstream applications cited include Kőszegi & Rabin (2005) and Sydnor (2005) on risk, Stone (2005) on intertemporal choice, present-bias models of Laibson (1997) and O'Donoghue & Rabin (1999), Gul & Pesendorfer (2001) on temptation, and Heidhues & Kőszegi (2005, 2006) on firm pricing under consumer loss aversion. The companion working paper Kőszegi & Rabin (2004) develops the consumer and hedonic-dimension details.

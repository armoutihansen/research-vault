> [!abstract] Abstract
> The most recent wave of decision research goes beyond the usual critiques of linear probability weighting, exponential discounting, and other specialized assumptions, and challenges some of the most basic assumptions of the decision-making paradigm itself. In response to these challenges, decision researchers have proposed alternative accounts of behavior, some of which bear little resemblance to decision making as it is commonly conceived. I provide an overview of some of the challenges confronting the decision-making paradigm, and I present the broad outline of what I see as an emerging alternative perspective.

## Summary

This is a short, agenda-setting "Reflections and Reviews" essay in which Loewenstein argues that behavioral decision research is, in Schumpeter's sense, undergoing *creative destruction*: even as the consequentialist decision-making paradigm wins converts across the social sciences (behavioral economics, behavioral finance, behavioral law and economics, behavioral marketing), its own practitioners are abandoning its core assumption that behavior is the product of deliberative choice based on an evaluation of consequences. He enumerates six lines of evidence against the paradigm and sketches an emerging alternative built on two themes: *situational construal* (people first decide "what kind of situation am I in?") and *choice rules / heuristics* (they then apply a rule that fits that construal), a two-stage process that looks little like attribute-weighting decision making.

## Research question

Is the standard consequentialist decision-making paradigm — in which behavior results from deliberatively weighing the attributes (probabilities, time delays, outcome values) of alternative actions — an adequate descriptive account of human behavior? If not, what alternative perspective can synthesize the anomalies that behavioral decision researchers have accumulated?

## Method / identification

None in the formal sense — this is a conceptual review / position essay, not a theoretical or empirical paper. Its "method" is argument by accumulation: Loewenstein assembles six classes of evidence, each drawn from prior experimental literatures (much of it his own), to show that attribute-based consequentialist models cannot account for the data, and then articulates a programmatic alternative. The six problems with the decision-making perspective are: (1) **introspective unreliability** — people confabulate deliberative reasons for behavior that is actually automatic (split-brain confabulation; Nisbett and Wilson 1977; Wegner and Wheatley 1999); (2) **bounded rationality** — decision-tree computation overwhelms cognition (Simon 1955; Gabaix and Laibson 2000), and naturalistic experts use pattern matching, not analysis (Klein 1989); (3) **impact of context** — labeling effects (Wall Street vs. Community prisoner's dilemma; gamble-vs.-insurance framing; ultimatum-as-purchase) shift behavior by changing situational construal rather than attribute weights; (4) **intraindividual variability** — inconsistency across risk, intertemporal, and interpersonal domains contradicts stable attribute-weighting; (5) **problems evaluating consequences** — preference uncertainty / coherent arbitrariness (the "Tom Sawyer Study," Ariely, Prelec, and Loewenstein 2001) shows valuations are anchored yet internally coherent; (6) **perverse effects of deliberation** — instructing people to reason about reasons degrades choice quality (Wilson et al. 1993). The proposed alternative is a *two-stage* account: construe the situation, then apply context-appropriate choice rules.

## Data

None — no new dataset or experiment is reported. The paper draws on previously published and working-paper studies by the author and others (sequence preferences, diversification bias, coherent arbitrariness, blood-donation and day-care-fine field results).

## Key findings

There are no theorems or estimates; the "findings" are the marshalled anomalies and the synthesizing claims:
- **Preference for improving sequences** (Loewenstein and Prelec 1993): people defer a superior outcome when embedded in a sequence, an effect anticipation/savoring mechanisms failed to explain; it is better read as a *framing-triggered improvement heuristic* (Frederick and Loewenstein 2000), with elicitation format shifting people between improvement-seeking, flatness-seeking, and time-value-of-money discounting.
- **Diversification bias** (Read and Loewenstein 1995; Read et al. 2001): more variety-seeking under simultaneous than sequential choice; information-seeking, misprediction, and time-contraction explanations are all ruled out, leaving a *diversification heuristic* triggered by salient categorization.
- **Coherent arbitrariness / preference uncertainty** (Ariely, Prelec, and Loewenstein 2001): absolute valuations are arbitrary (anchored on a random number, even the sign of value), yet relative valuations are coherent (longer poetry exposure commands more money in the elicited direction).
- **Two-theme synthesis:** behavior is governed by (i) subjective *construal* of the situation and (ii) *choice rules/heuristics*; discontinuities in construal explain abrupt behavioral shifts (Greenspan's confidence "break," boom-to-crash markets, motivation crowding-out when money enters a social exchange — blood donation, day-care fines).

## Contribution

It crystallizes a paradigm-level critique of consequentialist behavioral decision research from inside the field and names a constructive alternative — *category/construal-based, rule-governed* behavior — connecting otherwise scattered programs (case-based decision theory, image theory, recognition-primed decisions, identity-based and analogy-based choice) under a common two-stage banner. The essay has functioned as an influential framing for subsequent work on construal, framing, motivation crowding, and dual-process accounts of choice.

## Limitations & open questions

Loewenstein is explicit that he offers no finished theory: the two themes "do not add up to a well-defined alternative perspective." Stated open problems and project hooks: (a) **modeling decision modes** — Weber, Blais, and Tada's multiple "modes of decision making" require formalizing each mode *and* predicting which mode activates in which situation, a task he calls "daunting"; (b) **the integration/formalization gap** — the "key step" is to integrate construal and choice-rule elements and "formalize them in a fashion that produces concrete, testable predictions," which the essay does not do; (c) **a repertoire-and-cues research program** — identify the repertoire of choice heuristics people use and the cues that trigger each, rather than seeking attribute-based explanations for anomalies; (d) **new sources of individual differences** — study who construes which activities as risky/pleasurable (e.g., "rose-colored glasses" individuals), shifting individual-difference research from attribute weights to construal; (e) a normative caution that construal-malleability of welfare must not be misused to justify inequitable resource distributions.

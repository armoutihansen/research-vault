## Summary
This Journal of Economic Perspectives review argues that, for many workers, work is not merely a painful exchange of effort for money but a central source of *meaning*. Cassar and Meier synthesize a fast-growing experimental and survey literature showing that nonmonetary job characteristics shape job satisfaction, productivity, and labor supply, and they organize meaning around the organization's mission plus three psychological needs from self-determination theory: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. They sketch a utility function that adds a meaning term to the standard income-minus-effort-cost model and trace its implications for incentive design, screening, and labor supply.

## Research question
How should economics account for the fact that workers care about the meaning of their work beyond pay? Specifically: what makes work meaningful, who values it (and why is valuation heterogeneous), and how does incorporating work meaning into the worker's utility function change predictions about optimal incentive schemes, employer investment in job design, screening/sorting of workers, and aggregate labor supply?

## Method / identification
This is a conceptual review, not an empirical study. Its analytical core is a proposed extension of the neoclassical work-motivation model. The standard model has utility from income $Y(w,e)$ (financial compensation $w$, effort $e$) net of effort cost $C(e)$, with the worker choosing $e$ to equate marginal income benefit with marginal effort cost. Cassar and Meier add a meaning production function, yielding
$$U = Y(w,e) + M(\theta, x, e) - C(e),$$
where $x$ is a four-dimensional vector of meaning dimensions (mission, autonomy, competence, relatedness) and $\theta$ is a four-dimensional vector of agent-specific weights capturing preference heterogeneity. Three modeling choices carry the analysis: (i) meaning is a *flow* that depends on the worker's own effort $e$, not a fixed stock or additive constant, so $\partial M/\partial e > 0$; (ii) the marginal effect of effort on meaning is nondecreasing in each job dimension (a meaningless job yields little meaning however hard one works); and (iii) $M$ may also depend on the wage $w$ (e.g., pay as a signal of competence or as a determinant of relatedness), opening the door to complementarity between pay and meaning. The labor-supply version substitutes "hours" for "effort" and "utility of leisure" for "cost of effort." The authors then reason comparatively-statically about firm investment and incentive design within this framework.

## Data
None — this is a review and theoretical-framework paper. It marshals evidence from many cited lab experiments, field experiments, and representative surveys (e.g., Ariely-Kamenica-Prelec Lego task, Grant's fundraising-caller field experiment, ISSP Work Orientations, Gallup engagement polls), but generates no new dataset or estimates of its own.

## Key findings
The paper's contributions are organizing claims rather than theorems. (1) Meaningful work raises effort, retention, and labor supply and lowers reservation wages — evidence spans destroyed-output tasks, pro-social framing, charitable-donation incentives, and corporate-social-responsibility messaging. (2) Both organizational mission and job design (autonomy, competence via skill use and symbolic recognition, relatedness and fair treatment) increase meaning. (3) Preferences for meaning are substantially heterogeneous (roughly one-third to two-thirds of subjects act pro-socially), correlated with age, income, ability, and other-regarding preferences, but the deep determinants remain unresolved. (4) From the utility model: a profit-maximizing employer optimally invests in meaning and offers a *flatter, lower piece-rate* than the no-meaning benchmark, so meaning tends to substitute for performance pay; firms starting from high pay and no mission gain most from adding meaning, while high-mission/low-pay sectors (nonprofits, teachers) gain more from adding pay. (5) Instrumental or insincere use of meaning can backfire (greenwashing; the authors' own M-Turk slogan study where pro-social incentives reduced output). (6) Whether meaning and pay are substitutes or complements is genuinely ambiguous — the model's wage-dependence of $M$ and evidence on HR-practice complementarities (e.g., Ichniowski et al.; Bartling-Fehr-Schmidt's "good jobs vs. bad jobs") cut against pure substitution. (7) For labor supply, adding meaning raises optimal hours and makes employment *less procyclical*; unemployment imposes a meaning loss beyond income loss, with policy implications for valuing job preservation.

## Contribution
The paper provides a unifying utility-theoretic scaffold that connects previously fragmented models of intrinsic motivation — Besley and Ghatak's mission preferences (the mission dimension), Benabou and Tirole's incentives-as-signals (the competence dimension), Akerlof and Kranton's identity and Englmaier and Leider's reciprocity (the relatedness dimension), and Delfgaauw and Dur's exogenous motivation (here endogenized through job design). It brings organizational-psychology constructs (self-determination theory) into a tractable economic object and lays out an agenda for incentive theory, personnel economics, and macro labor supply.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems that are strong project hooks. (a) Are the four meaning dimensions complements or substitutes in producing meaning? Armouti-Hansen, Cassar, and Dereky (2018) find mission and reciprocity complementary, but the general structure of $M(\cdot)$ is unknown. (b) Is competence more salient under autonomy or under teamwork? (c) The central pay-vs-meaning substitute/complement question is unresolved and mixed in the evidence. (d) The ultimate determinants of heterogeneity in $\theta$ (age, income, ability cohort effects) are not identified. (e) Are monetary incentives still effective in already-high-meaning environments? (f) Empirically, the causal channel from unemployment to lost well-being via lost meaning has not been cleanly demonstrated. (g) The framework assumes meaning derives from work rather than leisure — an assumption the authors explicitly question.

## Connections
The framework deliberately nests and links several theory traditions: [[@Besley2005|Besley and Ghatak (2005)]] on mission-motivated agents and matching; [[@Benabou2003|Benabou and Tirole]] (2003, 2006) on incentives as signals and crowding-out of intrinsic motivation; Akerlof and Kranton (2005, 2008) on identity in organizations; [[@Englmaier2012|Englmaier and Leider (2012)]] on reciprocal contracting; Delfgaauw and Dur (2007, 2008) and Cassar (2016) on motivated agents and screening. The empirical backbone draws on Ariely, Kamenica, and Prelec (2008), Grant (2008), Burbano (2016), Tonin and Vlassopoulos (2015), [[@DellaVigna2018|DellaVigna and Pope (2018)]], Imas (2014), and Charness, Cobo-Reyes, and Sanchez (2016) on pro-social and charitable incentives; Bartling, Fehr, and Schmidt (2012, 2013) and Falk and Kosfeld (2006) on autonomy; Kosfeld and Neckermann (2011) and Gallus (2016) on symbolic awards; and Deci and Ryan (1985, 2000) for self-determination theory. It is positioned as complementary to Lazear's companion JEP piece on monetary incentives, and relates to the crowding-out reviews of Gneezy, Meier, and Rey-Biel (2011) and Frey and Jegen (2001).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> We investigate how and why the productivity of a worker varies as a function of the productivity of her co-workers in a group production process. In theory, the introduction of a high productivity worker could lower the effort of incumbent workers because of free riding; or it could increase the effort of incumbent workers because of peer effects induced by social norms, social pressure, or learning. Using scanner level data, we measure high frequency, worker-level productivity of checkers for a large grocery chain. Because of the firm's scheduling policy, the timing of within-day changes in personnel is unsystematic, a feature for which we find consistent support in the data. We find strong evidence of positive productivity spillovers from the introduction of highly productive personnel into a shift. A 10% increase in average co-worker permanent productivity is associated with 1.7% increase in a worker's effort. Most of this peer effect arises from low productivity workers benefiting from the presence of high productivity workers. Therefore, the optimal mix of workers in a given shift is the one that maximizes skill diversity. In order to explain the mechanism that generates the peer effect, we examine whether effort depends on workers' ability to monitor one another due to their spatial arrangement, and whether effort is affected by the time workers have previously spent working together. We find that a given worker's effort is positively related to the presence and speed of workers who face him, but not the presence and speed of workers whom he faces (and do not face him). In addition, workers respond more to the presence of co-workers with whom they frequently overlap. These patterns indicate that these individuals are motivated by social pressure and mutual monitoring, and suggest that social preferences can play an important role in inducing effort, even when economic incentives are limited.

## Summary
Using high-frequency scanner data on supermarket cashiers, Mas and Moretti show that a worker's effort rises when more productive co-workers are present, despite a production environment that is structurally prone to free-riding. A 10% increase in co-workers' permanent productivity raises a worker's own productivity by about 1.7%. The gains accrue mostly to low-productivity workers, so productivity is maximized by mixing skill levels within shifts rather than segregating them. Exploiting register geography and schedule overlap, the authors argue the mechanism is social pressure and mutual monitoring (workers respond only to co-workers who can see them, and more to co-workers they overlap with frequently) rather than altruism or free-riding.

## Research question
How does a worker's productivity respond to an exogenous change in the productivity of her co-workers in a group production process where individual contributions are imperfectly observed? Specifically: (1) does introducing a high-productivity co-worker raise or lower incumbents' effort (peer effects vs. free-riding)? and (2) what is the underlying mechanism — social pressure / mutual monitoring, contagious enthusiasm, or learning?

## Method / identification
The conceptual framework adapts Kandel and Lazear (1992). Worker $i$'s output is $y_i=f(e_i)$ with $f'>0$, $f''<0$; management observes a noisy signal $z_i=y_i+u_i$ and average output $\bar{y}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_j y_j$, and sets the wage to expected productivity $W_i=E(y_i\mid z_i)=b[z_i-\bar{z}]+\bar{y}$, where $b=\frac{\mathrm{var}(\bar{y})}{\mathrm{var}(\bar{y})+\mathrm{var}(u)}$. Without social interactions, the worker maximizes $U(W_i(e_i))-C_i(e_i)$, and with quadratic cost $C_i(e_i)=k_ie_i^2$ the comparative static $\frac{de_i^*}{dk_j}>0$ — a fall in a co-worker's effort cost (more productive peer) leads $i$ to free-ride and reduce effort. Adding a "peer pressure" term $P(\cdot)$ increasing in the gap between $i$'s productivity and co-workers' average productivity, the prediction can flip to $\frac{de_i^*}{dk_j}<0$ when peer effects are strong enough. The empirics seek to distinguish these two cases.

Empirical strategy is a two-step procedure. First, permanent productivity $\theta_i$ is recovered as worker fixed effects from a cell-level log-productivity regression that controls for co-worker composition, register dummies, number of active workers, and day-of-week $\times$ hour $\times$ store and calendar-date $\times$ store fixed effects (so $\theta_i$ is purged of spillovers). Second, the baseline spillover model is a within-day first-difference regression:
$$\Delta y_{itcs}=\beta\,\Delta\bar{\theta}_{-itcs}+\psi\,\Delta X_{tcs}+\gamma_{tds}+e_{itcs}$$
where $\bar{\theta}_{-itcs}$ is the average permanent productivity of $i$'s active co-workers (leave-one-out, estimated omitting the focal period to avoid mechanical correlation) and $\gamma_{tds}$ are day $\times$ ten-minute-period $\times$ store dummies. Standard errors are clustered by checker, store, and calendar day. Using permanent (predetermined) rather than current co-worker productivity sidesteps Manski's reflection problem. Identification rests on the staggered, two-weeks-in-advance, management-uninvolved scheduling making the within-day timing of entry/exit of able workers orthogonal to transitory demand/productivity shocks. The authors run a battery of placebo/validity tests (e.g., entry of fast workers is not concentrated before demand spikes; future co-worker composition does not predict current productivity). Mechanism tests interact the spillover with register line-of-vision (who faces whom) and with the degree of schedule overlap between entrant and incumbent.

## Data
Scanner data from six stores of a national US supermarket chain (Western Census region), 2003–2006, covering 370 unionized, fixed-hourly-wage cashiers. For every transaction the data record items scanned and transaction length in seconds; individual productivity is items scanned per second over ten-minute intervals. Register locations and spatial orientation are known, enabling the line-of-vision (mutual monitoring) tests. Managers' transactions and "10 items or less" registers are excluded; analysis is restricted to 7 AM–8 PM.

## Key findings
- Positive spillovers dominate free-riding: a 10% increase in co-worker permanent productivity raises individual productivity ~1.7% (baseline $\beta\approx 0.16$–0.18), closely matching the lab estimate of [[@Falk2006a|Falk and Ichino (2006)]]. Aggregate "social multiplier" regressions yield $1+\beta\approx 1.14$–1.17.
- A preliminary structure-free F-test strongly rejects the absence of co-worker interdependence (average p-value 0.000).
- Effects are asymmetric: entry of an above-average worker raises co-worker productivity ~1.1%; exit lowers it ~0.5%.
- Mechanism is social pressure / mutual monitoring: productivity rises only for incumbents in the entrant's line of vision (and decays with distance); incumbents not facing the entrant show no positive (even slightly negative) effect. Spillovers are larger when entrant and incumbent have high schedule overlap. Effort thus appears self-interested (conditional on being observed and on repeated future interaction), not altruistic.
- Heterogeneity: the spillover is a monotone decreasing function of own skill — large for low-productivity workers, near zero for high-productivity workers, rarely negative. Hence the productivity-maximizing allocation maximizes within-shift skill diversity.
- Counterfactual: optimally mixing workers across shifts could produce the same sales with ~123,500–124,000 fewer hours/year (~$2.5M), though higher equilibrium wages from removing schedule freedom may offset the gain, so the profit effect is unclear.

## Contribution
One of the first studies to "get inside the black box" of workplace productivity spillovers — not merely documenting their existence but identifying the behavioral mechanism. It supplies clean, high-frequency, worker-level field evidence that social preferences (social pressure, mutual monitoring) can internalize free-riding externalities even in a low-skill, standardized, fixed-wage occupation, supporting Kandel and Lazear's (1992) theory. It also derives a managerially relevant implication — maximize skill diversity within shifts — and quantifies it.

## Limitations & open questions
- The first-difference design captures contemporaneous responses; if spillovers build up over longer horizons a levels model might detect effects this design misses.
- The linear-in-means specification is assumed; alternatives (e.g., group productivity driven by the least- or most-productive member, à la Lazear 2001) are only partially tested.
- $\bar{\theta}_{-i}$ is estimated, inducing potential attenuation and understated standard errors, though argued negligible given sample size (and checked via bootstrap).
- Whether the firm could actually raise profits by reallocating workers is unresolved: removing workers' valued shift-choice freedom would likely raise equilibrium wages and offset productivity gains.
- External validity beyond a single standardized occupation, and the precise weights of shame vs. external sanctions within "social pressure," remain open.

## Connections
The theoretical backbone is Kandel and Lazear (1992) on peer pressure in partnerships; the comparative-static analysis also relates to Huck, Kubler and Weibull (2002) and Falk, Fehr and Fischbacher (2001). The headline magnitude is benchmarked against the laboratory experiment of [[@Falk2006a|Falk and Ichino (2006)]], and the social-preference interpretation connects to Fehr and Gächter (2000) on costly punishment in public goods, Fehr, Gächter and [[@Fehr1997|Kirchsteiger (1997)]] on reciprocity and contract enforcement, and [[@Charness2002|Charness and Rabin (2002)]] on caring about peers' regard. The field-experimental work of Bandiera, Barankay and Rasul (2005) on relative incentives and social ties is the closest contemporaneous workplace evidence. Identification of social effects engages Manski (1993) on the reflection problem and Glaeser, Sacerdote and Scheinkman (2003) on social multipliers. Broader links run to the productivity-spillover and externality tradition (Marshall 1890; Lucas 1988; Moretti 2004) and to team-formation/performance studies (Hamilton, Nickerson and Owan 2004; Hayes, Oyer and Schaefer 2006), as well as Ichino and Maggi (2000) on social interactions in shirking.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Tests of gift exchange, wherein agents receive excess wages which are noncontingent on performance in one-shot settings, have yielded contradictory evidence: they sometimes find effort boosts, consistent with gift exchange, whereas they sometimes find no effort increases, consistent with a standard model. We identify eight confounds that could have led to the mixed evidence—agent disutility from being viewed as selfish, small samples, insufficient wage raises, an effort ceiling, fatigue, selection of abler workers, reemployment concerns, and peer effects—and run a comprehensive test addressing them. Our test consisted of a field experiment hiring workers for a data entry job, followed by laboratory games assessing their prosocial behavior. After addressing these confounds, we find that behavior during the field test was consistent with a standard model: workers did not repay fixed wage raises with an effort boost, but they did raise effort in response to a piece rate. The piece-rate scheme was also more efficient: the effort boost came at lower expense than paying fixed wage raises. Further, workers who behaved prosocially in laboratory games did not behave prosocially in the field. This paper was accepted by John List, behavioral economics.

## Summary
Esteves-Sorenson argues that the conflicting workplace gift-exchange literature—where noncontingent wage raises sometimes boost effort (supporting Akerlof's gift exchange) and sometimes do not (supporting a standard principal–agent model)—stems from eight identifiable confounds. She runs a single field experiment engineered to be free of all of them, paired with post-experiment laboratory games to measure prosociality. The result: fixed (noncontingent) wage raises of 50%, 67%, and 100% produced statistically insignificant effort changes (elasticities near zero), while a cheaper piece rate raised effort substantially. Workers who were prosocial in the lab did not reciprocate raises in the field. The evidence is most consistent with the standard model and casts doubt on gift exchange as a powerful real-world motivator.

## Research question
Why have workplace tests of gift exchange produced such conflicting pay–effort elasticities (ranging from a statistically insignificant $-7.6$ to $2.9$), and—once the design confounds responsible for that conflict are stripped away—do noncontingent ("gift") wage raises actually induce reciprocal effort increases?

## Method / identification
The core is a between-subjects field experiment. 194 students from two universities were hired at a market wage of $12/hour for a one-time data-entry job (digitizing an academic library), six hours split into three two-hour weekly shifts. Workers were unaware they were in a study. Random assignment to four conditions: Control (47, no raise); 67%Raise (70, raised to $20/hour before starting); 50%–100%Raise (45, raised to $18/hour before starting then a surprise raise to $24/hour before shift 3); and PieceRate (32, a per-record piece rate averaging ~21% extra pay). All fixed raises were explicitly framed as a gift/kind act by the principal.

The design is constructed to neutralize the eight confounds, each mapped to a feature: (1) a between-subjects design shrouds individual baseline effort so workers can be selfish undetected, removing the "moral cost of being perceived as selfish" channel that Levitt & List (2007) argue contaminates lab and within-subject designs; (2) a large sample addresses both low power and the chance inflation of elasticities in small samples; (3) substantial raises (50–100%) rule out "raise too small"; (4) the piece rate diagnoses an effort ceiling (if effort can rise under piece rate, no ceiling); (5) weekly two-hour shifts limit fatigue and give time to reflect on productivity; (6) hiring at market rate avoids selecting abler workers (Weiss 1980); (7) a one-time job removes reemployment incentives (Shapiro & Stiglitz 1984); (8) solitary work with no peer information removes peer effects.

Identification of effort responses is by comparing treatment-group mean output (records entered) against Control. The paper reports pay–effort elasticities and uses multiple-hypothesis-testing (MHT) corrections as a robustness check. A second stage invited the same workers to play sequential prisoner's dilemma (SPD) games to classify them as Altruists (CCC), Conditional cooperators (CCD), or Selfish (DDD), then tested whether lab-prosocial workers in the wage-raise conditions exerted more field effort than Control.

## Data
Original field-experimental data: per-worker, per-shift output (records digitized) for 194 workers across four conditions, plus SPD game choices for the 138 (71%) who returned for the lab session and a post-experiment survey. Data and online appendix archived at the DOI.

## Key findings
- Fixed (noncontingent) wage raises of 50%, 67%, and a further raise to 100% yielded statistically insignificant effort changes of $-4\%$ to $4\%$ (elasticities of $-0.08$ to $0.06$), even in the specification most favorable to gift exchange—behavior consistent with the standard model.
- The PieceRate scheme, peaking at only ~30% extra pay by shift 3, boosted effort by a conservative 18% (elasticity ~$0.60$), and did so at lower total expense than the fixed raises—so it was strictly more efficient at eliciting effort.
- No effort ceiling: since the piece rate lifted effort, the flat response to fixed raises is not because the task capped output.
- Lab prosociality did not transfer: ~42% of wage-raise workers behaved prosocially (CCC/CCD) in the SPD games, yet this subset showed similar statistically insignificant effort changes ($-3\%$ to $7\%$) in the field, where their actions were unobservable.
- Even first-shift (very short-run) elasticities were smaller than the short-lived positive elasticities of Gneezy & List (2006), consistent with the view that large positive elasticities arise partly from small samples and observability.

## Contribution
First study to jointly identify and address all eight confounds in a single paired field-and-lab test, whereas prior work tackled them piecemeal (e.g., effort ceiling in [[@Kube2013|Kube et al. 2013]]; small samples in Cohn et al. 2015). Two design novelties: introducing a within-experiment piece-rate benchmark (both to diagnose an effort ceiling and to compare cost-efficiency of incentive schemes) and splitting the task across three weeks (to defuse fatigue and allow productivity reflection). Substantively, it reframes the gift-exchange debate: the headline positive elasticities are likely artifacts of observability and small samples, and gift exchange is a weaker motivator than the influential lab tradition ([[@Fehr1993|Fehr et al. 1993]]) suggested.

## Limitations & open questions
- Generalizability of the worker–job pairing: the result is for a short-duration, solitary, low-skill data-entry job with students; the author notes gift exchange may still operate in other contexts (longer relationships, richer social settings).
- The between-subjects design buys clean identification at the cost of statistical power relative to within-subject designs; null results require careful power interpretation.
- The lack of lab-to-field transfer of prosociality raises an open question about the external validity of lab social-preference measures—why prosocial types do not reciprocate when actions are unobservable.
- Whether the standard-model conclusion would survive in settings where the employment relationship is repeated or where peer observation is present remains untested here.
- The piece rate's superiority is shown for this task; the relative efficiency of incentive schemes in jobs where output is hard to measure is left open.

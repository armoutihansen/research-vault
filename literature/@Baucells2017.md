> [!abstract] Abstract
> We propose the anticipation-event-recall (AER) model. Set in a continuous time frame, the AER model formally links the three components of total utility (i.e., utility from anticipation, event utility, and utility from recall). The AER model predicts the temporal profiles of instant utility experienced before, during, and after a given event. Total utility is calculated as the integral of instant utility. The model builds on the psychological elements of conceptual consumption, adaptation, and time distance. By virtue of its rich formulation, the AER model produces a wide set of insights and testable predictions, including the U shape of instant utility during anticipation and the optimal duration of anticipation for a given event. Using both real and hypothetical events, we provide empirical evidence in support of the main implications of the AER model.

## Summary
Baucells and Bellezza propose the anticipation-event-recall (AER) model, a continuous-time formalization of Kahneman et al.'s (1997) idea that total utility is the integral of instant (moment-by-moment) utility. The model joins three previously separate components—utility from anticipating an event, utility during the event, and utility from recalling it afterward—into a single parametric process built on conceptual consumption, adaptation of a reference point, and time-distance discounting with magnitude effects. From a handful of inputs it generates rich temporal profiles and nine testable predictions, headlined by a U-shaped profile of anticipatory utility and an interior optimal duration of anticipation, both of which they test experimentally.

## Research question
What does the moment-by-moment profile of instant utility look like before, during, and after an event, and how do anticipation, occurrence, and recall jointly determine total utility? In particular: what is the shape of anticipatory utility over time, and is there an optimal amount of time to spend anticipating a given event?

## Method / identification
This is primarily a theory paper with supporting experiments. The model is set in continuous calendar time $t$, with four landmarks: anticipation begins at $t_0$, the event runs over $[t_b,t_e)$, and recall runs over $[t_e,t_1)$. Durations are $\Delta_a=t_b-t_0$, $\Delta_e=t_e-t_b$, $\Delta_r=t_1-t_e$ (with $t_1=\Delta_r=\infty$ by default). A consumption rate $c_t$ (exogenous during the event, a conceptual-consumption choice variable during anticipation and recall) drives a reference point $r_t$ that adapts via $dr_t/dt=\alpha(c_t-r_t)$. The carrier of utility is effective consumption $c_t-r_t$ passed through a (loss-averse) value function $v$. Instant utility is
$$u(t)=v(c_t-r_t)\,f(\tau_t),\qquad U=\int_{t_0}^{t_1}u(t)\,dt=U_A+U_E+U_R,$$
where $f(\tau)=e^{-\pi(\tau)}$ discounts by psychological distance $\tau_t$ to/from the event. Five assumptions pin down the parametric form: A1 constant consumption $c_t=c$ (so $C=\{c\}$); A2 reference-point adaptation with speed $\alpha$; A3 piecewise-linear loss-averse value $v(c)=c\,\mathbf 1_{\{c\ge0\}}+\lambda c\,\mathbf 1_{\{c<0\}}$, $\lambda\ge1$; A4 discount rates carrying a magnitude effect, $\rho_a=\rho_0/|v(c)|^\mu$ and $\rho_r=\rho_a e^{\alpha\mu\Delta_a}$, $\mu\ge0$; A5 a concave (substationary) distance function, either power $\pi(\tau)=\tau^\delta$ or quasi-linear $\pi(\tau)=(1-\delta)+\delta\tau$, $\delta\in(0,1]$. The five internal parameters $(\lambda,\alpha,\rho_0,\mu,\delta)$ and three external decisions $(c,\Delta_a,\Delta_e)$ can each be switched on or off ($\alpha=0$ kills adaptation, $\lambda=1$ kills loss aversion, $\delta=1$ gives exponential discounting, $\mu=0$ kills the magnitude effect). Empirically, the U-shape is tested with an ordered-probit regression of self-reported excitement on relative anticipation position $\theta=(t-t_0)/(t_b-t_0)$ and $\theta^2$, the curvature coefficient on $\theta^2$ identifying the U.

## Data
Three sets of survey experiments. Study 1A: 282 Amazon Mechanical Turk respondents pick among five excitement-profile graphs for an imagined concert. Study 1B: 373 registrants for upcoming Tough Mudder obstacle races report current excitement (1–7), yielding cross-sectional variation in $\theta$. Study 2: respondents compare memorability of kissing a movie star once vs. seven times. Study 3 (Table A.1): 155 respondents state how far in advance they would ideally like to learn about events of varying magnitude (best friend's wedding down to a weekend vacation). Stimuli mix real and hypothetical events.

## Key findings
Proposition 1: instant (dis)utility during anticipation is unimodal—decreasing then increasing—producing the characteristic U shape, because shrinking time distance and ongoing adaptation pull in opposite directions; under power sensitivity the trough is at $t_m=\max\{t_0,\,t_b-(1/\rho_a)(\delta\rho_a/\alpha)^{1/(1-\delta)}\}$. Proposition 2 gives the closed-form total utility. Proposition 3: the elasticity of $|U|_A$, $|U|_E$, $|U|_R$ w.r.t. magnitude $|c|$ is $1+\mu\psi$, $1$, and $1+\mu$—so small events are mostly event utility (ice cream), medium events are anticipation-dominated (weekend outing), and large events are recall-dominated (honeymoon). Proposition 4: longer event duration $\Delta_e$ induces more adaptation and lowers memorability, so a single occurrence can dominate repetition (matched by Study 2). Proposition 5: $U_A$ is unimodal in $\Delta_a$ with peak $\Delta_A$, but total $U$ is maximized at $\Delta^*\in[0,\Delta_A)$—the optimal anticipation is strictly shorter than the anticipation-maximizing one, and may be zero (a surprise is optimal). Proposition 6 gives $\Delta_A$ in closed form under quasi-linear sensitivity. Propositions 7–8 treat negative events: total disutility tends to zero as $\Delta_a\to\infty$, so maximal anticipation minimizes pain, motivating a threefold coping strategy. Empirically, 61% of subjects pick the U-shape (Study 1A), the ordered-probit curvature coefficient $b_{\theta^2}=2.03$ is positive and significant ($p=0.026$, Study 1B), and ideal anticipation lead time rises monotonically with event magnitude (Study 3).

## Contribution
The paper supplies the first comprehensive process model unifying anticipation, event, and recall utility in continuous time, going beyond prior anticipation models ([[@Loewenstein1987|Loewenstein 1987]]; [[@Caplin2001|Caplin and Leahy 2001]]; [[@Koszegi2009|Koszegi and Rabin 2009)]] by letting anticipation feed back onto event and recall utility through adaptation, and by embedding magnitude effects that reproduce the peak-end rule. It delivers an interior optimal duration of anticipation, prescriptions for "engineering" one's own happiness, and managerial implications for sequencing surprise and recovery in services.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors restrict attention to certainty and single, flat (constant-$c$) events; extending to uncertain or multi-level events makes conceptual consumption a nontrivial choice over a range $C=[0,\bar c]$, where preliminary numerics suggest front-loading conceptual consumption then tapering it to preserve a final surprise. A multi-event extension would need to specify how attention switches between competing events (divided attention is unmodeled). Negative anticipation ("defensive pessimism," setting $r_t<0$ to leave room for pleasant outcomes) is only sketched. The reference point is held constant during recall purely for tractability. They note their assumptions sit on a Pareto frontier of realism, tractability, and fruitfulness that future work could re-explore. The experiments measure predicted/intuited rather than necessarily accurate utility.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> This article examines the behavioral implications and empirical testability of the game-theoretic models of bargaining that follow in the tradition begun by Nash. The classical game-theoretic models are reviewed with particular attention to the assumptions they make concerning the kinds of information shared by the bargainers. The experimental literature concerned with testing these models is also reviewed, with particular attention to the extent to which these experiments have conformed to the assumptions of the models they are designed to test. Some of the principal differences between the theoretical predictions and the results reported in these experiments seem to be due to questions concerning the information shared by the bargainers. A new game-theoretic model is presented, based on assumptions about information that more closely correspond to the conditions under which many of these experiments have been conducted. An experiment manipulating the appropriate kind of information supported the hypothesis that Nash's bargaining model has predictive value in situations that conform to its assumptions about information, but the results also suggest that only a relatively narrow range of situations may conform completely to these assumptions.

## Summary
Roth and Malouf confront a foundational mismatch between cooperative bargaining theory and the experiments designed to test it. Nash's (1950) axiomatic solution is defined on the space of expected-utility payoffs, where utilities are unique only up to independent positive affine transformations (interval scales). Most experiments, however, give bargainers information about each other's *monetary* payoffs, which live on a common absolute scale and therefore permit interpersonal comparisons the theory forbids. The authors argue this informational difference, not a failure of the bargaining process per se, explains many observed deviations from Nash's predictions (notably the recurrent drift toward equal monetary splits). They dissect Nash's four axioms, isolate which do the work, introduce a new "equal gains" solution $E$ built on an interpersonal-comparison axiom, and run a lottery-ticket experiment that experimentally manipulates the kind of information shared.

## Research question
Does Nash's solution describe bargaining behavior when bargainers share exactly the information the model assumes (only von Neumann–Morgenstern utility information), and does it fail when they additionally share interpersonally comparable monetary-payoff information? More broadly: which of Nash's axioms is responsible for predictions, and what role does the *quality* of shared information play in determining bargaining outcomes?

## Method / identification
This is primarily a theory paper with a confirmatory experiment. A pure bargaining game is a pair $(S,d)$ where $S$ is the feasible set of expected-utility payoff vectors and $d$ the disagreement point, with $S$ compact, convex, containing some $x \succ d$. A solution $f:B\to R^2$ assigns $f(S,d)\in S$. The authors review Nash's four axioms: (1) Pareto optimality, (2) symmetry, (3) independence of irrelevant alternatives, (4) independence of equivalent utility representations (invariance to positive affine transforms of each player's utility). Nash (1950) proved his unique solution maximizes the product of gains $(z_1-d_1)(z_2-d_2)$.

The key analytic move re-weights the axioms. Drawing on Roth (1977b, 1978), they show Nash's solution is characterized by Properties 2–5 alone (Theorem 2, where Property 5 is strong individual rationality $f(S,d)\succ d$), and that dropping symmetry yields the asymmetric family maximizing $(z_1-d_1)^p(z_2-d_2)^q$ with $p+q=2$ (Theorem 3). Hence Nash's result rests "hardly at all" on Pareto optimality or symmetry and "almost entirely" on the two independence properties (3 and 4). Property 4 is the informational crux: it forbids any use of interpersonal payoff comparisons.

The new model replaces Property 4 with **Property 4' (independence of ordinal transformations preserving interpersonal comparisons)** on the broader class $B^*$ of compact (not necessarily convex) games with connected Pareto frontier. Property 4' (a) treats each player's payoffs ordinally and (b) *permits* ordinal comparisons across players. The unique individually rational solution satisfying Properties 1, 2, 3, 4' is the **equal gains solution** $E$, which selects the Pareto-optimal $z$ maximizing $\min\{z_1-d_1, z_2-d_2\}$ — i.e., equal gains whenever a Pareto-optimal equal-gains point exists (Theorem 4; proof in the Appendix via a fixed-point argument on a convex-hull transformation).

The experiment uses the PLATO computerized terminal system. Subjects bargain over how to split 100 "lottery tickets" determining each player's probability of winning a personal monetary prize (winner-take-all between the player's own prize and zero). This design ensures the feasible set in *utility* space is identical across information conditions, since utility for a lottery is just the win probability. Four games vary monetary prizes and feasibility constraints (Table 1); the manipulated variable is **information**: full (each told both prizes) vs. partial (each told only own prize). The outcome statistic is $D$ = Player 2's percentage of tickets minus Player 1's. Nash's solution predicts $D=0$ in all games; the equal gains solution $E$ predicts $D=0$ in Games 1–2 but $D=-50$ in Games 3–4.

## Data
Original experimental data: 76 bargaining games played by college sophomores over the PLATO system, 11 pairs in the full-information condition and 8 pairs in the partial-information condition, each pair playing all four games against anonymous, randomly re-paired opponents communicating by binding proposals and non-binding messages. 72 of 76 games (95%) reached agreement; 71 of 72 (99%) were Pareto optimal. Results reported as means/SD of $D$ (Table 2) and disaggregated ticket splits (Table 3).

## Key findings
- **Theorem 2/3**: Nash's solution depends essentially on the two independence axioms, not on symmetry or Pareto optimality, redirecting attention to the informational content of Property 4.
- **Theorem 4**: The equal gains solution $E$ (maximize the minimum gain) is the unique individually rational solution on $B^*$ satisfying Properties 1, 2, 3, 4'. This is, in effect, a formal derivation of an equity/equal-gains principle from an interpersonal-comparison axiom.
- **Experiment**: In the **partial information** condition, $D$ did not differ significantly from zero in any game (consistent with Nash's solution, and with Property 3 holding throughout). In the **full information** condition, Games 3 and 4 (where prizes were unequal so equal tickets means unequal money) produced agreements significantly shifted toward equal monetary gains: Games 3 and 4 differed significantly from Games 1 and 2, and from their partial-information counterparts ($t(10.28)=5.88, p<.001$ for Game 3; $t(10.91)=2.76, p<.02$ for Game 4). Of the 22 full-information Games 3–4 outcomes, 19 reached agreement and 17 (87%) fell *between* the Nash and equal-gains predictions.
- The variance of outcomes was higher under full than partial information, indicating that interpersonally comparable information widens the range of "salient" focal outcomes bargainers can invoke.

## Contribution
The paper reframes the bargaining-theory-vs-evidence debate as a question about *which kind of information bargainers share*, gives the first clean experimental confirmation that Nash's solution is descriptive precisely when its informational assumptions (Property 4) hold, and provides a falsification when they do not. It introduces the equal gains solution and the interpersonal-comparison axiom (Property 4'), supplying an axiomatic foundation for equity-based focal points. Methodologically, the lottery-ticket design is a durable template for controlling utility while manipulating monetary-payoff information — a cornerstone of subsequent experimental bargaining and the broader Nash program (Roth's later work).

## Limitations & open questions
The authors stress that only a "relatively narrow range" of real situations conform completely to Nash's pure information assumptions; most bargaining likely sits between the two informational regimes, producing outcomes between Nash and equal gains. Open problems they name: (1) how *different* kinds of information (beyond the full/partial dichotomy) are incorporated into the bargaining process and how their distribution between bargainers shapes outcomes; (2) more sophisticated models handling several information types simultaneously; (3) the mechanism by which "salience"/focal points emerge as a function of shared information, linking to social-psychology research on salience and mutual expectations; (4) extending the equal-gains and interpersonal-comparison machinery to $n>2$ players (Nash generalizes; Raiffa's $G$ does not). The single-commodity, money-prize setting and student-subject pool bound external validity.

## Connections
Foundational antecedents are von Neumann & Morgenstern (1944) on expected utility and Nash (1950, 1953) on the bargaining solution and variable-threat games. The axiomatic re-weighting builds on Roth (1977a, 1977b, 1978) and Roth's monograph on axiomatic bargaining; alternative solutions are Raiffa (1953), Kalai & Smorodinsky (1975) and Kalai (1977) on monotonic/proportional solutions, and Harsanyi & Selten (1972) and Myerson (1977) on asymmetric/incomplete-information generalizations. The experimental review engages Nydegger & Owen (1975) and Nydegger (1977, 1979), Rapoport, Frenkel & Perner (1977), Stone (1958), Crott (1971), Heckathorn (1978), Hoggatt et al. (1978), Komorita & Kravitz (1979), and the classic Fouraker & Siegel (1963) bilateral-monopoly experiments; survey context comes from Rubin & Brown (1975), Chertkoff & Esser (1976), Morley & Stephenson (1977), and Murnighan (1978). The salience/focal-point thread connects naturally to Schelling-style coordination and to later work on equity and social preferences in distribution.

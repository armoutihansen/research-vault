> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper is an attempt to broaden economic discourse by importing insights into human behavior not just from psychology, but also from sociology and anthropology. Whereas in standard economics the concept of the decision-maker is the rational actor, and in early work in behavioral economics it is the quasi-rational actor influenced by the context of the moment of decision, in some recent work in behavioral economics, the decision-maker could be called the enculturated actor. This actor's preferences, perception, and cognition are subject to two deep social influences: (a) the social contexts to which he has become exposed and, especially, accustomed; and (b) the cultural mental models—including categories, identities, narratives, and worldviews—that he uses to process information. The paper traces how these factors shape behavior through the endogenous determination of preferences and the lenses through which individuals see the world—their perception and interpretation of situations. The paper offers a tentative taxonomy of the social determinants of behavior and describes the results of controlled and natural experiments that only a broader view of these determinants can plausibly explain. The perspective suggests more realistic models of human behavior for explaining outcomes and designing policies.

## Summary
Hoff and Stiglitz argue that economics should add a third conception of the decision-maker — the *enculturated actor* — to the standard *rational actor* and the behavioral *quasi-rational actor*. The enculturated actor's preferences, perception, and cognition are endogenous to two durable social forces: (a) the social contexts he has been exposed to and habituated within, and (b) the cultural *mental models* (categories, social identities, narratives, worldviews) he uses to process information. Whereas standard economics admits only social determinants of *choice sets* (prices, rules of the game), and first-strand behavioral economics adds social influences *in the moment of decision* (priming, framing), this paper develops a taxonomy of *durable* social determinants and shows they generate endogenous preferences, socially-determined cognition, multiple social equilibria, and societal rigidities. The argument is built from a chain of simple formal models plus a wide catalogue of laboratory, field, and natural experiments. I read the full paper (33pp) including all eight sections and the three formal appendices.

## Research question
How do social context and culture shape not just *what* people can choose (their choice sets) but *who they are* — their preferences, perception, and cognition — and what are the implications for economic modeling, equilibrium multiplicity, societal rigidity, and policy? The paper seeks a taxonomy and a set of formal/empirical building blocks for "the social determination of behavior" that goes beyond Arrow's social determination of choice sets.

## Method / identification
This is a synthesis/manifesto paper, not a single empirical study. Its contribution is a conceptual taxonomy backed by a sequence of nested formal models and a curated body of causal evidence.

The organizing scheme is a two-by-two taxonomy (Table 2) crossing *non-social vs. social* influences with *momentary (strand one) vs. durable (strand two)* effects, and a three-column comparison (Table 1) of standard economics, behavioral economics with quasi-rational actors, and behavioral economics with enculturated actors.

The formal core is a ladder of equilibrium models:
- **Interdependent utility (Section 2).** The standard extension is $\max_{x_i} U^i(x_i, x_{-i})$ with non-separability; separability $U^i = u(x_i) + \Phi(x_{-i})$ is the only case where choices are independent of others'. Special cases: *relative consumption* (Veblen/Arrow–Dasgupta — equilibrium leisure can be invariant to the wage) and *coordination utility* (multiple equilibria in vacation length).
- **Recursive/learning-by-consuming preferences (Eq. 2.3):** $\max_{x_t^i} U_t^i(x_t^i,x_t^{-i}; x_{t-1}^i,x_{t-1}^{-i};\dots) + \max_{x_{t-1}^i} \Psi(x_{t-1}^i,x_{t-1}^{-i};\dots)$, in which today's marginal utility depends on past own and others' consumption; separability is the only case where rankings are time-invariant.
- **Endogenous preferences (Section 4):** adult action $a_t = H(p_t, a_{t-1})$, with social equilibrium $a^* = H(p^*, a^*)$ and market clearing $Z(p,a)=0$ (Eqs. 4.1–4.3). A refinement makes susceptibility to priming endogenous: $P(a_t; S, p_t) = H(p_t, a_{t-1})$ (Eq. 4.4), so past experience sets the probability that a given prime elicits a given identity.
- **Socially-determined perception (Section 5):** introduce perception $P$, so $a_t = H(p_t; P(a_{t-1}, S))$ with equilibrium $a^* = H(p^*, P(a^*, S))$ (Eqs. 5.1–5.3). Agents respond to *perceptions* of others' actions, not the objective actions, enormously enlarging the equilibrium set and the policy levers.
- **Role-model model of girls' education (Eqs. 5.4–5.7):** parents weight two mental models $A$ (autonomy esteemed) and $P$ (patriarchy) by salience $\omega(s)$ where $s$ is the community fraction educating daughters: $U = \omega(s)V_A + [1-\omega(s)]V_P + W$. Marginal type $\hat{\theta} = \omega(s^*)V_A + [1-\omega(s^*)]V_P + W$ with $s^* = F(\hat{\theta})$ yields multiple stable equilibria; a small rise in female wages $W$ can tip the economy from near-zero to near-universal education ("frame switch" by many interdependent actors).
- **Dynamics with shocks (Eq. 6.1):** $a_t = H(p_t; P(a_{t-1}, S, \varepsilon_t)) + u_t$; small shock variance implies rigidity, large shocks move the system between basins of attraction.

Three formal appendices supply named mechanisms: **Appendix A** (a variant of Hoff–Stiglitz 2004) constructs multiple trust equilibria — a critical type $\hat{\theta}(x^e)$ with $1 - F(\hat{\theta}) = x^*$ (Eq. A.2), and an extension where the type distribution itself depends on the social environment (Eq. A.3) so low trust *re-shapes* preferences toward untrustworthiness; **Appendix B** formalizes belief interdependence $p_i = f^i(p_{-i}, I^i)$ and derives a *social multiplier* $dp^1/dI^1 = [\partial f/\partial I^1]/(1 - \tfrac{\partial f}{\partial p^2}\tfrac{dg}{dp^1})$ (Eq. B.6); **Appendix C** builds an *equilibrium fiction of racial hierarchy* from confirmatory bias plus confidence-enhanced performance (Compte–Postlewaite), where an inferior-race label prevents one group from "forgetting" failures, generating a self-confirming performance gap between otherwise identical types.

## Data
None of its own; the paper is a synthesis. Its evidentiary base is a wide set of others' lab, field, and natural experiments, used as identification proofs-of-concept: money priming (Vohs et al. 2006); lockable savings boxes in Kenya (Dupas–Robinson 2013); banker-identity priming and dishonesty (Cohn et al. 2014); the day-care fine ([[@Gneezy2000|Gneezy–Rustichini 2000]]); dictator-game in-group generosity in Ugandan cooperatives (Baldassarri–Grossman 2013); microfinance meeting frequency and default (Feigenberg et al. 2010); Yale Law ideology exposure and distributional preferences (Fisman et al. 2009); the caste stag-hunt and culture-of-honor retaliation (Brooks–Hoff–Pandey 2015); caste-priming and maze performance (Hoff–Pandey 2006, 2014); randomized political reservations for women in India and IAT/aspirations effects (Beaman et al. 2009, 2012); Bihar self-help groups (Sanyal et al. 2015); soap operas and fertility in Brazil (La Ferrara et al. 2012) and India (Jensen–Oster 2009); call-center recruiting and girls' outcomes (Jensen 2012); the Hajj lottery (Clingingsmith et al. 2009); rice-vs-wheat agriculture and holistic/analytic cognition with an ecological IV (Talhelm et al. 2014); Russian-blues color discrimination (Winawer et al. 2007); motivated numeracy on gun control (Kahan et al. 2013); and cultural-neuroscience evidence on the mPFC, eye movements, and London-taxi hippocampi.

## Key findings
- **A taxonomy with a new agent type.** The *enculturated actor* (Tables 1–2) has endogenous preferences, perception, and cognition; durable social influence is the dividing line from earlier behavioral economics, which treated biases as universal and hard-wired.
- **Endogeneity of preferences and of priming-susceptibility.** Who an individual "becomes" — and which latent identity a given prime elicits — is set by accumulated social experience (Eqs. 4.1–4.4); the banker, Yale-Law, and day-care results instantiate this.
- **Perception, not objective action, drives behavior** (Eqs. 5.1–5.3); the caste stag-hunt shows high-caste men perceive coordination failure as an honor insult and retaliate, blocking the efficient social contract that low-caste pairs reach.
- **Multiple equilibria and frame switches.** The role-model education model (Eqs. 5.4–5.7) and Jensen's call-center experiment show small earnings shocks can produce large, near-discontinuous behavioral shifts via simultaneous mental-model switching.
- **Trust shapes fundamentals.** Appendix A's multiple trust equilibria differ from social-capital/repeated-game accounts: low trust does not merely change payoffs, it primes a durable untrustworthy identity (hysteresis), so history matters and shocks can be path-dependent.
- **Social multiplier on beliefs** (Appendix B): one informed agent's belief change is amplified through a network because others (mistakenly) read others' belief changes as informative.
- **Equilibrium fictions** (Appendix C): purely artificial social categories (race, caste) become self-fulfilling through confirmatory bias and confidence effects, sustaining performance gaps and discriminatory rigidities absent any innate difference.
- **Cognition itself is cultural and neurally plastic:** language affects color perception; rice/wheat ecology predicts holistic vs. analytic thinking and individualism (validated with an ecological instrument); culturally patterned tasks reshape the brain.

## Contribution
The paper integrates economics, psychology (including cultural psychology), sociology (DiMaggio's culture-and-cognition), and anthropology into a single positive framework, extending Arrow's "social determinants of choice sets" to the social determinants of the *decision-making process itself*. It supplies a portable taxonomy plus a graded set of equilibrium models that nest standard interdependent-utility models as special cases (separability/temporal separability) yet deliver multiple equilibria, societal rigidity, hysteresis, and novel policy instruments (changing salient mental models via role models, media, reservations, or legal prohibition that re-coordinates an equilibrium at low cost). It draws heavily on the World Development Report 2015 (Mind, Society, and Behavior), which Hoff co-directed, and reframes that agenda for economic theory.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit gaps and frontier problems:
- **The set of possible mental models is infinite and not well-defined** (fn. 4), so the framework lacks a closed state space.
- **Identification of "perception" models is hard** because perceptions are usually unobserved; the authors note we typically observe only actions and must rely on emerging IAT/neuroscience proxies (Section 5.2).
- **Untested mechanisms:** whether being a banker durably changes deep preferences (vs. constant priming) is *not* tested by Cohn et al.; they propose an experiment with random hiring and post-tenure behavioral measurement. The hunger/food-prime cross-cultural hypothesis is also untested.
- **Normative indeterminacy:** when policy changes the utility function, *which* utility function should welfare analysis use — ex ante or ex post? Standard welfare economics breaks down with endogenous preferences; "what do we mean by a good society?" is left open.
- **How societies move between equilibria** (e.g., the rise in Japanese worker–management trust post-WWII) is named as "one of the important areas of ongoing research."
- **Disturbing implication of social-capital models:** essentially any Pareto-efficient equilibrium — including exploitative ones like Jim Crow — can be supported, raising the question of which equilibria interventions should target.
- The field is "still young" and the alternative-interpretation problem (a "die-hard devotee of fixed preferences" can recast results as Bayesian belief updating) is acknowledged as not fully closed for every experiment.

## Connections
This is a flagship statement of the *endogenous-preferences / cultural mental models* program, building on Hoff and Stiglitz's own work on equilibrium fictions (Hoff–Stiglitz 2010), post-communist rule of law and trust (Hoff–Stiglitz 2004), and discrimination as durable inequality (Hoff–Pandey 2006, 2014; Hoff–Kshetramade–Fehr 2011). Theoretically it sits against the standard interdependent-utility and social-preferences tradition (Fehr–Tyran; Bowles–Polania-Reyes 2012, whose alternative taxonomy is cited) and against repeated-game social-capital accounts (Ostrom 1990; Abreu 1988; Becker 1957 on discrimination, explicitly contrasted). It imports DiMaggio (1997), Markus–Kitayama (2010), Bruner, Swidler, and Berger–Luckmann from cultural psychology and sociology, the Whorf hypothesis from linguistics, and Skyrms's stag-hunt framing of the social contract. The dictator-game and stag-hunt material, and the endogenous social-preferences theme, connect directly to work on predicting and comparing social preferences. Empirically it is anchored in the development-economics natural-experiment literature (Beaman et al.; Jensen; La Ferrara et al.; Clingingsmith et al.) and the cultural-neuroscience literature (Kitayama–Park; Nisbett–Masuda).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Employment contracts are often incomplete, leaving many responsibilities subject to workers' discretion. High work morale is therefore essential for sustaining voluntary cooperation and high productivity in firms. We conducted a field experiment to test whether workers reciprocate wage cuts and raises with low or high work productivity. Wage cuts had a detrimental and persistent impact on productivity, reducing average output by more than 20%. An equivalent wage increase, however, did not result in any productivity gains. The results from an additional control experiment with high monetary performance incentives demonstrate that workers could still produce substantially more output, leaving enough room for positive reactions. Altogether, these results provide evidence consistent with a model of reciprocity, as opposed to inequality aversion.

## Summary
A natural field experiment in which a German university library hired workers for a one-shot, six-hour book-cataloguing job after announcing a projected wage of €15/hour. Immediately before work began, workers were told their actual wage: €15 (Baseline), €20 (PayRaise), or €10 (PayCut). Wage cuts reduced output by more than 20% — a large effect, stable across the entire working period and across the performance distribution — whereas an equivalent pay raise produced no significant productivity gain. A separate piece-rate control shows workers had ample physical headroom to produce more, so the null pay-raise effect is not a ceiling artifact. The asymmetry is argued to favor action-based reciprocity models over outcome-based inequality aversion.

## Research question
Do wage cuts damage work morale, i.e. do workers reciprocate unkind (wage-cut) and kind (wage-raise) deviations from a reference wage with lower or higher effort, in a real labor market freed of confounds (reputation, repeated-game punishment, efficiency-wage discipline, self-selection)? Secondarily: which social-preference model — inequality aversion or reciprocity — best rationalizes the resulting effort pattern?

## Method / identification
A controlled natural field experiment (Card–DellaVigna–Malmendier "competing models" category). The projected €15 wage announced during recruitment serves as an *exogenously set reference point*. The one-shot design (explicitly no possibility of re-employment, workers separated in individual rooms, unaware they are in an experiment, same research assistant for all) removes repeated-game punishment, efficiency-wage / firing-discipline channels, experimenter-demand and selection confounds, isolating pure work morale. Three flat-wage treatments (€10 / €15 / €20) plus a fourth incentivized control. Output is reconstructed from per-book timestamps logged by the cataloguing software.

Identification of effects: nonparametric two-sided Wilcoxon rank-sum tests on book counts, and a clustered OLS panel regression of entries per 90-minute quarter:
$$Y_{it} = \alpha + \beta_1 PR_i + \beta_2 PC_i + \beta_3 PR_i\cdot Q_{it} + \beta_4 PC_i\cdot Q_{it} + \gamma Q_{it} + \delta X_i + \varepsilon_{it},$$
where $PR_i$, $PC_i$ are PayRaise/PayCut dummies (Baseline omitted), $Q_{it}$ quarter dummies, $X_i$ controls (room FE, starting time, age, gender, field of study); standard errors clustered at the worker level. The PieceRate control pays a stepwise piece rate $\Pi_i(Y_i)=$ €0.15$\cdot Y_i$ if $Y_i<240$; €10$+$€0.30$\cdot Y_i$ if $240\le Y_i<260$; €0.40$\cdot Y_i$ if $Y_i\ge260$, to bound physical capacity.

The discussion formalizes two competing utility models. Inequality aversion (Fehr–Schmidt): $u_w(x_w,x_f)=x_w-\alpha\max\{0,x_f-x_w\}-\beta\max\{0,x_w-x_f\}$. With firm income $M$ from other projects far exceeding worker income $m$, the gap $x_f-x_w = M-m-2w+v(e)+c(e)$ is positive, so $u_w$ reduces to $x_w-\alpha[x_f-x_w]$ and optimal effort is *independent of the wage* — contradicting the data unless one adds narrow bracketing (workers ignore $M,m$). Action-based reciprocity (Cox–Friedman–Gjerstad, $\alpha=1$): $u_w(x_w,x_f)=x_w+\theta x_f$ with $x_f=v(e)-w$, $x_w=w-c(e)$, emotional-state $\theta$. If the firm is unkind ($\theta\le0$) effort collapses to the minimum $e_{\min}$; if kind ($\theta>0$) interior effort solves the first-order condition
$$\theta\, v'(e^*) = c'(e^*),$$
with $v$ increasing-concave and $c$ increasing-convex.

## Data
68 workers across two waves (2006, 2008) in the main treatments: 25 Baseline, 21 PayCut, 22 PayRaise (one no-show in wave 1); plus 18 new workers in the 2011 PieceRate control. Outcome measures: number of book entries per worker per 90-minute quarter (quantity), and the fraction of faultless entries (quality). About 200 applicants were recruited; treatment assignment randomized, three workers per day (one per treatment).

## Key findings
- **Wage cuts strongly reduce output.** PayCut output was 21% (about 47 books) lower than Baseline, highly significant (Wilcoxon $p=0.005$ overall). The gap is large and significant in every one of the four quarters — it is *persistent*, not a transitory reaction.
- **No positive reciprocity to raises.** PayRaise output was statistically indistinguishable from Baseline (218.6 vs 219.4 books; $p=0.991$). Regression interactions suggest a weak positive effect that strengthens after quarter one but never attains conventional significance.
- **Distribution-wide effect.** The PayCut CDF is shifted left across the whole distribution (e.g. ≤200 books: ~40% in Baseline vs 80% in PayCut), so results are not driven by outliers.
- **No ceiling effect.** PieceRate output was 24.7% higher than Baseline (274 books, $p<0.001$), with nearly the entire distribution above the Baseline median — workers had clear headroom, so the absent pay-raise response is genuine, not capacity-bound.
- **Quality is not sabotaged.** PayCut quality (90.4%) was *higher* than Baseline (84.4%, $p=0.030$); the cut shows up as lower work energy/quantity, not deliberate quality sabotage.
- **Model verdict.** The asymmetry (sharp downward response, muted upward response) is naturally produced by action-based reciprocity via the sign-switch of $\theta$ and convex effort costs, but requires an auxiliary narrow-bracketing assumption under inequality aversion. The authors therefore read the evidence as favoring reciprocity.

## Contribution
Provides scarce, clean field evidence on the *negative* side of gift exchange — most prior field work tested only pay raises and found weak positive reciprocity (Gneezy & List 2006). By embedding cuts and raises in one one-shot framework with an exogenous reference wage, the paper documents a robust asymmetry: morale is far more sensitive to losses than to gains. It rationalizes long-standing interview evidence (Bewley) on why firms resist wage cuts, and offers a competing-models field test that discriminates between inequality aversion and reciprocity.

## Limitations & open questions
- The design yields only *suggestive* model evidence; a rigorous test would require precise knowledge of workers' effort costs (estimable from output under randomized piece rates) and of workers' perceived output value (inducible or elicitable), plus additional treatments with varying wage-change magnitudes to pin down the curvature of the wage–output function.
- The determinants of the emotional state $\theta$ are unexplored: reactions may depend on the *explanation* given for a wage change (e.g. recession-justified cuts perceived as fair).
- External validity to ongoing relationships: in non-one-shot settings workers trade off negative reactions against firing risk, and self-selection (dissatisfied workers quitting and being replaced) could mitigate the measured morale effect.
- The PieceRate control assumes the worker skill distribution remained stable across the 2006–2011 waves.

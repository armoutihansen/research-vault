> [!abstract] Abstract
> A central tenet of economics is that individuals respond to incentives. For psychologists and sociologists, in contrast, rewards and punishments are often counterproductive, because they undermine "intrinsic motivation". We reconcile these two views, showing how performance incentives offered by an informed principal (manager, teacher, parent) can adversely impact an agent's (worker, child) perception of the task, or of his own abilities. Incentives are then only weak reinforcers in the short run, and negative reinforcers in the long run. We also study the effects of empowerment, help and excuses on motivation, as well as situations of ego bashing reflecting a battle for dominance within a relationship.

## Summary
Bénabou and Tirole give an information-economics microfoundation for the psychologists' claim that extrinsic rewards "crowd out" intrinsic motivation. The key device is the "looking-glass self": a privately-informed principal (boss, teacher, parent) chooses a policy $p$ (a bonus, delegation, help, or disclosure), and a rational agent reads that choice as a signal about a hidden parameter $\beta$ that governs his payoff (task cost, his own ability, or task value). Because effort and ability are complements, the principal wants the agent confident; but precisely for that reason, offering a strong reward credibly reveals that the principal feared the agent would not work, which is bad news. Rewards thus become weak short-run reinforcers and negative long-run reinforcers — but only under an explicit sorting condition; otherwise incentives "crowd in" motivation. The same logic is then run through delegation, help, praise/excuses, and "ego bashing" in dominance battles. This is a foundational theory paper; I read the full 32 pages (general model, all eight propositions, and the conclusion).

## Research question
When and why do explicit performance incentives (and more broadly, the actions of an informed principal) undermine rather than reinforce an agent's motivation? The paper seeks to give precise content to "intrinsic" vs. "extrinsic" motivation, and to characterize exactly when extrinsic motivators crowd out versus crowd in intrinsic motivation, reconciling the economic ("incentives work") and psychological ("incentives backfire") views.

## Method / identification
Pure game theory: a two-stage signalling game solved for perfect Bayesian equilibria, refined where needed by Cho-Kreps Never-a-Weak-Best-Response (NWBR). Two players, an agent ("he") and a principal ("she"). The principal privately knows a parameter $\beta$ (task difficulty/cost, ability, or value); the agent observes only a noisy signal $\sigma$, where $\beta$ is a sufficient statistic for $(\beta,\sigma)$. Stage 1: principal learns $\beta$ and picks policy $p$. Stage 2: agent observes $p$, learns $\sigma$, and chooses effort $e$. The agent's posterior $\hat\beta(\sigma,p)$ conditions on $p$ — that conditioning *is* the looking-glass self. The principal's first-order condition decomposes her policy choice into three terms,
$$E_\sigma\!\left[\frac{\partial U_P}{\partial p}+\frac{\partial U_P}{\partial e}\frac{\partial e^*}{\partial p}+\frac{\partial U_P}{\partial e}\frac{\partial e^*}{\partial\hat\beta}\frac{\partial\hat\beta}{\partial p}\;\Big|\;\beta\right]=0,$$
a direct cost, a standard incentive effect, and the novel confidence-management motive (third term). That motive runs through two channels: a **profitability effect** (the agent's type enters the principal's payoff, giving a Spence-Mirrlees sorting condition $\partial_\beta\!\left(\frac{\partial U_P/\partial p}{\partial U_P/\partial e}\right)$ of constant sign) and a **trust effect** (the type enters only the agent's problem, so a principal with bad $\beta$ fears a bad $\sigma$ and over-rewards). The trust effect is driven by a monotone likelihood ratio property (MLRP): $g(\sigma_1\mid c)/g(\sigma_2\mid c)$ decreasing in $c$ for $\sigma_1>\sigma_2$. In the workhorse specialization, $U_P=e\Lambda(\beta,p)$ with binary effort $e\in\{0,1\}$; the agent works iff $\sigma\ge\sigma^*(p)$. Multidimensional contracts (a lump-sum $a$ plus a success bonus $b$) are handled with the multidimensional implementability condition. Each application (rewards, lump sums, delegation, help, excuses, ego bashing) adds a tailored sorting/limited-informativeness assumption (Assumptions 1-4) and solves for equilibrium.

## Data
None — this is a theoretical paper. It is, however, explicitly disciplined by the experimental and field psychology literature: Deci's puzzle experiments, Lepper-Greene-Nisbett, Gneezy-Rustichini (2000a,b), Fehr-Falk, and Frey-Oberholzer-Gee's Swiss nuclear-siting survey (support halved when compensation was offered) all motivate and corroborate the model's predictions.

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 (hidden cost of rewards, trust effect, $\beta=c$).** In equilibrium: (i) rewards are positive *short-term* reinforcers ($\sigma^*(b_1)>\sigma^*(b_2)$ for $b_1<b_2$); (ii) rewards are bad news — a more confident principal (lower cost $c$) offers a *lower* bonus; (iii) rewards undermine the agent's assessment of task attractiveness, $E[c\mid\sigma_1,b_1]<E[c\mid\sigma_2,b_2]$, and this persists even after the outcome is realized. Corollaries: the **forbidden-fruit** effect and Tom Sawyer's fence (the optimal bonus may be zero or negative); **improper causal attributions** — an outside observer regressing outcomes on $b$ may wrongly conclude rewards are negative reinforcers, since principals give the highest rewards to the least-willing agents.
- **Proposition 2.** All of Prop 1 carries over when the private information concerns the agent's success probability $\theta$ (self-confidence) rather than cost $c$.
- **Proposition 3 (two-type, no lump sum).** A high-ability agent gets a low bonus; the principal mixes for low types. The unique NWBR equilibrium has $b=0$, and the probability of offering no bonus rises with the agent's prior self-confidence $f_H$ — the trust effect forces low-powered incentives, more so the more confident the agent.
- **Proposition 4 (lump sums allowed).** Under limited informativeness ($\theta_H G_H(\sigma)>\theta_L G_L(\sigma)$), a unique separating NWBR equilibrium exists with $b_k=b_k^*$ and $a_L=0<a_H=c-\theta_L V$. "Burning money" (a positive up-front $a$) signals high ability — a profitability effect, since the marginal signalling return $1/(\theta(W-b))$ is decreasing in $\theta$. Both lowering the piece rate and raising the fixed wage compress earnings inequality in a Lorenz sense.
- **Proposition 5 (empowerment).** Under Assumption 1 (delegation pays off more with abler agents) the profitability and trust effects pull in *opposite* directions. Still: (i) empowerment always raises effort; (ii) there is *more* delegation than under symmetric information ($\theta^{**}<\theta^*$); (iii) under Assumption 2 (limited informativeness) empowerment is always good news about ability.
- **Proposition 6 (help).** Under Assumption 3 (help matters more for weak agents) giving *more* help always *lowers* effort, and (Assumption 4) is bad news that permanently weakens self-confidence — formalizing how help breeds dependence. The sorting condition can reverse (help becomes a positive signal) when the principal's success payoff rises with ability or help (e.g. coauthoring vs. supervising a term paper).
- **Coaching / excuses.** With serially correlated noise $\mu=\Pr(\varepsilon_2=\varepsilon_1)$, offering an excuse for failure can backfire: truthful disclosure is an equilibrium iff $\frac{\mu}{1-\mu}\le\frac{\theta^e}{\theta_L}$. Praise boosts self-esteem but makes the agent doubt his environment; criticism lowers it yet need not discourage.
- **Propositions 7-8 (ego bashing).** When relative standing confers a private benefit $B$ (a dominance battle, condition (14)), individual 1 bashes individual 2's ego — disclosing bad news *and* feigning ignorance of good news (Prop 7) — to acquire *real* authority despite shared formal control. Prop 8: ego bashing is reversible "by omission" but disparaging is not, so good news is revealed earlier than bad news; relationships grow more antagonistic over time (or the reverse if effort is at the implementation rather than search stage).

## Contribution
Provides the first rational-agent, information-based reconciliation of the economic and psychological views of motivation, replacing Frey's reduced-form "motivation crowding" with explicit microfoundations. It pins down the precise conditions for crowding out — (a) the principal must have payoff-relevant private information the agent lacks, and (b) a sorting condition (she rewards more when the agent is weaker) must hold — and otherwise predicts crowding *in*. It unifies rewards, money-burning, delegation, help, praise, and ego bashing under one looking-glass-self mechanism, and connects confidence management to wage-inequality compression. It is the conceptual anchor for the authors' later self-confidence and identity work.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors list four explicit avenues: (1) combine the looking-glass self with the *reverse* signalling problem — **self-presentation**, where the agent signals his own information to the principal; (2) the **dynamics** of motivation and its management in long-term relationships; (3) extend to **asymmetric information about feelings** (altruism, friendship, love), so parties infer how much the other cares; (4) extend to **groups** and the institutional/organizational design of educational and work environments. They further flag the open question of whether intrapersonal confidence-maintenance, the looking-glass self, and self-presentation are mutually reinforcing. Built-in modelling restrictions (assumed away rather than resolved): fully rational Bayesian agents, $\beta$ as a sufficient statistic, risk neutrality, mostly binary effort and two-type cases, and the principal knowing $\beta$ exactly.

## Connections
Directly extends agency theory (Holmström-Milgrom multitasking; Laffont-Tirole's dynamic ratchet) by adding the third, informational term to the principal's problem, and refines Frey's (1997) motivation-crowding hypothesis. The delegation analysis is contrasted with the *initiative* effect of [[@Aghion1997|Aghion-Tirole (1997)]] and Dessein (2002) on delegation as congruence-signalling; the reputation-for-praise tradeoff parallels Fang-Moscarini (2002). The looking-glass self is Cooley's (1902) sociological concept formalized; the ex post rationalization result connects to cognitive-dissonance work (Festinger-Carlsmith, Bem, Staw). It is the theoretical companion to Bénabou-Tirole's program on self-confidence and willpower (Carrillo-Mariotti 2000; Bénabou-Tirole 2002a on self-knowledge), and a precursor to their work on intrinsic motivation, prosocial behavior, identity, and "Incentives and Prosocial Behavior" (Bénabou-[[@Benabou2006|Tirole 2006]]). Empirically it rationalizes Gneezy-Rustichini and Frey-Oberholzer-Gee's crowding-out findings.

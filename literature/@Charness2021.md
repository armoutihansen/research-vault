> [!abstract] Abstract
> Expectations are a critical factor in determining actions in a great variety of economic interactions. Hence, being able to measure beliefs is important in many economic environments. In this article, we review the approaches that have been used to measure beliefs and make comparisons of their effectiveness. We also discuss belief elicitation when the truth is not verifiable. We find that introspection (non-incentivized responses) seems to do as well as rather complex incentivized methods. We conjecture that simple and easy-to-comprehend incentivized methods are superior to introspection, in fact there are very few studies making such comparisons; this is an avenue for future research. We also discuss ways in which relatively complex methods could be made easier for usage in experimental work.

## Summary
A methodological survey (a companion to the authors' earlier survey on eliciting risk preferences) of techniques for measuring subjective beliefs in economic experiments. Its organizing principle is *complexity*: the cognitive cost an elicitation mechanism imposes on a participant trying to make a utility-maximizing report. Methods are ranked from simplest (introspection, frequency, interval, belief-coordination games) through intermediate (outcome matching, probability matching / stochastic BDM) to "very complex" (proper scoring rules, the binarized scoring rule, and the non-verifiable-truth methods: Bayesian Truth Serum, peer prediction, choice matching). The headline claim is contrarian: theoretically robust but complex incentivized methods do *not* systematically outperform unincentivized introspection in the lab, and the field's drift toward ever-more-robust mechanisms has not improved measured belief quality. The authors conjecture (without yet having the evidence) that *simple incentivized* methods dominate both. Coverage is full.

## Research question
Which belief-elicitation method gives the most accurate measure of subjective beliefs, and at what cost? More pointedly: do the theoretically attractive properties of complex incentivized mechanisms (incentive compatibility, robustness to risk attitudes) translate into empirically better belief measurement than the cheap, fast, but theory-free alternative of simply asking?

## Method / identification
This is a survey/conceptual paper, not an empirical study; its formal content is the comparative analysis of mechanisms. The canonical setup: a binary random variable $V$ with outcomes $E,E^c$; the participant's true subjective probability is $p$ and the elicited report is $r$; a method is good if it induces $r=p$ (or lets the experimenter back out $p$). A scoring rule pays $S_E(r)$ if $E$ occurs and $S_{E^c}(r)$ otherwise; it is *proper* if reporting $r=p$ maximizes expected payoff. The quadratic scoring rule (QSR, Brier 1950) sets $S_E(r)=\alpha-\beta(1-r)^2$ and $S_{E^c}(r)=\alpha-\beta r^2$; the expected score $E[S(r)]=\alpha-\beta[p(1-r)^2+(1-p)r^2]$ has its first-order condition satisfied at $r=p$. Proper scoring rules are not robust to risk aversion (risk-averse agents shade toward $0.5$ to cut payoff variance). The *binarized scoring rule* (BSR; [[@Hossain2013|Hossain & Okui 2013]], Schlag & van der Weele 2013) pays in lottery tickets / probability-of-prize rather than money, so any expected-utility maximizer (regardless of risk attitude) wants to maximize the win probability, restoring incentive compatibility under probabilistic sophistication and dominance — at the cost of an extra layer of randomization and compound-lottery reduction. *Probability matching* (revealed-probability / stochastic BDM / Karni mechanism) has the agent choose between an outcome lottery paying $F$ if $E$ and a constructed lottery paying $F$ with probability $r$; switching at $r=p$ identifies the belief and is robust to nonlinear probability weighting (needs only probabilistic sophistication and dominance), though it fails under ambiguity aversion. For non-verifiable truth, the *Bayesian Truth Serum* (Prelec 2004) elicits two reports — an opinion $r$ and a prediction $y_{ir}$ of the population frequency of each opinion — and pays a prediction score (proper-scoring-rule accuracy of $y$ against the realized frequency $x_r$) plus an information score $\log(x_r/\bar y_r)$ rewarding opinions that are *more common than collectively predicted*; truth-telling is incentive compatible under expected-payoff maximization, large samples, common belief in truthfulness, and the assumption that one's own opinion is an informative signal about the population distribution. *Peer prediction* (Miller et al. 2005) replaces the elicited $y$ with an experimenter-known opinion distribution; *choice matching* (Cvitanić et al. 2019) rewards an agent by the average score of others who reported the same opinion, requiring only that like-minded agents are "more likely to be right."

## Key findings
1. **Introspection vs. complex incentives.** Only two credible studies find a complex method beating introspection: Wang (2011) (QSR less "random" in a matching-pennies prediction, but correlation of beliefs with actions only $0.077$) and Massoni et al. (2014) (probability matching closer to a signal-detection Bayesian benchmark on confidence-rating distribution and ROC curve — but QSR there did *worse* than introspection, with confidence spikes at $0.5$ and $1$). The preponderance (Sonnemans & Offerman 2001; Trautmann & van de Kuilen 2014) finds QSR and introspection yield nearly identical belief distributions and effort. Conclusion: no reliable evidence that complex incentives systematically beat introspection.
2. **Among complex methods, QSR is weakest.** It is the only common method not robust to risk aversion. Proper rules (QSR, LogSR) beat the improper LinSR in Palfrey & Wang (2009), but Andersen et al. (2014) find QSR and LinSR statistically indistinguishable, undercutting the theory.
3. **BSR underwhelms.** [[@Hossain2013|Hossain & Okui (2013)]] find BSR beats QSR mainly for risk-averse agents and only in the binary-probability "P-experiment," not the point-prediction "M-experiment." Danz et al. (2020) is damning: explaining BSR incentives *precisely* raised the false-report rate (41.5% Information, 32.5% RCL-with-calculator) versus only telling participants truth-telling maximizes their win chance (20.6% No-Information), with shading toward $0.5$ — evidence that lottery-ticket payment fails to induce risk neutrality.
4. **Probability matching is the tentative empirical winner**, but its success is implementation-dependent (multiple-price-list cleaner than sequential-draws or auction) and may require heavy training; introspection still dominates it on simple tasks and for low-ability agents (Burfurd & Wilkening 2021).
5. **Complexity is first-order.** Both introspection (focal/default reports, image concerns, ex-post rationalization) and complex methods (confusion-driven biases) generate distortions of comparable seriousness; added theoretical robustness has not reduced empirical error.

## Contribution
Reframes the belief-elicitation literature around *comprehension/complexity* rather than theoretical incentive properties, and marshals the comparative evidence to argue that the field's investment in robust-but-opaque mechanisms (QSR, BSR, BTS) has not paid off empirically. It elevates two under-used *simple* incentivized methods — the frequency method (fixed prize iff the guessed count out of $N$ draws is exactly correct; incentive-compatible for the mode, robust to risk attitudes) and the interval method (a confidence band around the count) — and surveys the non-verifiable-truth toolbox for experimentalists, flagging choice matching as the most promising simple-and-applicable option there.

## Limitations & open questions
The central gap, named explicitly by the authors, is that **almost no study compares simple incentivized methods (frequency, interval, choice matching) against either complex methods or introspection** — so their core conjecture (simple incentives dominate) is untested. Other open problems: (i) whether merely eliciting a belief changes subsequent behavior is unsettled (mixed evidence: Croson 2000 vs. Gächter & Renner 2010 vs. Wilcox & Feltovich 2000); (ii) hedging between belief and action payoffs is hard to fully eradicate even with random-payment schemes (Blanco et al. 2010; [[@Armantier2013|Armantier & Treich 2013]]); (iii) "innocent" framing may not exist — different elicitation frames trigger different biases (Bauer & Wolff 2018); (iv) different mechanisms may suit different tasks (priors vs. Bayesian updating; high- vs. low-ability subjects), so a single best method may not exist; (v) developing genuinely simple, comprehensible incentivized mechanisms for non-verifiable settings remains open. Cross-study statistical comparison is itself hampered by high variance and low power.

## Connections
This is the belief-measurement companion to the authors' own Charness, Gneezy & Imas (2013) survey on eliciting risk preferences. The theoretical scaffolding on strictly proper scoring rules traces to Brier (1950) and Good (1952), with the unifying treatment in [[@Gneiting2007|Gneiting & Raftery (2007)]]. The risk-robust mechanisms build on the BDM/Karni mechanism ([[@Karni2009|Karni 2009]]) and the binarized rule of [[@Hossain2013|Hossain & Okui (2013)]] and Schlag & van der Weele (2013). The non-verifiable-truth strand originates with Prelec's (2004) Bayesian Truth Serum, extended by Miller, Resnick & Zeckhauser (2005) peer prediction and Cvitanić et al. (2019) choice matching, with small-sample variants from computer science (Witkowski & Parkes 2012; Radanovic & Faltings). It complements the broader surveys of Schotter & Trevino (2014) and Schlag, Tremewan & van der Weele (2015), departing from them by foregrounding complexity (a framing drawing on Simon's bounded rationality and Oprea 2020). Belief–action linkage and guilt-aversion connections run through Nyarko & Schotter (2002), Charness & Dufwenberg (2006), and Dufwenberg & Gneezy (2000); social-norm elicitation connects to Krupka & Weber (2013).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> A challenging issue in modern choice theory is how to enrich the kind of data available to the researcher along with our understanding of the decision-making process. We propose an axiomatic characterization of the 'satisficing' heuristic under various informational structures. In particular we examine the cases in which search behavior is both observable, partially observable, and unobservable. We also investigate behavioral definitions of preference and satisfaction and establish uniqueness results. Finally, we relate our framework to several well-known existing models.

## Summary
Papi formalizes Herbert Simon's satisficing heuristic as a revealed-preference model in which a decision-maker (DM) discovers alternatives sequentially, stops at the first satisfactory one, and picks the best alternative discovered so far. The DM is characterized by a primitive pair $(\succ, x_s)$: a strict linear order $\succ$ over alternatives and an aspiration alternative $x_s$ that partitions the grand set into satisfactory and unsatisfactory options. The paper's distinctive move is to characterize this procedure under three nested informational structures that differ in how much of the search order the analyst observes, and to show that the inferable information is monotonic in observability while the required volume of data is inversely related to it.

## Research question
Under what observable conditions can an analyst (i) test whether choices are consistent with the satisficing heuristic, and (ii) recover the DM's underlying preference and aspiration level from choice data? More precisely, how does the answer change as the informational structure varies from fully observable search order, to partially observable, to entirely unobservable? The paper also asks how the satisficing procedure formally relates to a cluster of existing bounded-rationality and consideration-set models.

## Method / identification
The setup is a finite grand set $X$ with $\mathcal{P}(X)$ its nonempty subsets. A **menu sequence** of budget set $A$ is a nested chain $\mathbf{A}\equiv\langle A_1,\dots,A_{N_A}\rangle$ with $A_j\subset A_k$ for $k>j$ and $A_{N_A}=A$; each $A_j$ is a *stage* explored at no extra cost. A **Satisficing Choice Function (SCF)** is defined by $(\succ,x_s)$ such that, for any sequence, $\{c(\mathbf{A})\}\equiv\max(\Gamma(\mathbf{A});\succ)$ where the consideration set is
$$\Gamma(\mathbf{A})\equiv\begin{cases}A_{j_s} & \text{if } UC(A;x_s)\neq\emptyset\\ A & \text{otherwise}\end{cases}$$
with the satisfactory set $UC(A;x_s)\equiv\{x\in A\mid\neg(x_s\succ x)\}$ and the stopping stage $j_s\equiv\min\{j\mid A_j\cap UC(A;x_s)\neq\emptyset\}$. The DM stops at the first stage containing a satisfactory alternative and maximizes $\succ$ there; if none exists she searches the whole set and takes the best unsatisfactory element. Memory demand is minimal — only the running best need be retained.

Three domains are analyzed. **$D_1$** (fully observable): the entire menu sequence is seen. **$D_2$** (partially observable): only the first stage $A_1$ and the budget set are seen, $(A_1,A)$. **$D_3$** (unobservable): only budget set and choice are seen, via a choice correspondence $C$ that records choices across multiple menu sequences (an Induced Satisficing Choice Correspondence, ISCC).

The identification strategy builds *behavioral* definitions of satisfaction and preference, then axiomatizes each domain with two properties: a **Limited Consideration WARP (LCWARP)** that imposes standard WARP only on alternatives that "attract attention"/"matter," plus a **Satisficing (SAT)** axiom requiring that a revealed-satisfactory alternative cannot be passed over for a later-discovered one. Identification is constructive: the paper specifies exactly which menu sequences must be observed (e.g., all $\langle\{x\},X\rangle$ to infer satisfaction; sequences with $|A|\le 3$ to build the order).

## Data
None — this is a pure choice-theoretic / axiomatic paper. No empirical dataset is used. The motivation cites experimental evidence consistent with satisficing ([[@Caplin2011|Caplin, Dean and Martin 2011]]; Reutskaja et al. 2011) and discusses lab technologies (mouselab, eye-tracking) and field traces (online click paths, IKEA-style forced store paths) that could *supply* the observable search order the framework presumes.

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 ($D_1$-revealed satisfaction):** under an SCF, $x\in UC(X;x_s)$ iff $x=c(\langle\{x\},X\rangle)$. A corollary: if $\{x_s\}=\max(X;\succ)$ the DM behaves as a standard maximizer.
- **Propositions 2–3 ($D_1$ attention and preference):** $x$ attracts attention at $\mathbf{A}$ iff $x\in A_{m(c(\mathbf{A}),\mathbf{A})}$ or the chosen $y$ is revealed unsatisfactory; and $x\succ y$ iff some $\mathbf{A}$ has $x=c(\mathbf{A})$ with $y$ attracting attention.
- **Theorem 1:** $c$ is an SCF iff it satisfies LCWARP and SAT. **Proposition 4:** the inducing pair $(\succ,x_s)$ is unique.
- **$D_2$ (Theorem 2, Propositions 5–7):** with only the starting stage observed, LCWARP$_2$ + SAT$_2$ characterize the procedure (SCF$_2$) and $(\succ,x_s)$ is still uniquely identified; attention is only partially inferable (Definition 4's notion of an alternative "mattering"), and more observations are needed than under $D_1$.
- **$D_3$ (Theorem 3, Propositions 8–9):** with search unobservable, $C$ is an ISCC iff it satisfies WARP and **Maximal Indifference (MI)** ($x,y\in C(A)\Rightarrow x\in C(X)$). Here satisfaction and preference are recoverable only *below* the aspiration level ($\succ_{unsat}$); the model becomes a special case of standard maximization by a weak order with indifference only among maximal alternatives. Uniqueness holds only over $x_s$ and $\succ_{unsat}$.
- **Monotonicity of information:** richer observation yields finer inference but needs fewer observations; coarser observation yields less inference and needs more data — a tradeoff implying few detailed observations can be more valuable to an analyst than many coarse ones.
- **Embedding results (Section 6):** an SCF on linear sequences is a special case of Rubinstein–Salant choice-from-lists (Prop. 10) but, unlike it, can rank *above* the threshold; an SCF is agenda-rationalizable (Prop. 11); SCF$_2$ is a special case of Masatlioglu–Nakajima choice-by-iterative-search (Prop. 12) with a strictly larger revealed-preference relation; SCF and SCF$_2$ map into Salant–Rubinstein choice-with-frames, with SCF a salient consideration function but SCF$_2$ not (it admits choice reversals, illustrated by a circular-shelf example); and SCF$_2$ is neither implied by nor implies Masatlioglu–Ok choice-with-initial-endowments (Prop. 13).

## Contribution
The paper supplies the first axiomatic revealed-preference characterization of Simon's satisficing heuristic across a graded family of informational environments, generalizing lists to nested menu sequences (so the DM need not examine alternatives strictly one-by-one, spanning the standard simultaneous case, the one-by-one polar case, and everything between). It delivers behavioral (choice-only) definitions of *satisfaction* and *preference*, exact data-requirement statements for falsification and recovery, and uniqueness theorems, while situating satisficing within the broader consideration-set/attention literature through a battery of formal nesting results.

## Limitations & open questions
- The aspiration threshold $x_s$ is **fixed**. The author explicitly flags as future research a model in which the threshold *varies* with characteristics of the choice problem (e.g., complexity).
- $D_1$'s assumption that search order is fully observable "might sound demanding in some circumstances," though it then requires relatively few observations — an acknowledged tension.
- Under $D_3$ preference above the aspiration level is **not** identified, and satisficing DMs may appear to violate WARP simply because they do not choose from certain "key" menu sequences — leaving open how robust the WARP+MI characterization is to incomplete sequence coverage.
- A proposed **application** (left open): firms competing on product quality facing a population of satisficing consumers when firms are uncertain about consumers' aspiration levels — characterizing equilibrium behavior is posed as a policy-relevant research question.

## Connections
The work sits squarely in the consideration-set and bounded-rationality revealed-preference tradition. Its intellectual root is Simon (1955). The closest formal relatives, each explicitly compared, are [[@Rubinstein2006|Rubinstein and Salant]] (2006, choice from lists), [[@Salant2008|Salant and Rubinstein]] (2008, choice with frames), [[@Tyson2008|Tyson]] (2008, cognitive constraints and contraction consistency, linked to [[@Sen1971|Sen's 1971]] property $\beta$), Masatlioglu and Nakajima (2009, choice by iterative search), Masatlioglu and Ok (2009, choice with initial endowments), Apesteguia and Ballester (2010, choice by sequential procedures / agenda rationalizability), [[@Caplin2011|Caplin and Dean]] (2011, search and revealed preference via choice-process data), Horan (2010), and Raymond (2011, revealed search theory). The attention/limited-consideration axiomatics connect to Masatlioglu, Nakajima and Ozbay's "Revealed Attention" and to [[@Manzini2007|Manzini and Mariotti (2007)]]. Empirically the satisficing premise leans on [[@Caplin2011|Caplin, Dean and Martin (2011)]] and Reutskaja et al. (2011). The LCWARP axiom is a deliberate weakening of the classical WARP of standard revealed-preference theory (Richter 1966; Mas-Colell, Whinston and Green 1995).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> This European collaborative study addresses the question whether a predictive test result for Huntington's disease (HD) has an effect on subsequent reproduction by comparing carriers and non-carriers of the Huntington mutation. A unique characteristic of this study is that this evaluation is done in persons at reproductive age who had a predictive test after the identification of the Huntington gene and who were counselled in one of the participating centres. Data were collected for 180 carriers and 271 non-carriers who received a predictive test result in the period 1993–1998 in Aberdeen, Athens, Cardiff, Leiden, Leuven, Paris or Rome. The mean age of the total study group was 31.5 years and for about half of the group the follow-up interval was 3 years or more, with a maximum of 7 years. The collaborative study clearly revealed an overall impact of the predictive test result on subsequent reproduction: 14% of the carriers had one or more subsequent pregnancies vs 28% of the non-carriers. In the total carrier group a prenatal test was carried out in about two thirds of the pregnancies and one child was born after preimplantation genetic diagnosis; artificial insemination by donor, egg cell donation or adoption were not reported. A more refined analysis was performed in the subgroup with a follow-up interval of at least 3 years and who reported 'family planning' as a motive to apply for predictive testing in the pretest period. The complexity of this motive is discussed. In this subgroup with a desire for children in the pretest period the effect of the predictive test result was more pronounced: 69% of the non-carriers had subsequent pregnancies while only 39% of the carriers who mentioned 'family planning' as one of the major reasons to apply for predictive testing had a subsequent pregnancy. Of the carriers with one or more subsequent pregnancies the percentage using prenatal diagnosis was slightly higher than the percentage not using it, although there were clear differences from one centre to another. The latter group's decisions may seem more intriguing but may be partially understood based on stage theories of health behaviour. Last, but not least, whatever option is chosen by a couple at increased risk of transmitting the Huntington mutation, it is of the utmost importance that professionals fully respect this decision and support the couple.

## Summary
A seven-centre, six-country European collaborative cohort study asking whether learning one's Huntington's disease (HD) mutation status changes downstream reproductive behaviour. Among 451 asymptomatic testees of reproductive age (180 mutation carriers, 271 non-carriers) who received a predictive DNA test result in 1993–1998 and were followed up to seven years, carriers were roughly half as likely as non-carriers to have a subsequent pregnancy (14% vs 28%). The gap widened in the most decision-relevant subgroup — those who cited "family planning" as a pretest motive and had at least three years of follow-up (39% of carriers vs 69% of non-carriers). The paper documents how carriers who did reproduce navigated prenatal diagnosis, preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD), and uptested pregnancies, with marked between-centre variation.

## Research question
Does a predictive (presymptomatic) DNA test result for HD causally shape subsequent reproductive decision making and actual reproduction? Concretely: (1) Do carriers and non-carriers of the Huntington mutation differ in whether they have post-test pregnancies, and what is the uptake of prenatal testing among carriers who do? (2) Do these reproductive histories differ across the seven participating centres (intercultural variation)? The design exploits the fact that genetic status is assigned by the test essentially independently of the couple's prior reproductive intentions, so non-carriers serve as a natural comparison group for carriers who all entered counselling with comparable a-priori 50% risk.

## Method / identification
Observational multi-centre cohort (no randomization; identification rests on the as-good-as-random assignment of carrier vs non-carrier status conditional on being an at-risk applicant). Eligibility: tested and counselled at one of seven centres (Leuven, Leiden, Cardiff, Aberdeen, Paris, Rome, Athens); age $\le 45$ at result disclosure; result communicated 1993–1998. Two testees with an intermediate (equivocal) allele were excluded, leaving $n=451$. Data were abstracted from pretest and post-test counselling files (and, where feasible, updated by contacting the testee) via a standardized nine-item questionnaire covering test date/result, sociodemographics, pretest motives, last contact, pregnancies, adoptions, pregnancy-at-disclosure, perceived family completeness, and PGD intentions; questionnaires were anonymized and centralized in Leuven. Analysis used SAS and was primarily descriptive. Associations between nominal variables were tested with chi-square ($\chi^2$). The central confirmatory test was an analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) — chosen over logistic regression despite the dichotomous outcome — of the binary outcome "having vs not having subsequent pregnancies" on the predictor "predictive test result" (carrier vs non-carrier), with two covariates: age at testing of the at-risk prospective parent, and number of children before testing. A second ANCOVA added "sex of the at-risk parent" as a main effect plus a test-result $\times$ sex interaction, with the same covariates. The headline contrast is restricted to the most appropriate stratum: follow-up $\ge 3$ years and "family planning" cited as a pretest motive.

## Data
451 asymptomatic testees of reproductive age (180 carriers, 271 non-carriers; 58% female; mean age 31.5 years, SD 7, range 18–45) tested 1993–1998 across seven European genetic centres. 77% had a stable relationship at disclosure. Pretest motives (multiple allowed): "having certainty / relief from uncertainty" 81%, "reproductive decision making / family planning" 38%, "informing the children" 28%, other 11%. Roughly half of each status group already had pregnancies before testing (442 prior pregnancies total). Follow-up: about half $\ge 3$ years (max 7); 19% under 1 year. Leiden contributed only a subset of eligible testees (those in a parallel neuropsychological study), a noted minor source of incompleteness. Family size was explicitly not "complete" at last contact (43% complete, 27% incomplete, 14% undecided).

## Key findings
Overall impact: $\chi^2$ and ANCOVA both show test result shapes reproduction. 26/180 carriers (14%) vs 77/271 non-carriers (28%) had $\ge 1$ post-test pregnancy. Across the 40 carrier-group pregnancies, 24 prenatal tests for HD were done (9 favourable, 15 unfavourable), 12 pregnancies resulted in a birth with no prenatal test, plus 3 miscarriages and 1 PGD birth. The point-biserial correlation of test result with having pregnancies was $r=0.16$ ($P<0.001$) overall, rising to $r=0.21$ ($P<0.01$) in the $\ge 3$-year follow-up subgroup. Refined subgroup (family planning motive, $n=171$): carriers 18/57 (32%) vs non-carriers 64/114 (56%) had pregnancies ($\chi^2 = 9.11$, $P<0.01$). Most-adequate subgroup (family-planning motive and follow-up $\ge 3$ years, $n=96$): carriers 15/38 (39%) vs non-carriers 40/58 (69%) had pregnancies ($\chi^2 = 8.23$, $P<0.01$). The confirmatory ANCOVA showed a significant effect of test result on subsequent pregnancy ($F=8.46$, $P<0.01$) net of age and prior children; the second ANCOVA found the effect robust ($F=7.03$, $P<0.01$) with no significant effect of the at-risk parent's sex and no test-result $\times$ sex interaction. Among carriers who did reproduce, slightly more used prenatal diagnosis than not, but uptake varied sharply by centre (e.g., in Leuven all carriers with further pregnancies used prenatal testing; lower in Paris; Athens had a carrier birth with no prenatal test). One striking case: a carrier with six post-test pregnancies — successive unfavourable prenatal results and a miscarriage — eventually achieved an unaffected birth via IVF/PGD. An unexplained finding: 22% of non-carrier family-planning pregnancies ended in miscarriage. The authors hypothesize the carrier/non-carrier reproduction gap might widen with longer follow-up because some carriers feel time pressure.

## Contribution
First systematic, multi-centre European cohort comparing actual post-predictive-test reproduction of HD mutation carriers vs non-carriers among people tested after the 1993 gene identification and embedded in defined pre/post-test counselling. Prior evidence was sparse, small-sample, short-follow-up, or intention-based (e.g., Manchester, Wales exclusion testing, the Canadian Collaborative Study). By using non-carriers as a comparison group and reporting behaviour (not just stated intentions), the study quantifies the real reproductive impact of predictive testing and documents the menu of carrier options (refraining, taking the risk, prenatal diagnosis, PGD, donor gametes, adoption), showing predictive testing lets most non-carriers proceed unburdened while carrier decisions remain complex and centre-dependent.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag explicitly: (1) small adequate-subgroup numbers in several centres meant intercultural differences could not be properly analysed — a larger sample with longer follow-up is needed; (2) follow-up (7 years max) captures only short-to-mid-term impact and families are not "complete," so estimates are provisional and the carrier/non-carrier gap may grow over time; (3) "family planning" is a heterogeneous motive (avoiding transmission vs not wanting an affected parent vs timing/number of children), conflating distinct decision logics; (4) motives are conscious, self-reported pretest statements only, omitting unconscious motives; (5) the large group at risk who never test is unstudied, limiting generalizability; (6) completeness of adoption / donor insemination / egg donation data cannot be judged; (7) Leiden's partial inclusion; (8) the 22% non-carrier miscarriage rate is unexplained; (9) counsellor characteristics may influence decisions despite nondirective counselling — beyond this study's scope. Intentions-versus-behaviour gaps are interpreted through stage theories of health behaviour (Weinstein).

## Connections
Squarely in the predictive/presymptomatic genetic-testing and reproductive-decision literature for late-onset dominant disease. It builds on the HD gene discovery (Huntington's Disease Collaborative Research Group, Cell 1993) and the IHA/WFN predictive-testing guidelines (Neurology 1994). On uptake and motives it draws on Decruyenaere et al. (1997) and the authors' own review of predictive-testing motives and psychological impact (Evers-Kiebooms & Decruyenaere 1998; Evers-Kiebooms et al. 2000). Comparable reproductive-behaviour studies include Adam et al. (1993, Canadian Collaborative Study), Tyler et al. (1990) and Tolmie et al. (1995) on prenatal exclusion testing, and Schulman et al. (1996) and Geraedts & Liebaers on PGD for HD. The interpretive frame of intentions preceding behaviour cites Weinstein, Rothman & Sutton's stage theories of health behaviour (1998). The intentions-behaviour discrepancy and nondirective-counselling caveat connect to broader behavioural-decision and genetic-ethics work.

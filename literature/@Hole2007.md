> [!abstract] Abstract
> This article describes the mixlogit Stata command for fitting mixed logit models by using maximum simulated likelihood.

## Summary
This is a Stata Journal software article that introduces the `mixlogit` command for fitting mixed (random-parameters) logit models by maximum simulated likelihood (MSL). It lays out the standard random-coefficients logit model in the tradition of [[@Revelt1998|Revelt and Train (1998)]] and Train (2003), then documents the command syntax and three companion commands (`mixlpred` for predicted probabilities, `mixlcov` for covariance-matrix postestimation). Worked examples replicate Huber and Train (2001) electricity-supplier choice and Haan and Uhlendorff (2006) teacher-rating data, showing normal, lognormal, and correlated random coefficients.

## Research question
How can mixed logit models, which require simulation to evaluate an analytically intractable likelihood, be fitted conveniently in Stata for general choice settings that include both individual-specific and alternative-specific attributes, allow normal/lognormal coefficient distributions, and permit correlation among random coefficients?

## Method / identification
The model assumes $N$ respondents each facing $J$ alternatives over $T$ choice occasions. The utility individual $n$ derives from alternative $j$ on occasion $t$ is
$$U_{njt} = \beta_n' x_{njt} + \varepsilon_{njt},$$
where $\beta_n$ is a vector of individual-specific (random) coefficients, $x_{njt}$ are observed attributes, and $\varepsilon_{njt}$ is i.i.d. extreme value (type I). The density of $\beta$ is $f(\beta\mid\theta)$ with distributional parameters $\theta$. Conditional on $\beta_n$, the choice probability is the conditional logit form (McFadden 1974):
$$L_{nit}(\beta_n) = \frac{\exp(\beta_n' x_{nit})}{\sum_{j=1}^{J}\exp(\beta_n' x_{njt})}.$$
The probability of the observed sequence of choices conditional on $\beta_n$ is $S_n(\beta_n)=\prod_{t=1}^{T} L_{ni(n,t)t}(\beta_n)$, where $i(n,t)$ is the chosen alternative. The unconditional sequence probability integrates over the mixing distribution:
$$P_n(\theta) = \int S_n(\beta) f(\beta\mid\theta)\, d\beta.$$
Because the log likelihood $LL(\theta)=\sum_{n=1}^{N}\ln P_n(\theta)$ has no closed form, it is approximated by the simulated log likelihood
$$SLL(\theta) = \sum_{n=1}^{N}\ln\left\{\frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^{R} S_n(\beta^r)\right\},$$
where $R$ is the number of replications and $\beta^r$ is the $r$th draw from $f(\beta\mid\theta)$. Estimation is by MSL. `mixlogit` is implemented as a `d0` `ml` evaluator; pseudorandom draws are Halton draws generated via Mata's `halton()` ([[@Drukker2006|Drukker and Gates 2006]]), using the first $K$ primes for $K$ random coefficients, with a default of 50 draws and 15 burned initial elements. Correlated coefficients are parameterized via the Cholesky factor $L$ of the covariance matrix $V=LL'$; uncorrelated specifications report means and standard deviations directly. Lognormal coefficients (sign-constrained) are available through the `ln()` option, with the implied coefficient median, mean, and standard deviation recovered as $\exp(b_p)$, $\exp(b_p+s_p^2/2)$, and $\exp(b_p+s_p^2/2)\sqrt{\exp(s_p^2)-1}$ respectively.

## Data
None original. Two illustrative datasets are reused: Huber and Train (2001) on residential households' choice among four electricity suppliers (attributes: price per kWh, contract length, local provider, well-known provider, time-of-day and seasonal rates; 4780 alternative-rows), and Haan and Uhlendorff (2006) on teachers' three-level ratings of pupil behavior (3939 rows after expansion).

## Key findings
This is a methods/software piece, so "findings" are demonstrations. (1) On the electricity data with a fixed price coefficient and normal random coefficients, consumers on average prefer lower prices, shorter contracts, local and well-known providers, and fixed over variable rates, with statistically significant preference heterogeneity (LR test rejects all SDs equal to zero). The fraction with opposite-sign preferences is $100\times\Phi(-b_k/s_k)$. (2) A lognormal price coefficient illustrates sign-constrained heterogeneity, with `nlcom` used to recover the mean/median/SD of the coefficient. (3) A correlated-coefficients model fits the 15-element Cholesky factor; a LR test of the off-diagonal elements (statistic $2\times(1137.7962-1060.8267)=153.939$, $\chi^2_{10}$) strongly rejects independence. (4) The command replicates Haan and Uhlendorff's (2006) multinomial-logit-with-heterogeneity results up to small differences attributable to using primes 2 and 3 rather than 7 and 11 for the Halton draws.

## Contribution
Provides a flexible, general-purpose Stata implementation of mixed logit by MSL, accommodating both individual-specific and alternative-specific regressors (analogous to `clogit`), normal and lognormal random coefficients, full correlation among random coefficients, and convenient postestimation for predictions and covariance recovery. It extends Haan and Uhlendorff's (2006) earlier Stata implementation to a broader class of models and has become a widely used applied tool for discrete-choice estimation.

## Limitations & open questions
The article notes the accuracy-versus-time tradeoff in choosing the number of Halton draws: accuracy increases with $R$ but so does estimation time, suggesting a small number (50) for specification search and a larger number (500) for the final model, with deeper discussion deferred to Train (2003), Cappellari and Jenkins (2006), and Haan and Uhlendorff (2006). It flags that simulation-based estimators generally yield slightly different results unless draws are generated identically (prime selection matters). The lognormal option requires manually multiplying attributes by $-1$ to accommodate negative coefficients. The sign of estimated standard deviations is arbitrary and must be read as positive. The non-concave iterations in the examples hint at the practical difficulty of the simulated likelihood surface. No formal treatment of optimal draw counts, alternative quasi-random sequences, or estimator consistency/efficiency is given.

## Connections
Builds directly on the mixed logit framework of [[@Revelt1998|Revelt and Train (1998)]] and the textbook treatment in Train (2003), with the underlying conditional logit from McFadden (1974). It extends the Stata MSL approach of Haan and Uhlendorff (2006) and relies on Halton-draw machinery from [[@Drukker2006|Drukker and Gates (2006)]] and the simulation methods surveyed by Cappellari and Jenkins (2006). The electricity application is from Huber and Train (2001), and the data-reshaping mirrors Long and Freese (2006). The command is foundational tooling for empirical work in random-utility discrete choice, complementing structural estimation of heterogeneous preferences and serving as a practical bridge between random-utility theory and applied microeconometrics, including the choice-modeling and social-preference estimation literatures (e.g., [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin, Fehr, and Schunk 2019]] on individual heterogeneity in social preferences).

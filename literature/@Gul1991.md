> [!abstract] Abstract
> An axiomatic model of preferences over lotteries is developed. It is shown that this model is consistent with the Allais Paradox, includes expected utility theory as a special case, and is only one parameter ($\beta$) richer than the expected utility model. Allais Paradox type behavior is identified with positive values of $\beta$. Preferences with positive $\beta$ are said to be disappointment averse. It is shown that risk aversion implies disappointment aversion and that the Arrow-Pratt measures of risk aversion can be generalized in a straight-forward manner, to the current framework.

## Summary

Gul derives a one-parameter generalization of expected utility (EU) over lotteries that resolves the Allais paradox while remaining as close to EU as possible. The single extra parameter $\beta>-1$ governs "disappointment aversion": outcomes below a lottery's own certainty equivalent are "disappointing" and are systematically overweighted relative to "elating" outcomes above it. The representation is pinned down by four axioms (completeness/transitivity, continuity, a weak/restricted independence axiom, and a symmetry axiom), is unique up to an affine transformation of $u$ and a unique $\beta$, nests EU at $\beta=0$, and the paper shows risk aversion implies disappointment aversion and that Arrow-Pratt risk-aversion machinery extends essentially unchanged.

## Research question

Can one build a normatively acceptable axiomatic model of choice under uncertainty that (i) nests expected utility as a special case, (ii) is consistent with the Allais paradox, and (iii) is the most restrictive such model — i.e., departs from EU only to the minimal extent needed to accommodate the Allais intuition?

## Method / identification

The object is a binary preference relation $\succeq$ over simple lotteries on a prize interval $X=[w,b]$. The central construct is the **elation/disappointment decomposition (EDD)**: any lottery $p$ is split at its certainty equivalent $CE(p)$ into an "elation" part $q\in B(p)$ (prizes preferred to $CE(p)$) and a "disappointment" part $r\in W(p)$ (prizes worse than $CE(p)$), with $p=\alpha q+(1-\alpha)r$ where $\alpha$ is the total elation probability. Elation and disappointment probabilities are $e(p)=\sum_{x\succ p}p(x)$ and $d(p)=\sum_{p\succ x}p(x)$.

Four axioms are imposed: **Axiom 1** (complete, transitive $\succeq$); **Axiom 2** (continuity in the $L_1$ metric, guaranteeing a well-defined $CE$); **Axiom 3 — Weak Independence** (the EU independence conclusion holds only when mixing does not change disappointment probabilities, i.e. no elation prize switches to a disappointment prize or vice versa — this is where independence is allowed to fail and the Allais intuition enters); and **Axiom 4 — Symmetry** (the preference over elation prizes and over disappointment prizes represent the same underlying utility, so any non-EU behavior is attributable solely to disappointment, not to two unrelated utility indices). Axioms 1–3 imply betweenness, placing these preferences inside Dekel's (1986) class.

The implied utility is defined implicitly. For an EDD $(\alpha,q,r)$ of $p$,
$$V(p)=\gamma(\alpha)\sum_x u(x)q(x)+\bigl(1-\gamma(\alpha)\bigr)\sum_x u(x)r(x),$$
where the weight function is
$$\gamma(\alpha)=\frac{\alpha}{1+(1-\alpha)\beta},\qquad \beta\in(-1,\infty).$$
$V(p)$ is the value $v$ solving $\sum_x \phi(x,v)\,p(x)=v$, with $\phi$ the local utility function (in Dekel's sense) that evaluates disappointment prizes with $u$ and elation prizes with $(u(x)+\beta v)/(1+\beta)$. A finite algorithm (Appendix) constructs all EDDs and computes $V(p)$.

## Data

None — this is a pure axiomatic decision-theory paper. It discusses Allais-type experimental evidence (common ratio / common consequence effects, the certainty effect of [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky 1979]]) for motivation and consistency checks, but runs no estimation or experiment.

## Key findings

- **Theorem 1 (representation & uniqueness):** $\succeq$ satisfies Axioms 1–4 iff there exist $u$ (continuous) and $\beta\in(-1,\infty)$ giving the $\gamma$-weighted EDD representation above; $u$ is unique up to a positive affine transformation and $\beta$ is unique. EU is exactly $\beta=0$ (then $\gamma(\alpha)=\alpha$).
- **Sign of $\beta$:** $\beta>0$ gives $\gamma(\alpha)<\alpha$ and convex $\gamma$ ("disappointment averse"); $-1<\beta<0$ gives $\gamma(\alpha)>\alpha$, concave $\gamma$ ("elation loving"). Disappointment aversion is a global property.
- **Theorem 2:** $\beta$ measures the propensity for Allais-paradox-type behavior; positive $\beta$ is equivalent to (the relevant range of) the fanning-out / Allais behavior.
- **Theorem 3:** $(u,\beta)$ is risk averse (dislikes mean-preserving spreads) iff $\beta\ge 0$ **and** $u$ is concave. Hence **risk aversion implies disappointment aversion**. Mixing the sign of $\beta$ with the curvature of $u$ yields agents who are risk-averse over some gambles and risk-loving over others (e.g. reject fair even-chance bets yet buy lottery tickets and less-than-fair insurance).
- **Theorems 4–5 (Arrow-Pratt extension):** With $R^a(x)=-u''(x)/u'(x)$ and $R^r(x)=-xu''(x)/u'(x)$, increasing/decreasing/constant absolute (relative) risk aversion corresponds to monotonicity of $R^a$ ($R^r$); $\beta$ drops out because it is independent of $x$. Comparative risk aversion (Theorem 5) requires higher $R^a$ everywhere, and in the stronger Machina (1984) sense additionally $\beta_1\ge 0\ge\beta_2$.

The functional form reduces, for binary lotteries, to a rank-dependent / subjective-weighting form and is a special case of [[@Quiggin1982|Quiggin (1982)]]; the indifference map exhibits a kink on the 45° line whenever $\beta\neq 0$ (so these preferences are non-differentiable and fall outside Machina's smooth class), and over three prizes displays "mixed fanning" (fanning out below, fanning in above the reference lottery), matching Neilson's (1989) reading of the evidence.

## Contribution

Introduces disappointment aversion as a parsimonious, normatively defensible departure from EU: a single parameter $\beta$ added to the von Neumann–Morgenstern utility, with a clean axiomatic foundation that isolates the Allais paradox as its sole source of EU violation. It shows EU's risk-aversion apparatus (Arrow-Pratt measures, comparative statics) survives almost verbatim, making the model tractable for applied work. It is a foundational reference for reference-dependent and rank-dependent utility and for the later Köszegi–Rabin reference-dependence program.

## Limitations & open questions

- With a **finite** prize set, Axioms 1–4 do **not** characterize $(u,\beta)$ preferences, because the proof requires well-defined certainty equivalents; the author flags this gap explicitly.
- For **nonsimple lotteries**, no finite algorithm computes $V(p)$; only a convergent approximation exists.
- The model cannot exhibit **all** of Machina's fanning-out violations simultaneously: any $(u,\beta)$ agent fans out in only the top or the bottom half of the probability triangle, not both.
- Acknowledged conflicting evidence: for $\beta>0$ the model is consistent with the common ratio effect and (partly) the common consequence effect but **inconsistent with the common ratio effect over losses/negative numbers**; for $\beta<0$ the reverse pattern of inconsistencies holds. Reconciling both domains is left open.
- Symmetry (Axiom 4) is a deliberate restriction; relaxing it yields a richer model with two distinct utility indices $u_e,u_d$ for elation and disappointment, only sketched here.

## Connections

The paper sits in the "modify the independence axiom" tradition alongside Chew & MacCrimmon (1979), Fishburn (1983), Dekel (1986), and Yaari (1987), and is built directly on the betweenness/local-utility framework of Dekel (1986). It contrasts itself with Machina (1982, 1984, 1987), who treats the Allais paradox as global fanning-out of smooth preferences, whereas Gul's kinked, non-differentiable preferences give a narrower mechanism. The disappointment terminology is borrowed from [[@Bell1985|Bell (1985)]] and [[@Loomes1986|Loomes & Sugden (1986)]] but with different motivation; it also relates to regret theory (Bell 1982; Loomes & Sugden 1982). The binary-lottery form coincides with rank-dependent expected utility ([[@Quiggin1982|Quiggin 1982]]) and echoes the certainty effect of [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky (1979)]]; Allais (1979) and Savage (1972) supply the motivating paradox, and Neilson (1989) the supporting mixed-fanning evidence. Downstream it is a touchstone for choice-process and reference-dependent models such as Köszegi & Rabin and for empirical work estimating risk preferences in the spirit of Bruhin, Fehr-Duda & Epper (2010).

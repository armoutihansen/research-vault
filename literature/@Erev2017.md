> [!abstract] Abstract
> Experimental studies of choice behavior document distinct, and sometimes contradictory, deviations from maximization. For example, people tend to overweight rare events in 1-shot decisions under risk, and to exhibit the opposite bias when they rely on past experience. The common explanations of these results assume that the contradicting anomalies reflect situation-specific processes that involve the weighting of subjective values and the use of simple heuristics. The current article analyzes 14 choice anomalies that have been described by different models, including the Allais, St. Petersburg, and Ellsberg paradoxes, and the reflection effect. Next, it uses a choice prediction competition methodology to clarify the interaction between the different anomalies. It focuses on decisions under risk (known payoff distributions) and under ambiguity (unknown probabilities), with and without feedback concerning the outcomes of past choices. The results demonstrate that it is not necessary to assume situation-specific processes. The distinct anomalies can be captured by assuming high sensitivity to the expected return and 4 additional tendencies: pessimism, bias toward equal weighting, sensitivity to payoff sign, and an effort to minimize the probability of immediate regret. Importantly, feedback increases sensitivity to probability of regret. Simple abstractions of these assumptions, variants of the model Best Estimate and Sampling Tools (BEAST), allow surprisingly accurate ex ante predictions of behavior. Unlike the popular models, BEAST does not assume subjective weighting functions or cognitive shortcuts. Rather, it assumes the use of sampling tools and reliance on small samples, in addition to the estimation of the expected values. (PsycInfo Database Record (c) 2020 APA, all rights reserved)

## Summary
A landmark 102-page *Psychological Review* paper that replaces a fragmented zoo of decision anomalies with a single predictive process model. Erev et al. take 14 classic deviations from expected-value maximization — including the Allais (certainty), reflection, St. Petersburg, and Ellsberg paradoxes, loss aversion, break-even, get-something, splitting, and the experience-based reversals (underweighting of rare events, reversed reflection, payoff-variability and correlation effects) — and replicate them all inside one 11-dimensional space of choice tasks. They then run a *choice prediction competition*: posting calibration data and a baseline model (BEAST), challenging 53 teams worldwide to beat it on a held-out test set generated only after the submission deadline. The punchline: a single model assuming high sensitivity to expected value plus four sampling-based tendencies (pessimism, payoff-sign sensitivity, equal-weighting, regret minimization) captures the entire anomaly set with no subjective probability-weighting function and no situation-specific parameters. *(This is a >50-page paper; I read targeted — front matter, full introduction, the Method/Results for all 14 phenomena, the complete BEAST formalization, the competition design and results, and the General Discussion/Summary — and sampled rather than ingested the figure-by-figure appendices.)*

## Research question
Can the contradictory anomalies of decision under risk, under ambiguity, and from experience be captured by **one** descriptive model that yields accurate **ex ante out-of-sample predictions**, rather than by a patchwork of paradigm-specific models? Concretely, the paper targets Alvin Roth's "1-800 critique": existing models (prospect theory, CPT, priority heuristic, small-sample models) each cover a slice of the choice-task space with unclear boundaries, so it is unknown which model to apply to an intermediate situation (e.g., a fully described gamble faced with a few trials of feedback, as in an in-vehicle safe-driving incentive). Which single process predicts behavior across the whole space?

## Method / identification
The design is a three-study **choice prediction competition** under a *generalization criterion* (Busemeyer & Wang 2000), explicitly chosen to defeat overfitting. (1) A **replication study** (125 students, 30 hand-picked problems, 25 trials each: first 5 no-feedback, then 20 with full feedback on obtained and forgone payoffs) confirms all 14 phenomena live in one **11-dimensional task space**. The dimensions are nine payoff-distribution parameters ($L_A,H_A,p_{HA},L_B,H_B,p_{HB}$, LotNum, LotShape, Corr), an ambiguity dimension Amb $\in\{0,1\}$, and a feedback dimension (studied within-problem). (2) A **calibration study** adds 60 randomly drawn problems (risk-reward correlation $\approx-0.6$, mimicking natural environments). (3) A **test study** (160 students, 60 new random problems) is the competition target, revealed only after submissions closed.

The baseline model **BEAST (Best Estimate And Sampling Tools)** asserts that Option A is strictly preferred to B after $r$ trials iff
$$[\text{BEV}_A(r)-\text{BEV}_B(r)] + [\text{ST}_A(r)-\text{ST}_B(r)] + e(r) > 0$$
where BEV is the (pessimistically estimated, under ambiguity) best estimate of expected value, ST is the mean of $\kappa_i$ draws from **sampling tools**, and $e(r)\sim N(0,\sigma_i)$ (zero under dominance). Under risk, $\text{BEV}_j(r)=\text{EV}_j$. There are four tools. The **unbiased** tool draws via a shared luck-level $x\sim U(0,1)$ percentile $F^{-1}(x)$ from each prospect's CDF (or, with feedback, from a randomly sampled past trial) — capturing regret minimization. The three **biased** tools are used with total probability $P_{\text{Bias}}(t)=\beta_i/(\beta_i+1+t^{\theta_i})$, which *decays with feedback* $t$. They are: **uniform** (each outcome equally likely → equal-weighting; explains Allais, rare-event overweighting, splitting); **contingent pessimism** (priority-heuristic-like: yields worst payoffs $\text{MIN}_A,\text{MIN}_B$ when $\text{SignMax}>0$ and $\text{RatioMin}\le\gamma_i$ → loss aversion, certainty effect, St. Petersburg risk aversion); and **sign** (positive draws replaced by range $R$, negatives by $-R$ → reflection, break-even, get-something). Ambiguity is handled by initializing $\text{BEV}_B(0)=(1-\varphi_i)(\text{UEV}_B+\text{EV}_A)/2+\varphi_i\cdot\text{MIN}_B$ (Gilboa-Schmeidler maxmin flavor) and updating toward the observed EV: $\text{BEV}_B(t{+}1)=(1-1/T)\text{BEV}_B(t)+(1/T)O_B(r)$. Six agent traits $\sigma_i,\kappa_i,\beta_i,\theta_i,\gamma_i,\varphi_i$ are drawn from uniform distributions parameterized by six fixed model parameters $\sigma,\kappa,\beta,\gamma,\varphi,\theta$ — of which only four ($\sigma,\kappa,\beta,\gamma$) are needed for risk-without-feedback. Parameters are fit by grid search minimizing MSD over 450 cell rates subject to reproducing all 14 phenomena; best fit $\sigma=7,\kappa=3,\beta=2.6,\gamma=.5,\varphi=.07,\theta=1$. Models are scored by **Mean Squared Deviation** (a proper scoring rule) and the interpretable **Equivalent Number of Observations (ENO)**.

## Data
Original experimental data: 125 + 60 + 29 (controls) + 160 participants (Israeli undergraduates at the Technion and Hebrew University), making 25 choices on each of up to 30 problems for real money (one trial randomly realized, excluding wealth effects). Roughly 94,000 lines of raw choice data plus the full problem sets and predictions were posted publicly (cpc2015 site). No field/secondary data.

## Key findings
1. **One space replicates all 14 anomalies** — but their magnitudes shrink versus the originals, and most *description* anomalies (certainty, reflection, rare-event overweighting, loss aversion) are eliminated or reversed by feedback. Feedback raises maximization when the EV-best option also pays best most trials, and impairs it otherwise (payoff-variability effect).
2. **BEAST predicts out-of-sample remarkably well**: test-set MSD $=0.0098$ (vs. $0.0073$ in-sample), correlations of $0.95$ between predicted and observed B-rates in both no-FB and with-FB blocks, and **ENO $=12.07$** (BEAST beats predicting the next subject from the mean of 12 prior subjects).
3. **Competition outcome**: 25 models submitted from five continents; all **12 top-ranked models were BEAST variants**, with 7 beating the baseline. The three prospect-theory-with-situation-specific-parameters submissions and the seven pure machine-learning submissions ranked lower. The **winning model, "Cohen's BEAST"** (MSD $=0.0089$, ENO $=13.51$), adds a tendency to avoid complex/ambiguous prospects when the payoff range is large and to favor them when narrow. A bootstrap shows the winner is *not* significantly better than 12 other models.
4. **Mechanistic claim**: the experience-description gap needs no separate process. Feedback simply shifts weight from the biased tools toward the unbiased tool, i.e. *raises sensitivity to the probability of immediate regret*. Individual differences across anomalies are weakly correlated (e.g., Allais vs. rare-event overweighting $r=0.0008$), supporting distinct tendencies rather than one latent risk trait.

## Contribution
Methodologically, it scales Kahneman & Tversky's "replicate-then-model" program from 4 to 14 phenomena and couples it with a prediction competition and random-problem sampling to make overfitting essentially impossible — a template for cumulative behavioral modeling. Substantively, it offers a *third* modeling paradigm beside subjective-weighting (PT/CPT) and simple heuristics: a **mental-sampling** account in which choice is the best EV estimate plus the additive output of sampling tools. It demonstrates that contradictory biases coexist without canceling, that the description-experience gap is quantitative rather than qualitative, and that EV considerations are "just-additional-estimates" weighed against tool outputs rather than overridden. BEAST became the foundation for subsequent CPC competitions and for psychological-forest / hybrid models of experiential choice.

## Limitations & open questions
- **BEAST is admittedly inelegant**: the exact implementation of the sampling tools contains *post-hoc* assumptions introduced to fit the data; the authors concede a simpler model may exist (none of 25 teams found one).
- **The winner is not significantly better than 12 rivals** — so the contribution is the *common structure* of leading models, not identification of the one "correct" model.
- **Individual differences and choice inertia are under-predicted**: observed inertia is $\approx0.87$ (no-FB) / $0.83$ (FB) but BEAST predicts $<0.65$, and cross-anomaly correlations are too low. The authors note this is fixable by changing the *timing* of the random draws (per-trial vs. at "birth") without affecting aggregate predictions, and leave that refinement to future work.
- **Population generalizability**: all data are Israeli undergraduates; they explicitly flag overgeneralization as a distinct risk from overfitting and call for future competitions on diverse populations.
- **Scope limits**: real (not hypothetical) payoffs only; bounded payoffs (so the genuine unbounded St. Petersburg game is untested); choice rather than bidding/pricing; two-option problems; only the extreme ambiguity cases Amb $\in\{0,1\}$.
- The *why* of the feedback effect is left open — two candidate mechanisms (pattern-seeking à la Plonsky et al. 2015, vs. reinforcement-driven strategy selection à la Rieskamp & Otto 2006/SSL) are offered but not adjudicated.

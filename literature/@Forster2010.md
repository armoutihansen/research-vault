## Summary
This is not a research paper but a German university mathematics textbook: Otto Forster's *Analysis 2* (9th, revised edition, Vieweg+Teubner 2011), the second volume of a standard analysis course for mathematics and physics students. It covers two large blocks: differential calculus of functions of several real variables in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and an introduction to ordinary differential equations (ODEs). Coverage note: a 233-page textbook in German; I read the front matter, both prefaces, and the full table of contents (Inhaltsverzeichnis), and sampled body sections rather than ingesting all chapters.

## Research question
None in the research sense. As a textbook, its pedagogical aim (stated in the Vorwort) is to teach multivariable differential calculus and the elementary theory of ordinary differential equations while avoiding excessive abstraction and grounding the general theory in many concrete, physics-relevant examples. The author deliberately defers multidimensional integration to a later (third-semester) course, since *Analysis 2* is typically taught in the shorter summer semester.

## Method / identification
Standard rigorous mathematical exposition: definitions, theorems, and proofs, organized into 16 paragraphs (sections) across two chapters. The pedagogical method emphasizes motivation before generality — e.g., the ODE chapter now opens with elementarily solvable equations before stating the general existence-and-uniqueness theorem, and a dedicated paragraph on simple (linear and nonlinear) second-order equations precedes the general solution theory for linear equations with constant coefficients. The 9th edition rearranges the first three paragraphs and adds, as an application of compactness, a proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra (Fundamentalsatz der Algebra).

## Key findings
Not findings but the canonical theorems and topics presented, by paragraph:

Chapter I — Differential calculus in $\mathbb{R}^n$:
1. Topology of metric spaces;
2. Limits and continuity;
3. Compactness (with the added proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra);

5. Partial derivatives;

8. Implicit functions (Implicit Function Theorem);
9. Submanifolds of $\mathbb{R}^n$ (Untermannigfaltigkeiten);
10. Integrals depending on a parameter (parameterabhängige Integrale).

Chapter II — Ordinary differential equations:
11. Elementary solution methods (incl. separation of variables, Trennung der Variablen);
12. Existence-and-uniqueness theorem (Existenz- und Eindeutigkeitssatz, Picard–Lindelöf type);
13. Linear differential equations;
14. Second-order differential equations;
15. Linear differential equations with constant coefficients;
16. Systems of linear differential equations with constant coefficients.

Representative formal content includes the total-differentiability characterization
$$f(x+h) = f(x) + Df(x)\,h + o(\lVert h\rVert),$$
the multivariate Taylor expansion, and the existence/uniqueness of solutions to the initial-value problem $y' = f(x,y),\ y(x_0)=y_0$ under a Lipschitz condition on $f$.

## Contribution
A long-standing, widely used reference textbook (1st edition 1976; this is the 9th edition, 2011) in the German-language mathematics curriculum. Its contribution is pedagogical and expository: a compact, example-driven, physics-aware second-semester analysis course. Revisions across editions added TeX typesetting, a paragraph on differentiable submanifolds of $\mathbb{R}^n$, a motivating reordering of the ODE chapter, second-order equation material relevant to physics, and (9th edition) the compactness-based proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.

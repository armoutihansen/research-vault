> [!abstract] Abstract
> Understanding the properties of intrinsic information preference is important for predicting behavior in many domains including finance and health. We present evidence that intrinsic demand for information about the future is increasing in expected future consumption utility. In the first experiment subjects may resolve a lottery now or later. The information is useless for decision making but the larger the reward, the more likely subjects are to pay to resolve the lottery early. In the second experiment subjects may pay to avoid being tested for HSV-1 and the more highly feared HSV-2. Subjects are three times more likely to avoid testing for HSV-2, suggesting that more aversive outcomes lead to more information avoidance. We also find that intrinsic information demand is negatively correlated with positive affect and ambiguity aversion.

## Summary
Two incentivized lab experiments show that people have *intrinsic* (non-instrumental) information preferences that scale with the expected consumption utility of the future outcome the information is about — an "information-consumption complementarity." People pay for useless good news (resolving a big-prize lottery early) and pay to avoid bad news (testing for the more-feared HSV-2 strain). Demand for information is higher the better the anticipated outcome and avoidance higher the worse it is. A secondary finding links higher current positive affect to lower information demand. Both patterns violate expected-utility theory and are best matched by Tasoff & Madarász's (2009) attention-based model of anticipatory utility.

## Research question
Does the intrinsic (non-instrumental) demand for information about a future event increase in the expected consumption utility of that event? I.e., do people seek information to enrich pleasant anticipation ("fantasy") and avoid it to suppress unpleasant anticipation ("dread"), with the strength of each scaling in how good/bad the news is?

## Method / identification
Within-subject lab study ($N=194$), four parts in randomized order; analysis focuses on each subject's *first* experiment to avoid spillover. Identification rests on stripping away instrumental value so any willingness-to-pay reveals intrinsic preference.

- **Lottery Experiment (good-news domain).** Two sealed envelopes, each a $10\%$ chance of money: "Small" ($\$10$) and "Big" ($\$100$). Information is useless — opening only resolves the lottery 20–40 minutes earlier with no decision consequence. Six binary $\$0.75$-vs-$\$0.25$ choices (Table 1) reveal whether a subject forgoes $\$0.50$ to open early, to delay opening, or to choose which envelope to open. EU predicts always choosing the $\$0.75$ option. The information-consumption complementarity (ICC) predicts more "open-early" demand for Big than Small.
- **STD Experiment (bad-news domain).** After a detailed clinical description of HSV-1 (cold sores) and HSV-2 (genital herpes, perceived worse by $\sim 94\%$), subjects choose in six $\$11$-vs-$\$1$ binary items (Table 2) whether to forgo $\$10$ to *avoid* a free, useful blood test. A $\sim 1/10$ chance (randomized at session level; 18 subjects) of an actual phlebotomist blood draw makes choices real; results confidential, doctor-communicated. EU forbids paying to avoid useful information; ICC predicts more avoidance of HSV-2 than HSV-1.
- **Positive-affect treatment (between-subjects).** Humorous video (Robin Williams) vs. neutral nature footage, with re-induction between segments; affect measured via PANAS. Tests whether higher current consumption utility lowers information demand.
- **Auxiliary elicitations.** Multiple-price lists for ambiguity aversion (Sutter et al. 2010), loss aversion (Gächter et al. 2007), present-bias parameters $\beta,\delta$, a coping-style inventory, and an exit survey on beliefs and stigma.

## Data
Original experimental data: 194 subjects (target 200), sexually-active 18–25-year-olds recruited via the CGU Center for Neuroeconomic Studies, run over 52 sessions in Qualtrics; mean payment $\$26.35$. Control $n=97$, treatment $n=97$.

## Key findings
- **Widespread intrinsic preferences (Lottery).** $83.4\%$ forgo $\$0.50$ to affect timing of useless information; no transitivity violations.
- **ICC, good-news (H1 confirmed).** Pay-to-open-early demand rises from $18.4\%$ (Small) to $41.8\%$ (Big), a 2.3-fold increase ($p=0.0002$). In the direct head-to-head, $59.2\%$ pay to open Big over Small vs. $6.1\%$ the reverse ($p<0.0001$). Some subjects also pay to *delay* (preference for suspense), but delay is lower for Big — also consistent with ICC.
- **ICC, bad-news (H2 confirmed).** $15.6\%$ forgo $\$10$ to avoid an HSV test; avoidance triples from $5.2\%$ (HSV-1) to $15.6\%$ (HSV-2), $p=0.009$. All HSV-1 avoiders are HSV-2 avoiders but not conversely. Avoidance persists despite a $\$40$-value free test plus $\$10$ and zero transaction cost.
- **Stated vs. revealed.** $18.7\%$ say they would rather not know; the dominant stated reason for avoiding is stress/anxiety ($64.7\%$, rising to $85.7\%$ among avoiders) — a direct admission of anticipatory emotion.
- **Affect (H3, correlational).** The video manipulation was too weak to identify a causal effect, but self-reported positive affect correlates with lower information demand in both experiments.
- **Correlates.** Ambiguity aversion positively correlates with STD-test avoidance; no robust correlation with loss aversion, gender, or across the two experiments (preferences are domain-specific, not a fixed personal trait).

## Contribution
First clean, incentivized evidence for the information-consumption complementarity in both the gain and loss domains, with information usefulness held to zero (lottery) or biased *against* the finding (STD). First un-confounded demonstration of costly information avoidance in a high-prevalence health domain. Theoretically, the joint pattern (seeking scales with good news, avoidance with bad news, plus the affect link) is rationalized only by Tasoff & Madarász (2009); the authors note Eliaz & Spiegler (2006, Prop. 2) prove Caplin–Leahy anticipatory utility with Bayesian beliefs *cannot* generate the ICC, and Karlsson–Loewenstein–Seppi (2009) generates it only via reference-point-dependent news.

## Limitations & open questions
- The mood manipulation was too weak to identify the *causal* effect of present consumption utility on information demand — the affect result is only correlational. (Explicit open problem.)
- Experiment was not designed to discriminate among anticipatory-utility models; the authors flag testing KLS vs. Tasoff–Madarász via the divergent predictions for "good news on a bad outcome / bad news on a good outcome" as future work.
- Whether non-Bayesian belief updating can reconcile Caplin–Leahy with the ICC (per Eliaz–Spiegler) "remains to be explored."
- External validity: cleanly identifying these preferences outside the lab in high-stakes domains (cancer screening, financial exposure) is left as a challenge.
- Affective-forecasting question: do people overestimate the pain of aversive information (adaptation), so that avoidance is itself a forecasting error? Open.
- The lab environment (tiny stakes, 20–40 min delay) likely *understates* real-world information seeking.

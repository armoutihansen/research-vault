It is a complete, axiomatic introduction to the differential and integral calculus of one real (and complex) variable, aimed at first-year students of mathematics, computer science, the natural sciences, and engineering.

## Summary
Forster & Lindemann's *Analysis 1* is a textbook, not a research paper: a self-contained, theorem-and-proof development of single-variable real and complex analysis, building from the Peano-style induction principle and the field/order/completeness axioms of $\mathbb{R}$ up through sequences and series, continuity, differentiation, the Riemann integral, the fundamental theorem of calculus, the Gamma function, uniform convergence, Taylor series, and Fourier series. It is one of the canonical German-language first-semester analysis texts (in print since 1975). Because the source is a 465-page textbook, this note is composed from a targeted reading of the front matter, the full table of contents, and the opening chapter; it characterises scope and structure rather than summarising every proof.

The pedagogical aim is to give a rigorous, axiomatic foundation for the calculus of one variable: starting from a small set of axioms for the real numbers, derive the entire apparatus of limits, continuity, differentiation, and integration with complete proofs, while supplying worked examples and exercises suitable for exam preparation.

## Method / identification
The method is the standard axiomatic-deductive method of pure mathematics. The text is organised as a chain of definitions, theorems ($S\ddot{a}tze$), and proofs. It opens with the principle of complete induction: to prove a statement $A(n)$ for all integers $n \ge n_0$, it suffices to show the base case $A(n_0)$ and the induction step "$A(n) \Rightarrow A(n+1)$". The real numbers are then introduced not by construction but by an axiom system in three groups — the field axioms (Körper-Axiome), the order axioms (Anordnungs-Axiome), and the completeness axiom (Vollständigkeits-Axiom), the last typically in the form that every bounded monotone sequence converges (equivalently, the supremum/Cauchy formulation). From completeness the existence of roots, convergence criteria for series, and the limit theory are derived. The integral is developed as the Riemann integral via upper and lower sums, and differentiation is connected to integration through the fundamental theorem. The 13th edition adds reworked and expanded material and exercises relative to earlier editions.

## Key findings
The "findings" are the standard theorems of single-variable analysis, presented and proved across 23 chapters: (1) the principle of complete induction; (2-3) the field and order axioms of $\mathbb{R}$; (4) sequences and limits; (5) the completeness axiom; (6) existence of $n$-th roots; (7) convergence criteria for series; (8) the exponential series and its functional equation; (9) point sets (open/closed, accumulation points); (10) functions and continuity, including the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition; (11) theorems on continuous functions — the intermediate value theorem (Zwischenwertsatz) and the extreme value theorem on compact intervals; (12) the logarithm and general power; (13) the complex exponential function; (14) the trigonometric functions defined analytically; (15) differentiation and the rules of the derivative; (16) local extrema, the mean value theorem (Mittelwertsatz), and convexity; (17) numerical solution of equations (e.g. Newton's method); (18) the Riemann integral via upper/lower sums; (19) the fundamental theorem of calculus linking integration and differentiation, plus integration techniques; (20) improper integrals and the Gamma function; (21) uniform convergence of sequences of functions; (22) Taylor series; (23) Fourier series. An appendix collects the axioms of $\mathbb{R}$, a list of symbols, and an index.

## Contribution
A standard, durable pedagogical contribution: a compact yet rigorous and complete reference for first-semester analysis in the German curriculum, valued for the economy and clarity of its proofs. Its lasting role is as a teaching and exam-preparation text rather than a source of new mathematics.

## Limitations & open questions
None stated — a textbook poses no open research problems. As scope limitations, it is confined to one real (and complex) variable: multivariable calculus, measure-theoretic (Lebesgue) integration, and functional analysis are outside its remit (these are the subjects of the companion volumes *Analysis 2* and *Analysis 3*). It assumes the axioms of $\mathbb{R}$ rather than constructing the reals from $\mathbb{Q}$. For the research vault this entry is primarily a reference for the mathematical machinery (limits, continuity, differentiation, the Riemann integral, Taylor and Fourier series) underlying applied and quantitative work, not a project-idea hook.

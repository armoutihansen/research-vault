> [!abstract] Abstract
> Recent research has shown that infants are more likely to engage with in-group over out-group members. However, it is not known whether infants' learning is influenced by a model's group membership. This study investigated whether 14-month-olds (N = 66) selectively imitate and adopt the preferences of in-group versus out-group members. Infants watched an adult tell a story either in their native language (in-group) or a foreign language (out-group). The adult then demonstrated a novel action (imitation task) and chose 1 of 2 objects (preference task). Infants did not show selectivity in the preference task, but they imitated the in-group model more faithfully than the out-group model. This suggests that cultural learning is beginning to be truly cultural by 14 months of age.

## Summary
A between-subjects experiment with 66 German 14-month-olds tests whether a model's group membership, signalled purely by spoken language (native German = in-group vs. foreign Russian = out-group), shapes early social learning. Infants were more likely to copy a novel, causally opaque action when the model had spoken their native language (43.9% of trials) than a foreign one (25.8%), but did not differentially adopt the model's object *preferences*. The authors read selective imitation as evidence that imitation is socially flexible rather than automatic, and that "truly cultural" learning - copying how "we" in our group do things - has begun by the first birthday.

## Research question
Does an infant's social learning depend on whether the demonstrating model is perceived as an in-group versus an out-group member? Concretely, by 14 months do infants (a) imitate a novel action more faithfully from an in-group model, and (b) selectively adopt an in-group model's object preferences? The study positions itself against two competing accounts of childhood imitation: the *automatic/overimitation* account (Gergely & Csibra; Lyons et al.; Whiten et al.), on which children copy causally opaque actions more or less indiscriminately because they are normally surrounded by in-group members, versus a *social/affiliative* account (Over & Carpenter; Uzgiris; Nielsen et al.), on which imitation is flexible and modulated by social context including group membership.

## Method / identification
This is an experimental developmental-psychology study, not a formal model; "identification" rests on the between-subjects manipulation of group membership. Design: a $2$ (condition: in-group vs. out-group, between subjects) layout, with two paired task types administered within subject - a pair of *imitation* tasks and a pair of *preference* tasks - and full counterbalancing of task-pair order, within-pair order, chosen object, and object side. Half the infants ($n=33$) were assigned to each condition.

Group membership was operationalized solely through language. The *same bilingual male model* told a ~30 s familiarization story in either German (in-group) or Russian (out-group), with intonation, prosody, and facial expression matched across conditions; four stories (varying protagonist and location) were used and counterbalanced. Holding the model constant means language was the only difference between conditions - the basis for attributing any behavioural difference to perceived group membership.

After each familiarization video, all infants saw the *same* silent test video (so the test stimulus is identical across conditions). Imitation tasks used a head-touch task (turning on a lamp by touching it with the forehead three times, after Meltzoff, 1988) and a sit-touch task (illuminating a box by sitting on it, after Buttelmann et al., 2007) - both unusual, causally opaque actions. Preference tasks showed the model silently choosing one of two novel objects while displaying satisfaction (nodding, caressing it). The experimenter was absent during videos and the response phase. Response windows: 60 s (head-touch), 120 s (sit-touch), 30 s (preference).

Coding: a condition-blind observer scored each task $0/1$ for copying the action (head touch; sitting/knee on box) or choosing the same object; infants got a $0$-$2$ score per task type. Only infants completing all four trials were included, giving $264$ trials. A second blind observer coded 100% of trials with excellent reliability (Cohen's $\kappa = .90$ imitation, $1.00$ preference). Nonparametric tests (Mann-Whitney $U$ between conditions, Wilcoxon and binomial against chance) with two-tailed $p$ throughout. Looking times to familiarization and test videos were coded to rule out an attention confound, analyzed via a $2\times2\times2$ (task $\times$ condition $\times$ phase) repeated-measures ANOVA.

## Data
Original lab data: 66 monolingual-German 14-month-olds (30 girls; $M = 14$ months 0 days) recruited from a child-development database in a mid-sized German city. Additional infants were tested but excluded for fussiness ($n=3$), refusal to touch objects ($n=5$), experimenter error ($n=1$), prior familiarity with apparatus ($n=5$), or not completing all four tasks ($n=21$). Final dataset = $264$ trials.

## Key findings
- **Selective imitation (the headline result).** Infants copied the unusual action more in the in-group condition ($43.9\%$ of trials, $SD = 34.8\%$) than the out-group condition ($25.8\%$, $SD = 30.9\%$), Mann-Whitney $U = 390.5$, $p = .03$. The pattern held in each task separately but reached significance only for head-touch ($46\%$ vs. $21\%$; $\chi^2(1,N=66) = 4.36$, $p = .04$), not sit-touch ($42\%$ vs. $30\%$; $\chi^2 = 1.05$, $p = .31$).
- **No selective preference adoption.** Same-object choice did not differ by condition - $42.4\%$ (in-group) vs. $57.6\%$ (out-group), $U = 667.0$, $p = .09$ - and matched chance in both conditions (Wilcoxon $Z = 1.21$, $p = .23$ each). Infants showed no group-level preference for either object in a pair.
- **Not an attention/effort artifact.** *All* infants in both conditions succeeded in illuminating the lamps (often using their hands), so out-group infants were equally engaged and clearly learned the goal - they just copied the *specific manner* less. Looking time did not differ by condition during test videos ($F(1,58)=1.03$, $p=.31$), and imitation scores did not correlate with familiarization looking time ($r_s = .128$, $p = .32$). Infants did look less during familiarization in the out-group condition (Condition $\times$ Phase interaction, $F(1,58)=8.45$, $p=.005$), but this did not translate into a test-phase attention gap.
- **Interpretation.** Selective imitation of the in-group model - even in a between-subjects design with no contrasting in-group model present (unlike Kinzler et al.) - indicates that by 14 months infants use a model's group-relevant characteristics to guide imitation; cultural learning has begun to be "truly cultural."

## Contribution
First demonstration that *infants'* social learning is modulated by a model's group membership, pushing the onset of group-sensitive cultural learning down to ~14 months (prior selective-trust work by Kinzler et al. concerned 4-5-year-olds; earlier infant work showed only differential looking/object-taking, not differential *learning*). Empirically favours social/affiliative accounts of imitation over purely automatic-overimitation accounts: faithful copying is selective and context-dependent, so automatic accounts must at minimum add the provision that the model is an in-group member. The dissociation between imitation (selective) and preference adoption (not selective) is itself a contribution, suggesting infants may treat preferences as individual and subjective rather than group-normative.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit about several open problems - useful project hooks:
- **Mechanism is unidentified.** Because language was the only manipulated cue, it is "too early to say" whether infants treated it as a *group* marker or reacted at a more individual level - to unfamiliarity with the language, felt dissimilarity ("this guy is not like me"), or lack of comprehension ("I do not understand this guy"). Familiarity, similarity, and comprehension are all normally confounded with group membership.
- **Proposed remedy: minimal-group designs.** Future work could use minimal-group manipulations (cf. Dunham, Baron, & Carey, 2011; Over & Carpenter, 2012, with 3-6-year-olds) that hold language, familiarity, and other variables constant across in- and out-group members - though the effect might then only appear in older children.
- **Level of social cognition is open.** What level of group understanding 14-month-olds possess is unresolved; the authors note even the adult literature debates whether group cognition reduces to reasoning about individuals vs. groups (Postmes, Haslam, & Swaab, 2005).
- **Null on preferences is ambiguous.** Whether infants genuinely see preferences as subjective, or whether the task lacked sensitivity, is not settled; the finding sits within an already-mixed literature on infants' sensitivity to others' preferences.
- The sit-touch task did not individually reach significance, and the high attrition rate (esp. $n=21$ non-completers) is a sample-robustness caveat the authors address by noting the pattern is unchanged when non-completers are included.

## Connections
Builds directly on the Kinzler program on language as a marker of social cognition: 6-month-olds look longer at native-language speakers and 10-month-olds prefer objects from them (Kinzler, Dupoux, & Spelke, 2007), and 4-5-year-olds selectively endorse native-accented demonstrations (Kinzler, Corriveau, & Harris, 2011). It extends affiliation-based accounts of imitation (Over & Carpenter, 2009, 2012; Uzgiris, 1981; Nielsen et al., 2008) from the dyadic to the group level, and stands in deliberate contrast to natural-pedagogy/overimitation accounts (Csibra & Gergely, 2009; Gergely & Csibra, 2006; Lyons, Young, & Keil, 2007; Whiten et al., 2009). The imitation paradigms reuse classic methods (Meltzoff, 1988, head-touch; Buttelmann et al., 2007, rational/sit imitation), and the authors' own prior work on how a model's reliability and age affect 14-month-olds' imitation (Zmyj, Buttelmann, Carpenter, & Daum, 2010; Zmyj et al., 2012) situates group membership alongside other model characteristics that gate selective copying. Within an economics-of-preferences vault, the paper is a developmental counterpoint on the *origins* of in-group/out-group discrimination and social learning, complementary to minimal-group and social-identity work referenced for older children (Dunham, Baron, & Carey, 2011).

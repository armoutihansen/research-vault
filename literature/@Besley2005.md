> [!abstract] Abstract
> A unifying theme in the literature on organizations such as public bureaucracies and private nonprofits is the importance of mission, as opposed to profit, as an organizational goal. Such mission-oriented organizations are frequently staffed by motivated agents who subscribe to the mission. This paper studies incentives in such contexts and emphasizes the role of matching the mission preferences of principals and agents in increasing organizational efficiency. Matching economizes on the need for high-powered incentives. It can also, however, entrench bureaucratic conservatism and resistance to innovations. The framework developed in this paper is applied to school competition, incentives in the public sector and in private nonprofits, and the interdependence of incentives and productivity between the private for-profit sector and the mission-oriented sector through occupational choice.

## Summary
Besley and Ghatak build a tractable principal-agent model in which some principals and agents are "motivated": they derive a non-pecuniary benefit from the success of a project whose *mission* matches their own preferences. The central message is that **selection (matching) substitutes for incentives**. When a principal and agent share a mission, the agent's intrinsic motivation acts as a perfect substitute for monetary bonuses, so well-matched, mission-oriented organizations achieve high effort with low-powered incentive pay. Embedding this in a competitive matching market, they show stable matches are assortative on mission, which raises productivity but, on the flip side, breeds conservatism and resistance to innovations that raise financial returns while lowering motivation. The framework rationalizes a range of stylized facts about nonprofits, schools, and public bureaucracies.

## Research question
How should incentives be structured in mission-oriented production (nonprofits, public bureaucracies, schools), and what is the role of competition between providers? Specifically: when agents care intrinsically about an organization's mission, how does matching principals and agents on mission preferences affect incentive pay, effort, and organizational productivity, relative to a profit-oriented benchmark?

## Method / identification
A pure theory paper. A "firm" is a risk-neutral principal plus a risk-neutral agent. The agent exerts unobservable, non-contractible effort $e$, which equals the probability of the high outcome $Y_H=1$ (vs. $Y_L=0$), at cost $c(e)=e^2/2$. The agent has no wealth, so a **limited-liability constraint** requires a minimum consumption $\underline{w}\ge 0$ in all states; this is the only departure from first best.

There are three types of principals $i\in\{0,1,2\}$ and agents $j\in\{0,1,2\}$, perfectly observable. A successful project pays principal $i$ a payoff $\pi_i>0$; type 0 is purely pecuniary, while $\pi_1=\pi_2\equiv\hat\pi$ (so matching is purely **horizontal**, not vertical). An agent of type $j$ matched with principal $i$ obtains a non-pecuniary success benefit
$$\theta_{ij}=\begin{cases}0 & i=0 \text{ and/or } j=0\\ \underline{\theta} & i,j\in\{1,2\},\, i\neq j\\ \bar\theta & i,j\in\{1,2\},\, i=j\end{cases}\qquad \bar\theta>\underline{\theta}\ge 0.$$
Sector $i=1,2$ is "mission-oriented", $i=0$ is "profit-oriented". Contracts have a fixed wage $w_{ij}$ and a bonus $b_{ij}$ paid on success. The principal solves $\max_{b_{ij},w_{ij}} (\pi_i-b_{ij})e_{ij}-w_{ij}$ subject to limited liability ($b_{ij}+w_{ij}\ge\underline{w}$, $w_{ij}\ge\underline{w}$), participation ($u^a_{ij}\ge\bar u_j$), and incentive compatibility, which reduces to $e_{ij}=b_{ij}+\theta_{ij}$. Motivation thus adds directly to the bonus in driving effort.

The analysis proceeds in three steps: (1) optimal contract for an exogenous match given reservation payoff $\bar u_j$; (2) endogenous missions, letting the principal choose a mission $x\in[0,1]$ that shifts $\theta_{ij}$; (3) a frictionless **stable-matching** market (à la Roth-Sotomayor) with a one-to-one matching function $\mu$, where reservation payoffs are endogenized by competition across the mission-oriented and profit-oriented sectors, under both unemployment ($n_{a0}>n_{p0}$) and full employment ($n_{a0}<n_{p0}$). Proofs use envelope-theorem arguments and a non-standard assortative-matching argument (the authors note standard NTU results of Legros-Newman do not apply to horizontal sorting).

## Data
None - this is a theoretical paper. The applications section discusses corroborating empirical evidence from others: Weisbrod (1988) on sorting and diversity in nonprofits; Ballou and Weisbrod (2003) and Arnould et al. (2000) on hospitals (for-profits use higher-powered bonus pay; HMO/managed-care competition strengthens the pay-performance link in nonprofit hospitals) - a finding the model explains as a rise in $\pi_0$ tightening the participation constraint.

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 (optimal contract).** The fixed wage is set at subsistence ($w^*_{ij}=\underline{w}$). The bonus is $b^*_{ij}=\max\{0,(\pi_i-\theta_{ij})/2\}$ when the outside option is low, and $b^*_{ij}=\sqrt{2(\bar u_j-\underline{w})}-\theta_{ij}$ when it is high; effort is $e^*_{ij}=b^*_{ij}+\theta_{ij}$. Three cases: (1) if the agent is more motivated than the principal and the outside option is low, *no incentive pay* ($b^*=0$); (2) if the principal is more motivated, $b^*=\tfrac12(\pi_i-\theta_{ij})$; (3) if the outside option is high, $b^*$ is the market level discounted by motivation. **Motivation is a perfect substitute for the bonus, so more motivated agents receive lower incentive pay.**
- **Corollary 1.** In the profit sector ($\theta_{0j}=0$) agents must always be given a bonus; Case 1 cannot arise.
- **Corollaries 2-3.** Holding reservation payoffs equal, effort is higher and the bonus lower when agent and principal types match ($i=j$). Consequently, across mission-oriented organizations, **incentive pay and productivity are negatively correlated** - a pure *selection effect* that looks paradoxical but reflects heterogeneity in agent preferences.
- **Proposition 2.** Any stable matching is **assortative**: $\mu(p_i)=a_i$. Assortatively matched pairs always generate more surplus than mismatched ones.
- **Propositions 3-4 (competition).** Competition operates through two channels. A **matching effect** reduces contract heterogeneity and raises productivity in the mission sector. An **outside-option effect** pins down reservation payoffs: under full employment, a sufficiently productive profit sector ($\pi_0+\sqrt{\pi_0^2-4\underline{w}}>\xi$) makes the participation constraint bind and forces *more* incentive pay (raising productivity via pay). Under unemployment, incentives in the two sectors are "unhinged", competition acts only through matching, and employed agents earn rents.
- **Substantive implications.** Incentives are systematically lower-powered in mission-oriented production (by at least $\bar\theta$, and zero if $\bar\theta>\hat\pi$) - a clean derivation, since technology is identical across sectors. Endogenous missions: a contractible "compromise" mission $x^*_{ij}=(\alpha_i+\alpha_j)/2$ raises $\theta_{ij}$ and substitutes for pay. **Conservatism**: a mission-oriented firm innovates only if $\pi_i+\theta_{ij}$ rises, so financially worthwhile innovations that reduce $\theta_{ij}$ are resisted. **Bureaucracy**: replacing a principal (e.g., via elections, Thatcher-era mission change) demotivates incumbents until rematching occurs, explaining transitional morale declines and short-run vs. long-run differences; rising private wages ($\pi_0$) in tight labor markets rationalize New Public Management's push for public-sector incentives.

## Contribution
Provides the first clean, unified contracting framework for collective-good provision that cuts across the public-private divide and treats **mission preferences and selection** - rather than incentives alone - as the central lever. It formalizes how intrinsic motivation substitutes for monetary incentives, why mission-oriented organizations use low-powered pay, why diversity of missions is productively valuable (not just for consumer choice), and why such organizations resist change. It explains otherwise-puzzling empirical patterns in hospital compensation, and offers a foundation for debates on nonprofit status, decentralization, donor influence ("mission drift"), and public-sector pay reform.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors explicitly flag several open problems: (i) understand how the existence of motivated agents affects the **choice of organizational form** (e.g., why and when nonprofit status is adopted); (ii) the analysis "cries out for a more complete treatment of the sources of motivation" and the possibility that motivation is **crowded in or out** by the principal's actions - here the level of intrinsic motivation is treated as exogenously given; (iii) develop the **content of mission choice** and how it interacts with governance and market pressures, since the paper treats full contractibility and non-contractibility of the mission as the two extreme cases; (iv) the sharp dichotomy between mission-oriented and profit-oriented sectors is maintained even though private firms frequently adopt missions. Other modeling restrictions: matching is purely horizontal ($\pi_1=\pi_2$); types are perfectly observable (no screening); the level of motivation is independent of monetary incentives (unlike the behavioral-economics literature); private benefits are contractible (unlike Hart-Holmström 2002); donors/external finance are only sketched informally.

## Connections
The paper is the canonical theoretical statement linking intrinsic motivation, mission, and incentive design. It builds on Tirole (1994) on the internal organization of government and on the "compensating differentials" tradition (Rosen 1986). It is part of a wave on motivation outside the standard private-goods model: [[@Benabou2003|Benabou and Tirole (2003)]] on intrinsic/extrinsic motivation, Akerlof and Kranton (2005) on identity in organizations, Francois (2000) on public-service motivation, Murdock (2002), Dixit (2001, 2002 survey), Prendergast (2001), Delfgaauw and Dur (2004), and Seabright (2003). On non-congruent objectives it relates to Crawford and Sobel (1982) and [[@Aghion1997|Aghion and Tirole (1997)]], but emphasizes *selection* over incentives. The matching machinery follows Roth and Sotomayor (1989); the authors contrast their horizontal-sorting argument with NTU assortative-matching results of Legros and Newman (2003/2007). On nonprofits it engages Weisbrod (1988) and Glaeser and Shleifer (2001) (nonprofit status as a response to contracting imperfections). Companion and follow-on work includes Besley and Ghatak (2001) on public vs. private ownership, their (2006) JEEA piece on sorting and school competition, and their (2008) "Status Incentives", plus Ghatak and Mueller (2011) on not-for-profits and motivated agents. The multitasking (Holmström-Milgrom 1991) and career-concerns (Dewatripont et al. 1999; Alesina-Tabellini) strands are the main alternative accounts of low-powered public-sector incentives, synthesized in Acemoglu et al. (2003).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper investigates the effect of early exposure to neighborhood crime on subsequent criminal behavior of youth exploiting a unique natural experiment between 1986 and 1998 when refugee immigrants to Denmark were assigned to neighborhoods quasi-randomly. We find strong evidence that the share of young people convicted for crimes, in particular violent crimes, in the neighborhood increases convictions of male assignees later in life. No such effects are found for other measures of neighborhood crime including the rate of committed crimes. Our findings suggest social interaction as a key channel through which neighborhood crime is linked to individual criminal behavior.

## Summary
Damm and Dustmann exploit Denmark's 1986-1998 quasi-random dispersal of refugee families across municipalities to estimate the causal effect of childhood exposure to neighborhood crime on a youth's own later criminal convictions. They find that the share of convicted young criminals living in the assignment municipality at the time of assignment raises the probability and number of later convictions for assigned boys (but not girls), whereas conventional rates of committed/reported crime have no effect. Because it is the share of resident young convicts (especially violent and co-ethnic offenders) rather than the volume of crime that matters, they interpret social interaction as the key transmission channel rather than correlated institutional or contextual effects.

## Research question
Does growing up in a high-crime neighborhood causally increase a young person's own subsequent criminal behavior, and if so, through which mechanism? The authors specifically aim to separate social-interaction effects from Manski's "correlated effects" (shared institutions, policing, neighborhood culture) and contextual effects.

## Method / identification
The identification strategy is a natural experiment: between 1986 and 1998 the Danish Refugee Council assigned approved refugees to counties and then municipalities proportional to local population, using only questionnaire data (birth date, marital status, number of children, nationality) and explicitly not education, criminal record, or income, with no location wishes honored and no face-to-face meetings. Assignment is therefore quasi-random conditional on the observed questionnaire characteristics. The authors validate this with balancing regressions of assignment-area crime measures on household-head characteristics (Table 1), finding education dummies jointly insignificant.

The core linear probability / count model for child $i$ assigned to municipality $r$ in year $t$ is

$$y_{itr} = a + a^{M} G_i C_{itr} + a^{F}(1 - G_i) C_{itr} + X_{it}'b + T_t + e_{itr},$$

where $y_{itr}$ indicates (or counts) a conviction for a crime committed in age range 15-21 (also split 15-17, 18-21), $C_{itr}$ is the standardized area youth crime conviction rate at assignment, $G_i = 1$ for males, $X_{it}$ holds individual/family controls, and $T_t$ are assignment-year dummies. A richer specification adds a vector $A_{tr}$ of two municipality characteristic sets (socioeconomic: log inhabitants, poverty rate, immigrant share, teacher hours per pupil, pupils-per-teacher; policing: crime detection rate, police per 1,000) and municipality fixed effects $R_r$:

$$y_{itr} = b + b^{M}_1 G_i C_{itr} + b^{F}_2 (1 - G_i) C_{itr} + b_3 G_i + X_{it}'b + A_{tr}'d + T_t + R_r + e_{itr}.$$

The prolonged multi-cohort assignment is essential: it identifies the crime effect from within-municipality cross-cohort variation in arrival-year crime rates, letting the authors absorb all time-invariant municipality characteristics via $R_r$. Standard errors are clustered by assignment municipality.

## Data
Danish administrative registers linked by unique ID across the Central Police Register (charges/convictions for the full population), demographic Administrative Registers, and Educational Institution Register, for 1980-2006. The estimation sample is 4,425 refugee children (55 percent male) from eight source countries (Lebanon, Iran, Iraq, Somalia, Sri Lanka, Vietnam, Afghanistan, Ethiopia) who arrived before age 15 (average age at assignment about nine) with at least one parent and are observed every year to age 21. Convictions are recorded from age 15 (Denmark has no juvenile justice system; minimum age of criminal responsibility is 15). Crime is grouped into property, violent (incl. sexual assault), drug, and other offenses, excluding traffic. The headline regressor is the "youth crime conviction rate": the share of residents aged 15-25 convicted of a crime committed in year $t$ in municipality $r$.

## Key findings
- For males, a one standard deviation higher area youth crime conviction rate at assignment raises the probability of a later (15-21) conviction by about 2 percentage points (rising toward 4 percentage points with municipality fixed effects) and the number of convictions by roughly 7-12 percent; estimates are stable across control sets and robust to dropping the five largest and 22 largest municipalities. There are no effects for females.
- Crime-type specificity (Table 5): it is the youth violent crime conviction rate that drives the effect; youth property and drug crime conviction rates have no significant effect.
- Mechanism evidence (Table 6) against correlated/contextual effects: conventional measures (reported crimes per capita, reported violent crimes per 10,000) have small, insignificant effects, and including them alongside the conviction-rate measure leaves the conviction-rate coefficient essentially unchanged. Overall (all-age) conviction rates and beat-police measures do not matter; only the share of young convicted criminals living in the area matters.
- Social-interaction signatures (Table 7): only youth (not older) conviction rates matter, and the youth crime conviction rate of co-nationals adds about 2 percentage points (roughly 4 percent) over and above the overall youth rate, while other immigrant groups add nothing. Susceptibility is greatest for assignment ages 10-14.
- Parents of sample children show no neighborhood-crime effect, ruling out intergenerational transmission and confirming it is youth who are affected.
- Longer-term (Table 8): higher assignment-area youth violent crime conviction rates reduce the probability of being employed/active by age 23-25 for men (a one SD increase lowers employment probability by roughly 2.8 percent), with no clear effect on educational attainment.

## Contribution
The paper provides rare causal evidence on early exposure to neighborhood crime, addressing endogenous neighborhood selection through a large multi-cohort quasi-random assignment that uniquely permits conditioning on municipality fixed effects. Its key methodological departure is distinguishing the share of convicted criminals living in a neighborhood from the rate of committed/reported crime, which lets it isolate social interaction as the operative channel and reconcile mixed prior evidence (e.g., MTO).

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open issues: (i) Manski's point that even when endogenous social interactions are identified, the specific endogenous channel (constraints, expectations, preferences) generally cannot be pinned down. (ii) Neighborhoods are municipalities (about 18,800 inhabitants), coarser than census tracts, so co-offender residence cannot be precisely localized. (iii) Long-term outcomes are tentative because the sample is young (only the last wave reaches age 25), so education and employment effects await later data. (iv) The estimated effects combine genuine age-at-assignment susceptibility with mechanical differences in years of exposure. (v) The sample is high-crime young refugee men, so external validity to other populations is asserted rather than tested. The authors also note that contact with delinquent peers as a mediating mechanism raises interesting avenues for future work.

## Connections
The design follows the random-assignment neighborhood-effects literature, especially Moving to Opportunity studies (Katz, Kling & Liebman 2001; Kling, Ludwig & Katz 2005; Kling, Liebman & Katz 2007; Sanbonmatsu et al. 2011) and Ludwig & Kling (2007), and the Swedish refugee-dispersal design of Edin, Fredriksson & Aslund (2003). The conceptual framework is Manski (1993, 2000) on endogenous, contextual, and correlated social effects, with social-multiplier ideas from [[@Glaeser1996|Glaeser, Sacerdote & Scheinkman]] (1996, 2003). On crime peer effects it connects to Case & Katz (1991), Bayer, Hjalmarsson & Pozen (2009), [[@Sacerdote2001|Sacerdote (2001)]], Carrell & Hoekstra (2010), and Damm & Dustmann's own crime-of-place discussion; on crime and population size to Glaeser & Sacerdote (1999); on police and crime to Levitt (1997, 1998). On downstream outcomes it relates to schooling-crime work by Lochner & Moretti (2004), Deming (2011), Hjalmarsson (2008), Grogger (1995), and Jacob & Lefgren (2003), and on intergenerational crime to Hjalmarsson & Lindquist (2012).

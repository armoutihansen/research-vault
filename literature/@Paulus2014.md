> [!abstract] Abstract
> It has been proposed that already in infancy, imitative learning plays a pivotal role in the acquisition of knowledge and abilities. Yet the cognitive mechanisms underlying the acquisition of novel action knowledge through social learning have remained unclear. The present contribution presents an ideomotor approach to imitative learning (IMAIL) in infancy (and beyond) that draws on the ideomotor theory of action control and on recent findings of perception–action matching. According to IMAIL, the central mechanism of imitative and social learning is the acquisition of cascading bidirectional action–effect associations through observation of own and others' actions. First, the observation of the visual effect of own actions leads to the acquisition of first-order action–effect associations, linking motor codes to the action's typical visual effects. Second, observing another person's action leads to motor activation (i.e., motor resonance) due to the first-order associations. This activated motor code then becomes linked to the other salient effects produced by the observed action, leading to the acquisition of (second-order) action–effect associations. These novel action–effect associations enable later imitation of the observed actions. The article reviews recent behavioral and neurophysiological studies with infants and adults that provide empirical support for the model. Furthermore, it is discussed how the model relates to other approaches on social-cognitive development and how developmental changes in imitative abilities can be conceptualized.

## Summary

Paulus advances IMAIL (the Ideomotor Approach to Imitative Learning), a parsimonious cognitive model of how human infants come to imitate. Extending the ideomotor theory of action control to social learning, the core claim is that imitative and social learning reduces to the acquisition of *cascading bidirectional action–effect associations* through observation of one's own and others' actions, requiring no inborn intermodal matching scheme or dedicated mirror-neuron endowment. The paper is a theoretical review that articulates the model, then surveys infant, adult, and neurophysiological evidence consistent with it, and discusses how developmental change, action sequences, scaffolding, and language fit within the framework.

## Research question

How is imitation in infancy cognitively possible? Specifically, by what mechanism do preverbal infants acquire novel action knowledge by observing others, and how can the observed developmental timeline and selectivity of infant imitation be explained without positing inborn imitation machinery?

## Method / identification

This is a theoretical-review paper, not an empirical study. The contribution is a process model, IMAIL, built on two foundations: (1) the ideomotor theory of action control, which holds that intentional actions are cognitively represented by their sensory consequences and are selected by activating the motor code bound to a desired effect code, so that acquired bidirectional action–effect associations are the substrate of voluntary control; and (2) findings of perception–action matching (motor resonance), where observing an action within one's motor repertoire activates the corresponding motor code.

The model posits a two-stage cascade. First-order learning: executing an action and perceiving its visual effect (e.g., the displacement of one's own hand) binds the motor code to that visual effect code. Second-order learning: observing another's action activates the now-associated motor code (motor resonance) via the first-order association; this activated motor code then becomes bound to the *other* salient effect the observed action produces (e.g., a light or sound), yielding a novel bidirectional action–effect association. Later, the infant's intention to reproduce the effect activates the effect code, which by spreading activation drives the associated motor program past threshold, executing — and thus imitating — the observed action. The binding mechanism is treated as automatic, incidental associative learning (Hebbian, Rescorla–Wagner, or anticipative variants), requiring no explicit intention to learn. Competition among multiple motor codes associated with a shared effect is described as plausibly a dynamic neural field process.

## Data

None — this is a theoretical review. It does not analyze original data but synthesizes prior behavioral and neurophysiological studies (e.g., the author's own 14-month-old head-touch/lamp imitation experiments derived from Gergely, Bekkering & Király (2002); a 1-week rattle-training EEG study in 9-month-olds using mu desynchronization; adult observational stimulus–response compatibility experiments adapting Elsner & Hommel (2001); and Jones's (2007) developmental survey of 162 infants).

## Key findings

There are no theorems; the paper's outputs are explanatory and predictive claims supported by reviewed evidence. (1) Infant imitation is gated by *motor resonance* and *salient effects*: imitation drops to baseline when the action is demonstrated in a way incompatible with the infant's own motor repertoire, or when it yields no salient outcome. (2) A motor-resonance account, not "rational imitation," better explains the classic head-touch findings, since infants spontaneously perform the head-touch with hands on the table. (3) Neurophysiological evidence (stronger mu desynchronization to rattle sounds previously produced by an observed action; mu suppression predicting later imitation) supports observational acquisition of action–effect associations. (4) Contingency matters: actions that always produce an effect are imitated more precisely than intermittently effective ones, as predicted by associative learning. (5) The model explains the heterogeneous developmental timeline in Jones (2007) — clapping (salient auditory effect, bimanual coordination emerging at 8–10 months) is imitated early, whereas hand-to-head (only a visual displacement, weak self-observability) emerges around 16 months; even the ">ah< before >eh<" vowel-imitation ordering follows ease of motor production (consistent with Jakobson's phonological universals). (6) The mechanism extends to adults: observers acquire others' action–effect mappings as if their own.

## Contribution

Provides a unified, parsimonious cognitive mechanism for imitative and social learning that subsumes Bandura's four-phase social-learning process model (giving its "memorization" and "motor reproduction" phases concrete ideomotor substrates) while reframing the motivation for imitation as goal-directed effect reproduction rather than behaviorist reinforcement. It dissolves the need for an inborn intermodal matching scheme (Meltzoff & Moore) by showing how the very capacity to mirror can itself be learned through self-observed action–effect contingencies. By grounding imitation in modality-specific (not amodal) effect representations and simple associative learning, it links infant imitation to sensorimotor approaches to social cognition and to ideomotor/event-coding theory.

## Limitations & open questions

The author explicitly flags several open problems that double as research hooks. (1) Whether *inferred/imagined* action effects (e.g., the unseen end-state of a failed attempt, as in Meltzoff 1995) support action–effect binding as effectively as perceived effects, versus being explained by stimulus enhancement or pre-existing associations, remains untested. (2) The precise binding rule is undetermined — Hebbian vs. Rescorla–Wagner vs. anticipative learning. (3) How infants acquire associations for *non-self-observable* actions (facial gestures) where there is no shared visual effect space; the proposed solution (caregiver mirroring providing the visual feedback) needs more evidence. (4) How development of action *sequences* with mutually constraining steps proceeds — via a learned hierarchy of effects or via growing motor abilities. (5) The strong falsifiable prediction that no action–effect association forms when an action is demonstrated outside the infant's motor repertoire awaits direct test (e.g., a rattle shaken in an un-imitable manner should abolish motor activation to its sound). (6) Whether the model can fully capture imitation of *meaningless/mimed* gestures lacking salient effects, or whether socially provided effects (a parent's smile or vocalization) supply the missing effect code.

## Connections

The model directly extends the ideomotor and event-coding tradition of Hommel, Müsseler, Aschersleben & Prinz (2001), James (1890/1981), and Elsner & Hommel (2001, 2004) on action–effect binding. It is positioned against Meltzoff & Moore (1989) and Meltzoff (2007) on innate active intermodal mapping and the "like-me" hypothesis, and against the rational/teleological imitation accounts of Gergely, Bekkering & Király (2002), Gergely & Csibra (2003), and Csibra (2010) on natural pedagogy. It builds on Bandura (1977, 1986) social learning theory and historically on Guillaume (1925) and Piaget (1962). The associative-learning view of mirroring aligns with Heyes (2010, 2013) and Cooper, Cook, Dickinson & Heyes (2013), and the empirical anchors include Jones (2007) on the developmental course of mimicry, Elsner & Aschersleben (2003) and Hauf & Aschersleben (2008) on action-effect learning in infants, Brand, Baldwin & Ashburn (2002) on "motionese"/scaffolding, and Sebanz, Bekkering & Knoblich (2006) on shared task representations. Mirror-neuron framing draws on Rizzolatti & Craighero (2004).

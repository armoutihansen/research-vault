> [!abstract] Abstract
> We propose an abstract method of systematically assigning a "rational" ranking to non-rationalizable choice data. Our main idea is that any method of ascribing welfare to an individual as a function of choice is subjective, and depends on the economist undertaking the analysis. We provide a simple example of the type of exercise we propose. Namely, we define an individual welfare functional as a mapping from stochastic choice functions into weak orders. A stochastic choice function (or choice distribution) gives the empirical frequency of choices for any possible opportunity set (framing factors may also be incorporated into the model). We require that for any two alternatives x and y, if our individual welfare functional recommends x over y given two distinct choice distributions, then it also recommends x over y for any mixture of the two choice distributions. Together with some mild technical requirements, such an individual welfare functional must weight every opportunity set and assign a utility to each alternative x which is the sum across all opportunity sets of the weighted probability of x being chosen from the set. It therefore requires us to have a "prior view" about how important or representative a choice of x at a given situation is.

## Summary
A methodological/axiomatic contribution to behavioral welfare economics. When choice data are not rationalizable by a single consistent preference, "better" and "worse" become undefined within classical revealed-preference theory. Chambers and Hayashi reject the search for the one "true" decision model and instead argue that welfare is an inherently *subjective* construct chosen by the analyst (the "economist"). They formalize an **individual welfare functional** mapping a situation-dependent stochastic choice distribution into a complete, transitive ordering, and show that a single consistency axiom (Combination) plus mild technical axioms forces a **linear scoring rule**: each alternative's utility is a weighted sum of choice probabilities, with weights encoding the economist's prior about how important each opportunity set is. Adding independence-type axioms collapses the weights so that an alternative's score depends only on its own choice probabilities.

## Research question
How can one assign a coherent welfare ranking (a complete, transitive order over alternatives) to an individual whose observed choices may be inconsistent — i.e., not rationalizable by any single preference, in either the deterministic or stochastic sense? Equivalently: what is the *minimal* set of consistency requirements on a rule that turns arbitrary choice data into a welfare order, and what functional form do those requirements imply? The paper deliberately stays agnostic about the "right" proxy for welfare (decision vs. experienced utility, neural correlates) and asks instead what structure follows from cross-data consistency alone.

## Method / identification
Pure decision-theoretic axiomatics; no estimation, no data. Setup: a finite alternative set $X$ with $|X|\ge 4$; a family $\mathcal{B}\subseteq 2^X$ of observable opportunity sets ($|B|\ge 2$); framing factors $F(B)$ per set (e.g. orders of appearance, reference points). A **situation** $\theta=(B_\theta,f_\theta)$ pairs an opportunity set with a frame; the data primitive is a situation-dependent choice distribution $p$, where $p(x\mid\theta)$ is the empirical frequency of choosing $x$ in situation $\theta$. The domain $P=\prod_{\theta\in\Theta}\Delta(B_\theta)$ is a product of simplices (not a vector space — a technical departure from related work). An **individual welfare functional** is a family $\{\succeq_p\}_{p\in P}$ of complete, transitive orders indexed by distributions. A key implicit normalization: only relative frequencies matter, and mixing distributions $\lambda p+(1-\lambda)q$ is interpreted as replicating and merging two datasets.

The central axiom is **Combination**: for all $p,q\in P$ and $\lambda\in(0,1)$, if $x\succeq_p y$ and $x\succeq_q y$ then $x\succeq_{\lambda p+(1-\lambda)q} y$ (with the strict version preserved). Two technical axioms accompany it — **Diversity** (for any four distinct alternatives some $p$ ranks them strictly in any given order, ensuring full-dimensionality) and **Continuity** (the set $\{p:x\succeq_p y\}$ is closed). The proof strategy mirrors the mixture-space / expected-utility machinery used by Gilboa–Schmeidler (games, case-based decision, inductive inference), Ashkenazi–Lehrer (relative utility), and Young (social aggregation), adapted to the product-of-simplices domain; proofs are in Appendix A. Notably the authors stress this welfare order is *not* meant to generate choice — it is a prescriptive proxy, marking the departure from revealed preference.

## Data
None — this is a theory paper. The "data" objects are abstract empirical choice distributions over opportunity sets; the framework explicitly admits choice that violates classical (Chernoff/Sen/Arrow) deterministic axioms and stochastic-consistency conditions (Luce, Falmagne), as well as more general menu/reference-dependent rationalizability.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1 (general representation).** $\{\succeq_p\}$ satisfies Combination, Diversity, Continuity iff there is a *diversified* series of weight vectors $\{u_x\}_{x\in X}$ with $x\succeq_p y \iff \sum_{\theta\in\Theta}\sum_{b\in B_\theta} u_x(\theta,b)\,p(b\mid\theta) \ge \sum_{\theta\in\Theta}\sum_{b\in B_\theta} u_y(\theta,b)\,p(b\mid\theta)$. The ranking is *linear* in all choice probabilities; strikingly, the utility of $x$ may depend on the probability that some other alternative $y$ is chosen, even in situations where $x$ is absent. Weights are unique up to a common positive scalar plus additive terms satisfying constraints (i)–(ii); only the *direction* of $u_x-u_y$ carries welfare meaning.
- **Theorem 2 (binary independence, multinomial domain).** Adding **Binary Independence/Monotonicity** and **Dummy** (alternatives never chosen are indifferent), on domains where every $|B_\theta|\ge 4$ and each $\Theta_x\ne\emptyset$, collapses the rule to $x\succeq_p y \iff \sum_{\theta\in\Theta_x} u_x(\theta)p(x\mid\theta)\ge\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_y} u_y(\theta)p(y\mid\theta)$ with strictly positive weights $u_x\in\mathbb{R}^{|\Theta_x|}_{++}$, **unique up to common positive scaling** — so each $u_x$ now carries normative meaning.
- **Theorem 3 (triples).** With opportunity sets down to size 3, **Semi-strong Dummy** restores enough identification; the same scoring representation holds, with the side condition $u_x(\theta)=u_y(\theta)$ whenever $B_\theta\supseteq\{x,y\}$ and $|B_\theta|=3$.
- **Theorem 4 (binary choices).** When pairwise choices are admitted, Binary Independence becomes vacuous, so a **Proportionality** axiom (the effect of raising $p(x\mid\{x,y\})$ on the $x$-vs-$y$ relation is double its effect on the $x$-vs-$z$ relation) plus **Strong Dummy** are needed; the scoring form survives with $u_x(\theta)=u_y(\theta)$ for $|B_\theta|\in\{2,3\}$.
- **Corollary 1 (alternative-neutrality).** Imposing permutation invariance forces weights to depend only on set cardinality, $u_x(B)=u(|B|)$. Two corollaries-of-the-corollary: under deterministic rational choice the assigned order agrees with the true preference for *any* $u$; and under Luce's stochastic-consistency condition the score depends only on $p(x\mid X)$, so standard Logit/Probit welfare analysis is a special case of this framework. When choice *is* consistent, the rationalizing preference is exactly the assigned welfare.

## Contribution
Reframes the entire behavioral-welfare debate: rather than competing for the "correct" generalization of revealed preference (multi-self, attention, reference-dependence, satisficing, etc.), the paper argues that *no* choice-based model can be universally "correct" because rival models differ only in predicted choices, and welfare is unobservable. It therefore offers a permissive **language** — the individual welfare functional plus the Combination consistency principle — within which any economist can encode her own subjective priors as weights, while guaranteeing a tractable scoring representation. It cleanly nests both classical revealed preference and standard discrete-choice welfare analysis as special cases, and connects behavioral welfare to the mixture-space axiomatics of social choice and case-based decision theory.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit that the framework is an *example*, not a universal prescription, and flag several open directions:
- **Combination can fail normatively.** Their status-quo example (choosing $x$ 60% from $\{x,y\}$, plus disjoint budgets $\{z_i,w_i\}$) shows a reasonable analyst might violate Combination — so the axiom is context-dependent, not compelling everywhere.
- **Partial / non-distributional data.** Data need not arrive as full stochastic choice functions; only partial observability may be available.
- **Time-dependence.** The base model cannot accommodate genuinely time-dependent data (e.g. preferences that drift) — flagged as a needed extension.
- **Alternative rationality notions.** Completeness/transitivity is one normative target; an analyst might instead demand the welfare order satisfy, e.g., the **independence axiom** over lotteries even when observed choice violates it.
- **Weight selection ("reasonable" weights).** Section 4 only sketches ways to discipline weights (alternative-neutrality, framing weights via rank of appearance, normalizing by $|\Theta_x|$ to offset unequal appearance counts); a general theory of which priors are justified is left open.
- **Identification vs. prescription.** Because the aim is normative, weight uniqueness is downplayed; the authors note understanding when distinct weights yield identical judgments still matters for practice.
- Compatibility of **libertarianism and paternalism** when inconsistency stems from opportunity-dependence (not just framing) is raised as needing formal analysis.

## Connections
Squarely in the **behavioral welfare economics** lineage. Closest is **Bernheim & Rangel** (welfare as a normative exercise via choice-derived binary relations, with framing) and **Green & Hojman** / **Ambrus & Rozen** (the individual as a "society" of selves whose conflicting preferences are aggregated by a scoring rule — for all but the Borda score, any choice is rationalizable). **Rubinstein & Salant** posit a latent "true" welfare relation generating observable rankings; **Apesteguia & Ballester** axiomatize a "swaps"-style measure of irrationality whose closest-preference selection is itself an instance of an individual welfare functional; **Kőszegi & Rabin** elicit mistakes/biases. The decision/experienced-utility distinction traces to **Kahneman–Wakker–Sarin**, and the choice-architecture motivation to **Thaler & Sunstein**'s libertarian paternalism. On the positive side it surveys generalized rationalizability — **Samuelson** (market revealed preference), **Chernoff/Arrow/Sen** (deterministic axioms), **Luce** and **Falmagne** (stochastic choice), **Manzini–Mariotti** and **Apesteguia–Ballester** (sequential elimination), **Masatlioglu–Nakajima–Ozbay** (limited attention), **Cherepanov–Feddersen–Sandroni** (rationalization), **Tyson** (satisficing), **Masatlioglu–Ok** and **Rubinstein–Salant** (reference points / framing / lists). Methodologically it borrows mixture-space axiomatics from **Gilboa–Schmeidler** (games, inductive inference, case-based decision), **Ashkenazi–Lehrer** (relative utility), and the social-choice/tournament tradition (**Young**'s Kemeny rule, **Rubinstein** on tournaments-to-orders, **Moulin**'s survey).

## Summary
This is the canonical graduate/practitioner textbook on derivatives — Hull's *Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives* (here the eighth-edition Hull & Basu adaptation). It is a 432-page, 35-chapter reference that builds from the institutional mechanics of forward, futures, options, and swap markets up through the no-arbitrage and risk-neutral pricing machinery (binomial trees, Itô calculus, the Black–Scholes–Merton model, the Greeks), and on to fixed-income derivatives, exotic and credit derivatives, value-at-risk, real options, and the lessons of derivatives mishaps. It is not a research paper; it is the standard pedagogical synthesis of arbitrage-pricing theory used to teach the field. ## Research question
A textbook rather than a hypothesis-driven study, so it has no single research question. Its organizing problem is: *how should a derivative — a financial instrument whose value derives from more basic underlying variables — be priced and hedged so as to preclude arbitrage, and how do the markets in which such instruments trade actually operate?* Every chapter is an instance of that problem (pricing forwards, options, swaps, rate derivatives, credit derivatives, etc.) plus the institutional context needed to apply it.

## Method / identification
The unifying method is **no-arbitrage / risk-neutral valuation**. The book develops it in escalating layers:
- **Static replication and cost-of-carry.** Forward and futures prices are pinned by a replicating portfolio: for an investment asset paying no income, $F_0 = S_0 e^{rT}$; with known income $I$ or yield $q$, $F_0 = (S_0 - I)e^{rT}$ and $F_0 = S_0 e^{(r-q)T}$. Bounds and **put–call parity** $c + K e^{-rT} = p + S_0$ are derived purely from dominance arguments.
- **Binomial trees.** A one-step model with up/down moves $u, d$ is hedged by a delta-neutral portfolio, yielding the **risk-neutral probability** $p = \frac{e^{r\Delta t} - d}{u - d}$ and price $f = e^{-r\Delta t}\,[\,p f_u + (1-p) f_v\,]$; multi-step trees converge to continuous time and handle American early exercise by backward induction.
- **Stochastic calculus.** Stock prices follow geometric Brownian motion $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dz$; **Itô's lemma** gives the dynamics of any function of $S$ and $t$.
- **Black–Scholes–Merton.** A continuously rebalanced riskless portfolio yields the PDE $\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + r S \frac{\partial f}{\partial S} + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial S^2} = r f$, whose solution for a European call is $c = S_0 N(d_1) - K e^{-rT} N(d_2)$ with $d_1 = \frac{\ln(S_0/K) + (r + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}$ and $d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$.
- **Extensions:** the Greeks ($\Delta, \Gamma, \Theta, \nu, \rho$) for hedging; numerical methods (trees, Monte Carlo, finite differences); short-rate and HJM/LMM term-structure models for interest-rate derivatives; and reduced-form/structural approaches for credit risk and CDOs.

## Data
None — this is a pedagogical text. It uses stylized worked examples, market quotes for illustration, and the bundled **DerivaGem** software for hands-on valuation rather than an empirical dataset or estimation exercise.

## Key findings
Not findings but the foundational *results* it teaches: the cost-of-carry relations for forwards/futures; put–call parity; the binomial-tree risk-neutral pricing formula and its convergence to BSM; Itô's lemma and the lognormal property of stock prices; the Black–Scholes–Merton differential equation and closed-form option formulas; risk-neutral valuation as a general pricing principle; delta/gamma/vega hedging; the term-structure and credit-derivative pricing frameworks; and value-at-risk for portfolio risk measurement. The eighth edition adds Chapter 8 on **securitization and the 2007 credit crisis**, explaining how CDOs created from subprime mortgages became worthless and why post-crisis markets are more heavily regulated.

## Contribution
It is the standard reference and teaching text that codified arbitrage-pricing theory for a generation of finance students and practitioners — translating the Black–Scholes–Merton and Cox–Ross–Rubinstein advances into a coherent, example-driven curriculum spanning market mechanics, valuation, hedging, fixed income, exotics, credit, and risk management. Its lasting contribution is pedagogical synthesis and a common vocabulary for the field.

## Limitations & open questions
As a textbook it states no open research problems, but it flags live tensions useful as project hooks: the gap between model assumptions (constant volatility, lognormal returns, frictionless continuous hedging) and observed phenomena such as the **volatility smile/implied-volatility surface**; the inadequacy of pre-crisis credit-derivative and securitization models, made explicit in the new credit-crisis chapter; model risk and the limits of value-at-risk; and the recurring theme (Chapter 35, "Derivatives mishaps and what we can learn from them") that hedging instruments become speculative liabilities when governance and risk controls fail. These are normative warnings rather than formal conjectures.

## Connections
The pricing core rests on Black & Scholes (1973) and Merton (1973) for continuous-time option valuation, and Cox, Ross & Rubinstein (1979) for the binomial model and the risk-neutral pricing interpretation formalized by Harrison & Kreps (1979) and Harrison & Pliska (1981). The term-structure chapters draw on Vasicek (1977), Cox, Ingersoll & Ross (1985), Hull & White (1990), Heath, Jarrow & Morton (1992), and the LIBOR market model of Brace, Gatarek & Musiela (1997). Credit modeling connects to Merton (1974) and reduced-form approaches such as Jarrow & Turnbull (1995). The real-options chapter links to Dixit & Pindyck (1994). Within the broader research-economics context of this vault, the book is a methodological reference for asset pricing under no-arbitrage rather than a behavioral or social-preference contribution.

## Summary

A two-channel analytical IO/marketing model in which two manufacturers sell vertically (quality-) differentiated products, each through either an integrated or a decentralized (independent-retailer) channel. By endogenizing product quality alongside price, the paper shows that the welfare and profit effects of channel decentralization are asymmetric across the high- and low-quality channels: decentralization makes the high-quality channel lower its quality but makes the low-quality channel raise its quality. Decentralization raises prices and lowers consumer welfare in all cases, and once quality is endogenous, full integration ($II$) is the unique equilibrium channel structure — so firms' private incentives favor integration, contradicting regulators' usual presumption that divestiture protects consumers.

## Research question

How does the structure of distribution channels (vertical integration vs. decentralization) influence firms' joint quality and price strategies in a competitive market with vertically differentiated products, and how do those strategic responses in turn affect consumer welfare and the equilibrium choice of channel structure? In particular: does the conventional regulatory preference for decentralization (divestiture) actually benefit consumers once product positioning is endogenous?

## Method / identification

A multi-stage game of complete information solved by backward induction. Two manufacturers $i=1,2$ each produce at quality $a_i \ge 0$ with marginal quality cost $c(a_i)$; channel 2 is labeled the high-quality channel ($a_2 > a_1$). Each manufacturer either owns its retailer (integrated, $I$) or sells through an independent retailer (decentralized, $D$), giving four market structures $II$, $DD$, $ID$, $DI$. Game timing: (1) manufacturers simultaneously choose qualities; (2) they set wholesale prices $w_i$ (moot under integration); (3) retailers (or the integrated manufacturer) simultaneously set retail prices $p_i$.

Consumers have unit demand with utility $U(t)=t\,u(a)-p$, where $t\sim\mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]$ indexes willingness-to-pay for quality and $u(a)$ is increasing and concave. The marginal consumer participating satisfies $t_1=p_1/u(a_1)$ and the consumer indifferent between the two products is at $t_2$ where $t_2 u(a_1)-p_1=t_2 u(a_2)-p_2$. This yields linear demands $\lambda_1=\alpha p_2-\beta p_1$ and $\lambda_2=1-\alpha(p_2-p_1)$, with $\alpha=1/[u(a_2)-u(a_1)]$ and $\beta=1/[u(a_2)-u(a_1)]+1/u(a_1)$. Two regularity conditions (A: $u(a_1)/p_1>u(a_2)/p_2$; B: $u(a_2)-u(a_1)>p_2-p_1$) guarantee both products sell.

The pricing stage admits a unique pure-strategy Nash equilibrium (best responses are linear and profit functions strictly concave, with $\partial^2\pi_1^{II}/\partial p_1^2=-2\beta$, $\partial^2\pi_2^{II}/\partial p_2^2=-2\alpha$). Price/quality comparisons across the four structures are derived analytically for general $u(\cdot)$ and $c(\cdot)$ (Theorem 1). For equilibrium quality and welfare, specific functional forms are adopted: the power utility $u(a_i)=\sqrt[n]{a_i}$ (constant elasticity of marginal utility $1/n-1$; equivalently CRRA with coefficient $1-1/n$) for $n=2,\dots,9$, plus a robustness check with exponential utility $u(a)=1-\exp(-na)$. Equilibrium qualities are obtained by numerically solving the first-order conditions $\partial\pi_1/\partial a_1=0$, $\partial\pi_2/\partial a_2=0$. The channel-structure choice is then treated as a prior stage and solved as a $2\times2$ game on manufacturer profits.

## Key findings

- **Theorem 1 (asymmetric quality responses).** For any $u(a)$ and cost functions yielding well-behaved (unique-equilibrium) objectives, and for any quality of the rival channel: the low-quality channel chooses *higher* quality when decentralized than integrated ($r_1^{DI}>r_1^{II}$, $r_1^{DD}>r_1^{ID}$), while the high-quality channel chooses *lower* quality when decentralized ($r_2^{ID}<r_2^{II}$, $r_2^{DD}<r_2^{DI}$). Intuition: decentralization raises a channel's price via double marginalization; strategic complementarity in prices pushes the rival's price up too, freeing a consumer segment. Which segment (medium/high vs. low WTP) is freed differs by which channel decentralizes, driving opposite optimal quality moves.
- **Demand/price effects.** Unilateral decentralization halves the decentralizing channel's market share ($\lambda_1^{DI}/\lambda_1^{II}=\lambda_2^{ID}/\lambda_2^{II}=50\%$); the own-demand loss exceeds the rival's gain, so total demand falls and both retail prices rise.
- **Profit asymmetry under $DD$.** Moving from $II$ to $DD$ raises *total* channel profits for both channels (less differentiation but higher prices buffer competition, echoing [[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin 1983]]), yet at the manufacturer level it *hurts* the high-quality manufacturer ($\pi_{M2}^{DD*}<\pi_{M2}^{II*}$) while *benefiting* the low-quality manufacturer ($\pi_{M1}^{DD*}>\pi_{M1}^{II*}$) — a profit-enhancing role for decentralization via product positioning.
- **Welfare.** Decentralization (unilateral or bilateral) lowers consumer welfare through higher prices, even when quality rises; integration's elimination of double marginalization is welfare-enhancing, most so in the high-quality channel. Equilibrium product differentiation exceeds the social-planner-efficient level in all four structures (extending [[@Moorthy1988|Moorthy 1988b]]).
- **Proposition 1 (unique equilibrium).** Once product positioning is endogenized and regulation does not interfere, integration in both channels ($II$) is the *only* channel-structure equilibrium for all $n$; $DD$ is no longer an equilibrium (unlike the fixed-quality case, where $DD$ is also an equilibrium when $u(a_1)>0.87\,u(a_2)$). Firms can reposition quality to soften price competition rather than resorting to decentralization.

## Contribution

The paper endogenizes product quality (and hence the degree of substitution) within the channel-structure literature, which had largely treated substitutability as exogenous ([[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin 1983]]; [[@Moorthy1988|Moorthy 1988a]]). This "unpacks the black box" of product substitution and reveals high-/low-quality asymmetries in quality, demand, profit, and welfare that exogenous-substitution models cannot show. It adds manufacturer competition (vs. the cannibalization focus of Villas-Boas 1998 and the joint-component setup of Economides 1999) and delivers a clean antitrust message: firms' private incentives favor integration and, net of price and quality effects, integration can also benefit consumers — so a regulatory presumption in favor of divestiture may harm both firms and consumers.

## Limitations & open questions

The authors explicitly flag: (1) some results require specific functional forms — robustness should be checked beyond power and exponential utility; (2) only linear per-unit wholesale/retail pricing is modeled, so nonlinear/two-part contracts (Bonanno & Vickers 1988; Desai et al. 2004) and advance selling (Xie & Shugan 2001) remain open; (3) differentiation is purely vertical (quality) — extending to horizontal differentiation (Neven & Thisse 1990) is open, and the authors conjecture an extra differentiation dimension would favor integration even more; (4) consumer types are assumed uniform — bimodal/unimodal preference distributions (Blattberg & Wisniewski 1989) are acknowledged as more realistic in some markets but "challenging" to solve.

## Connections

The model builds directly on [[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin (1983)]], whose downstream-integration equilibrium analysis it extends from exogenous substitutability to endogenous quality, and on [[@Moorthy1988|Moorthy]] (1988a, 1988b) for strategic decentralization, duopoly product/price competition, and excess differentiation relative to efficiency. The vertical-differentiation demand structure follows Motta (1993), and the comparative methodology across channel structures follows Economides (1999) (quality choice and vertical integration of complementary components) and Villas-Boas (1998) (product-line design through a distribution channel, where the contrast is cannibalization vs. this paper's competition). The profitable-decentralization result connects to Bonanno & Vickers (1988) (vertical separation as collusion-inducing) and Desai, Koenigsberg & Purohit (2004) (strategic decentralization and channel coordination in durable goods), while Bruce, Desai & Staelin (2005) and Raju & Zhang (2005) situate it in the broader channel-coordination literature. Welfare framing draws on Tyagi (2004) and Shugan (2004); equilibrium existence/uniqueness invokes Fudenberg & Tirole (1991). Related substitutability work includes [[@Coughlan1985|Coughlan (1985)]], [[@Choi1991|Choi (1991)]], and [[@Gupta1998|Gupta & Loulou (1998)]].

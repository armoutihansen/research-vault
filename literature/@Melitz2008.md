> [!abstract] Abstract
> We develop a monopolistically competitive model of trade with firm heterogeneity—in terms of productivity differences—and endogenous differences in the "toughness" of competition across markets—in terms of the number and average productivity of competing firms. We analyse how these features vary across markets of different size that are not perfectly integrated through trade; we then study the effects of different trade liberalization policies. In our model, market size and trade affect the toughness of competition, which then feeds back into the selection of heterogeneous producers and exporters in that market. Aggregate productivity and average mark-ups thus respond to both the size of a market and the extent of its integration through trade (larger, more integrated markets exhibit higher productivity and lower mark-ups). Our model remains highly tractable, even when extended to a general framework with multiple asymmetric countries integrated to different extents through asymmetric trade costs. We believe this provides a useful modelling framework that is particularly well suited to the analysis of trade and regional integration policy scenarios in an environment with heterogeneous firms and endogenous mark-ups.

## Summary

Melitz & Ottaviano build a monopolistically competitive trade model with heterogeneous firms (Pareto productivity draws) and *endogenous* mark-ups generated by a quasi-linear quadratic demand system. Because demand is linear rather than CES, the price elasticity each firm faces depends on the "toughness" of competition—the number and average productivity of competitors—so mark-ups respond endogenously to market size and trade. The framework remains analytically closed-form: a single cost cut-off $c_D$ summarizes the entire equilibrium distribution of prices, mark-ups, output, and welfare. Larger and more integrated markets exhibit tougher selection, higher aggregate productivity, lower average prices, and lower mark-ups. The model unifies, within one tractable structure, the selection, variety, and pro-competitive welfare channels that earlier "new trade theory" had isolated in separate models.

## Research question

How do market size and (costly, possibly asymmetric) trade jointly determine the toughness of competition, firm selection, aggregate productivity, mark-ups, and welfare when firms are heterogeneous in productivity and mark-ups are endogenous? In particular, can a single tractable model capture simultaneously the productivity (selection), variety, and pro-competitive (mark-up) gains from trade and regional integration?

## Method / identification

The model is a closed-form general-equilibrium monopolistic-competition model. Consumers have quasi-linear preferences over a numeraire and a continuum of differentiated varieties with a quadratic sub-utility:
$$U = q_0^c + \alpha \int_{i\in\Omega} q_i^c\,di - \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma \int_{i\in\Omega} (q_i^c)^2\,di - \tfrac{1}{2}\eta\left(\int_{i\in\Omega} q_i^c\,di\right)^2.$$
This yields linear demand and a finite choke price $p_{\max}=\frac{1}{\eta N+\gamma}(\gamma\alpha+\eta N\bar p)$ at which a variety's demand is driven to zero. The key departure from CES is that the demand elasticity $\varepsilon_i=[(p_{\max}/p_i)-1]^{-1}$ is *not* pinned down by $\gamma$ alone: lower average prices $\bar p$ or more competitors $N$ raise the elasticity—"tougher" competition. Firms pay a sunk entry cost $f_E$, then draw marginal cost $c$ from $G(c)$; survivors charge $p(c)=\tfrac12(c_D+c)$ and set mark-up $\mu(c)=\tfrac12(c_D-c)$, so more productive firms charge *higher* mark-ups. Free entry plus the zero-cutoff-profit condition jointly determine the cut-off $c_D$ and the number of firms $N$. Productivity $1/c$ is parametrized as Pareto with shape $k$ (so $G(c)=(c/c_M)^k$), delivering tractable closed forms such as the closed-economy cut-off
$$c_D = \left(\frac{2(k+1)(k+2)\gamma (c_M)^k f_E}{L}\right)^{1/(k+2)},$$
which falls in market size $L$. The open economy adds per-unit iceberg-type trade costs $\tau^l$ and segmented markets; with "freeness of trade" $\rho^l\equiv(\tau^l)^{-k}$ the two-country cut-off is $c_D^l=\big(\frac{\gamma\phi}{L^l}\frac{1-\rho^h}{1-\rho^l\rho^h}\big)^{1/(k+2)}$. The authors also build a *short-run* version (fixed incumbents, no entry) to separate the immediate pro-competitive effects of liberalization from the long-run firm-relocation effects driven by entry, and extend the model in the Appendix to many asymmetric countries with an arbitrary trade-cost matrix.

## Data

None—this is a theory paper. The authors cite external empirical evidence as validation rather than estimating the model: U.S. cross-regional establishment studies (Syverson; Campbell & Hopenhayn) corroborating the market-size comparative statics, the Pareto-productivity estimates of Del Gatto, Mion & Ottaviano (mean $k\approx 2$), and microeconometric trade-selection studies (Clerides–Lach–Tybout; Bernard & Jensen; Pavcnik).

## Key findings

- **Endogenous mark-ups and selection:** $p(c)=\tfrac12(c_D+c)$, $\mu(c)=\tfrac12(c_D-c)$. The single cut-off $c_D$ summarizes all firm-level performance measures and welfare $U=1+\frac{1}{2\eta}(\alpha-c_D)\!\left(\alpha-\frac{k+1}{k+2}c_D\right)$, which is monotone decreasing in $c_D$.
- **Market-size effects (closed economy):** Bigger markets ($L$ up) lower $c_D$, raising average productivity and variety, lowering average prices and mark-ups, while average firm size and profits rise. Survival probability $G(c_D)$ falls (more churning). These comparative statics match the U.S. regional evidence.
- **Costly trade does not fully integrate markets:** With symmetric trade costs the larger country keeps a lower cut-off (higher productivity, more variety, lower mark-ups, higher welfare); size-induced autarky differences persist. Strikingly, in the two-country case the *partner's* size does not affect a country's cut-off, due to offsetting export-opportunity and import-competition effects.
- **Gains from trade decompose** into productivity gains (selection), pro-competitive mark-up reductions, and added variety. The selection channel here works through *product-market* (import) competition raising demand elasticities—unlike Melitz (2003), where it works through factor-market competition under CES.
- **Reciprocal dumping** arises ($c_X^l<c_D^l$ implies exporters set FOB prices below domestic prices), extending Brander & Krugman (1983) to heterogeneous firms; dumping is equivalent to a no-arbitrage condition and implies no predation.
- **Asymmetric liberalization:** Unilateral liberalization yields a short-run pro-competitive welfare gain for the liberalizer, but the long-run entry relocation toward the trading partner can reverse it into a welfare loss; preferential (three-country) liberalization produces analogous network effects.

## Contribution

The paper's central contribution is integrative and methodological rather than the discovery of a new welfare channel: it shows that the selection/reallocation, love-of-variety, and pro-competitive (mark-up) channels—previously isolated in disparate models (Krugman 1979/1980; Markusen 1981; Venables; Melitz 2003)—can all be captured in a single, fully tractable framework with heterogeneous firms and endogenous, non-degenerate mark-up distributions. It provides closed-form comparative statics for market size and asymmetric trade costs, making it a workhorse ("Melitz–Ottaviano") model for regional-integration and trade-policy analysis.

## Limitations & open questions

- **Functional-form dependence:** Several sharp results (e.g., partner size not affecting the cut-off, the exact signing of variance effects) hinge on linear demand and Pareto productivity; the authors flag that outcomes "are naturally influenced by our functional form assumptions" (Combes et al. extend to general distributions).
- **Abstracted-away frictions:** No fixed overhead production costs and no fixed export costs are modeled (justified only by tractability), so all selection runs through per-unit costs; richer extensive-margin export dynamics are left open.
- **No factor-market channel:** Perfectly elastic labour supply to the differentiated sector shuts down the real-wage channel of Melitz (2003); interactions of both channels are unexplored.
- **Static / deterministic:** No firm dynamics, stochastic productivity evolution, or innovation; the closed economy is only "related to" the dynamic Asplund & Nocke (2006) setup.
- The authors explicitly position the model as "a useful foundation for future empirical investigations," inviting structural estimation and welfare quantification.

## Connections

The heterogeneous-firm trade architecture builds directly on Melitz (2003), with the key contrast being the source of selection (product-market vs. factor-market competition). The quasi-linear quadratic demand and endogenous-markup machinery come from Ottaviano, Tabuchi & Thisse (2002). The pro-competitive and de-location welfare channels synthesize Krugman (1979, 1980), Markusen (1981), Venables (1985, 1987), and Horstmann & Markusen (1986), as surveyed in Helpman & Krugman (1989) and Baldwin et al. (2003). Reciprocal dumping extends Brander & Krugman (1983) and Weinstein (1992); arbitrage links draw on Feenstra, Markusen & Rose (2001). Bernard, Eaton, Jensen & Kortum (2003) is the closest alternative heterogeneous-firm/endogenous-markup model (but with markups invariant to geography), while Asplund & Nocke (2006) provides the dynamic closed-economy counterpart, and Del Gatto, Mion & Ottaviano and Combes, Duranton, Gobillon, Puga & Roux extend the empirical and distributional generality.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> We use machine learning to provide a tractable measure of the amount of predictable variation in the data that a theory captures, which we call its "completeness." We apply this measure to three problems: assigning certain equivalents to lotteries, initial play in games, and human generation of random sequences. We discover considerable variation in the completeness of existing models, which sheds light on whether to focus on developing better models with the same features or instead to look for new features that will improve predictions. We also illustrate how and why completeness varies with the experiments considered, which highlights the role played in choosing which experiments to run.

## Summary
This is the arXiv preprint (October 2019) of "Measuring the Completeness of Theories" by Fudenberg, Kleinberg, Liang, and Mullainathan; the published version appears as "Measuring the Completeness of Economic Models." The paper proposes a methodology for asking not merely whether an economic model predicts behavior, but how much of the predictable variation in a domain it captures. The central problem is that predictive accuracy alone is uninterpretable: 55% accuracy is excellent for predicting discretized stock movements but terrible for predicting planetary motion, because the two domains differ in how much of the outcome is determined by the available features. Prediction error therefore conflates two distinct quantities — the room for a more predictive model given the current feature set, and the intrinsic noise (irreducible error) baked into that feature set. The authors separate these by benchmarking existing theories against an estimate of the best achievable prediction, computed via a Table Lookup algorithm.

## Research question
How close is a given theory's predictive performance to the best achievable performance in its domain, holding fixed the set of measurable features? Equivalently: of the predictable variation in the data, how much does the theory capture, and how much room remains for improvement using the same features versus needing new features?

## Method / identification
The paper is methodological and theoretical in framing, applied to existing experimental datasets. A prediction problem has features $X = X_1 \times \cdots \times X_N$ and an outcome in $\mathcal{Y}$; a model class is a parametric family of prediction rules $F = (f_\theta)_{\theta \in \Theta}$, with a loss function $\ell(y', y)$ (mean-squared error or classification loss). Three error levels anchor the construction. The expected error of the best rule in the class is $E_P(f_{\theta_P^*})$ where $\theta_P^* = \arg\min_\theta E_P(f_\theta)$. The irreducible error $E_P(f_P^*)$ is the error of the ideal rule $f_P^*(x) = \arg\min_{y'} E_P[\ell(y', y) \mid x]$ — the lowest achievable given the features. A naive rule $f_{\text{naive}}$ (e.g. predict uniformly at random) sets the lower bound on performance.

Completeness is the ratio of the model's error reduction to the achievable reduction:
$$\frac{E_P(f_{\text{naive}}) - E_P(f_{\theta_P^*})}{E_P(f_{\text{naive}}) - E_P(f_P^*)}.$$
A value near 100% means little improvement is possible without new features; a low value signals untapped regularities exploitable with the existing feature set.

On finite data, all expectations are estimated by tenfold cross-validation. The model and naive errors become $\mathrm{CV}_F$ and $\mathrm{CV}_{\text{naive}}$. The irreducible error is approximated by Table Lookup ($\mathrm{CV}_{TL}$): on each training fold, fit the unrestricted best mapping from feature vectors to outcomes — operationally, predict the empirical mean (or modal) outcome for each unique feature vector. The empirical completeness measure is $(\mathrm{CV}_{\text{naive}} - \mathrm{CV}_F)/(\mathrm{CV}_{\text{naive}} - \mathrm{CV}_{TL})$. Table Lookup approximates the irreducible error only when the data is large relative to the number of unique feature vectors, which requires either discrete features or finitely many distinct instances. The authors validate Table Lookup by reporting small cross-validated standard errors and by checking it against bagged decision trees (which suit smaller samples), finding Table Lookup comparable but better across applications. A key conceptual point: completeness depends on the distribution $P$, and especially on the marginal over features (which lotteries or games an experimenter runs) — a choice variable of the analyst — so the same model can have different completeness across experimental designs.

## Data
Three pre-existing experimental datasets, except the randomness strings collected here. (1) Risk: 8,906 certainty equivalents from 179 subjects over 50 two-outcome lotteries, from [[@Bruhin2010|Bruhin et al. (2010)]]. (2) Initial play in games: 23,137 observations of initial play in 486 $3 \times 3$ matrix games, from Fudenberg and Liang (2018), pooling a meta-dataset (Wright and Leyton-Brown), MTurk random-payoff games, and algorithmically designed games. (3) Randomness: 21,975 human-generated length-8 binary strings from 167 (final) subjects collected on Mechanical Turk, where subjects were incentivized to mimic a fair coin and paid only if an algorithm could not flag their strings as human-generated.

## Key findings
Completeness varies dramatically across domains and is decoupled from raw accuracy. In the risk domain, Cumulative Prospect Theory has a mean-squared error of 67.38 against a Table Lookup floor of 65.58 and a naive (expected-value) error of 103.81 — yielding 95% completeness, while Expected Utility reaches only 11%. So despite a large absolute error, CPT leaves almost no room for improvement using $(z^1, z^2, p)$; gains require new features (e.g. subject grouping). In initial play, the Poisson Cognitive Hierarchy Model is roughly 67-68% complete across two subsets of games (Data Set A and B) even though its absolute misclassification rates differ (0.49 vs 0.44) — illustrating that similar completeness can hide different accuracy, and vice versa, because the irreducible error differs by subset. On games where the level-1 action is sharply best, PCHM reaches 97% completeness. In the randomness domain, the best model (Rabin and Vayanos, 2010) is only 14% complete: negative-autocorrelation models capture a real but small slice of the structure, and the gap between the achieved error (0.2492) and the Table Lookup floor (0.2441) is large relative to model improvements, leaving substantial unexploited structure. An extension shows Table Lookup also evaluates feature sets: for randomness, using only the number of Heads attains 59% completeness and using only flips 4-7 attains 36%, demonstrating predictive content in the early flips. A subject-heterogeneity extension (k-means grouping into three types) again finds CPT near-complete (96%).

## Contribution
The paper's main contribution is methodological: a tractable, domain-agnostic measure that reframes model evaluation from "is the theory predictive?" to "how complete is it?" It provides actionable guidance for theory development — high completeness says invest in new features rather than refining within the same feature set; low completeness says there are unmodeled regularities to capture with existing features. By using machine learning (Table Lookup) to estimate the predictive frontier rather than to replace economic models, it complements the literature comparing ML algorithms to economic models (e.g. Peysakhovich and Naecker, 2017), and relates to Erev et al.'s equivalent-number-of-observations. It also surfaces the experimenter's design as a lever: completeness depends on which experiments are run.

## Limitations & open questions
Table Lookup approximates the irreducible error only when data is large relative to the number of unique feature vectors, so the measure does not apply to genuinely continuous, sparsely sampled feature spaces (kernel regression is suggested instead). Completeness is distribution- and dataset-specific: CPT's 95% completeness on Bruhin et al.'s two-outcome lotteries need not generalize to richer lottery supports, and the authors note a high completeness could reflect either genuine fit or the model's flexibility to mimic Table Lookup. The paper abstracts from the bias-variance trade-off and overfitting, relying on large samples where the most predictive model is approximately the most complex. It focuses solely on predictiveness, deliberately setting aside interpretability and generality, where one might prefer a less complete model. The authors flag cross-domain transferability — within-domain completeness may be an incomplete measure of "overall" completeness — as open for future work.

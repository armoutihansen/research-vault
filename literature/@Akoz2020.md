> [!abstract] Abstract
> We study a signal‐jamming model of product review manipulation in which rational consumers consult product reviews and price to better estimate a product's quality, and a firm, whose quality is either high or low, chooses its price and how much bias to insert into product reviews. We show that both firm types always exert positive effort to manipulate product reviews, and, depending on the equilibrium price level, one or both of them can increase its sales. When the high‐type firm exerts more effort than the low‐type, review manipulation benefits consumers by raising [lowering] their demand for the high‐quality [low‐quality] product.

## Summary
A monopolist selling an experience good of privately-known high or low quality can pay to insert hidden upward bias into the noisy product-review signals consumers consult, and also chooses price. Consumers are rational and anticipate the equilibrium bias but cannot observe it or the firm's type. The paper combines signal-jamming with price signaling and shows that *both* types always manipulate (an "arms race"), that which type benefits more depends on whether price sits above or below ex-ante expected quality, and that manipulation can actually raise consumer surplus when prices are high. It draws out policy lessons: type-revealing certification can hurt consumers, while fake-review detection helps only at low prices, and punishing the low type always helps.

## Research question
When and how does manipulation of online product reviews affect market demand and consumer welfare, once the firm chooses both price and (hidden, costly) review bias, and consumers rationally discount the anticipated bias? In particular, which firm type manipulates more, when is manipulation "effective" (raises sales relative to no manipulation), and which regulatory interventions help consumers?

## Method / identification
A game-theoretic signal-jamming model solved for Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium (PBE). A firm of type $j\in\{L,H\}$ with quality $0\le v_L<v_H$ (marginal cost normalized to zero) first observes its quality, then chooses price $p_j$ and non-negative bias $b_j\ge 0$. A unit-mass continuum of consumers $i\in[0,1]$ each receive a private noisy signal
$$x_i = v_j + b_j + \varepsilon_i,$$
where $\varepsilon_i$ is iid with density $f$. Consumer $i$ observes $x_i$ and $p_j$ and buys (utility $u=v_j-p_j$, else $0$). Firm sales are $S(v_j,b_j,p_j)=\int_0^1 s(v_j+b_j+\varepsilon_i,p_j)\,di$ and profit is
$$\pi_j = p_j\,S(v_j,b_j,p_j) - C(b_j).$$
Key assumptions: (1) $f$ is continuous, log-concave, symmetric, with unbounded support, finite moments, and tail property $\lim_{x\to-\infty} f'(x)/f(x)=\infty$ (the normal qualifies); (2) cost $C$ with $C'(0)=0$, $C',C''>0$; (3) $\min_b C''(b) > v_H\max_x f'(x)$, ensuring strict concavity of profit in bias and ruling out multiple bias levels. The solution concept is "strong" PBE with the most pessimistic off-path beliefs (any deviation attributed to the $L$ type). The authors characterize two equilibrium classes: pooling-price PBE (both types post the same price $\bar p$, so price is uninformative) and partially-separating PBE (the $L$ type mixes between a pooling price and a low separating price $=v_L$, so price is partly informative). A separate variant gives the firm commitment power over a state-contingent bias schedule.

## Data
None — this is a pure theory paper. The authors note their hypotheses (e.g., manipulation prevalence for over- vs under-priced goods) are in principle testable by measuring prior quality expectations $E(v)$ via surveys, fake-review-detection algorithms, and quality classification, but they conduct no empirical estimation. Numerical solutions for ambiguous welfare cases are relegated to an Online Appendix.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1 (existence/uniqueness of pooling PBE).** For each admissible pooling price $\bar p\in(v_L,v_H)$ the PBE is unique and features *strictly positive* bias for both types, $b_L>0$ and $b_H>0$, plus a purchase threshold $\bar x$. Both quality types always manipulate — distinguishing the model from advertising models where only the low type misleads.
- **Proposition 2 / comparative statics on bias.** When the product is underpriced relative to consumer priors ($\bar p<E(v)$), the low-quality firm exerts more effort; when overpriced ($\bar p>E(v)$), the high-quality firm exerts more.
- **Theorem 2 (effect on sales and welfare).** There exist unique thresholds $p_L<E(v)<p_H$ characterizing when manipulation is "effective." The type exerting more effort always raises its own sales versus the no-manipulation benchmark; at extreme prices *both* types raise sales. When the $H$ type manipulates more (high prices), manipulation *raises* ex-ante consumer surplus (consumers buy the high-quality good more, the low-quality good less); at low prices it lowers consumer surplus.
- **Corollary 2.** Low equilibrium prices ⇒ negative effect of biased reviews on consumer surplus; high prices ⇒ positive effect.
- **Theorem 3 (partially-separating PBE).** Whenever a pooling PBE does not exist (for $v_L>0$), a partially-separating mixed PBE supports the price: the $H$ type plays a pure strategy, the $L$ type mixes between the pooling price and the separating price $v_L$. Here manipulation is *always* effective for the $L$ type and tends to be more harmful to consumers.
- **Commitment result.** If the firm could pre-commit to a state-contingent bias schedule, it would set zero bias in at least one quality state — the arms race is profit-dissipating, so commitment (akin to demarketing) can be valuable.
- **Policy.** A regulator that reveals firm type may hurt consumers under monopoly; fake-review-deletion algorithms help only at low prices; punishing only the $L$ type always raises consumer surplus.

## Contribution
First paper to combine signal-jamming (hidden, unobservable review bias on an unbounded space) with endogenous price signaling as complementary persuasion tools. Unlike prior false-advertising models, the high type can "counter-advertise," generating an arms race in which both types manipulate. It identifies a novel price-driven mechanism: at higher prices the marginal mass of persuadable indifferent consumers is larger for the high type, reversing at low prices. This yields nuanced, price-contingent welfare and policy conclusions and a testable cross-product prediction about where manipulated reviews concentrate.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Monopoly only.** "The welfare consequences of manipulation in oligopolies remains... an interesting open question."
- **Homogeneous valuations.** Consumers are ex-ante identical; heterogeneity enters only through signal noise. The authors sketch an alternative with ex-ante preference heterogeneity (match quality $\psi_i$) and a common signal but do not fully analyze it.
- **Stylized for empirics.** The model is "too stylized to directly guide empirical tests"; building a market-tailored model with sharper testable predictions is left for future research.
- **Equilibrium multiplicity.** A range of pooling prices is supportable, driven by factors outside the model — selection is left open.
- Off-path beliefs are pinned by an assumption (most pessimistic / $L$-type), not derived.

## Connections
Closest antecedents are the signal-jamming reviews of Mayzlin (2006) — promotional chat where the low type spends more — and Dellarocas (2006), where manipulation can rise with quality and benefit consumers; both fix or neutralize price, whereas this paper endogenizes informative price. The price-signaling lineage runs through [[@Milgrom1986|Milgrom & Roberts (1986)]], Bagwell & Riordan (1991), and Riordan & Judd (1994). Recent manipulative-advertising work it speaks to includes Rhodes & Wilson (2018), Piccolo, Tedeschi & Ursino (2015, 2017), and Janssen & Roy (2017), all of which allow low-type deception without high-type counter-ads. False-advice and lying-cost models include Kartik (2009) and Drugov & Troya-Martínez (2019) (whose seller cannot condition bias on quality). The commitment/demarketing result connects to Miklós-Thal & Zhang (2013). Demand-rotation language borrows from Johnson & Myatt (2006), and the persuasion welfare logic invokes Kamenica & Gentzkow (2011). Empirical motivation draws on [[@Mayzlin2014|Mayzlin, Dover & Chevalier (2014)]] and Anderson & Simester (2014). The experience-good framing follows [[@Nelson1970|Nelson]] (1970, 1974), and the authors' own prior signal-jamming work is Aköz & Arbatlı (2016).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Virtually all current theories of choice under risk or uncertainty are cognitive and consequentialist. They assume that people assess the desirability and likelihood of possible outcomes of choice alternatives and integrate this information through some type of expectation-based calculus to arrive at a decision. The authors propose an alternative theoretical perspective, the risk-as-feelings hypothesis, that highlights the role of affect experienced at the moment of decision making. Drawing on research from clinical, physiological, and other subfields of psychology, they show that emotional reactions to risky situations often diverge from cognitive assessments of those risks. When such divergence occurs, emotional reactions often drive behavior. The risk-as-feelings hypothesis is shown to explain a wide range of phenomena that have resisted interpretation in cognitive–consequentialist terms.

## Summary
This is a wide-ranging theoretical review (a *Psychological Bulletin* lead article) proposing the **risk-as-feelings hypothesis**: that behavior under risk is driven not only by cognitive evaluations of outcomes and probabilities but also, in part, by *anticipatory* emotions (fear, dread, anxiety, worry) experienced at the moment of decision. Because these feelings respond to a different set of determinants than cognitive risk assessments do, the two systems can diverge—and when they do, emotion often dominates behavior. The authors synthesize evidence from clinical, neuroscientific, social, and decision psychology to argue that consequentialist models (EU, prospect theory, regret/disappointment theories) miss this affective channel.

## Research question
Why do risk-related behaviors so often depart from what cognitive–consequentialist models of choice under risk predict, and what role do emotions experienced *at the moment of decision* (rather than anticipated future emotions) play in generating these departures?

## Method / identification
None empirical in the primary sense—this is a **theoretical synthesis and conceptual framework paper**, not an estimation or single experiment. The contribution is a process model, illustrated as a path diagram (the paper's Figure 3), that augments the standard pipeline. The authors draw two key conceptual distinctions:
- **Anticipated emotions** vs. **anticipatory emotions.** Anticipated emotions are future feelings (e.g., regret, disappointment) that a decision maker forecasts and folds into the expected consequences—these are still cognitive/consequentialist (cf. regret theory). Anticipatory emotions are immediate visceral reactions felt *now*, at the decision moment.
- In consequentialist models, the causal chain runs subjective probabilities $\times$ subjective outcome values $\to$ *cognitive evaluation* $\to$ **decision**, with feelings epiphenomenal. The risk-as-feelings model instead posits two interacting inputs to **behavior** (the term "decision" is deliberately replaced, since panic-type responses are not decisions): (i) the cognitive evaluation, and (ii) anticipatory **feelings** that are fed *directly* (not cortically mediated) by probabilities, outcomes, and additional factors. Cognitive evaluations produce feelings, feelings reciprocally color cognition, and behavior emerges from the interplay—often a conflict—between the two.

Two more "controversial" claims structure the evidence review: (a) feelings can arise **without cognitive mediation** (direct thalamus→amygdala pathway, Zajonc's primacy-of-affect, procaine/amygdala studies), and (b) the effect of cognition on behavior is itself **mediated by affect** (somatic-marker evidence: prefrontal-lesion patients, psychopathy, trait anxiety).

## Data
None of the authors' own beyond two small illustrative studies reported in passing (a self–other risk-preference projection study; a drunk-taxi-driver scenario with 115 students) and a re-analysis of Weber & Hsee (1998) worry ratings. The paper's "evidence" is a **literature review** spanning neuroscience (LeDoux, Damasio/Bechara et al. 1997 Iowa Gambling Task), clinical psychology (anxiety, phobias), and J/DM (Slovic's affect heuristic, Johnson et al. insurance studies).

## Key findings
The synthesis yields several robust regularities the risk-as-feelings view organizes:
- **Affect mediates good risky decision making.** Prefrontal-lesion patients and psychopathic individuals, lacking anticipatory somatic markers (no anticipatory skin-conductance response before risky draws), behave myopically and "go bankrupt" in the Iowa Gambling Task despite understanding it.
- **Insensitivity to probability.** Anticipatory fear/worry responds to the *possibility* rather than the *probability* of a bad outcome—an all-or-none character. In shock-anticipation experiments, arousal tracks shock *intensity* but is roughly flat in probability (except at $p=0$). Worry ratings in Weber & Hsee were sensitive to outcomes but not to probability, $F(1,6634)=1.69, p>.10$, while buying prices were sensitive to both.
- **Vividness effects.** Emotional reactions scale with the vividness of mental imagery (description framing, personal experience), explaining anomalous insurance behavior (willingness to pay more for "terrorist death" than "all-cause death" coverage; chronic under-insurance against floods).
- **Evolutionary preparedness.** Fear conditions rapidly to snakes/spiders/heights but slowly to guns/cars/smoking/unsafe sex, and fear conditioning is highly extinction-resistant—decoupling felt fear from objective hazard.
- **Defensive avoidance:** anxiety induction can backfire (information avoidance), so it is no panacea for risk communication.

## Contribution
Reframes the affect-and-decision literature by emphasizing **divergence** rather than the complementary/informational role stressed by the somatic-marker hypothesis, the affect-as-information hypothesis (Clore & Schwarz), and Slovic's affect heuristic. Its lasting contribution to behavioral economics is the **anticipated/anticipatory** distinction and a parsimonious account of phenomena (probability insensitivity, source/format dependence, panics, self–other gaps) that resist consequentialist explanation. It became a foundational citation for affective and dual-process accounts of risk.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag these explicitly:
- **No causal demonstration.** Correlations between affective deficits (lesions, psychopathy, trait anxiety) and decision myopia "may result from some type of collateral damage to neural systems"—they do not conclusively establish a causal link.
- **Affect is not always beneficial.** Lacking fear can *improve* outcomes when the high-variance option has higher expected value (the framework cannot, on its own, say when emotion helps vs. hurts).
- **Anxiety induction is not a reliable policy lever** (defensive avoidance), leaving open how to design risk communication that mitigates rather than provokes maladaptive responses.
- The framework is qualitative; it lacks a formal functional form mapping probabilities/outcomes/vividness/immediacy into a measurable "feeling state," and predictions about the *interplay* of the two systems are under-specified—open hooks for a structural or computational model.

## Connections
Builds on and critiques expected-utility theory and its descriptive generalizations (Kahneman & Tversky prospect theory; rank- and sign-dependent models surveyed by Starmer (2000)) and the anticipated-emotion theories of regret and disappointment (Bell (1982, 1985); Loomes & Sugden (1982, 1986); Mellers et al. (1997, 1999)). It situates itself against the complementary affect theories of Damasio (1994) and Bechara et al. (1997) (somatic markers), Clore & Schwarz (affect-as-information), and Slovic et al. (affect heuristic), and against the affect-primacy work of Zajonc (1980) and LeDoux (1996). Dual-process antecedents include Sloman (1996) and Epstein et al. The myopic-loss-aversion examples connect to [[@Benartzi1995|Benartzi & Thaler (1995)]], Thaler et al. (1997), and Gneezy & Potters (1997). The visceral-factors program is Loewenstein's own (Loewenstein (1996, 1999)). Downstream, this paper anchors later work on emotion and economic decision making and connects naturally to social-preference and choice-modeling debates over whether observed risk attitudes reflect stable utility curvature or context-dependent affective responses.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> This chapter proposes an analysis of the role of advertising in the transmission of information in markets. It also describes how the economic analysis of informative advertising provides a satisfactory account of advertising practices and discusses the extent to which resorting to alternative approaches to advertising might be fruitful. In doing so, it provides an overview of what has been identified in the literature as the main incentives of firms to resort to advertising as well as the main arguments pertaining to the welfare economics of advertising. The chapter provides some simplified expositions of the various theories and describes some related empirical literature. The exposition starts with a presentation of the analysis of informative advertising. It is first explained how the informative role of advertising can be understood from the theory of search with particular attention to price advertising. Advertising that contains direct product information is then considered, looking at the nature and the amount of such information provided by advertisers and including some considerations on legal restraints on misleading advertising. Finally, the argument that advertising may provide indirect information in the form of quality signaling or as a coordination device is described and discussed. The chapter then moves on to an analysis of the technology through which advertising conveys information to consumers, considering in turn advertising costs, targeting, and information congestion. The perspective is expanded in the final part of the chapter by allowing advertising to have some role other than the transmission of information. In doing so, the welfare implications of advertising are reconsidered while accounting for a potentially persuasive role of advertising or viewing advertising as a good that complements the advertised product in the consumer's preferences. The strict consumer rationality assumption is also relaxed. The exposition ends with the dynamics of advertising resulting from its role in the accumulation of goodwill for a product or a brand.

## Summary

This is a handbook chapter (Handbook of Media Economics, Vol. 1A, Ch. 4) surveying the economics of advertising, organized around the premise that advertising's central function is the transmission of information to buyers. Renault asks how much of observed advertising behavior and its welfare consequences can be explained while treating consumers as rational agents with stable tastes and advertising as purely informative, then asks what alternative (persuasive, complementary-good, behavioral) views add. The chapter moves through four informative-advertising paradigms — search-and-price advertising (Stigler/Diamond), direct product/match information and disclosure (Nelson, persuasion games), quality signaling and coordination (Nelson again, money-burning), and the advertising technology itself (costs, targeting, congestion) — before relaxing the strict informative and rationality assumptions to consider persuasion, consumer naivety, and goodwill dynamics.

## Research question

How much of real-world advertising practice and its welfare implications can be accounted for by treating advertising solely as a device for transmitting information to consumers with imperfect information, and how much additional explanatory power is gained by alternative (persuasive, complementary, behavioral) approaches? A secondary question is normative: is advertising over- or under-provided relative to the social optimum?

## Method / identification

A theoretical survey that builds simplified, self-contained versions of canonical models rather than reproducing them in full generality. Renault repeatedly uses a unifying workhorse: a monopoly seller with constant marginal cost $c\in[0,1)$ facing a single unit-demand buyer whose willingness to pay $r$ is the realization of a random variable, initially $r\sim U[0,1]$ and unknown to both parties.

- **Search and price advertising.** Stigler's (1961) consumer search for a low price under price dispersion, and the Diamond (1971) paradox showing that with positive search costs and no advertising the market unravels to the monopoly price (or fully). Advertising enters by enlarging the set of options a consumer is aware of, and the search–advertising interaction generates equilibrium price dispersion with no search on path.
- **Match advertising (4.3.3).** Ads are noisy signals about how well a product matches the buyer's taste; the firm chooses signal informativeness $\alpha$. In the Lewis–Sappington (1994) reduction, the buyer observes $\sigma\in[0,1]$ equal to true $r$ with probability $\alpha$ and uniform-independent with probability $1-\alpha$; posterior expected willingness to pay is $\alpha\sigma+\tfrac{1}{2}(1-\alpha)$. The firm's problem is analyzed graphically à la Johnson and Myatt (2006) by pivoting the inverse demand around the point $(\tfrac12,\tfrac12)$; the optimum is a corner, $\alpha\in\{0,1\}$ (full disclosure or none).
- **Disclosure / persuasion games (4.3.4).** Both sides hold private information (consumer knows taste, firm knows attributes), so disclosure is a signaling game. For pure vertical quality the **unraveling** result holds: full revelation is the unique equilibrium. For horizontal match attributes, Koessler and Renault (2012) show a fully revealing equilibrium always exists when firm and consumer types are independent (sustained by worst-case off-path beliefs), but multiplicity is generic; **pairwise monotonicity** of the match function $r(s,t)$ is the sufficient (and essentially necessary) condition for full disclosure to be the unique outcome.
- **Signaling / money-burning (4.4).** Following Nelson, advertising on experience goods conveys indirect information through dissipative expenditure (money burning) and repeat-purchase incentives, plus a coordination role.
- **Cheap talk / misleading ads (4.3.5).** Credible cheap-talk revelation requires expected profit constant across types (e.g., products on a circle with uniform consumer locations); Chakraborty and Harbaugh (2014)-style comparative cheap talk works because praising one attribute implies adverse inference on another.
- **Technology and behavior (4.5–4.6).** Advertising costs and reach, targeted advertising, information congestion; then persuasive and complementary-good views, consumer naivety, and goodwill accumulation dynamics.

## Data

None — this is a theoretical survey. It does, however, summarize empirical "content analysis" of ad informativeness: Resnik and Stern's (1977) 14 information-cue categories; Abernethy and Franke's (1996) finding of ~1.06 cues per US TV ad (37.5% with no category); and Anderson et al.'s (2013) coded dataset of US over-the-counter analgesic TV ads, finding higher-quality products and comparative ads carry more cues while large-share and large-generic-counterpart brands carry fewer.

## Key findings

- **Diamond paradox.** Without advertising and with positive search costs, equilibrium collapses to the monopoly price or full unraveling; advertising is therefore socially beneficial as a search-cost-reducing device (Stigler).
- **Corner solution in match informativeness.** With a single uninformed buyer, the firm optimally chooses either fully informative or fully uninformative ads, never interior $\alpha$.
- **Unraveling for vertical quality** vs. **pervasive multiplicity for horizontal match.** Quality is always fully revealed; horizontal-attribute disclosure admits pooling equilibria unless the match function is pairwise monotone (Koessler and Renault, 2012).
- **Competition can reduce disclosure.** Board (2009): a vertically differentiated duopolist may hide even high quality to soften price competition when disclosure and pricing are sequential; comparative advertising and simultaneous pricing restore disclosure (Janssen and Teteryanikova, 2014).
- **Welfare ambiguity.** Advertising is socially valuable but not optimally provided: under-provided when consumer surplus is non-appropriable and there is a market-expansion effect, but excessive when it is "combative" (Marshall) — merely reshuffling market shares via the market-stealing externality. With product differentiation the net direction is indeterminate. Advertisers' willingness to pay for ad space does not track the social benefit, complicating welfare analysis of media markets.

## Contribution

Provides a unified, search-centric synthesis of the informative-advertising literature that complements [[@Bagwell2007|Bagwell (2007)]] by foregrounding the link between consumer search and advertising and by incorporating post-2007 work on strategic product-information disclosure (Section 4.3.4). It clarifies that the classic opposition between the "persuasive" (anti-competitive, welfare-reducing) and "informative" (pro-competitive, welfare-enhancing) views is partly unfounded: even purely informative advertising can be combative and wasteful, and can enhance market power by sharpening perceived differentiation.

## Limitations & open questions

- The informative view "involves great strategic complexity," presupposes substantial consumer sophistication, and yields indeterminacy through equilibrium multiplicity — an explicit unresolved tension.
- It overemphasizes price advertising, which fits poorly with much observed advertising, and is better suited to new products than to established brands — motivating persuasive, complementary-good, and consumer-naivety approaches the chapter flags as worth pursuing despite their conceptual difficulties.
- Ordered/directed consumer search (vs. the standard random-search assumption) is identified as a growing but unsettled frontier, especially for internet and sponsored-search advertising.
- Specifying how disclosure costs depend on the "quantity" or nature of revealed information remains unresolved.
- The welfare evaluation of advertising within media markets is left open because the private cost of ad space may diverge from its social cost.

Ordered-search connections include Arbatskaya (2007), Armstrong, Vickers and Zhou (2009), Zhou (2011), and Anderson and Renault (2015).

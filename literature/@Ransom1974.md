## Summary
This is a short (three-page) book review by Harry Howe Ransom, published in *The Journal of Politics* (1974), of Irving L. Janis's *Victims of Groupthink: A Psychological Study of Foreign-Policy Decisions and Fiascoes* (1972). The reviewed book introduces the now-canonical concept of "groupthink" - a decision-making pathology in which concurrence-seeking within cohesive groups degrades rational deliberation. Ransom summarizes Janis's central hypothesis, his six case studies, and his catalogue of procedural defects, while flagging a core methodological weakness: the analysis tends toward tautology and lacks objective criteria for classifying decisions as good or bad.

## Research question
The reviewed work (Janis) asks: what produces good or bad policy decisions, and specifically how do the internal dynamics of cohesive small groups cause "incredibly gross miscalculations about both the practical and moral consequences of their decisions"? Ransom's own question as a reviewer is evaluative: is "groupthink" a defensible analytical construct relative to competing models of foreign-policy decision-making?

## Method / identification
The reviewed book is a qualitative, comparative case-study design rather than a formal model or estimator. Janis defines groupthink as "a deterioration of mental efficiency, reality testing, and moral judgment that results from in-group pressures." The proposed mechanism is a "hidden agenda" oriented toward preserving friendly intergroup relations, which crowds out independent critical thinking. Janis advances a quasi-Parkinsonian proposition: the more amiability and esprit de corps within a policy-making in-group, the greater the danger that independent critical thinking is replaced by groupthink, with irrational, dehumanizing actions directed against out-groups. Identification rests on contrasting matched "fiasco" cases against "success" cases and reading off the presence/absence of concurrence-seeking pathologies. Ransom notes the design sits at the intersection of social psychology, political science, and history. The review itself is interpretive textual criticism, citing page numbers from the book.

## Data
None of its own - this is a book review. The underlying data in Janis's book are six historical case studies of US national-security decisions: four fiascoes (the 1941 Pearl Harbor failure, the 1950 invasion of North Korea, the 1961 Bay of Pigs, and the escalation of the Vietnam War from 1964) and two successes (the 1948 Marshall Plan for European recovery and the 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis).

## Key findings
The review reports Janis's principal claims. (1) The fiasco cases were all group products marked by gross miscalculation; the success cases featured realistic appraisals, independent critical thinking, and "hardheaded" deliberation. (2) Janis identifies at least six procedural defects of faulty decision-making: failure to survey the full range of alternatives; inattention to nonobvious risks and drawbacks; inattention to courses of action initially judged to carry prohibitive costs; neglecting to seek expert advice; downgrading advice or information that challenges preferred positions; and inadequate attention to expected implementation difficulties. (3) These defects motivate prescriptive remedies for avoiding groupthink, which Janis develops at length. Ransom's own evaluative finding is that "a tautological tendency pervades this work, to wit: bad decisions are the consequence of bad decision-making procedures," and that Janis pays inadequate attention to objectifying the criteria for labeling decisions good or bad.

## Contribution
As a review, the piece situates and appraises Janis's contribution rather than making an original one. Ransom credits the groupthink construct with offering "valuable new insights for both teachers and researchers" and frames it as a useful supplement to - not a replacement for - existing decision-making analyses. He explicitly positions it alongside the "rational actor," "governmental structure," and "bureaucratic politics" models, denying that groupthink is "a single analytical key" to foreign-policy decision-making.

## Limitations & open questions
The review names several explicit open problems in the reviewed work. (1) Tautology: bad outcomes are inferred from bad procedures and vice versa, with no independent measure. (2) The unresolved problem of objectifying criteria for classifying policy decisions as good or bad - a measurement/identification gap. (3) Data adequacy: Janis himself is "sensitive to the difficulties involved in gaining adequate data to prove his experimental hypothesis or to develop a good theory." (4) Confounding: the procedural defects "could also result from stupidity, fatigue, prejudice, or other causes," so attributing them specifically to groupthink is not established. (5) The broader challenge that an adequate theory of decision-making will require integrating social psychology, political science, and history.

## Connections
The review explicitly positions Janis's groupthink against competing models of foreign-policy decision-making, naming Richard Neustadt, Graham Allison (the "rational actor" / "governmental structure" / "bureaucratic politics" models of Allison's *Essence of Decision*), Alexander George, and Morton Halperin. The tautology critique - inferring decision quality from procedure - connects to broader methodological debates on outcome- versus process-based evaluation of decisions. The underlying themes of in-group pressure, concurrence-seeking, and conformity link to the social-psychology tradition on conformity (Asch) and group polarization, and to later work on collective decision-making and information aggregation in committees. For an economics-of-social-preferences reader, the in-group/out-group dynamics and the trade-off between group cohesion and decision accuracy connect to literatures on identity, conformity, and the welfare costs of social pressure in group choice.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> A claim has been that 97% of the scientific literature endorses anthropogenic climate change (Cook et al., 2013. Environ. Res. Lett. 8, 024024). This claim, frequently repeated in debates about climate policy, does not stand. A trend in composition is mistaken for a trend in endorsement. Reported results are inconsistent and biased. The sample is not representative and contains many irrelevant papers. Overall, data quality is low. Cook's validation test shows that the data are invalid. Data disclosure is incomplete so that key results cannot be reproduced or tested.

## Summary
A short communication in *Energy Policy* re-analyzing the widely cited Cook et al. (2013) "97% consensus" study. Tol does not dispute that the climate-science literature overwhelmingly supports anthropogenic global warming, but argues the *measurement* by Cook et al. is unsound: the sample is unrepresentative, the abstract ratings are error-prone and internally inconsistent, most papers counted are irrelevant to the *causes* of climate change, and the headline time trend reflects a shift in the composition of the literature rather than rising endorsement. He concludes the 97% figure is not supported by the data and that incomplete data disclosure prevents replication.

## Research question
Does the dataset and rating procedure of Cook et al. (2013) actually support the quantitative claim that ~97% of the climate-change literature endorses anthropogenic global warming? More specifically: is their sample representative, are the abstract/paper ratings reliable and consistent, are the surveyed papers relevant to the attribution question, and is the reported upward trend in endorsement real?

## Method / identification
This is a re-analysis / replication study rather than a model-based paper. Cook et al.'s procedure: a Web of Science query on "global climate change" or "global warming" (English, 1991–2011) yielded 11,944 abstracts after cleaning, each rated by volunteers on a 1–7 scale from explicit quantified endorsement (1) to explicit quantified rejection (7), with 4 = no position. Tol attacks this on several fronts:

- **Representativeness checks.** He re-runs the query (finding 13,458 papers in 2013), drops "global" to recover a larger population of 53,359 papers, and repeats the search in Scopus (20,772 papers, 54% more than Web of Science). He compares disciplinary composition of narrow vs. broad samples with $\chi^2$ tests (e.g., $\chi^2_{108}=4076$, $p<0.001$), and examines coverage of the 100 most prolific authors and 50 most cited papers.
- **Bias / consistency diagnostics on the rating time series.** Treating the 24,273 abstract ratings as a sequence, he computes 50-, 100-, and 500-abstract rolling standard deviation, first-order autocorrelation (fatigue tests), and rolling average and skewness (drift tests). He bootstraps 10,000 times to build 95% confidence intervals and tabulates how often indicators fall outside those intervals (Table 1).
- **Inter-rater / abstract-vs-paper agreement.** He compares abstract ratings to author "paper" ratings using Cohen's $\kappa$ with linear, quadratic, cubic and quartic weights ($\kappa_0=10\%$, $\kappa_1=16\%$, $\kappa_2=22\%$, $\kappa_3=26\%$, $\kappa_4=26\%$), and tests subsample representativeness with $\chi^2$.
- **Classification / relevance reclassification.** He argues impacts and mitigation papers do not bear on the *causes* of warming and should be neutral, then recomputes consensus rates after reclassifying.

## Data
Cook et al.'s released data: year, title, journal, authors, classification, the 1st–4th ratings, reconciled rating, and author rating for 11,944 abstracts and 2,142 (2,136 released) paper ratings. Tol supplements with his own Web of Science and Scopus query re-runs (2012–2013). Withheld and requested-in-vain: rater ID, time of rating, survey protocol, and lab notes — whose absence blocks fatigue/drift attribution to individuals and prevents full reproduction.

## Key findings
- **Sampling is unrepresentative in both directions.** The narrow query excludes ~75% of climate papers, undersamples relevant disciplines (meteorology, geosciences, physical geography, oceanography) and the most prolific authors, but oversamples the most-cited papers (only 17 of the top 50 cited appear). Scopus comparisons show opposite biases. Net: sample is unrepresentative, but the *direction* of bias is unknown.
- **Rating data show fatigue and drift.** Rolling averages, standard deviations, autocorrelation and skewness fall outside bootstrap confidence intervals far more often than chance; early ratings skew toward endorsement, later toward rejection. Data are not homoskedastic and autocorrelation is non-zero — the survey instrument is inconsistent.
- **Errors are sizable and biased.** Taking Cook's own ~33% disagreement rate, roughly 7% of ratings are wrong; reconciliation/re-rating was biased toward rejection. Abstract vs. paper ratings disagree heavily (63% differ; $\kappa_0=10\%$), and the author-rated subsample is unrepresentative.
- **Most papers are off-topic.** ~34.6% of papers that should be neutral were rated non-neutral (99.4% as endorsements). Correcting, ~95% of surveyed papers are silent on attribution; explicit dissent rises from ~3% to up to ~10%.
- **The trend is compositional.** Endorsement does not trend up within either impacts/mitigation or methods/palaeoclimate; instead the *share* of impacts/mitigation papers rises over time ($p=0.00003$), creating an apparent but spurious trend in overall consensus.
- **Failed own validation test and incomplete disclosure** mean the headline result is not reproducible.

## Contribution
Provides a quantitative, diagnostics-driven critique of the most-cited single estimate of scientific consensus on climate change, distinguishing a *trend in composition* from a *trend in endorsement* and demonstrating that consensus measurement is sensitive to sampling frame, rater fatigue/drift, and the relevance of surveyed papers. Notably, Tol affirms the substantive consensus while rejecting the specific measurement, framing flawed advocacy analyses as counterproductive to climate policy.

## Limitations & open questions
- Direction of the sampling bias is left undetermined — Tol cannot say whether corrections raise or lower the "true" rate.
- Key diagnostics (per-rater profiles, inter-rating time histograms) could not be run because rater IDs, timestamps, protocol and lab notes were withheld; reproducing or fully testing Cook's results remains impossible.
- Whether the disputed 4th rating round ("4a/4b") was an ad hoc addition that invalidates the dissensus adjustment is unresolved absent a published protocol.
- The paper itself is openly normative in places (e.g., on the value of "consensus" in science/policy), inviting scrutiny of its own framing; it offers no alternative model of how consensus *should* be quantified, leaving open the design of a defensible consensus-measurement instrument.

## Connections

(2010), and the meta-discussion of consensus in science and policy (Anderegg et al., 2010a; Bray, 2010; Grundmann, 2007; O'Neill and Boykoff, 2010; Poortinga et al., 2011; Schulte, 2008). On survey best practice it draws on Mohler et al. (2008), Lyberg and Biemer (2008), and Singer (2008). Methodologically it is an exercise in replication and measurement-validity auditing — kin to broader concerns about reproducibility and researcher degrees of freedom in empirical work, and to the use of inter-rater reliability statistics (Cohen's $\kappa$) in content analysis.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Decision analysis requires that two equally desirable consequences should have the same utility and vice versa. Most analyses of financial decision making presume that two consequences with the same dollar outcome will be equally preferred However, winning the top prize of $10,000 in a lottery may leave one much happier than receiving $10,000 as the lowest prize in a lottery. This paper explores the implications of disappointment, a psychological reaction caused by comparing the actual outcome of a lottery to one's prior expectations, for decision making under uncertainty. Explicit recognition that decision makers may be paying a premium to avoid potential disappointment provides an interpretation for some known behavioral paradoxes, and suggests that decision makers may be sensitive to the manner in which a lottery is resolved. The concept of disappointment is integrated into utility theory in a prescriptive model.

## Summary
Bell formalizes *disappointment* (and its positive counterpart, *elation*) as a psychological reaction to comparing a realized outcome against one's prior expectation of a lottery, and shows that a decision maker willing to pay dollars to avoid disappointment will behave in ways that look like risk aversion even under constant marginal value for money. The resulting utility-of-money form coincides with a Kahneman-Tversky-style probability-weighting model $\pi(p)$, explains the Allais common-ratio effect and Ellsberg's paradox, and crucially predicts that the *method* by which a lottery is resolved (one stage vs. sequential stages) is not a matter of indifference. This is a companion to Bell's regret work and an explicitly *prescriptive* contribution: it argues that psychological satisfaction is a legitimate decision criterion, not merely a descriptive anomaly to be patched into normative theory.

## Research question
Can the well-documented violations of expected-utility theory (Allais-type common-ratio violations, Ellsberg ambiguity aversion, sensitivity to how a gamble is staged) be rationalized by a single, economically interpretable psychological factor — the premium a decision maker pays to avoid the disappointment of an outcome falling short of expectations — while holding the marginal value of money constant? And what are the prescriptive implications (for staging gambles, breaking news, and bidding in auctions)?

## Method / identification
This is a normative/prescriptive modelling paper, not an empirical one. Bell builds a two-attribute utility model: total utility = economic payoff + psychological satisfaction (positive for elation, negative for disappointment), assumed linear and additive. Marginal value for money is held constant, and regret, envy, and loss-over-the-status-quo are deliberately excluded.

**Simple model (Section 1).** For a binary gamble $(x,p,y)$ with $x\succeq y$ and psychological expectation $px+(1-p)y$, disappointment on receiving $y$ is $d\,p(x-y)$ and elation on receiving $x$ is $e\,(1-p)(x-y)$, with constants $d,e>0$. This yields the certainty equivalent
$$px+(1-p)y+(e-d)\,p(1-p)(x-y).$$
When $d>e$ the agent is risk averse despite linear consumption value; setting $k=d-e$, the implied utility of money is $u(x)=\big(k-1+\sqrt{(1-k)^2+4kx}\big)/2k$, which is increasing, concave, and exhibits decreasing risk aversion.

**Axiomatic model (Section 2).** Eleven assumptions are imposed on preferences over three states: owning the unresolved lottery $L_0$, the winning outcome $L_1$, and the losing outcome $L_2$. Key axioms are Simple Orderings, Sure-thing Indifference, Monotonicity (in win-probability and in prize value), Solvability, Constant Marginal Value (Assumption 6), and Risk Neutrality in the Absence of Disappointment (Assumption 7: $c_0=p\,c_1+(1-p)\,c_2$). Section 3 gives an interview-based assessment scheme for the elation/disappointment functions $w(p)$ and $l(p)$, with the dollar-vs-psychology tradeoffs concentrated in the two constants $w(1/2)$ and $l(1/2)$. Section 4 adds Backward Substitution (Assumption 9), Maximize-the-Maximum-Elation (Assumption 10), and Additive Psychological Effects (Assumption 11) to handle multi-stage resolution.

## Data
None — this is a theoretical paper. Bell calibrates illustratively against existing empirical findings rather than collecting data: the [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman-Tversky (1979)]] $3000/4000 common-ratio choices imply $-0.65 < e-d < -0.31$; the Preston-Baratta (1948) probability-weighting data put the risk-neutral breakeven at $p^*\approx 1/4$, implying $d=3e$ with $e\approx 0.1$.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1.** Under Assumptions 1, 2, 5, 6, 7, each state has a certainty equivalent $y+(x-y)\pi_i(p)$. Writing $\pi\equiv\pi_0$, this matches the prospect-theory form $v(y)+\pi(p)\big(v(x)-v(y)\big)$, with $\pi(p)$ interpretable as a *behavioral subjective probability*. The model differs from prospect theory in making no special treatment of the status quo / gains-vs-losses.
- **Common-ratio effect.** The simple model reproduces the Allais common-ratio violation *iff* $d>e$ (disappointment-aversion dominates elation-seeking).
- **Theorem 2 (risk proneness).** Under Assumption 8 there is a threshold $p^*$ such that the agent is risk averse for $p>p^*$, risk neutral at $p^*$, and *risk prone* for $0<p<p^*$. Ever being risk prone requires $w(0)+l'(1)>0$ (i.e. $e>d$ in the simple form). This unifies risk-prone behaviour with the everyday tendency of uncommitted spectators/voters to **support the underdog** — both come from buying the chance of elation while capping downside disappointment.
- **Theorems 3-5 (staging matters).** A two-stage gamble $((1,q,0),p,0)$ has certainty equivalent $\pi(p)\pi(q)$ (Theorem 3); independent gambles resolved sequentially add their certainty equivalents and order is irrelevant (Theorem 4). The common-ratio effect and Assumption 10 (Maximize the Maximum Elation) have equivalent implications for $\pi$ (Theorem 5). Since $\pi(pq)>\pi(p)\pi(q)$, the agent prefers a $(1,pq,0)$ gamble resolved **all at once** rather than in stages.
- **Ellsberg's paradox.** Reinterpreting the ambiguous urn as a two-stage lottery, the unambiguous urn is preferred iff $\pi(1/2)<1/2$ (equivalently $w(1/2)<l(1/2)$) — exactly the disappointment-dominates-elation condition.
- **Breaking news.** Bad news should be broken *gently* (in stages) and good news *abruptly* (all at once), governed by whether $f(x)/(1-x)$ is decreasing for $f\in\{w,l\}$; these hold under the odds model.
- **Auctions.** Disappointment lowers the optimal bid in a sealed-bid auction (low-probability/high-payoff prospects become relatively more attractive) but *raises* the price one will pay in a Dutch auction (as the descending price nears your value, rising expectations make losing painful — a lever exploited by expert negotiators). Ranking auctions by kindness to the psyche, the **English auction** dominates, and a *blind* English auction may be best of all.

## Contribution
Bell isolates disappointment as a distinct, economically interpretable component of risk aversion, separate from decreasing marginal utility and from regret (his earlier work, and Loomes-Sugden 1982). The paper (i) gives an axiomatic derivation that recovers a prospect-theory-like probability weighting $\pi(p)$ from explicit psychological primitives; (ii) offers a unified account of the common-ratio effect, risk proneness at small $p$, underdog support, and Ellsberg's paradox; and (iii) opens the largely unstudied normative question of *sensitivity to the method of uncertainty resolution* (staging, breaking news, auction design). It frames these psychological reactions as legitimate prescriptive criteria rather than mere descriptive deviations to be retrofitted into normative theory.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Scope restrictions.** The analysis holds marginal value of money constant and excludes regret, envy, and status-quo loss-aversion; Bell flags a promised future paper integrating decreasing marginal value with multiple reference effects.
- **Expectation formation is left implicit.** The model deliberately avoids specifying how prior expectations are formed (mean? mode? certainty equivalent? optimist vs pessimist), assuming only that expectations are linear in payoffs.
- **The simple linear model is admittedly too poor.** Figures 3-4 suggest disappointment/elation depend not just on expectation level but directly on the *likelihood* of the outcome; the odds-based variant implies implausibly *infinite* disappointment as $p\to 0$ (e.g. rare catastrophes / "nuclear power?"), so extreme-probability results need caution.
- **Two-outcome restriction.** Results are derived for binary gambles; generalization to multi-outcome gambles and to continuous distributions is incomplete, and forms like $(1,p,(1,q,0))$ "require quite different algebra."
- **Assessment is non-routine and untestable.** Eliciting $w$ and $l$ requires comparing *outcomes and their psychological consequences*, which Bell concedes is not testable in any satisfactory way and admits internal inconsistencies (the Schelling work-late example).
- **Descriptive realism is uncertain.** Bell doubts that subjects in hypothetical lab experiments become emotionally involved enough for disappointment to be a primary motivator.

## Connections
The paper sits at the intersection of *regret theory* (Bell 1982, 1983; Loomes and Sugden 1982) and *prospect theory* ([[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman and Tversky 1979]]): regret compares the outcome to a foregone alternative, disappointment compares it to one's own prior expectation, and Bell explicitly contrasts his $\pi(p)$ with the prospect-theory weighting function and value function. It builds on classical expected-utility and decision-analysis foundations (von Neumann-Morgenstern; Keeney and Raiffa 1976; Keeney 1982; Pratt 1964 on risk premia; Raiffa 1968), and on relative-risk-attitude ideas of Dyer and Sarin (1982). It engages the paradoxes of Allais (via the common-ratio effect) and Ellsberg (1961), invokes Machina (1981), Stone (1973) on generalized risk measures, and the auction literature (Engelbrecht-Wiggans 1980). The empirical anchors are Preston and Baratta (1948) on probability weighting and the business risk-taking studies of Wehrung et al. (1978) and MacCrimmon and Wehrung (1983). Together with Loomes-Sugden, this is a foundational reference for the modern literature on reference-dependent and expectation-based preferences (a direct ancestor of Kőszegi-Rabin-style expectations-based reference points).

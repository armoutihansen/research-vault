> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper considers the role of reasons and arguments in the making of decisions. It is proposed that, when faced with the need to choose, decision makers often seek and construct reasons in order to resolve the conflict and justify their choice, to themselves and to others. Experiments that explore and manipulate the role of reasons are reviewed, and other decision studies are interpreted from this perspective. The role of reasons in decision making is considered as it relates to uncertainty, conflict, context effects, and normative decision rules.

## Summary

Shafir, Simonson, and Tversky propose **reason-based choice** as an alternative to value-maximization accounts of decision making: rather than assigning a number $u(x)$ to each option and choosing the maximizer, people search for and construct *reasons* for and against the available alternatives, and choose the option for which the balance of reasons is most compelling and easiest to justify. The paper synthesizes a battery of experiments (mostly the authors' own) showing systematic violations of value-based axioms — choose/reject asymmetries, conflict-driven search and deferral, context effects (asymmetric dominance, extremeness aversion), the disjunction effect, and the influence of irrelevant "non-valued" features — each of which is hard to reconcile with stable utilities but natural once choice is seen as governed by reasons. The recurring theme is that preferences are *constructed*, not merely revealed, and that framing/context matter because they bring different reasons to mind.

## Research question

Can the qualitative, "reason-based" tradition of explaining decisions (history, law, business) be turned into a testable account of *experimental* choice behavior? Specifically: when people face conflict, do they choose by maximizing value, or by seeking compelling reasons that justify one option over another — and if the latter, what predictable anomalies follow?

## Method / identification

The paper is a **theoretical synthesis built on controlled choice experiments** (largely between-subjects, hypothetical and some real-payoff). The central object is the contrast between the value-maximization benchmark and the reason-based hypothesis, each operationalized through manipulations that the two accounts predict differently. Key designs:

- **Choose vs. reject (compatibility).** An *enriched* option (more positive and more negative features) versus an *impoverished* one (few salient features). Let $P_c$ and $P_r$ be the percentages choosing and rejecting a given option. Under value maximization choosing and rejecting are complementary, so $P_c + P_r = 100$. The reason-based hypothesis — positive features loom larger when choosing, negative features when rejecting — predicts $P_c + P_r > 100$ for the enriched option and $<100$ for the impoverished one. This is a clean test of **procedure invariance**.
- **Search under conflict ([[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Shafir 1992b)]].** Subjects choose between two options or pay a fee to add a random option. The *conflict* pair (each option better on one dimension) is contrasted with a *dominance* pair (one option strictly dominates). Since the best dominance option is also present in the conflict set, value maximization implies the search rate cannot be *higher* under conflict.
- **Adding options (regularity / IIA).** Tests of the **regularity condition** — adding an option cannot raise another option's market share — via high- vs. low-conflict CD-player sets (with a defer option), real-prize choices (Zebra/Pilot pens), asymmetric dominance (Cross pen + decoy; microwaves), and extremeness aversion (Minolta cameras: a middle option more popular in the trinary set).
- **Disjunctive reasons ([[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Shafir 1992a)]].** Pass/fail/disjunctive vacation problem and win/lose/disjunctive gambles, testing the **sure-thing principle**: subjects who would take the action under each resolved outcome nonetheless decline (or pay to defer) when the outcome is unknown.
- **Non-valued features (Simonson et al.).** Adding an irrelevant "positive" feature (a previous respondent's inapplicable reason; a worthless bonus like a collector's plate) to test whether value-neutral additions still shift choice.

Identification rests on the qualitative *direction* of the manipulation: each design is constructed so value maximization makes a sharp prediction (complementarity, monotone search, regularity, sure-thing) that the data violate, with significance tests ($z$-tests, $p$-values) on the aggregate choice shares.

## Data

Original lab experiments on undergraduate / business-school subjects, sample sizes ranging roughly $n \approx 60$–$172$ per problem (e.g., custody problem $n=170$, vacation $n=172$, CD players $n=124$ and $121$, pen study $n=80$). Mix of hypothetical scenarios and incentivized real-payoff choices (gambles played out; real prizes/pens awarded). No econometric dataset — the paper aggregates many small designed experiments, several previously reported by the authors elsewhere.

## Key findings

- **Choose/reject discrepancy.** For the enriched option, $P_c + P_r$ significantly exceeds 100: the custody Parent B was the majority choice both for being *awarded* (64%) and *denied* (55%) custody ($64+55=119$, $z=2.48$, $p<.02$); vacation Spot B likewise ($67+48=115$, $p<.05$; 67% preferred vs. 52% canceled, $p<.001$). A predictable failure of procedure invariance.
- **Conflict raises search.** Subjects requested an extra option 64% of the time under conflict vs. 40% under dominance ($p<.05$) — inconsistent with value maximization, since the dominant option was available in both.
- **Regularity violations.** Adding a competing CD player raised the deferral rate (high-conflict 46% wait vs. low-conflict 34%); the Zebra was chosen 75% alone but only 47% when the Pilot was added ($p<.05$). **Asymmetric dominance**: a decoy raised the Cross pen's share 36%→46%; **extremeness aversion**: the middle Minolta camera was chosen by 57% in the trinary set despite an even split in binary choice.
- **Disjunction effect / sure-thing violation.** Students bought the Hawaii vacation if they knew they passed (54%) or failed (57%), but only 32% bought when the outcome was unknown, and 61% paid $5 to defer. In gambles, accepting after a win (69%) and after a loss (59%) but rejecting under disjunction (36%); 27% of subjects showed the accept/accept-but-reject-disjunction pattern — a direct violation of Savage's sure-thing principle. The effect disappears when *the same* reason applies regardless of outcome (modified all-win gamble: ~73% in disjunction).
- **Non-valued features hurt.** An inapplicable reason lowered choice of Northwestern (43%→23%, $p<.05$); a worthless free collector's plate lowered cake-mix choice (31%→14%, $p<.05$). Value-neutral additions still supply a reason *against* an option.
- **Normative rules as reasons.** Axioms (transitivity, dominance, sure-thing) are obeyed when their applicability is *transparent* — i.e., they act as compelling arguments, not as universal constraints.

## Contribution

The paper crystallizes **reason-based choice** as a unifying lens for a wide class of preference anomalies and articulates the **constructed-preference** view: framing, context, and elicitation matter precisely because they surface different reasons. It links to Tversky & Simonson's formal componential-context model as a quantitative analog of the qualitative account, and reframes the rationality axioms as detectable arguments. It has become a foundational reference for behavioral economics on context effects, justification, and choice deferral.

## Limitations & open questions

The authors are explicit that reasons are a slippery primitive: *"almost anything can be counted as a reason, so that every decision may be rationalized after the fact,"* and self-reported reasons may diverge from the actual determinants of choice (Nisbett & Wilson 1977), so they define reasons as factors affecting choice "whether or not they can be articulated." They concede the approach is "qualitative in nature... difficult to apply... and typically vague" relative to formal models, and that whether the formal or qualitative analysis is more useful "is likely to depend on the nature of the problem." They flag that reasoning is not universal — some choices stem from affect (Zajonc) or standard operating procedures, and over-deliberation can yield inferior decisions (Wilson & Schooler 1991). Finally: *"The nature of the reasons that guide decision, and the ways in which they interact, await further investigation"* — leaving open the central project of a predictive theory of *which* reasons get generated in a given context, and how they aggregate.

## Connections

Builds directly on Tversky and Kahneman's framing work and prospect theory ([[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky 1979)]] and on elicitation/compatibility effects (Tversky, Sattath & Slovic 1988; Slovic 1975 on the more-important-dimension heuristic). The context-effect strand draws on [[@Huber1982|Huber, Payne & Puto]] (1982, asymmetric dominance), [[@Simonson1989|Simonson]] (1989, extremeness aversion / justification), and [[@Simonson1992|Simonson & Tversky]] (1992, tradeoff contrast), formalized by Tversky & Simonson's componential-context model. The disjunction effect and sure-thing violations come from [[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Shafir (1992a)]] and [[@Simonson1992|Shafir & Tversky]] (1992, one-shot prisoner's dilemma), challenging Savage (1954). The constructed-preference theme connects to Payne, Bettman & Johnson (1992) and Slovic's later work. The opening Benjamin Franklin "moral algebra" quote frames the pro/con weighing that the paper shows is *not* invariant to choosing vs. rejecting.

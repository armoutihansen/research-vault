## Summary
Tirole's *The Theory of Industrial Organization* (MIT Press, 1988) is the canonical graduate textbook that recast industrial organization as an application of modern game theory and information economics, displacing the older structure-conduct-performance tradition. It systematically develops monopoly pricing, price discrimination, vertical control, oligopoly (Bertrand, Cournot, differentiated products), dynamic price competition and tacit collusion, entry/accommodation/exit, strategic behavior under asymmetric information (limit pricing, predation, reputation), R&D and adoption of new technologies, and a self-contained noncooperative game theory primer. **Coverage note:** the ingested PDF is a partial extract (35 pages) — title page, the complete table of contents, and the whole of Chapter 1, "The Theory of the Firm" (pp. 15-51). The note below is comprehensive on Chapter 1 (read in full, visually, since pdftotext recovered no text) and describes the remaining chapters from the detailed table of contents; the substantive chapters 1-11 themselves were not in the extract.

## Research question
How can the firm and the markets it operates in be modeled rigorously using game theory and the economics of information? Two foundational sub-questions drive the ingested Chapter 1: (i) *What is a firm* — why do some transactions occur inside hierarchies rather than across markets? and (ii) *Is the profit-maximization hypothesis defensible* despite the separation of ownership and control, and does industrial-organization theory survive if managers do not maximize profit?

## Method / identification
The book is a theory text: the method is formal microeconomic modeling under complete and incomplete information, with most chapters resting on noncooperative game theory (Nash, subgame-perfect, Bayesian, and perfect Bayesian equilibrium, summarized in the Chapter 11 "user's manual"). Chapter 1 proceeds through worked models rather than data.

The theory-of-the-firm material is built on the Grossman-Hart-Moore property-rights / incomplete-contracts framework. Two parties (a buyer and a supplier) trade after a noncontractible quality improvement is learned at date 2; the buyer chooses ex ante investment $x$ raising the probability of a valuable improvement, at cost $I=x^2/2$, with second-period value $v>c$. *Authority* (residual rights of control) is the power to fill unspecified contingencies; it shifts the status-quo point in ex post Nash bargaining and therefore reshapes ex ante investment incentives. Under Nash bargaining the final benefits split as
$$B_1 = B_1(d_1^\*) + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[B_1(d^\*)+B_2(d^\*)-B_1(d_1^\*)-B_2(d_1^\*)\bigr].$$
The social optimum solves $\max_x [x(v-c)-x^2/2]$, giving $x^\*=v-c$ and joint surplus $W^\*=(v-c)^2/2$.

The profit-maximization material uses a principal-agent moral-hazard model. With a risk-neutral principal (shareholders) and risk-averse agent (manager), the optimal contract solves $\max_{\{w_i\}}\sum_i p_i(\Pi_i-w_i)$ s.t. $\sum_i p_i u(w_i)\ge U_0$, whose first-order condition $u'(w_i)=1/\lambda$ implies full insurance absent incentive concerns. Discrete two-outcome and continuous linear-contract ($w(\Pi)=a+b\Pi$) examples derive the insurance-incentive trade-off; effort follows the slope, $e=b/R$, and $b=1$ gives residual claimancy.

## Key findings
- **Authority redistributes the gains from trade and distorts investment.** Under unconstrained bargaining the buyer underinvests ($x^B=(v-c)/2$). *Supplier control* is equivalent to unconstrained bargaining here ($x^{SC}=x^B$, $W^{SC}=3W^\*/4$). *Buyer control* induces *overinvestment* ($x^{BC}=v-c/2>x^\*$) because the buyer with authority avoids internalizing production cost $c$. The optimal allocation of ownership is the second-best solution that best protects relationship-specific investment; nonintegration leaves authority split across dimensions.
- **Profit-maximization rests on residual claimancy and control devices.** Selling the firm to a risk-neutral agent makes him residual claimant and restores efficiency. For a risk-averse manager, inducing effort requires a wage that grows with profit, destroying insurance and raising the wage bill (Jensen's inequality); shareholders may rationally tolerate slack. Limited punishment generates managerial rents (an efficiency-wage logic, Calvo-Wellisz, Shapiro-Stiglitz).
- **Several mechanisms curb managerial discretion:** yardstick / relative-performance competition (works under correlated technologies, with the sufficient-statistic result of Holmstrom; underlies dual sourcing); takeovers (Manne, Marris) — limited by the Grossman-Hart free-rider problem, dilution, poison pills, greenmail, and possible myopia; career concerns and reputation (Fama, Holmstrom 1982a); and direct supervision (Alchian-Demsetz's monitor as residual claimant vs. Holmstrom's budget-breaking source for team production).
- The neoclassical profit-maximizing model is shown to be a robust *as-if* approximation: even with X-inefficiency, the realized cost is a sufficient statistic, so the monopoly-pricing model of later chapters remains valid (observational equivalence).

## Contribution
This book defined the "new" industrial organization for a generation: it unified IO around game-theoretic and information-economic foundations, made tools such as perfect Bayesian equilibrium, reputation, and incomplete contracts standard in the field, and supplied the teaching infrastructure (worked models, exercises, the game-theory appendix) that trained most economists working on imperfect competition, firm boundaries, and regulation after 1988.

## Limitations & open questions
The author is explicit that the neoclassical firm leaves much unmodeled: bounded rationality and "satisficing" versus optimization; communication and the "codification" of knowledge (Arrow); organizational memory and culture (Nelson-Winter, Schein); the *dynamics of reorganizations*; and group/coalitional behavior emphasized by sociologists. Empirically, "unforeseeability" and contract complexity are hard to measure — good proxies are needed to test the Coase-Williamson vertical-integration prediction. Whether IO conclusions survive once the profit-maximization postulate is replaced by a full model of internal organization is flagged as a leading open research agenda. These are direct project-idea hooks toward integrating internal organization with product-market behavior.

## Connections
The theory-of-the-firm chapter synthesizes Coase's "The Nature of the Firm" and Williamson's (1975, 1985) transaction-cost economics with the property-rights / incomplete-contracts approach of Grossman & Hart (1986) and Hart & Moore (1988). The principal-agent treatment draws on Holmstrom (1979, 1982a, 1982b), Hart & Holmstrom (1987), Grossman & Hart (1983), and the team-production analysis of Alchian & Demsetz (1972). Efficiency-wage and managerial-rent material connects to Shapiro & Stiglitz (1984) and Calvo & Wellisz (1978, 1979); yardstick competition to Shleifer (1985), [[@Lazear1981|Lazear & Rosen (1981)]], and [[@Nalebuff1983|Nalebuff & Stiglitz (1983)]]; takeovers to Manne (1965), Marris (1964), and Grossman & Hart (1980); product-market selection arguments to Alchian and to Winter (1971) and Hart (1983). The broader IO program parallels and feeds the later survey by Tirole and collaborators and the regulation work of Laffont & Tirole (1986).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> This review provides a critical survey of psychology-and-economics ("behavioral economics") research in contract theory. First, I introduce the theories of individual decision making most frequently used in behavioral contract theory, and formally illustrate some of their implications in contracting settings. Second, I provide a more comprehensive (but informal) survey of the psychology-and-economics work on classical contract-theoretic topics: moral hazard, screening, mechanism design, and incomplete contracts. I also summarize research on a new topic spawned by psychology and economics, exploitative contracting, that studies contracts designed primarily to take advantage of agent mistakes.

## Summary
A 45-page *Journal of Economic Literature* survey (read in full from a clean `pdftotext` extraction; the text was lightly truncated at the very end, so the concluding "general thoughts" of section 8 are covered only from earlier signposting). Kőszegi organizes behavioral contract theory not by psychological phenomenon but by classical contract-theoretic *principle* — moral hazard, screening, mechanism design, incomplete contracts — plus a genuinely new topic, *exploitative contracting*. Section 2 introduces the four workhorse decision-making models (loss aversion, present bias, inequity aversion, quasi-Bayesian/overconfidence beliefs) and works each through a self-contained contracting application. The maintained convention throughout the literature is asymmetric: a behaviorally biased agent (she) faces a fully rational, profit-maximizing principal (he). The recurring message is that psychologically realistic agents rationalize observed but classically puzzling contract forms — bonus/threshold pay, wage compression and rigidity, flat-rate tariffs, weak explicit incentives, all-pay auctions, shrouded add-on fees — and that small amounts of naïveté can have first-order welfare consequences.

## Research question
What does it change for *optimal contract design* when one replaces the classical "simplistic" agent (expected-utility maximizer trading off money against effort) with an agent who is loss averse, present-biased, inequity averse, or holds systematically incorrect beliefs? Across moral hazard, screening, mechanism/auction design, and incomplete contracts, which classical results survive, which reverse, and what *new* contracting problem — exploiting agent mistakes — arises that has no classical counterpart?

## Method / identification
A critical literature survey built on a common formal scaffold. Section 2 presents four models, each with a worked principal–agent application:

1. **Loss aversion / reference dependence (Kőszegi–[[@Koszegi2006b|Rabin 2006]], 2007).** Reference-dependent utility $u(w\mid r)= w + \eta(w-r)$ if $w\ge r$ and $w + \eta\lambda(w-r)$ if $w<r$, with weight $\eta>0$ and loss-aversion coefficient $\lambda>1$. The reference point is the agent's *rational-expectations* outcome distribution $F$, so utility integrates over it: $U(w\mid F)=\int u(w\mid r)\,dF(r)$. This generates *first-order* risk aversion — expected gain-loss utility is strictly negative for any nondegenerate wage lottery, decreasing linearly in dispersion. The worked application (Herweg–Müller–Weinschenk 2010) is a three-output moral-hazard problem; minimizing the expected wage subject to participation (PC) and incentive compatibility (IC) shows the principal optimally uses *exactly two* wage levels with $w_L<w_M=w_H$ — a **bonus contract** — because finely varying pay with output is too costly to a loss-averse agent.

2. **Present bias / time inconsistency (β–δ; Laibson 1997, O'Donoghue–Rabin).** Self $t$ values $u_t+\beta\delta u_{t+1}+\beta\delta^2 u_{t+2}+\cdots$ with $\beta,\delta\le 1$; perceived future bias $\hat\beta$ indexes naïveté ($\hat\beta=\beta$ sophisticated, $\hat\beta=1$ fully naïve). The worked credit-contract application (Heidhues–Kőszegi 2010; Eliaz–Spiegler 2008) solves for installment menus under a perceived-choice constraint (PCC) on the believed plan and an IC on the actually-chosen plan, showing the optimal contract *maximizes the consumer's misprediction* about repayment.

3. **Inequity aversion ([[@Fehr1999|Fehr–Schmidt 1999]]).** Utility $x_i-\frac{\alpha_i}{N-1}\sum_{j:x_j>x_i}(x_j-x_i)-\frac{\beta_i}{N-1}\sum_{j:x_j<x_i}(x_i-x_j)$, disliking disadvantageous more than advantageous inequality. In a gift-exchange game the agent equalizes material payoffs, choosing effort with $c'(e)=1$ (efficient); a voluntary-bonus scheme reaches the same efficiency via the principal's inequity aversion. With heterogeneity (a fraction $q$ inequity-averse, $1-q$ selfish), effort falls below first-best ($c'(e)=2q-1$ for gift exchange vs. $c'(e)=q/(2-q)$ for bonuses), and the bonus scheme dominates because the first mover risks less when the costly action is cheaper.

4. **Quasi-Bayesian beliefs / overconfidence.** Bayesian updating off an *incorrect prior* $\hat q>q$. The worked example yields an optimal wage spread $w_H-w_L=\big(\hat q-q\big)\big/\big(2\hat q(1-\hat q)\big)$ plus risk terms, identifying a basic *exploitation motive*: overpay for high output, underpay for low. But exploitation of *mildly* overconfident agents is limited, and screening optimists (Eliaz–Spiegler 2008) generates an information-rent logic implying exploitation is "all or nothing" — *if observed, it should be large*.

The remaining sections (3–7) are informal surveys keyed to each formal device.

## Key findings
- **Moral hazard.** (i) Intrinsic motivation matters: gift exchange and voluntary bonuses (Akerlof; Englmaier–Leider; Fehr–Klein–Schmidt) let output-independent or ex-post pay sustain effort; bonuses dominate under heterogeneity because of the lower-cost first move. (ii) **Reduced wage sensitivity**: inequity aversion compresses wages and can favor team/tournament incentives purely to insure against painful inequality (Englmaier–Wambach; Bartling); loss aversion generates bonus-structured or threshold-flat schemes (Herweg et al.; Macera; de Meza–Webb), and flat-rate tariffs for uncertain demand (Herweg–Mierendorff). (iii) **Crowding out**: informed-principal models (Bénabou–[[@Benabou2003|Tirole 2003]]), social signaling (Bénabou–[[@Benabou2006|Tirole 2006]]), reciprocity ([[@Rabin1993|Rabin 1993]]; von Siemens; Ellingsen–Johannesson), and anticipatory utility (Immordino et al.) all rationalize weak explicit incentives and the negative effect of high-powered pay on effort. (iv) Mild overconfidence can *raise* efficiency and benefit a competed-over agent (de la Rosa; Gervais–Heaton–Odean).
- **Screening.** A novel motive: present-biased agents *demand commitment* even at the cost of flexibility (DellaVigna–Malmendier; Amador–Werning–Angeletos's minimum-savings rule; Galperti; Esteban–Miyagawa–Shum via Gul–Pesendorfer temptation). A general impossibility: agents with the *same beliefs and ex-ante preferences cannot be separated by self-selection*, so biased agents are pooled and priced at an average, imposing externalities on rationals (Sandroni–Squintani's insurance results; Spinnewijn; Manove–Padilla on over-lending). Framing can screen (Salant–Siegel); social-comparison sorting (von Siemens; Kosfeld–von Siemens) and reference-dependent menus (Hahn et al.; Carbajal–Ely) reshape optimal menus.
- **Auctions / mechanism design.** Predictions hinge on *separate vs. net evaluation* of money and the object. Under expectations-based loss aversion, bidders are first-order risk averse and bid aggressively in formats that insure them; Eisenhuth shows the optimal auction is an **all-pay auction with a minimum bid** under separate evaluation and a **first-price auction with a minimum bid** under net evaluation. Reference points tied to reserve/buy prices raise optimal reserves (Rosenkranz–Schmitz; Shunda). Non-equilibrium thinking (Jehiel–Lamy on absolute/secret reserves; Crawford et al. level-k; Augenblick's sunk-cost penny auctions) and anticipated regret (Filiz-Ozbay–Ozbay) explain overbidding and exotic revenue-extracting designs. Social preferences raise efficiency in public-interest mechanisms (Kucuksenel; Bierbrauer–Netzer).
- **Exploitative contracting (the new topic).** Formally equivalent to contracting under non-common priors where the *principal knows the agent better than she knows herself*. Core mechanisms: products made to look *seemingly cheap* by loading revenue onto underappreciated (add-on, back-loaded) prices; **cross-subsidies** from naïve to sophisticated consumers (Gabaix–Laibson); and **exacerbation of mistakes via high fees/penalties** (DellaVigna–Malmendier; Heidhues–Kőszegi credit contracts; Grubb; Gottlieb–Smetters). Competition does not cure this and unshrouding is often unprofitable (Heidhues–Kőszegi–Murooka). Welfare-improving interventions mainly *limit contract forms* (caps on penalties/commissions) or redistribute from sophisticated to naïve consumers.
- **Incomplete contracts.** Hart–Moore (2008) "contracts as reference points": an aggrieved party inefficiently *shades* if she gets less than the best outcome the initial contract allowed, so optimal contracts fix the most conflictual terms (e.g., price) and allocate control over the rest to the party who cares most.

## Contribution
The first integrative, formally grounded map of behavioral contract theory, written to be legible to mainstream contract theorists by organizing around contract-theoretic principles rather than psychological phenomena. It standardizes the four building-block models, demonstrates each in a transparent worked contracting problem, and — most originally — carves out *exploitative contracting* as a distinct field, recasting it as a disciplined, welfare-relevant form of non-common-priors contracting in which the firm exploits documented consumer mistakes. It thereby answered the Bolton–Dewatripont (2005) lament that contract theory had "relatively few explorations of the implications" of non-classical choice for optimal contracting.

## Limitations & open questions
The paper is explicit about open problems (rich project-idea hooks):
- **No theory of reference-group formation** for inequity aversion — which others' outcomes an agent compares to — blocking disciplined analysis of how reorganizations change behavior.
- Inequity-aversion models **fail to predict gift exchange** when the employer is much wealthier and largely self-interested; reciprocity-based explanations are incomplete (Netzer–Schmutzler show a selfish principal's wage is never "kind"); a heterogeneous-agent model is at best a starting point.
- For loss aversion, **no paper has carefully embedded it in an insurance model with moral hazard** to derive optimal contracts; a simple-deductible result is conjectured.
- **Screening is understudied**: behavioral insights are absent from optimal income taxation and from standard preference-parameter screening; the planner's ability to screen optimal-vs-suboptimal agents needs work.
- Auction emotions ("bidding fever," joy of winning, spite) are poorly understood; why auctions trigger them while other settings evoke social preferences is open; informational mechanics across formats need linking to behavioral models.
- Exploitative contracting is "in its infancy": effects of **ex-post competition and switching** (Gottlieb 2008 is only a first step; procrastination-on-switching is unmodeled), why purely-exploitative features are *rare* (a "meta-sophistication" puzzle), how to educate consumers without merely shifting exploitation to "choice engines," and **why helping contracts are scarcer than exploitative ones**.
- Open: a disciplined account of *when a bias benefits the agent* (it must improve perceived outside option or contract terms), connecting to classical commitment/reputation literatures; and how bounded-rationality/costly-cognition models relate to psychology-grounded ones.

## Summary
*Einführung in die Statistik* by Jürgen Lehn and Helmut Wegmann (5th, revised edition, B. G. Teubner, 2006) is a compact German-language undergraduate textbook in the Teubner Studienbücher Mathematik series. It is a mathematically rigorous yet accessible introduction that moves from descriptive statistics through the probability foundations needed for inference, culminating in classical inferential ("schließende") statistics: estimation, confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, the chi-squared apparatus, distribution-free tests, one-way ANOVA, and simple linear regression. The guiding theme stated in the introduction is that statistical inference deals with observations generated under the influence of chance, and that every statistical procedure must come with *quantifiable* statements about the probabilities of drawing wrong conclusions (controllable, calculable error risk).

## Research question
Not a research paper but a pedagogical text. Its organizing question is methodological: how does one analyse observations that arise under the influence of randomness, and how can one make probabilistically calibrated inferences about the underlying chance mechanism (the data-generating "Zufallsgesetz") while keeping the risk of erroneous conclusions explicitly bounded?

## Method / identification
The book is structured as a three-part formal development.

**Part 1 — Beschreibende Statistik (Descriptive statistics).** Features/variables (Merkmale), display of measurement series, summary measures (Maßzahlen) for one- and two-dimensional data, and remarks on the robustness of these measures.

**Part 2 — Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitstheorie (Foundations of probability).** A self-contained measure-theoretic-flavoured probability primer: probability spaces, conditional probability and independence, random variables and distribution functions, expectation and variance, multidimensional random variables and independence, the normal, $\chi^2$, $t$ and $F$ distributions, laws of large numbers and limit theorems (central limit theorem), and empirical distribution functions with the central theorem of statistics (Glivenko–Cantelli, the "Zentralsatz der Statistik").

**Part 3 — Schließende Statistik (Inferential statistics).** The methodological core: estimation procedures and their properties (unbiasedness, consistency, efficiency), the maximum-likelihood method, confidence intervals, tests under normality assumptions ($z$-, $t$-, $\chi^2$- and $F$-tests), the $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test and contingency tables, several distribution-free (nonparametric) tests, one-way analysis of variance, and simple linear regression. The estimation theory is built on the formal apparatus of comparing estimators $\hat{\theta}$ for a parameter $\theta$ via bias and risk; the ML estimator is obtained by maximizing the likelihood $L(\theta)=\prod_i f(x_i\mid\theta)$. Confidence intervals are constructed so that the coverage probability $P(\theta\in[\underline{\theta},\overline{\theta}])\ge 1-\alpha$, and tests are framed through controlling the type-I error probability $\alpha$.

## Data
None — this is a textbook. It uses small illustrative measurement series and worked numerical examples (Beispiele) plus an appendix of statistical tables ($\chi^2$, $t$, $F$, normal quantiles) rather than an empirical dataset.

## Key findings
As a textbook it transmits established results rather than new ones. The named theoretical pillars it develops and proves at an introductory level include: the laws of large numbers and the central limit theorem (Part 2.7); the central theorem of statistics / Glivenko–Cantelli convergence of the empirical distribution function (Part 2.8); the properties of point estimators (unbiasedness, consistency, efficiency) and the maximum-likelihood method (Parts 3.2–3.3); the duality between confidence intervals and tests under normal-distribution assumptions (Parts 3.4–3.5); the $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test and independence testing in contingency tables (Part 3.6); distribution-free tests (Part 3.7); the $F$-based decomposition underlying one-way ANOVA (Part 3.8); and least-squares estimation in simple linear regression (Part 3.9).

## Contribution
A concise, proof-aware introduction to statistics for German-speaking mathematics, science and engineering students, situating inferential statistics on an explicit probability-theoretic foundation. Its pedagogical contribution is the insistence that statistical procedures be accompanied by explicit, calculable error probabilities, and its self-contained build-up from descriptive measures to the limit theorems that justify inference. Now in its fifth revised edition (1st 1985, 5th 2006), it is a long-standing reference in the Teubner series.

## Limitations & open questions
By design the book is an introduction: it restricts attention to classical frequentist inference under largely parametric (often normality) assumptions and to *simple* (univariate-predictor) linear regression and *one-way* ANOVA. It does not treat Bayesian inference, multivariate/multiple regression, generalized linear models, time series, resampling/bootstrap, or modern computational and high-dimensional statistics. These omissions are the natural next steps for a reader and mark the boundary at which more advanced texts take over.

## Connections
As an introductory German statistics text it sits alongside standard references such as Fahrmeir, Künstler, Pigeot & Tutz (*Statistik*) and Georgii (*Stochastik*), and in the English tradition alongside Casella & Berger (*Statistical Inference*) and Wasserman (*All of Statistics*). Its probability foundations connect to the laws of large numbers and central limit theorem of classical probability (Kolmogorov), and the empirical-distribution result it calls the "Zentralsatz der Statistik" is the Glivenko–Cantelli theorem. The maximum-likelihood and estimator-property material descends from Fisher; the contingency-table and goodness-of-fit machinery from Pearson.

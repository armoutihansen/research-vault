> [!abstract] Abstract
> Mixture models capture heterogeneity in data by decomposing the population into latent subgroups, each of which is governed by its own subgroup-specific set of parameters. Despite the flexibility and widespread use of these models, most applications have focused solely on making inferences for whole or sub-populations, rather than individual cases. The current article presents a general framework for computing marginal and conditional predicted values for individuals using mixture model results. These predicted values can be used to characterize covariate effects, examine the fit of the model for specific individuals, or forecast future observations from previous ones. Two empirical examples are provided to demonstrate the usefulness of individual predicted values in applications of mixture models. The first example examines the relative timing of initiation of substance use using a multiple event process survival mixture model whereas the second example evaluates changes in depressive symptoms over adolescence using a growth mixture model.

## Summary
This methodological note shows how a fitted finite mixture model can be used to generate predicted values for *individuals* (real or hypothetical), not just for latent classes or the whole population. The authors distinguish two kinds of individual prediction: **marginal** predicted values, which average over the latent variables (latent class and continuous factors) using covariate-implied mixing probabilities; and **conditional** predicted values, which condition on a person's most likely latent-variable scores via empirical Bayes posteriors. They supply a parametric-bootstrap recipe for attaching confidence and prediction intervals to either quantity, and illustrate the whole apparatus on a survival mixture model of substance-use onset and a growth mixture model of adolescent depression. They claim this is the first general treatment of individual prediction across arbitrary mixture specifications.

## Research question
How can the results of a fitted mixture model (latent class analysis, latent profile analysis, growth mixture models, survival mixture models) be used to compute, interpret, and quantify uncertainty around predictions at the level of the individual case rather than only the latent class or population? The motivation is that mixture models are routinely described as "person-centered," yet individual-level prediction (standard in multilevel, latent-curve, and IRT models) is rarely exploited in mixture practice.

## Method / identification
The paper is methodological/derivational. Index individuals by $i$ and latent classes by $k=1,\dots,K$, with class-indicator vector $c_i$ multinomially distributed, endogenous variables $y_i$ (dimension $p$), exogenous covariates $x_i$ (dimension $q$), and continuous latent factors $\eta_i$ (dimension $r$). Following Muthen & Shedden (1999), the joint density factors as $[y_i,\eta_i,c_i\mid x_i]$, and marginalizing over the latent variables gives the finite-mixture observed-data distribution
$$[y_i\mid x_i]=\sum_{k} P(c_{ik}=1\mid x_i)\int [y_i\mid x_i,\eta_i,c_{ik}=1]\,[\eta_i\mid x_i,c_{ik}=1]\,d\eta_i.$$
Mixing probabilities follow a multinomial logit in the covariates, $P(c_{ik}=1\mid x_i)\propto \exp(\alpha_{0k}+x_i'\gamma_k)$, identified by fixing a reference class. Parameters are estimated by maximum likelihood (EM), occasionally Bayesian methods.

**Marginal prediction.** The marginal expected value is $E(y_i\mid x_i)=\sum_k P(c_{ik}=1\mid x_i)\,E(y_i\mid x_i,c_{ik}=1)$; the sample analog plugs in estimated mixing probabilities and within-class marginal expectations.

**Conditional prediction.** If latent variables were known, $E(y_i\mid x_i,\eta_i,c_{ik}=1)$ depends only on the realized class. In practice the authors substitute empirical Bayes predictors: posterior class probabilities by Bayes' rule, $P(c_{ik}=1\mid y_i,x_i)\propto [y_i\mid x_i,c_{ik}=1]P(c_{ik}=1\mid x_i)$, and expected-a-posteriori (EAP) factor scores from the posterior $[\eta_i\mid y_i,x_i,c_{ik}=1]$. Conditional predictions then weight within-class predicted values (evaluated at the EAP scores) by the posterior class probabilities. These are "postdictions" in the Skrondal–Rabe-Hesketh sense, since $y_i$ informs the latent scores that re-predict $y_i$.

**Uncertainty.** Treating the MLE as asymptotically normal, $\hat\theta \sim N(\theta, \Sigma_{\hat\theta})$, they draw $B$ (e.g. 2500–5000) parametric bootstrap parameter vectors from $N(\hat\theta,\hat\Sigma_{\hat\theta})$ (with $\hat\Sigma_{\hat\theta}$ from the observed Fisher information via the Louis (1982) method). For each draw they recompute the predicted value; the 2.5th/97.5th percentiles give a 95% **confidence interval**. **Prediction intervals** add resampling steps that draw class membership, then $\eta_i$, then a realized $y_i$ from the implied conditional distribution, yielding wider intervals that capture observation-level variability. Marginal PIs suit forecasting new individuals; conditional PIs suit individuals with partial data already observed.

## Data
Two applied demonstrations (re-analyses), not new data collection. (1) National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH), $N=55{,}772$, ages 10–30, age-of-onset for nine substances; a MEPSUM survival mixture model with sex and ethnicity covariates. (2) NLSY97 subsample of 14-year-olds, $N=1{,}460$, six waves (2000–2010), CES-D depression sum score (5–20); a freed-loading growth mixture model with race, gender, parental physical health, and college attendance as covariates.

## Key findings
- A unified, distribution-agnostic framework: marginal vs. conditional individual predicted values follow directly from the mixture factorization (Eqs. for $E(y_i\mid x_i)$ and its conditional analog), differing only in whether within-class quantities are weighted by prior (covariate-implied) or posterior class probabilities.
- In the MEPSUM example a six-class solution emerged (abstainer 35.7%; late/early/progressive soft-drug classes; late/early hard-drug classes). Because factor variances were fixed to zero, marginal and conditional predictions differed only through prior vs. posterior class weighting. Conditional predictions forecast non-medical prescription use (NMUP) from observed alcohol/marijuana onset: an early-onset marijuana user had ~70% lifetime NMUP probability by age 20 vs. ~40% for late onset.
- In the GMM example BIC selected three depression classes (low-stable 40.2%; low-rising 23.9%; high-stable 35.9%); a freed-loading model fit significantly better than linear ($\chi^2(12)=97.30$, $p<.001$). Female gender and non-attendance of college predicted the high-symptom class. Marginal predicted trajectories visualized covariate effects; conditional predicted values assessed person fit (Figures 5–6 flag poorly-fit individuals).
- The bootstrap CIs/PIs explicitly propagate uncertainty in class membership, which the authors argue is essential to honest mixture inference.

## Contribution
Provides what the authors call the first general treatment of individual-level prediction in mixture models, applicable to any within-class parametric specification (discrete or continuous indicators, longitudinal or cross-sectional). It transfers individual-prediction tools from multilevel, latent-curve, and IRT modeling into the mixture setting, and supplies a practical parametric-bootstrap procedure for both confidence and prediction intervals that accounts for class-membership uncertainty. This operationalizes the "person-centered" rhetoric often attached to mixture models.

## Limitations & open questions
- Validity of the parametric bootstrap rests on strong assumptions: $\hat\theta$ consistent and asymptotically normal, large samples, and a correctly specified $\hat\Sigma_{\hat\theta}$. Both structural and distributional misspecification can corrupt class enumeration and within-class estimates.
- The authors explicitly flag that whether the predicted values and intervals are *robust* to assumption violations (given an adequate approximation to the data-generating process) is unresolved and "more research is needed."
- They do not adjudicate among the many competing methods for computing latent class scores and factor scores (e.g. Bolck–Croon–Hagenaars, Vermunt three-step), simply adopting empirical Bayes.
- Alternative within-class distributions (e.g. skew-normal) and alternative latent covariance structures are noted as possibilities but not systematically compared.
- A fully non-parametric bootstrap (re-estimating the model on resampled raw data) is acknowledged as more robust but computationally infeasible here.

## Connections
The mixture-model factorization and EM estimation follow Muthen & Shedden (1999) and McLachlan & Peel (2000); the growth mixture and trajectory traditions trace to Nagin (1999), Muthen & Muthen (2000), and Ram & Grimm (2009), with the freed-loading latent basis from Meredith & Tisak (1990). The survival mixture (MEPSUM) model is from Dean, Bauer & Shanahan (2014) and the substance-use application from Dean, Cole & Bauer (2015). The marginal/conditional prediction distinction and the prediction-interval machinery are adapted from Skrondal & Rabe-Hesketh (2009) for random-effects models, with parametric-bootstrap intervals building on Pek, Losardo & Bauer (2011) and Efron & Tibshirani (1993); the observed-information computation uses Louis (1982). The person-fit application connects to the IRT literature (Reise, 2000; Woods, Oltmanns & Turkheimer, 2008), and the predicted-trajectory plotting to Curran, Bauer & Willoughby (2004) and Preacher, Curran & Bauer (2006). The direct/indirect-application distinction is from Titterington, Smith & Makov (1985), and the person-centered framing from Bergman & Magnusson (1997) and Bauer & Shanahan (2007). Misspecification concerns draw on Bauer & Curran (2003, 2004) and Satorra (1990).

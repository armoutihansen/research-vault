> [!abstract] Abstract
> We propose a new way to quantify the restrictiveness of an economic model, based on how well the model fits simulated, hypothetical data sets. The data sets are drawn at random from a distribution that satisfies some application-dependent content restrictions (such as that people prefer more money to less). Models that can fit almost all hypothetical data well are not restrictive. To illustrate our approach, we evaluate the restrictiveness of popular behavioral models in two experimental settings---certainty equivalents and initial play---and explain how restrictiveness reveals new insights about each of the models.

## Summary
A good fit to real data is ambiguous: a model may fit well because it captures genuine structure, or because it is so flexible it fits almost anything. This paper proposes **restrictiveness**, a measure of how much empirical content a (typically parametric) model has, computed entirely from *synthetic* data: sample prediction rules uniformly from an "eligible" set encoding known background constraints, and measure the model's average approximation error to them, normalised by a baseline. Paired with the **completeness** measure of [[@Fudenberg2022|Fudenberg et al. (2022)]] (fit to *real* data), it traces a Pareto frontier on which the preferred models rule out many regularities yet still capture those present in the data. The key practical virtue is that restrictiveness is computable without representation theorems or analytic knowledge of the model's implications. Applied to risk-preference models (CPT, DA), initial-play models (PCHM and logit variants), and microfinance diffusion on Indian village networks, it overturns the intuition that parameter count tracks flexibility. I read the full paper (63 pp.); the appendices (estimation proofs, supplementary Application 1 material) were sampled.

## Research question
When a parametric functional form fits observed data well, how can we tell whether this reflects genuine structure captured by the model versus mere flexibility (the model would fit almost any conceivable data)? Concretely: how do we quantify the *empirical content* / restrictiveness of an economic model, in a way that (i) goes beyond counting parameters, (ii) accounts for constraints the model is already known to impose, and (iii) is computable in realistic applications where no representation theorem is available?

## Method / identification
The primitives are a finite covariate set $X$ with known marginal $P_X$, an outcome space $Y\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n$, and the space of prediction rules $F\equiv Y^{|X|}$. A parametric model is $F_\Theta=\{f_\theta\}_{\theta\in\Theta}$ with $f_\theta$ continuous in $\theta$ over compact $\Theta$. A **discrepancy** $d:F\times F\to\mathbb{R}_+$ measures how different two rules are, with $d(f,f')=0\iff f=f'$ (e.g. expected mean-squared distance, or expected KL-divergence when outcomes are distributions). The **eligible set** $\mathcal{F}\subseteq F$ is the compact set of rules satisfying user-specified background constraints (e.g. FOSD-monotonicity); taking $\mathcal{F}=F$ asks whether the model restricts at all.

Restrictiveness is the normalised expected discrepancy to a uniformly drawn eligible rule:
$$r(F_\Theta,\mathcal{F})=\frac{E_{\lambda_{\mathcal{F}}}[d(F_\Theta,f)]}{E_{\lambda_{\mathcal{F}}}[d(f_{\text{base}},f)]},\qquad d(F_\Theta,f):=\inf_{f_\theta\in F_\Theta}d(f_\theta,f)$$
where $\lambda_{\mathcal{F}}$ is uniform on $\mathcal{F}$ and $f_{\text{base}}$ is a baseline rule (often nested in $F_\Theta$). Then $r=0$ means completely unrestrictive, and $r=1$ means the model fits synthetic data no better than the baseline. Completeness reuses [[@Fudenberg2022|Fudenberg et al. (2022)]]: $\kappa(F_\Theta)=\dfrac{e_P(f_{\text{base}})-\inf_{\theta}e_P(f_\theta)}{e_P(f_{\text{base}})-e_P(f^*)}$ with $f^*$ the loss-minimising rule on real data. When $d$ and the loss $l$ are *paired*, $\kappa(F_\Theta)=1-r(F_\Theta,\{f^*\})$, so completeness is the restrictiveness with respect to the true model — the two measures are duals.

**Axiomatic foundation (Prop. 1).** For the un-normalised approximation error $e(F_\Theta,\mathcal{F},d)$, Axioms 1–4 (nonnegativity, monotonicity, homogeneity in the unit of $d$, and *linearity*: $e$ is a measure-weighted convex combination of ex-post errors as $\mathcal{F}$ is partitioned) characterise $e=E_{f\sim\mu_{\mathcal{F}}}[c(f)\cdot\inf_{g\in F}d(g,f)]$. Adding Axiom 5 (*symmetry*, a principle of indifference over eligible rules) forces a constant weight $c$ and the uniform $\lambda_{\mathcal{F}}$, yielding the measure used. Dropping symmetry recovers a broader prior-weighted class.

**Estimation (Sec. 5).** Restrictiveness is estimated by $\hat r_M=\frac{\frac1M\sum_m d(F_\Theta,f_m)}{\frac1M\sum_m d(f_{\text{base}},f_m)}$ over $M$ uniform draws from $\mathcal{F}$; this is purely computational (no sampling error from real data) and $\sqrt{M}(\hat r_M-r)/\hat\sigma_{\hat r}\to N(0,1)$ (closed-form variance given). Completeness $\hat\kappa$ uses $K=10$-fold cross-validation of out-of-sample loss and is asymptotically normal (Prop. 2, adapting Austern–Zhou 2020), so confidence intervals are available for both.

## Data
- **Application 1 (certainty equivalents):** 25 binary lotteries $(z,\underline z,p)$ from [[@Bruhin2010|Bruhin et al. (2010)]]; outcome = average certainty equivalent. Robustness sets include loss-domain lotteries, three-outcome lotteries (Bernheim–Sprenger 2020), six-outcome lotteries (Fudenberg–Puri 2021), and lotteries from five further papers.
- **Application 2 (initial play):** 466 unique $3\times3$ matrix games from [[@Fudenberg2019a|Fudenberg & Liang (2019)]] (pooling Wright–Leyton-Brown meta-data, MTurk-random, and algorithmically-designed games); outcome = distribution over row actions.
- **Application 3 (diffusion):** 43 Karnataka villages from Banerjee et al. (2013, 2019); outcome = non-leader microfinance takeup rate; village configuration $(N_i,A_i,L_i)$ with social-network adjacency matrix.

## Key findings
- **CPT is flexible, DA is restrictive (App. 1).** A 3-parameter CPT $(\alpha,\gamma,\delta)$ reaches **95% completeness** vs. **27%** for 2-parameter Disappointment Aversion, yet CPT's restrictiveness ($\approx0.28$) is far *lower* than DA's. So FOSD-monotonicity is a large part of CPT's content on binary lotteries, while DA imposes substantial extra (and apparently data-unsupported) restrictions. Adding parameters weakly raises completeness and lowers restrictiveness, but *which* parameter matters: the probability-weighting **curvature** parameter $\gamma$ buys large completeness gains for little flexibility, whereas an "elevation"/$\delta$ parameter mostly adds flexibility "in the wrong direction."
- **Restrictiveness is context-dependent (App. 1 robustness).** CPT's restrictiveness rises with lottery support size: $\approx0.28$ (binary), $0.57$ (three-outcome), $0.83$ (six-outcome). The DA restrictiveness distribution first-order stochastically dominates CPT's across lottery sets. Results are robust to $\beta(a,b)$ reweightings and to dropping FOSD from $\mathcal{F}$.
- **Parameter count ≠ flexibility (App. 2).** PCHM, Logit Level-1, Logit PCHM are highly restrictive (0.992, 0.970, 0.971) but differ in completeness (0.436, 0.727, 0.729). Logit Level-1 (one parameter) and Logit PCHM (two) are nearly identical on *both* axes despite unrelated functional forms; almost all of Logit PCHM's gain over PCHM comes from the single logit parameter, suggesting initial play is largely unstrategic. Constraints on strictly dominated/dominant actions are a tiny part of these models' content (restrictiveness stays $\ge0.89$ even under tightened constraints).
- **A structural model can be beaten by simple OLS (App. 3).** The 4-parameter partially-linear "network-gossip-centrality" diffusion model has high restrictiveness (0.94) but completeness only 0.07, and is **Pareto-dominated** by a one-regressor OLS on leaders' average eigenvector centrality (restrictiveness 0.976, completeness 0.258). Adding OLS regressors lowers restrictiveness roughly linearly while completeness plateaus — extra regressors keep adding flexibility long after they stop improving fit.

## Contribution
Provides a *computable*, axiomatically-grounded measure of how much a parametric model restricts behaviour, requiring no representation theorem — its central advantage over VC dimension, Rademacher complexity, metric/bracketing entropy, and Selten's (1991) exact-fit area measure. By using *approximate* fit on uniformly sampled synthetic data and allowing background constraints, it quantifies content *beyond* what is already known about a model. Paired with completeness, it yields a model-evaluation framework distinct from AIC/BIC (which trade fit against parameter count on observed data): here restrictiveness is intrinsically valuable even with infinite data and does not depend on sample size. The framework has already been reused (Schwaninger 2022 on inequity-averse bargaining; Ellis et al. 2022 on demand from budget sets; Ba et al. 2023 on reaction to information).

## Limitations & open questions
- **Context-specificity.** Restrictiveness depends on the chosen $X$, outcome, and especially the **eligible set** $\mathcal{F}$, for which "there is not a single correct choice." The authors flag as open the design of a measure that is "generally more restrictive" across contexts (e.g. via FOSD-dominance of restrictiveness distributions).
- **Choosing $\mathcal{F}$ under ignorance.** When little is known about a model's content (App. 3), one defaults to $\mathcal{F}=F$ and only tests whether *any* restriction exists; principled selection of background constraints is unresolved.
- **Non-concave Pareto frontier.** The completeness–restrictiveness frontier need not be concave, so the preferred model may *not* maximise any weighted sum; aggregating the two scores is "a matter of taste" and context-specific, with no general rule offered.
- **Scope disclaimers.** The measures deliberately say nothing about parameter estimation or causal inference, and uniform sampling, while axiomatised and tractable, is one choice among a broader prior-weighted class the paper does not fully explore empirically.
- **Why restrictiveness is *good* is assumed.** The desirability of restrictiveness rests on an informal transfer/out-of-distribution argument (more-structured models generalise better, citing [[@Andrews2022|Andrews et al. 2022]]), not a formal generalisation guarantee.

## Connections
The companion **completeness** measure is [[@Fudenberg2022|Fudenberg, Kleinberg, Liang & Mullainathan (2022)]]; this paper is its natural dual on synthetic data. It generalises Koopmans & Reiersol's (1950) "observational restrictiveness" (a model is restrictive iff its permitted distributions are a proper subset) by *quantifying* degree. It contrasts sharply with Selten (1991)'s area/exact-fit flexibility measure (and its smoothed revealed-preference variant in Beatty & Crawford 2011), the Afriat-index distributions of Choi et al. (2007) and Polisson et al. (2020), and power-of-test simulations à la Bronars (1987) and Andreoni et al. (2013). Relative to statistical complexity (VC dimension — Basu & Echenique 2020; Rademacher complexity; metric/bracketing entropy — Van der Vaart & Wellner 1996) and model selection (AIC/BIC; Cox 1961/62; de [[@deClippel2022|Clippel & Rozen 2022]]), the distinguishing features are tractability, the use of background constraints, and independence from sample size. The applications connect to behavioural risk preferences (Tversky–Kahneman CPT; [[@Gul1991|Gul 1991]] DA) and level-k / cognitive-hierarchy game theory (Camerer et al. 2004; Wright & Leyton-Brown 2014; [[@Fudenberg2019a|Fudenberg & Liang 2019]]), and to social-network diffusion (Banerjee et al. 2013, 2019). The motivating concern — economic structure vs. flexible ML — links to [[@Andrews2022|Andrews et al. (2022)]] on transfer performance.

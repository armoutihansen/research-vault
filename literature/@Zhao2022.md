> [!abstract] Abstract
> Practitioners and academics have long appreciated the benefits that experimentation brings to firms. For online web-facing firms, however, it still remains challenging in balancing covariate information when experimental units arrive sequentially in online field experiments. In this paper, we study a novel online experimental design problem, which we refer to as the "Online Blocking Problem." In this problem, experimental units with heterogeneous covariate information arrive sequentially and must be immediately assigned into either the control or the treatment group, with an objective of minimizing the total discrepancy, which is defined as the minimum weight perfect matching between the two groups. To solve this problem, we propose a novel experimental design approach, which we refer to as the "Pigeonhole Design." The pigeonhole design first partitions the covariate space into smaller spaces, which we refer to as pigeonholes, and then, when the experimental units arrive at each pigeonhole, balances the number of control and treatment units for each pigeonhole. We analyze the theoretical performance of the pigeonhole design and show its effectiveness by comparing against two well-known benchmark designs: the match-pair design and the completely randomized design. We conduct extensive simulations to study the numerical performance of the different designs and conclude with practical suggestions.

## Summary
The paper formalizes the *online blocking problem*: experimental units arrive sequentially with covariates revealed on arrival, must be irrevocably assigned to control or treatment in equal numbers, and the experimenter wants to minimize covariate imbalance measured as the minimum-weight perfect matching between the two groups. The authors propose the *pigeonhole design*, which partitions the covariate space into cells ("pigeonholes") and balances control/treatment counts within each cell, with a final block of "balancing periods" that fix up residual imbalance. They prove the pigeonhole design closes most of the gap to the infeasible offline match-pair benchmark and strictly beats complete randomization, and corroborate this with simulations.

## Research question
How should an experimenter assign sequentially arriving units (whose covariates are revealed only on arrival, with assignment immediate and irrevocable) to control vs. treatment so as to minimize covariate discrepancy between the two equally-sized groups, when the well-studied offline blocking/matched-pair designs are infeasible because future covariates are unknown?

## Method / identification
The setup is a finite horizon of $T$ (even) periods. At each $t\in[T]$ a unit arrives with covariate $x_t\in S=[0,1]^p$, chosen **adversarially**. The experimenter immediately and irrevocably administers $W_t\in\{0,1\}$ (0 = control, 1 = treatment), with the hard constraint $|A|=|B|=T/2$ for control set $A$ and treatment set $B$. Pairwise discrepancy is the Euclidean distance $d_{t,t'}=\lVert x_t-x_{t'}\rVert_2$, and group discrepancy $d_{A,B}$ is the **minimum-weight perfect matching**

$$d_{A,B}=\min_{y}\ \sum_{t\in A}\sum_{t'\in B} d_{t,t'}\,y_{t,t'}\quad\text{s.t.}\ \sum_{t'\in B} y_{t,t'}=1\ \forall t\in A,\ \sum_{t\in A} y_{t,t'}=1\ \forall t'\in B,\ y_{t,t'}\in\{0,1\}.$$

An **online design** $\mu=(\mu_1,\dots,\mu_T)$ maps only past-and-present covariates and past assignments to a treatment probability; an **offline design** $\nu$ sees all covariates (a clairvoyant oracle) and yields the match-pair benchmark. The **pigeonhole design** takes a $K$-partition $P(S)=\{S_1,\dots,S_K\}$ and a balancing parameter $\beta\in[0,1]$. For the first $T-\lceil\beta T\rceil$ users, it routes $x_t$ to its pigeonhole $S_k$ and assigns to whichever arm currently has fewer units in that pigeonhole (coin flip on ties); for the last $\lceil\beta T\rceil$ *balancing periods* it ignores covariates and only corrects the overall control/treatment imbalance. The key design levers are: pigeonholes must be **uniform** (to hedge the adversary) and the number of pigeonholes must be tuned — too few collapses to Efron's (1971) biased-coin design ignoring covariates, too many leaves one unpaired unit per cell that must be matched cross-cell. The analytical workhorses are a **counting/worst-case argument** for the single-dimensional bounds and a **coupling argument** showing the unmatched-unit assignments behave like a coupled completely-randomized process, plus a packing/contradiction argument for the multi-dimensional match-pair lower bound and an **induction** for the completely-randomized upper bound on a special instance family.

## Data
None — this is a theory paper. The empirical content is **simulation only**: single-dimensional setups (point-mass at endpoints; deterministic equally-spaced; uniform random) and multi-dimensional uniform $x_t\sim U[0,1]^p$ for $p\in\{2,5,20\}$, each design evaluated by 1000 repetitions, with the matched-pair minimum-weight matching solved in Gurobi 9.1 via formulation (3).

## Key findings
- **Theorem 1** (match-pair, $p=1$): discrepancy $\le 1$, i.e. $\Theta(1)$ — the offline benchmark no online design can beat.
- **Theorem 2** (complete randomization, $p=1$): expected discrepancy is exactly $\Theta(T^{1/2})$ (both upper and lower bound), via a permutation-invariance lemma and a worst-case endpoint instance.
- **Theorem 3 / Corollary 1** (pigeonhole, $p=1$): with uniform pigeonholes of width $T^{-\eta}$ and $\beta=T^{\eta/2-1}$, expected discrepancy $O\!\big(T^{1-\eta}+T^{1/2}(\log T)^{1/2}\big)$; at $\eta=\tfrac{2}{3}$ this gives $O\!\big(T^{1/3}(\log T)^{1/2}\big)$, strictly improving on complete randomization.
- **Theorem 4** (match-pair, $p\ge2$): $\Theta(T^{(p-1)/p})$ — discrepancy grows with dimension.
- **Theorem 5** (complete randomization, $p\ge2$): $\Omega(T^{(p-1)/p})$ (cannot beat match-pair).
- **Theorem 6 / Corollary 2** (pigeonhole, $p\ge2$): at $\phi=\tfrac1p$, expected discrepancy $O\!\big(T^{(p-1)/p}(\log T)^{3/2}\big)$ for $p=2$ and $O\!\big(T^{(p-1)/p}\big)$ for $p\ge3$ — i.e. it **matches the offline match-pair order** up to (at most) logarithmic factors, a surprising near-optimality despite never seeing future covariates.
- Simulations confirm the orderings; the pigeonhole advantage over complete randomization shrinks as $p$ grows (negligible at $p=20$), but binary covariates do not hurt performance.

## Contribution
It introduces and names the online blocking problem, situating it precisely against three adjacent literatures (online vector balancing, online $D_A$-optimal design, online bipartite matching) and clarifying the distinctions (equal-group constraint; minimum-weight-matching objective evaluated at horizon end; adversarial arrivals). It delivers the pigeonhole design with matching upper and lower bounds in both single- and multi-dimensional regimes, establishing near-optimality relative to the infeasible offline benchmark, and gives practitioners concrete tuning guidance (uniform cells, cell count, treatment of binary vs. continuous covariates).

## Limitations & open questions
The authors explicitly flag three open problems. (1) The **equal-group constraint** ($|A|=|B|=T/2$) is undesirable when treatment is costly; relaxing it would require a new objective such as discrepancy-plus-variance or extra modeling assumptions on covariates. (2) Whether the **balancing parameter $\beta$ is necessary** is unresolved — simulations suggest dropping the dedicated balancing periods and instead balancing whenever an arm hits $T/2$ achieves comparable, sometimes better, performance. (3) The **adversarial arrival assumption** may be loose: if covariates are drawn from a known/estimable distribution, better designs (and different pigeonholes) may exist.

## Connections
The blocking/stratification tradition runs through Fisher (1936), Cochran and Cox (1957), Imbens and Rubin (2015), and Athey and Imbens (2017), with the matched-pair design as the limiting case and optimal-design treatments in Rosenbaum (1989), Greevy et al. (2004), Imai et al. (2009), Lu et al. (2011), Higgins et al. (2016), Bai (2019), and Harshaw et al. (2019). The online tension is framed by Bhat et al. (2020) on online $D_A$-optimal design (building on Atkinson (1982, 1999)), and the design connects to Efron's (1971) biased-coin design and its covariate-adaptive generalizations in Pocock and Simon (1975). The minimum-weight-matching objective draws on Bertsimas and Tsitsiklis (1997) and matching-estimator work by Abadie and Imbens (2012) and Dehejia and Wahba (2002). The three adjacent online literatures are online vector balancing following Spencer (1977) and Bansal et al. (2020, 2021), online bipartite matching following Karp et al. (1990) and Mehta (2013), and the matching-on-the-fly heuristic of Kapelner and Krieger (2014).

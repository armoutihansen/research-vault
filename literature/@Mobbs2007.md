> [!abstract] Abstract
> Humans, like other animals, alter their behavior depending on whether a threat is close or distant. We investigated spatial imminence of threat by developing an active avoidance paradigm in which volunteers were pursued through a maze by a virtual predator endowed with an ability to chase, capture, and inflict pain. Using functional magnetic resonance imaging, we found that as the virtual predator grew closer, brain activity shifted from the ventromedial prefrontal cortex to the periaqueductal gray. This shift showed maximal expression when a high degree of pain was anticipated. Moreover, imminence-driven periaqueductal gray activity correlated with increased subjective degree of dread and decreased confidence of escape. Our findings cast light on the neural dynamics of threat anticipation and have implications for the neurobiology of human anxiety-related disorders.

## Summary
Mobbs and colleagues built a "virtual predator" video-game paradigm in which participants flee through a 2D maze from an AI agent that can chase, capture, and deliver electric shocks, while being scanned with high-resolution fMRI. As the predator closes spatial distance, the locus of brain activity shifts from the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) under *distal* threat to the midbrain periaqueductal gray (PAG) under *proximal* threat. The forebrain-to-midbrain shift is amplified when a more painful outcome (three shocks vs. one) is anticipated, and PAG activity scales with self-reported dread and lowered confidence of escape. The study translates rodent "predatory imminence continuum" models into human neuroimaging.

## Research question
Do the neural dynamics of threat anticipation in humans mirror the staged defensive systems documented in rodents, such that *distal* (cognitive/strategic) threat recruits prefrontal regions while *proximal* (reflexive) threat recruits phylogenetically older midbrain structures (PAG)? And is this forebrain-to-midbrain switch modulated by the magnitude of anticipated harm?

## Method / identification
This is an experimental fMRI study, not a formal economic model. The design is an active "escape-pain" task instantiating the rodent **predatory imminence continuum** (pre-encounter, post-encounter, circa-strike) in a 9 x 13 maze. Each trial proceeds through phases: a neutral phase (a gray AI circle, $\text{AI}_{\text{neutral}}$, wanders ~6 s, jitter ±2 s); a 2 s cue phase where the circle flashes red signaling it has become a predator, followed by a 2 s cue of the shock level it will deliver on capture — one shock ($\text{AI}^{\text{predator}}_{\text{low}}$), no shock (yoked control), or three shocks ($\text{AI}^{\text{predator}}_{\text{high}}$); a chase phase in which the subject moves a triangle to escape (or, in control, mimics a replayed path); and a rest period. Trial lengths were randomized (16/20/24/28/32 s) and not disclosed, so subjects could not anticipate the end. Mazes were designed as long unimpeded runs to enhance perceived spatial distance.

Identification of the imminence effect rests on contrasting BOLD responses by threat **distance** (distal vs. proximal predator location during the chase) crossed with anticipated **pain magnitude** (high vs. low). Key contrasts: predator cue vs. yoked control cue; distal vs. proximal within each shock level; and direct $\text{AI}^{\text{predator}}_{\text{high}}$ minus $\text{AI}^{\text{predator}}_{\text{low}}$ subtractions. Subject-specific PAG activity was regressed against post-scan questionnaire ratings of dread and confidence of escape.

## Data
n = 14 healthy human subjects. High-resolution fMRI BOLD measurements with reported MNI-space peak coordinates and $Z$-statistics; behavioral measures (avoidance/survival time, movement speed in squares per second, capture frequency); and post-scan self-report questionnaires (dread of capture, confidence of escape). No economic/market data — a within-subject neuroimaging experiment.

## Key findings
- **Behavioral motivation tracks stakes.** Avoidance time was longer under high threat ($24.2 \pm 1.6$ s) than low threat ($19.4 \pm 2.0$ s), $t_{13} = -9.59$, $P < 0.0005$, and subjects moved more efficiently (faster second-half speeds) when escaping $\text{AI}^{\text{predator}}_{\text{high}}$.
- **Threat cue (post-encounter) engages prefrontal/cingulate value circuitry.** The predator cue vs. control cue activated rostral anterior cingulate (rACC), medial orbitofrontal cortex, ventral ACC, and vmPFC.
- **The imminence shift.** *Distal* threat increased vmPFC (incl. subgenual ACC) activity for both shock levels; *proximal* threat increased PAG activity for both. Direct subtraction confirmed $\text{AI}^{\text{predator}}_{\text{high}}$ recruited PAG more than $\text{AI}^{\text{predator}}_{\text{low}}$, while $\text{AI}^{\text{predator}}_{\text{low}}$ recruited anterior vmPFC and basolateral amygdala (BLA) more.
- **Amygdala dissociation.** Proximal high threat engaged the central nucleus (CeA)/bed nucleus of the stria terminalis (BNST); distal high threat engaged the BLA — consistent with the BLA gating strategic behavior and CeA/BNST driving reflexive PAG-mediated defense.
- **Subjective coupling.** PAG activity under proximal high threat correlated with greater dread and lower confidence of escape across individuals; both dorsal and ventral PAG were engaged, with dorsal PAG implicated in active avoidance.

## Contribution
First human demonstration that the rodent "predatory imminence continuum" maps onto a forebrain-to-midbrain dynamic switch, using an ecologically engaging active-avoidance game rather than passive Pavlovian conditioning. It links the *magnitude* of anticipated aversive outcome to the strength of the PAG shift and ties PAG engagement to subjective fear, providing a candidate neurobiological substrate for human anxiety-related disorders (panic, phobia).

## Limitations & open questions
- The authors note that human PAG functional territories (dorsal/ventral, rostral/caudal flight-vs-fight subdivisions) are difficult to dissociate at this resolution and "should be interpreted with caution."
- The interpretation of vmPFC activity is ambiguous — it may reflect comparison of goal-directed avoidance strategies, or downstream amygdala-driven valuation; the design cannot adjudicate.
- Small sample (n = 14); generalization to clinical anxiety populations is proposed but untested here.
- The mapping from a virtual game threat to real-world predation/clinical fear is an inferential leap left open for future work.

## Connections
The paradigm sits within the defensive-systems tradition of Blanchard & Blanchard and the predatory imminence continuum of Fanselow & Lester (1988) and Fanselow (1994), and the fight/flight/freeze functional anatomy of the PAG from Bandler, Keay, Floyd & Price (2000). It draws on McNaughton & Corr (2004) and Deakin & Graeff (1991) for forebrain-midbrain fear circuitry, and on [[@Berns2006|Berns et al. (2006)]] for human neuroimaging of dread (relevant to this vault's interest in anticipatory utility and the timing of aversive outcomes). The amygdala/PAG dissociation builds on Price (2005) and LeDoux's lesion tradition (Phelps, Delgado, Nearing & LeDoux, 2004). For an economist reading this vault, the work connects to neuroeconomic studies of valuation under threat and to anticipatory/dread utility models (e.g., Loewenstein's dread literature), where the disutility of waiting for a known aversive outcome has a measurable neural correlate.

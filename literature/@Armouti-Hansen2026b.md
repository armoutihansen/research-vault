> [!abstract] Abstract
> An experimenter elicits a subject's latent multinomial beliefs through an incentivized count report under a scoring rule. We recast the inference as a partial-identification problem: each rule maps the report to the set of beliefs under which it is optimal, from which coordinate and linear-functional bounds follow. We characterize three rules—squared-distance scoring (closed-form coordinate bounds, linear-program means), frequency-guessing (the known closed-form fixed-prize rule), and Manhattan distance (sharp one-dimensional bounds via threshold root-finds)—unified by a single structural condition: separability and discrete convexity of a per-coordinate cost. No rule dominates: squared-distance for concentrated beliefs, frequency-guessing for balanced, with the two closed-form rules' coordinate widths crossing in the number of positive-report coordinates—a crossover the design comparison locates in the Dirichlet concentration. Manhattan is rarely tightest but barely moves across regimes, the robust choice when the regime is unknown. The body assumes risk neutrality; a binary-lottery implementation extends to risk-averse subjects.

## Summary
The paper studies *frequency-report scoring rules*—mechanisms that ask a subject to report, out of $n$ independent trials, how many times each of $k$ outcomes will occur, paying more when the reported count vector is close to the realized one. Rather than asking whether such rules are *proper* (they are not; the optimal report is a functional of the multinomial sampling distribution, not the belief itself), it asks the *inverse* question: given the rule $S$ and an observed count report $r$, which latent beliefs $p\in\Delta^k$ are consistent with $r$ being optimal? This recasts incentivized belief elicitation as a *partial-identification* problem in the sense of Manski, where the identifying restriction comes from the scoring-rule mechanism rather than from repeated sampling. The central output is the identified set $P_S(r)$ and the coordinate and linear-functional bounds it implies; *informativeness* is the narrowness of those bounds.

## Research question
Which frequency-report count-loss scoring rule lets an experimenter recover the most about a subject's latent multinomial belief $p$ from a single incentivized count report, and is there a uniformly most-informative rule across belief regimes?

## Method / identification
This is a theory paper with a simulation-based design comparison. The environment: $k$ outcomes, latent belief $p\in\Delta^k$, $n$ trials, realized counts $\omega\sim\mathrm{Mult}(n,p)$ over the feasible simplex $\Omega=\{\omega\in\mathbb{N}_0^k:\sum_i\omega_i=n\}$. A scoring rule $S(r,\omega)=a-bD(r,\omega)$ rewards small count error $D$. The forward object is the optimal-report correspondence $R_S(p)=\arg\max_{r\in\Omega}E_p[S(r,\omega)]$; the inverse object is the *mechanism-induced identified set* $P_S(r)=\{p\in\Delta^k:r\in R_S(p)\}$, with sharp coordinate bounds $[\,\underline{p}_{S,i}(r),\overline{p}_{S,i}(r)\,]$ and sharp linear-functional bounds on $\mu(p;x)=\sum_i p_i x_i$.

The organizing result is **Lemma 1 (single-transfer characterization)**: if an optimal report minimizes a separable objective $\sum_i \ell(r_i;p_i)$ over $\Omega$ and each per-coordinate cost $\ell(\cdot;p_i)$ is *discrete-convex* (its successive differences are nondecreasing), then $r$ is optimal iff no single-count transfer lowers the objective, i.e. $\ell(r_i+1;p_i)-\ell(r_i;p_i)\ge \ell(r_j;p_j)-\ell(r_j-1;p_j)$ for all $i,j$ with $r_j>0$, and $P_S(r)$ is *exactly* the set of beliefs satisfying these $O(k^2)$ inequalities. This is the classical greedy-optimality criterion for separable integer resource allocation (Fox; Federgruen & Groenevelt; Ibaraki & Katoh; Murota), here recognized as the structure governing frequency-report elicitation. All three headline rules satisfy the hypothesis; they differ only in whether the single-transfer inequalities are *linear* in $p$ (closed-form polytope bounds) or *transcendental but monotone* (sharp, semi-analytical bounds).

The design comparison (Section 4, script `scripts/design_efficiency.py`) draws $p\sim\mathrm{Dirichlet}(\alpha,\dots,\alpha)$, computes the optimal report and induced bounds for each rule over the grid $n\in\{5,10,20,50\}$, $k\in\{2,3,5,10\}$, $\alpha\in\{0.1,0.3,1,3,10\}$, with 5,000 draws per cell, and reports *win shares* and *cell-best regret* for both an average-coordinate-width target and a linear-mean target.

No empirical data—this is a partial-identification theory paper. ## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 (squared-distance scoring, $S_Q=a-b\lVert r-\omega\rVert_2^2$):** the optimal report is the feasible integer projection of the expected count $np$; the identified set is the *polytope* $P_Q(r)=\{p:n(p_i-p_j)\le r_i-r_j+1\ \forall i,j\text{ with }r_j>0\}$. With $m(r)=|\{j:r_j>0\}|$, the sharp coordinate intervals are closed form: for $r_i>0$, $\big[\tfrac{r_i-1}{n}+\tfrac{1}{nk},\ \tfrac{r_i+1}{n}-\tfrac{1}{nm(r)}\big]$, and for $r_i=0$, $\big[0,\ \tfrac{m(r)}{n(m(r)+1)}\big]$. Mean/functional bounds are linear programs over the same polytope. Mean-oriented (the count analogue of squared-error loss eliciting the mean, à la Gneiting).
- **Proposition 2 (frequency-guessing scoring, $S_0=a-b\,\mathbf{1}\{r\ne\omega\}$):** the fixed-prize exact-match rule of Schlag & Tremewan; the optimal report is a *multinomial mode*. $P_0(r)=\{p:r_j p_i\le(r_i+1)p_j\ \forall i,j\text{ with }r_j>0\}$, with closed-form coordinate interval $\big[\tfrac{r_i}{n+k-1},\ \tfrac{r_i+1}{n+1}\big]$. Restated to make the comparison self-contained; robust to risk aversion under direct payment.
- **Proposition 3 (Manhattan scoring, $S_M=a-b\sum_i|r_i-\omega_i|$):** separable in binomial marginals $W_i\sim\mathrm{Bin}(n,p_i)$; optimal iff $F_i(r_i;n,p_i)\ge F_j(r_j-1;n,p_j)$ for all $i,j$ with $r_i<n,r_j>0$, equivalently a threshold $c\in[0,1]$ with $F_i(r_i-1;n,p_i)\le c\le F_i(r_i;n,p_i)$ for all $i$. The identified set is exact; coordinate bounds are *sharp and semi-analytical*—each is the unique root of a monotone scalar equation (Lemma 2), located by bisection rather than grid search. Median-oriented (count analogue of absolute-error loss eliciting the median).
- **Corollary 1 (the crossover):** the frequency-guessing average coordinate width is a report-independent constant $W_0(n,k)=\tfrac{(k-1)(2n+k)}{k(n+1)(n+k-1)}$; the squared-distance average width $W_Q(m;n,k)$ depends on $r$ only through $m$, and the two cross, $W_Q(1;n,k)<W_0(n,k)<W_Q(k;n,k)$. So which closed-form rule is tighter is decided entirely by report concentration $m$.
- **Design comparison:** report concentration tracks belief concentration, so the ranking is governed by Dirichlet $\alpha$. Squared-distance wins for sparse beliefs (win share $0.84$ at $\alpha=0.1$), frequency-guessing for balanced beliefs (near $1.0$ at $\alpha\ge 3$), crossover near $\alpha=1$; averaged over the whole grid the three rules barely differ ($\approx 0.103$ mean coordinate width) because favorable and unfavorable regimes cancel. **Manhattan is never the single best rule but its regret is nearly flat in $\alpha$ ($0.007$–$0.016$)—the regime-robust, minimax-regret choice.** The frequency-guessing exact-match payment probability collapses as $n,k$ and balance grow (essentially zero at $n=50,k=10$), an implementation cost.

## Contribution
First, the *characterizations*: a closed-form identified set and coordinate bounds for squared-distance scoring (the centerpiece), sharp semi-analytical bounds for Manhattan, alongside the restated frequency-guessing bounds—all unified as instances of Lemma 1, with discrete convexity the sharp dividing line between closed-form and semi-analytical tractability. Second, a *contingent decision rule*: no rule dominates; the most informative rule is matched to the anticipated belief regime and inferential target, with Manhattan as the regime hedge. The paper extends the partial-identification program of [[@Schlag2021|Schlag & Tremewan (2021)]] from their single exact-match rule to a cross-rule comparison, with unified linear-functional inference on one polytope (closed-form rules) or threshold-indexed box-simplex slice (Manhattan).

## Limitations & open questions
- **Optimal-reporting is assumed, not tested.** The whole comparison is conditional on subjects reporting optimally; the informativeness ranking is an *upper bound* on what a rule delivers, not a behavioral prediction. [[@Danz2022|Danz, Vesterlund & Wilson (2022)]] show the binarized scoring rule produces center-biased misreporting even after the risk-attitude problem is neutralized—a comprehension channel left open.
- **Two boundary rules resist tractable bounds.** Hamming distance is separable but not discrete-convex (single-transfer optimality is necessary but not sufficient; $P_H(r)$ is a non-convex semialgebraic set, with a closed-form modal-box inner bound and a transfer-inequality outer bound), and Chebyshev distance is not separable at all. Both become combinatorially intractable for $k=10$. Sharp bounds for these remain open.
- **Behavioral premise untested.** The cognitive-ease claim (subjects reason more readily in counts than abstract probabilities) is inherited from the frequency-format literature, not established here. The symmetric-Dirichlet draw is also restrictive—the asymmetric check shows gradient steepness, not just marginal concentration, matters within the balanced regime.
- **The proposed induced-beliefs experiment is the explicit project hook (Section 5.3):** induce $p$ via a known urn/signal structure so the true belief is known, then check empirical *coverage* of $p$ by $P_S(r)$ across rules, crossing frequency-report vs. standard probability scoring and direct vs. binary-lottery payment, with response times and dominated-report rates—testing rather than presuming the case for frequency reports.

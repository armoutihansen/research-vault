> [!abstract] Abstract
> We develop a theory of moral behavior, individual and collective, based on a general model of identity in which people care about “who they are” and infer their own values from past choices. The model sheds light on many empirical puzzles inconsistent with earlier approaches. Identity investments respond nonmonotonically to acts or threats, and taboos on mere thoughts arise to protect beliefs about the “priceless” value of certain social assets. High endowments trigger escalating commitment and a treadmill effect, while competing identities can cause dysfunctional capital destruction. Social interactions induce both social and antisocial norms of contribution, sustained by respectively shunning free riders or do-gooders.

## Summary
Bénabou and Tirole propose a "third-generation" theory of moral behavior grounded in identity management: people are uncertain about their own deep values (altruism, faith, character), have imperfect access to the motives behind their past actions, and therefore *infer who they are from what they did*. Beliefs about one's type become assets that are valued either for affective reasons (self-esteem, anticipatory utility) or functional ones (self-control). This single mechanism — self-signaling under imperfect recall — generates a hill-shaped (nonmonotonic) demand for identity-affirming behavior in one's prior confidence, which reconciles a long list of otherwise contradictory experimental findings (moral licensing vs. foot-in-the-door, fighting vs. conforming responses to threats), explains taboos on *mere thoughts*, and rationalizes the coexistence of social and antisocial punishment. The same model yields escalating commitment, a welfare-reducing "treadmill effect," and self-defeating destruction of valuable capital under competing identities. This is a theory paper (51 pages); I read the front matter, full model, all propositions, welfare analysis, applications, and conclusion via pdftotext (not visual), sampling appendix proofs only lightly.

## Research question
Why is moral behavior so "flickering" — why do the same people behave generously and then seize on the thinnest veil to revert to selfishness, punish free-riders in one setting and "do-gooders" in another, and refuse even to *contemplate* trading off "sacred" values? The authors ask whether a single cognitive mechanism — beliefs about one's own type, held as valued assets and inferred from one's own actions — can unify these puzzles that defeat standard social-preference (altruism, fairness, reciprocity) and social-identity models.

## Method / identification
A three-period ($t=0,1,2$) game-theoretic model of self-inference. An agent holds relational capital $A_t$, accumulating via $A_{t+1}=A_t+a_t r_t$ where $a_t\in\{0,1\}$ is the (im)moral action and $r_t\ge0$ the return. His type (long-run value of the asset) is $v\in\{v_H,v_L\}$ with prior $\rho$, drawn per $v=v_H$ w.p. $\rho$ and $v_L$ w.p. $1-\rho$. The **supply side** of motivated beliefs is imperfect memory (Assumption 2): at date 1 the agent recalls his true $v$ only with probability $\lambda$; with probability $1-\lambda$ ("malleability") he infers his type from his own past action $a_0$, forming posterior $\hat v\equiv\hat\rho v_H+(1-\hat\rho)v_L$ via Bayes' rule. The **demand side** is a continuation value $V(v,\hat v,A_1)$ satisfying $V_2>0$, $V_{12}\ge0$, $V_{13}>0$ (Assumption 3). Two canonical specifications:
- **Self-esteem / Anticipatory utility (SE/AU):** $V(v,\hat v,A_1)\equiv(s\hat v+\delta v)A_1$, where $s$ is salience/intensity of savoring and $\delta$ the discount factor; self-esteem is the special case $A_t\equiv1$, $r_t\equiv0$, $\delta=0$.
- **Self-control (SC):** present-biased "Self 1" with $\beta<v_L/v_H$ perceives moral cost as $c/\beta$; a stronger self-view $\hat v$ raises the investment threshold $c_1\le\beta\delta\hat v r_1$, so identity buys self-restraint, with $V(v,\hat v,A_1)\equiv\delta v A_1+\int_0^{\beta\delta\hat v r_1}(\delta v r_1-c_1)\,dF(c_1)$.

Equilibrium is monotonic Perfect Bayesian (the $H$ type always invests at least as much: $x_H\ge x_L$; intuitive off-path beliefs imposed), with the Pareto-dominant equilibrium selected under multiplicity. A *sorting condition* ($c_0^H\le c_0^L$, plus $V_{13},V_{12}\ge0$) makes the net return to "good behavior" higher for $H$, so actions are informative. Extensions add a two-agent **ostracism game** (with situational excuse probability $\theta$, separation cost $b$, exogenous break-up $\nu$), a **taboo/sell-out-price** module (price $p$ revealed only by "contemplation," $r_0=z$, $c_0=zp_H$), and a **competing-identities** module (traditional asset $A$ of uncertain value vs. modern asset $B$ of known value $v_B$, with consumption rivalry $\max\{v_A A_2, v_B B_2\}$).

## Data
None — this is a pure theory paper. It is, however, explicitly disciplined against a large body of *existing* experimental and field evidence used as motivating facts and as ex-post validation: "moral wriggle room" (Dana, Kuang & Weber 2007), costly exit from dictator games (Dana, Cain & Dawes 2006), delegation of the "dirty deed" (Hamman, Loewenstein & Weber 2010), Ten-Commandments/token-cheating cues (Mazar, Amir & Ariely 2008), foot-in-the-door (DeJong 1979), moral licensing/credentialing (Monin & Miller 2001; Mazar & Zhong 2010), antisocial punishment of "do-gooders" (Jordan & Monin 2008), cross-country civic-norm correlations (Herrmann et al. 2008), homophobia-arousal (Adams, Wright & Lohr 1996), escalating commitment (Staw 1976), stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson 1995), and identity-priming consumption/time-preference effects (LeBoeuf et al. 2010; [[@Benjamin2010|Benjamin, Choi & Strickland 2010]]).

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 (Equilibrium):** A unique monotonic, undominated equilibrium exists, characterized by thresholds $\tilde\rho\le\bar\rho$ and investment probabilities $x_H(\rho),x_L(\rho)$, partitioning the prior into four regimes — *No investment* (high $\rho$, $H$ can coast), *Separation* ($x_L=0$), *Randomization by* $v_L$ ($0<x_L<1$), and *Universal investment* ($x_L=1$).
- **Proposition 2 (Comparative statics):** Identity investment rises with malleability (lower $\lambda$), lower cost, higher salience $s$ (SE/AU), and higher capital $A_0$ (AU). Critically, the prior has a **hill-shaped** effect on overall investment $\bar x$: it rises in $\rho$ on $[0,\tilde\rho)$, equals 1 on $[\tilde\rho,\bar\rho)$, then collapses to 0. This nonmonotonicity is the engine that reconciles conformity (weak identity) vs. forceful counter-reaction (strong identity), and licensing vs. foot-in-the-door.
- **Proposition 3 (Welfare, SE/AU):** Greater malleability $(1-\lambda)$ *always* reduces welfare; higher (per se valuable) capital $A_0$ can make the agent worse off — the **treadmill effect**, a self-defeating pursuit of the belief that wealth/status/achievement bring happiness; higher salience $s$ can also lower welfare (with implications for advertising). Identity management is in fine wasteful, zero-sum signaling across types.
- **Proposition 4 (Welfare, SC):** Under self-control motives, *more* malleable beliefs can *raise* welfare, by improving date-1 choices ($E[\Delta V]>0$) and/or date-0 choices, but only under specific conditions on the severity of the self-control problem.
- **Taboos (Sec. V):** Contemplating the "sell-out price" is a special case of the model; a taboo on mere thought arises when $V(v,\hat v(1),A_0)-V(v,\hat v(0),A_0-z)\ge zp_H$. Because exploring terms of trade itself destroys the protected belief, agents avoid even hypothetical valuation of "priceless" goods — the "mere contemplation" effect ("To compare is to destroy").
- **Proposition 5 (Social vs. antisocial norms):** In the two-agent ostracism game, exclusion lowers $\lambda$ ("out of sight, out of mind"). Ostracism arises only when actions differ; it targets the *virtuous* peer under benchmarking-on-the-person (correlated types) and the *unvirtuous* peer under benchmarking-on-the-situation (shared, recallable excuse). Both a **prosocial norm** ($x=1$, sustained by shunning sinners) and an **antisocial norm** ($x=0$, sustained by shunning do-gooders) are equilibria over a positive-measure parameter range — endogenous social conformity from purely self-image concerns.
- **Proposition 6 (Competing identities):** Under AU, an agent invests (weakly) less in a known-value modern identity $B$ when it competes with an uncertain-value traditional identity $A$; this is likelier for higher $A_0$, $1-\lambda$, $s$, and is always welfare-reducing. With $c_B<0$ it extends to *active destruction* of valuable capital (e.g., riots destroying one's own community's schools; resistance to assimilation/structural change).

## Contribution
The paper unifies, under one cognitive primitive, two previously separate literatures — beliefs as *consumption* goods (Akerlof–Dickens, Loewenstein, Caplin–Leahy, Brunnermeier–Parker, Kőszegi) and beliefs as *instrumental* self-discipline devices (Carrillo–Mariotti, Bénabou–Tirole 2002/2006, Bodner–Prelec) — showing they generate near-identical positive behavior but *opposite* welfare verdicts. Relative to the economics-of-identity tradition (Akerlof–Kranton), it *endogenizes* identity prescriptions, payoffs, and cognitive costs from an explicit belief-management problem, delivering distinctive predictions — nonmonotonicity, information aversion, taboos on thought, and the result that the *ability* to manage one's self-image can make a person worse off — that social-preference and social-categorization models cannot produce.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit on several fronts, offering clear project hooks:
- **Welfare scope:** the normative analysis is conducted *from a single agent's perspective* and abstracts from direct externalities of his behavior on others; even at the social level, the value of identity must lie entirely in its "side-products" (footnote 40 flags how externalities/informational spillovers would change conclusions).
- **Bayesian sophistication:** agents are modeled as sophisticated ("metacognition"); relaxing this (partial naïveté about pooling) strengthens positive results but alters welfare implications — and naïve updating is in fact *required* for AU welfare *gains* (footnote 27).
- **Memory structure:** the type-independent recall ($\lambda_H=\lambda_L$), the extreme no-recall-after-separation assumptions in the ostracism game, and linearity of $V$ in $A_1$ are simplifications the authors flag as relaxable.
- **Section VII explicitly names two avenues for further research:** (1) **sacred values and taboos** — bringing the markets-vs.-morals debate into formal analysis; (2) **pride/dignity in bargaining and distributive conflict** — self-serving beliefs that shrink the set of sustainable sharing agreements and can cause bargaining impasse under symmetric information (Bénabou–[[@Benabou2009|Tirole 2009]]), with open questions on optimal contract/organization design and the political economy of reform when agents hold motivated beliefs.

## Connections
The paper sits at the intersection of behavioral economics, the economics of identity, and political economy. It is the deepest synthesis of the authors' own self-signaling program (Bénabou & Tirole 2002 "Self-Confidence and Personal Motivation," 2004 "Willpower and Personal Rules," 2006 "Incentives and Prosocial Behavior") and is closest in mechanism to Bodner & Prelec (2003) on self-signaling and Bénabou & Tirole (2004) on rule-based behavior; Young (2006) and Dal Bó & Terviö (2008) extend single-agent self-reputation to steady states, and Battaglini, Bénabou & Tirole (2005) and Bernheim & Thomadsen (2005) to one-shot strategic interaction. It generalizes Akerlof & Kranton's (2000–2005) identity economics and relates to Greif (2009) on externally defined moral standards, Shayo (2009) on identity and redistribution, and Austen-Smith & Fryer (2005) on "acting white." The taboo analysis connects to Fiske & Tetlock (1997), Tetlock et al. (2000), and Roth (2007) on repugnant markets; the social-norm results to Fehr & Gächter (2000) and Herrmann et al. (2008). The opening Adam Smith epigraph (the impartial spectator, "praiseworthiness") frames self-judgment as the paper's intellectual lineage.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Recent developments in social choice theory are critically surveyed in the light of a categorization of interpersonal aggregation problems into four distinct types that seem to require varying treatment but typically do not receive it. Informational inadequacy of the usual social choice framework is discussed in this context. A fairly thorough exploration of the correspondences between consistency conditions for choice functions and regularity properties of the binary relation of preference leads to a re-examination of the class of "impossibility" results in social choice theory, necessitating reinterpretations of various theorems (including Arrow's).

## Summary
Sen surveys and re-organizes a decade-plus of post-Arrow social choice theory around two orthogonal distinctions: whether one aggregates individual *interests* ($I$) or individual *judgements* ($J$), and whether the goal is to reach a *decision* ($D$) or a *welfare judgement* ($W$). This yields four exercise types — $IW$, $ID$, $JW$, $JD$ — that he argues demand different treatment but are routinely forced into one uniform framework. He then maps out the full correspondence between consistency conditions on choice functions (Properties $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$, $\delta$, $\beta(+)$) and regularity properties of preference relations (transitivity, quasi-transitivity, acyclicity, triple-acyclicity), showing that Arrow's theorem and its descendants are best read as theorems about the *base relation* $R_c$ of a choice function. The headline reinterpretation: contraction-consistency is the real source of impossibility; expansion-consistency is cheap. The "score card" by exercise type is $IW$: not disturbing; $ID$: not disturbing; $JD$: quite disturbing; $JW$: dismal.

## Research question
What is actually being aggregated, and to what end, in "social choice" — and once those questions are separated, how damaging are the Arrovian impossibility results for each distinct kind of aggregation exercise? A subsidiary question: are functional (choice-function) formulations genuinely more permissive than relational (binary-relation) formulations, or has that contrast been overdrawn?

## Method / identification
A critical survey plus a self-contained axiomatic consolidation. Sen formalizes a *collective choice rule* (CCR) $R=f([R_i])$ over a finite set $X$ of $\ge 3$ social states with $n\ge 2$ individuals, each holding an ordering $R_i$ with asymmetric/symmetric factors $P_i,I_i$. The Condorcet choice set is $C(S,R)=\{x\in S\mid xRy\ \forall y\in S\}$. He distinguishes *relational* CCRs $R=f([R_i])$ from *functional* CCRs $C(\cdot)=f([R_i])$. From a choice function he extracts two relations: the *revealed* relation $xR^cy$ iff $x\in C(S)$ for some $S\ni y$, and the *base relation* $xR_cy$ iff $x\in C(\{x,y\})$. A choice function is *normal* iff $C(S)=C(S,R^c)$, equivalently *basic binary* iff $C(S)=C(S,R_c)$.

He then catalogues consistency properties: Property $\alpha$ (contraction-consistency: $x\in C(T)\cap S,\ S\subseteq T\Rightarrow x\in C(S)$); $\gamma$ (expansion under union); $\beta$ (if $x,y\in C(S)$ and $S\subseteq T$ then $y\in C(T)\Rightarrow x\in C(T)$); and the strengthened $\beta(+)$ — the "strongest expansion-consistency". Each is tied to a regularity property of $R_c$ or $R^c$. Identification of the impossibility mechanism proceeds by re-deriving the Arrow conditions $U$ (unrestricted domain), $P$ (weak Pareto), $D$ (non-dictatorship), $I$ (independence of irrelevant alternatives, stated choice-theoretically), and showing $I$ collapses to the much weaker relational $I^*$ once one targets the base relation $R_c$. This lets relational impossibility theorems be transported wholesale to functional CCRs via the interpretation $R_c=f([R_i])$.

## Data
None — this is a theoretical survey and axiomatic synthesis. "Empirical" material is restricted to constructed counterexamples: the cake-division problem (majority improvement by impoverishing the worst-off), the paradox of voting, and Ferejohn–Grether's four-alternative profile where the majority-closure method selects a Pareto-inferior state.

## Key findings
- **Weakening rationality buys little (Props. 2–6).** Quasi-transitive social decision functions satisfying $U,I,P$ must be *oligarchic* (Gibbard; Prop. 2); adding positive responsiveness $PR$ restores *dictatorship* (Mas-Colell–Sonnenschein; Prop. 3); under $U,I,P,PR$ with $\ge 4$ alternatives *someone has a veto* (Prop. 4); even *triple-acyclicity* — the weakest rationality permitting choice over triples — yields a veto (Prop. 6). Impossibility extends almost to the floor of collective rationality.
- **Choice-function/relation correspondences (Props. 7–15).** Normal $\Leftrightarrow$ basic binary (Prop. 7); normal $\Leftrightarrow$ $\alpha\wedge\gamma$ (Prop. 8) $\Leftrightarrow$ $\alpha_2\wedge\gamma_2$ (Prop. 9); $\alpha\wedge\beta\Leftrightarrow$ normal with $R^c$ an ordering (Prop. 11); $\beta(+)$ implies both $\beta$ and $\gamma$ but not conversely (Prop. 12); $\beta(+)\Rightarrow R^c$ transitive (Prop. 13) yet does **not** force even triple-acyclicity of the base relation $R_c$ (Prop. 14); $\alpha\Rightarrow R_c$ acyclic (Prop. 15).
- **The central asymmetry.** *Contraction-consistency* ($\alpha$ and even its weakest vestige $\alpha(--)$) precipitates impossibility, while *expansion-consistency* ($\beta,\gamma,\delta,\beta(+)$) is freely accommodated. The majority-closure method (MCM, choice via the transitive closure $R^*$) satisfies $\beta(+)$ and the weak path-independence $*PI$, escaping impossibility — but only by abandoning all contraction-consistency, which lets a Pareto-inferior alternative be chosen through a legitimate path.
- **Reinterpreting Arrow.** Arrow's GPT never actually uses the binariness of his own choice function; the relational framework with $R$ read as the base relation $R_c$ is *more* general than the functional one, not less (Props. 22–27). The Borda rule illustrates the trade-off: the narrow version satisfies $I$ but violates $\alpha$; the broad version satisfies $\alpha$ but violates $I$ — no positional rule satisfies both.
- **Verdict by type.** $IW,ID$: classic framework informationally inadequate, impossibility results "of little interest"; $JW$: dismal; $JD$: quite disturbing, hinging on how much contraction-consistency one demands.

## Contribution
Reframes the entire impossibility literature: instead of cataloguing escape routes, Sen shows that (a) the binary/functional and welfare/decision dichotomies have been conflated, (b) the operative culprit is contraction-consistency on the base relation, and (c) the genuine cure for interest aggregation is *informational* — widening inputs beyond $n$-tuples of orderings to admit interpersonal comparisons (utilitarian $W=\sum_i W_i$, Rawlsian $W=\min_i W_i$). This informational-basis diagnosis became a foundational theme of Sen's later welfare economics.

## Limitations & open questions
- Results are stated for *finite* $X$; Arrow's theorem and the consistency correspondences need substantial modification for infinite alternative sets (Sen flags this explicitly, citing Fishburn, Kirman–Sondermann, Brown).
- The $JD$ verdict is left deliberately open: it "depends much on the importance we attach to contraction-consistency" — a normative question Sen does not settle, an explicit hook for further work on which weak consistency axioms decision procedures should satisfy.
- For *judgement* aggregation, *level comparability* of preference intensities has "no clear counterpart" — how to ground interpersonal intensity comparisons for judgements (as opposed to interests) is left unresolved.
- Domain restrictions are deliberately not imposed; Sen notes the effects of restricted domains "are of interest" but outside scope.
- Whether positional rules other than Borda can reconcile $\alpha$ and $I$ under weaker requirements is left to the cited Gärdenfors / Fine–Fine program.

## Connections
This is the integrative companion to Sen's *Collective Choice and Social Welfare* (1970) and presupposes Arrow's *Social Choice and Individual Values* (1951/1963). It consolidates the oligarchy/veto results of Gibbard (1969, unpublished), Mas-Colell–Sonnenschein (1972), and Blau–Deb (1975), and the functional-CCR impossibility theorems of Blair, Kelly, Suzumura, and Bordes (jointly 1976). The choice-function machinery draws on Richter's rationalizability (1966), Herzberger's base relation (1973), and Plott's path independence (1973). The Gibbard–Satterthwaite manipulability theorem is invoked to qualify the "firmness" of revealed committee preferences. The closing turn toward interpersonal comparisons connects to Harsanyi (1955), Rawls (1971), d'Aspremont–Gevers (1975), and Hammond (1975), and forward to Sen's own measurement work on real national income, poverty, and inequality. For a choice-modeling reader, the $\alpha$-vs-expansion asymmetry and the base-relation reduction are the conceptual bridge between revealed-preference/rationalizability theory and aggregation theory.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Increased consumer valuation or demand for a firm's product may give the firm an incentive to increase its price to extract more surplus from consumers. However, the extant literature has shown that when a firm charges a higher price than what is considered fair (a reference point), some consumers may experience a psychological disutility when buying the firm's product. In this paper, we analyze how consumers' fairness concerns will affect the optimal pricing strategies and profits of the product manufacturer and its retailer in a dynamic setting where market demand grows over time. More specifically, will some consumers' fairness concerns deter an increase in price, benefiting the consumers and making the manufacturer and the retailer worse off? One might intuit that the existence of consumers with fairness concerns would induce the retailer to strategically mitigate their fairness concerns by charging a high initial price so as to be able to charge a high future price without provoking consumers' fairness concerns. Our analysis reveals that the retailer can actually have a cost-reduction incentive, which induces the retailer to charge a low price in the initial period so as to induce the manufacturer to reduce its wholesale price in the later period. Interestingly, consumers' fairness concerns will lead to lower retail prices in each time period when the market growth rate is relatively small, and an increase in the market growth rate can lead to a decrease in both wholesale and retail prices in each period. Furthermore, we show that consumers' fairness concerns can actually lead to an all-win situation for the manufacturer, the retailer and the consumers.

## Summary
A two-period analytical (game-theoretic) marketing/IO model of a manufacturer–retailer channel facing a growing market in which a fraction $\lambda$ of consumers have fairness concerns: they suffer disutility (and reduce demand) if the second-period retail price exceeds the first-period price, which serves as a reference point. The paper shows that, contrary to the naive intuition that fairness concerns hurt firms by deterring price hikes, in a *decentralized* channel they can be all-win. The mechanism is a strategic link between periods: a lower first-period retail price commits the retailer to a low future markup, alleviating double marginalization and inducing the manufacturer to cut its second-period wholesale price. This gives the retailer a "cost-reduction incentive" that can dominate the "fairness-mitigation incentive," producing lower prices in both periods, higher profits for both firms, and higher total consumer surplus.

## Research question
How do a segment of fairness-concerned consumers affect the optimal dynamic (two-period) wholesale and retail pricing decisions and equilibrium profits of a manufacturer and its retailer in a market with growing demand? Specifically: does the common intuition — that fairness concerns deter price increases, benefiting consumers and hurting firms — survive in a decentralized channel?

## Method / identification
Analytical (closed-form) game theory; subgame-perfect equilibrium solved by backward induction. A manufacturer with marginal cost normalized to $0$ sells through a retailer over $t\in\{1,2\}$. Sequence of moves: (1) manufacturer sets first-period wholesale price $w_1$; (2) retailer sets first-period retail price $p_1$ and first-period demand realizes; (3) manufacturer sets $w_2$; (4) retailer sets $p_2$ and second-period demand realizes. The retailer's marginal cost equals the wholesale price.

Demand is reduced-form linear. First period: $D_1=1-\beta p_1$, where $\beta$ is demand elasticity. Without fairness concerns (superscript NF), the second-period demand intercept grows: $D_2^{NF}=1+\delta-\beta p_2^{NF}$, where $\delta$ is the market growth rate. The fairness friction enters second-period demand as a fraction $\lambda\in(0,1)$ of consumers who experience a kinked, loss-averse demand drop when $p_2>p_1$:
$$D_2=\lambda\big(1+\delta-\beta p_2-\gamma\max\{p_2-p_1,0\}\big)+(1-\lambda)\big(1+\delta-\beta p_2\big),$$
where $\gamma$ measures the strength of fairness concerns and $p_1$ is the reference price. The model is solved against two benchmarks: (i) no fairness concerns ($\lambda=0$), and (ii) a centralized (vertically integrated) channel selling directly to consumers. The authors note the reduced form can be micro-founded by a quality-valuation utility model $u=q\theta-p$ with $\theta\sim U[0,1]$ and a rising upper bound in period 2.

## Data
None for the core theory. As motivating evidence the authors run a small online survey ($n=140$) in which subjects' valuation is \$100 and the current price \$95, with a randomly assigned (between-subjects) past price of \$50/\$60/\$70/\$80; purchase rate rises with the past price (i.e., falls with the size of the price increase), consistent with reference-dependent fairness concerns. The survey is illustrative, not used for estimation.

## Key findings
- **Lemma 1** (NF benchmark): closed-form equilibrium, e.g. $w_1^{*NF}=\frac{1}{2\beta}$, $w_2^{*NF}=\frac{1+\delta}{2\beta}$, $p_1^{*NF}=\frac{3}{4\beta}$, $p_2^{*NF}=\frac{3(1+\delta)}{4\beta}$; both prices rise with growth, $w_2^{*NF}>w_1^{*NF}$ and $p_2^{*NF}>p_1^{*NF}$.
- **Lemma 2** (centralized benchmark): fairness concerns *raise* the optimal first-period price ($\tilde p_1^*>\tilde p_1^{*NF}$) and *lower* the second-period price ($\tilde p_2^*<\tilde p_2^{*NF}$) unless $\delta$ is very large; here the naive intuition holds.
- **Proposition 1** (strategic link): in the decentralized channel, for $p_1<\hat P$ the second-period equilibrium wholesale price is increasing in $p_1$ ($\partial w_2^*(p_1)/\partial p_1>0$). A lower $p_1$ raises the fairness-induced demand drop $\gamma(p_2-p_1)$, committing the retailer to a low markup and a higher pass-through, which induces the manufacturer to cut $w_2$. This link is absent when $\lambda=0$.
- **Lemma 3**: full closed-form equilibrium with a paradigm-shift threshold $\delta^*$ (above which the manufacturer switches strategy) and a threshold $\delta^{**}$ (above which first-period sales vanish).
- **Proposition 2**: fairness concerns raise the first-period and lower the second-period wholesale price: $w_1^*>w_1^{*NF}$ and $w_2^*<w_2^{*NF}$.
- **Proposition 3**: for small growth, $\delta<\frac{\gamma\lambda}{4\beta}$, fairness concerns *reduce both* retail prices, $p_1^*<p_1^{*NF}$ and $p_2^*<p_2^{*NF}$ — the cost-reduction incentive dominates the fairness-mitigation incentive (the opposite of the centralized result).
- **Proposition 4**: counterintuitively, an *increase* in the growth rate $\delta$ can *decrease* retail and wholesale prices in both periods, driven by the manufacturer's paradigm shift at $\delta^*$.
- **Proposition 5**: there exists $\bar\delta>0$ such that for $\delta\in[0,\bar\delta)$ fairness concerns raise the manufacturer's profit in *both* periods, via reduced double marginalization.
- **Proposition 6**: fairness concerns can benefit *both* manufacturer and retailer when growth is not too small, and can raise total consumer surplus — an "all-win" outcome.

## Contribution
First analytical treatment of dynamic wholesale-and-retail pricing in a *decentralized* channel facing fairness-concerned consumers who reference past prices. The core conceptual contribution is identifying the retailer's two competing incentives — *fairness-mitigation* (raise $p_1$ to soften the later fairness drop) versus *cost-reduction* (lower $p_1$ to commit to a low future markup and induce a lower $w_2$) — and showing that the latter can dominate, so that consumer fairness concerns *alleviate* double marginalization and become Pareto-improving for firms and consumers. This overturns both the naive "fairness hurts firms" intuition and the centralized-channel result.

## Limitations & open questions
- Growth rate $\delta$ is treated as an exogenous trend; the authors flag as future work how fairness concerns affect firms' *endogenous* incentives to create demand (advertising, marketing campaigns).
- Monopoly channel only; extending to a *competitive* market is proposed but noted to be far less tractable, likely requiring simplifying other model features.
- Core analysis restricted to $\delta\in[0,\delta^{**}]$ (positive first-period sales); the $\delta>\delta^{**}$ case reduces to a reference-price-only model where, contrasting Zhang et al. (2014), the retailer prefers a relatively low (not high) reference price.
- Fairness modeled as a specific kinked, one-sided ($\max\{p_2-p_1,0\}$) loss-aversion form with two consumer types and a static reference equal to $p_1$; richer reference-formation dynamics are not explored.

## Connections
The fairness/loss-aversion demand primitive draws on Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler (1986), Campbell (1999), Sinha & Batra (1999), and Maxwell (2002) on perceived price (un)fairness, and on [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] inequity aversion and [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]] — though the authors frame fairness here as loss aversion relative to a reference price rather than social inequity aversion. On reference-dependent firm pricing it builds on Greenleaf (1995), Kopalle, Rao & Assunção (1996), and Kuksov & Wang (2014). Within distribution channels it relates to Ho & Zhang (2008) on reference-dependent retailer utility, Cui, Raju & Zhang (2007) on distributional channel-fairness, Yi et al. (2018) on selling through intermediaries to mask margins, and most directly Zhang et al. (2014) on dynamic reference-price pricing in a channel, whose "firms prefer higher reference prices" conclusion this paper reverses. Monopoly analyses of consumer fairness — Guo (2015), Guo & Jiang (2016), and Li & Jain (2015) on cross-cohort price discrimination — provide the centralized counterpoint. The micro-foundation uses the standard linear-channel demand setup of [[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin (1983)]] and [[@Moorthy1988|Moorthy (1988)]].

## Summary
Akerlof and Dickens import the psychological theory of cognitive dissonance into a formal economic model by granting agents preferences over their own beliefs, partial control over those beliefs, and persistence of beliefs once chosen. They build a two-period competitive labor-market model of hazardous work in which fully rational, forward-looking workers choose to believe their job is safe to avoid the disutility of fear, and consequently fail to adopt cost-effective safety equipment when it later appears. The paper shows that even under rational expectations and complete information there can be a Pareto-improving role for mandatory safety legislation, and sketches parallel applications to innovation, advertising, Social Security, and crime.

## Research question
Can the psychologists' finding that people hold systematically biased, preference-driven beliefs (cognitive dissonance) be incorporated into a standard maximizing economic model, and what are its welfare consequences? Concretely: if workers can choose to believe a dangerous job is safe, when is government safety regulation welfare-improving even though workers are rational and fully informed?

## Method / identification
The contribution is a formal two-period general-equilibrium model with competitive labor and product markets. The three behavioral premises (highlighted by the user) are: (1) agents have preferences over beliefs as well as over states of the world; (2) agents can choose their beliefs; (3) chosen beliefs persist. Operationally, a worker in the hazardous industry faces true accident probability $q$ but may choose any subjective probability $q^{*}\in[0,q]$, after which he must act as if $q^{*}$ is true. The cost of an accident is $c_a$; the per-period cost of the safety device is $c_s$; fear is $f=q^{*}/q$ priced at unit cost $c_f$. The safe industry pays a fixed anchoring wage $w_s$. The model is solved by backward induction from period 2. Key results are stated as four propositions: in period 2 a worker buys safety equipment iff $q^{*}>qc_s/(qc_a+c_f)$; in period 1 the worker chooses either $q^{*}=0$ (full denial, minimizing fear) or $q^{*}=qc_s/(qc_a+c_f)$ (just enough belief to later buy the device), comparing the residual fear cost $c_sc_f/(qc_a+c_f)$ against the wrong-decision cost $qc_a-c_s$. Equilibrium wages are pinned down by indifference with the safe industry, and the welfare comparison to a regime with mandatory safety equipment yields the legislation result. Crucially the model assumes rational expectations: workers foresee their own future dissonance, so any welfare gain does not rest on workers being naive.

## Data
None — this is a theory paper. The behavioral premises are motivated by citing classic social-psychology experiments (race-track bettors revising odds after betting; Brehm's appliance-rating study; Davis and Jones and Glass on derogating victims of one's own cruelty; Jellison and Mills on biased information receptivity; Axsom and Cooper's effort-justification weight-loss study showing year-long persistence) and anecdotes (benzene workers, unworn radiation badges, unpurchased flood/earthquake insurance).

## Key findings
- Proposition 1: the period-2 hazardous wage equals $w_s+c_s$.
- Proposition 2: in period 2 a worker buys safety equipment iff $q^{*}\geq qc_s/(qc_a+c_f)$.
- Proposition 3: in period 1 the worker sets $q^{*}=0$ if $(qc_a-c_s)<c_sc_f/(qc_a+c_f)$, and $q^{*}=qc_s/(qc_a+c_f)$ otherwise.
- Proposition 4: the period-1 hazardous wage is $w_s+qc_a+\min(qc_a-c_s,\ c_sc_f/(qc_a+c_f))$.
- Main welfare result: when workers rationally choose $q^{*}=0$ they later refuse cost-effective safety equipment because they have an incorrect marginal rate of substitution between safety and income; mandatory safety legislation then lowers the first-period reservation wage and hence the product price, leaving workers with the same two-period expected income $2w_s$ while making consumers strictly better off — a Pareto improvement (the gain equals the shaded area in Figure 2). The legislation's benefit accrues to consumers only when workers foresee their dissonance; otherwise the gain accrues to the workers themselves.
- Precommitment cannot substitute for legislation: if recontracting is allowed, rational workers foresee they will want to renege once they have convinced themselves the job is safe, so they never precommit; reputation could sustain precommitment but only by stepping outside the competitive model.

## Contribution
This is a foundational paper in behavioral economics and the economics of motivated/endogenous beliefs. It demonstrates a methodological template — translate a psychological regularity into preferences, a choice set, and a constraint, then embed it in standard equilibrium analysis — and shows that motivated belief-choice can generate a normative case for paternalistic regulation without abandoning rational expectations, competitive markets, or full information. It reframes anomalies (noninformational advertising, popularity of Social Security and safety mandates, non-purchase of actuarially favorable disaster insurance) as predictions of belief-choice rather than as misinformation.

## Limitations & open questions
- Beliefs are assumed "totally plastic" ($q^{*}$ freely chosen in $[0,q]$); the authors flag the more realistic and general case in which available information constrains feasible beliefs as an open modeling direction.
- The complete-information / rational-expectations assumption is acknowledged as unrealistic ("we would not expect people to be aware of their future behavior"); relaxing it changes who captures the welfare gains and makes precommitment contracts impossible — an explicit open problem.
- Whether reputation-based institutions can sustain precommitment (and thereby substitute for legislation) is left as a non-competitive extension.
- The applications to innovation, advertising, Social Security, and crime are described by the authors as "much more speculative" than the safety case and are offered as conjectures inviting formal modeling.

## Connections
The paper extends Hirschman (1965), who used cognitive dissonance to describe attitude change during development, and positions itself against Becker (1962), who treated irrational behavior as random deviations rather than predictable bias. The crime application directly contests Tullock (1974) and Becker (1968) on deterrence. The psychological substrate draws on Festinger-style dissonance theory via Aronson (1979). It is an early anchor for the modern literature on motivated beliefs and self-deception — notably Bénabou and Tirole's work on self-confidence, identity, and motivated reasoning, and Bénabou's models of group and organizational beliefs — and for belief-based utility and anticipatory-utility models in the spirit of Caplin and Leahy. The endogenous-safety-perception channel connects to Dickens (1981) on noisy risk estimates and self-selection, and the welfare-of-regulation theme prefigures behavioral-paternalism arguments.

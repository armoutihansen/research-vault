> [!abstract] Abstract
> Eight alternative methods of eliciting preferences between money and a consumption good are identified: two of these are standard willingness-to-accept and willingness-to-pay measures. These methods differ with respect to the reference point used and the dimension in which responses are expressed. The loss aversion hypothesis of Tversky and Kahneman's theory of reference-dependent preferences predicts systematic differences between the preferences elicited by these methods. These predictions are tested by eliciting individuals' preferences for two private consumption goods; the experimental design is incentive-compatible and controls for income and substitution effects. The theory's predictions are broadly confirmed.

## Summary
The paper builds an incentive-compatible laboratory test of Tversky and Kahneman's [1991] reference-dependent preference theory by exploiting a clever insight: there are not two but *eight* distinct ways to elicit an individual's preference between the same two bundles of (good, money), differing along two axes — the reference point at which the individual is endowed, and the response mode (the dimension in which the answer is expressed). Conventional Hicksian theory says all eight elicitation methods must reveal the *same* implicit preference between two fixed bundles $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{d}$, because choice is endowment-independent. Loss aversion instead predicts a specific *ordering* of how the proportion preferring $\mathbf{a}$ falls as the reference point moves. Eliciting all eight measures for Coke and for luxury chocolate vouchers from 156 subjects, the authors find that the Hicksian common-preference null is decisively rejected and that the divergences run in the directions loss aversion predicts.

## Research question
Are individuals' riskless preferences reference-dependent (loss-averse) in a way that conventional Hicksian consumer theory cannot accommodate, even after controlling for the income and substitution effects that Hanemann [1991] argued could explain WTA–WTP gaps? More sharply: do implicit preferences between two *fixed* bundles systematically shift with the reference point used to elicit them?

## Method / identification
Consider two goods $i,j$ (one a specific good, one money). For an endowment-based change in good $i$ from $x_i'$ to $x_i''$, valued in units of good $j$, the authors define four measures: willingness to pay ($\mathrm{WTP}_{ji}$), willingness to accept ($\mathrm{WTA}_{ji}$), equivalent loss ($\mathrm{EL}_{ji}$), and equivalent gain ($\mathrm{EG}_{ji}$). These are *theory-free, independently observable* magnitudes; the four differ only in which bundle the subject is endowed with.

Under Hicksian theory with utility $u(x_i,x_j)$, each measure solves an indifference equation, e.g. $u(x_i'',\,x_j'-\mathrm{WTP}_{ji})=u(x_i',x_j')$. This forces the equalities $\mathrm{EL}_{ji}=\mathrm{WTP}_{ji}$ and $\mathrm{EG}_{ji}=\mathrm{WTA}_{ji}$, and (for a normal good) the inequality $\mathrm{WTP}_{ji}(x_i',x_i'',x_j')\le\mathrm{WTA}_{ji}(x_i'',x_i',x_j')$, with a gap bounded by the price flexibility of income.

Under reference-dependent theory there is a preference relation $\succeq_{\mathbf{r}}$ conditional on reference point $\mathbf{r}$, represented by a (possibly kinked) utility $u_{\mathbf{r}}(\cdot,\cdot)$; the four measures now solve equations relative to *different* reference utilities, so the Hicksian equalities need not hold. The key behavioral axiom is **loss aversion**, formalized via the relation "$s$ favors $\mathbf{x}$": for bundles with $x_i>y_i$, $y_j>x_j$ and reference points with $r_j=s_j$, $s$ favors $\mathbf{x}$ in relation to $\mathbf{y}$ and $\mathbf{r}$ iff $\mathbf{x}\sim_{\mathbf{r}}\mathbf{y}\Rightarrow\mathbf{x}\succ_{s}\mathbf{y}$ and $\mathbf{x}\succ_{\mathbf{r}}\mathbf{y}\Rightarrow\mathbf{x}\succ_{s}\mathbf{y}$ (a slight weakening of Tversky–Kahneman so Hicks is a limiting case).

Identification trick: fix four bundles $\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c},\mathbf{d}$ on a grid ($a_1=c_1=x_1'$, $b_1=d_1=x_1''$, $a_2=b_2=x_2''$, $c_2=d_2=x_2'$) so that each of the eight elicitation tasks asks "is $\mathbf{a}$ preferred to $\mathbf{d}$?" but from a different reference point ($\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c},\mathbf{d}$) and in a different response mode (money vs. the good). Because all eight rankings concern the *same* $\mathbf{a},\mathbf{d}$, every Hicksian income and substitution effect is held fixed. Loss aversion then yields signed predictions: $\mathbf{b}$ favors $\mathbf{d}$, $\mathbf{c}$ favors $\mathbf{d}$, $\mathbf{d}$ favors $\mathbf{d}$ — so the share with implicit preference $\mathbf{a}\succ\mathbf{d}$ should fall as the reference point moves $a\to b,c\to d$. Contingent-weighting theory [Tversky, Sattath, Slovic 1988] adds a prediction that switching the response mode to the good shifts preference toward $\mathbf{d}$.

Elicitation used an incentive-compatible **Becker–DeGroot–Marschak** mechanism (one of eighteen tasks chosen at random for real), preferred to repeated auctions because it precludes cross-subject price contamination. The null hypothesis is a **stochastic / random-preference Hicksian model** (Becker–DeGroot–Marschak 1963; Loomes–Sugden 1995): each subject draws a Hicksian ordering at random per task, so the probability of revealing $\mathbf{a}\succ\mathbf{d}$ is constant across tasks and (since subgroups are random samples) across subgroups. Tests: pairwise $\chi^2$ tests of association between implicit preference and reference point / response mode, plus an aggregate probit (LIMDEP) with dummies BREF, CREF, DREF (reference point, benchmark $\mathbf{a}$) and RMODE (response mode = good).

## Data
Primary experimental data: 156 subjects (mostly UEA undergraduate/postgraduate students). Two goods specifications — Coke ($x_1'=2$, $x_1''=6$ cans; $x_2'=£2.20$, $x_2''=£3.00$) and vouchers for premium Belgian chocolates ($x_1'=2$, $x_1''=12$; $x_2'=£1.50$, $x_2''=£3.50$). Subjects were split at random into two subgroups (I, $n=80$; II, $n=76$), each facing four of the eight tasks per good, with task order randomized and Hicksian-equivalent measures assigned to different subgroups to limit contamination. ~30 minutes of scripted instruction plus four practice exercises with biscuits; comprehension checks gave an 89% success rate (123/156 correct on both test questions).

## Key findings
1. **Reference-point effects (main result).** As the reference point moves from $\mathbf{a}$ to $\mathbf{d}$, the share with implicit preference $\mathbf{a}\succ\mathbf{d}$ drops sharply (Coke, money mode: 60% → 50% → 26% → 16% across reference points $a,b,c,d$). The stochastic-Hicksian null is rejected in **14 of 20** pairwise $\chi^2$ comparisons (and in all four aggregate tests), with violations signed as reference-dependent theory predicts.
2. **A new class of WTA–WTP test.** The $\mathbf{a}$-vs-$\mathbf{d}$ comparison in the *consumption-good* response mode (WTA$_{12}$ vs. WTP$_{12}$) is a novel disparity test beyond the standard money-mode WTA/WTP gap; both modes show highly significant, correctly-signed divergences despite income/substitution effects being controlled.
3. **Probit estimates.** Aggregating, the restriction BREF=CREF=DREF=RMODE=0 (Hicks) is rejected at 1% for both goods. All six reference-point dummies are correctly signed; DREF has the largest magnitude in each model; BREF, CREF, DREF are significant at 1% in all but one case (BREF insignificant for Coke). Both $\mathrm{BREF}>\mathrm{DREF}$ and $\mathrm{CREF}>\mathrm{DREF}$ are confirmed at 1%.
4. **Weak response-mode effects.** Contingent-weighting predicts RMODE $<0$; the coefficient is correctly signed in both models but **never significantly different from zero**, and only 2 of 8 pairwise response-mode $\chi^2$ tests are significant. So response mode matters far less than reference point.
5. **Coke vs. chocolates.** Contrary to the authors' prior hunch that effects would be much stronger for the unfamiliar good, the two goods behave very similarly; chocolates are only *marginally* more supportive (three vs. two significant reference dummies).

## Contribution
The paper converts a loose, pre-existing body of WTA–WTP evidence into a sharp, falsifiable test of reference-dependent preferences. Its core methodological innovation is the recognition that one preference comparison can be elicited eight ways, generating *new* directional predictions (over equivalent-gain and equivalent-loss measures, and over response modes) that go beyond the familiar WTA/WTP disparity and that cannot be explained away by Hanemann-style substitution effects. By holding the compared bundles fixed and using a BDM mechanism with a stochastic-preference null, it provides arguably the cleanest controlled confirmation that loss aversion is a robust feature of riskless preferences, while showing response-mode (framing) effects are comparatively weak.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Conditional endowments may understate effects.** Because the relevant endowment materializes only if a task is later drawn "for real," subjects may not fully integrate it into their "current position"; citing Loewenstein–Adler [1995], the authors note real endowments raise WTA more than conditional ones, so the design likely *under*-detects reference effects — "a particularly severe test."
- **Market-experience erosion (the central open problem).** Loss aversion might be a "relatively superficial phenomenon" eroded by repeated buying/selling, so experienced agents could behave Hicksian even when subjects do not. The experiment was not designed to test this; the authors explicitly call for defenders of Hicks to "propose testable hypotheses about the precise mechanisms by which market experience induces Hicksian behavior."
- **Which welfare measure to use.** If loss aversion is real, the discipline must build welfare measures compatible with reference dependence; the consensus preference for WTP over WTA (Arrow et al. 1993) is questioned — equivalent-gain measures might be *less* biased than WTP for valuing nontraded goods. The authors hope to spur work on elicitation methods beyond WTP/WTA.
- **Pairwise inference is limited.** Significant $\mathbf{a}\to\mathbf{d}$ shifts mechanically imply a shift on at least one intermediate pair, so intermediate ($b,c$) comparisons give only weak independent support; the authors lean on the aggregate probit instead.
- **Not fully between-subjects.** Sample-size constraints forced a partial within-subject design; cross-task contamination is mitigated by spacing and randomization but not eliminated. A companion binary-choice experiment (comparing implicit valuations against revealed choices) is deferred to another paper.

## Connections
The theoretical target is Tversky and Kahneman's [1991] reference-dependent / loss-aversion model; the response-mode predictions draw on their contingent-weighting theory (Tversky, Sattath, Slovic 1988) and the preference-reversal literature (Slovic–Lichtenstein 1983; [[@Tversky1990|Tversky, Slovic, Kahneman 1990]]). The WTA–WTP disparity tradition includes Knetsch–Sinden [1984], Knetsch [1989], and Kahneman, Knetsch, Thaler [1990] on endowment effects, with related field/lab evidence from Samuelson–Zeckhauser [1988] (status quo bias), Andreoni [1995], Myagkov–Plott [1995], and Benartzi–Thaler [1995] (equity premium). The Hicksian-defense / substitution-effect counterargument is Hanemann [1991], building on Randall–Stoll [1980] and Hicks [1943, 1956]; Shogren et al. [1994] is the closest prior attempt to separate substitution from loss aversion. The contingent-valuation policy backdrop is Mitchell–Carson [1989] and the NOAA panel (Arrow et al. 1993). The stochastic-preference econometric framework comes from Becker–DeGroot–Marschak [1963] and Loomes–Sugden [1995], and the incentive mechanism from Becker–DeGroot–Marschak [1964].

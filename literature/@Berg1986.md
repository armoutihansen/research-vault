> [!abstract] Abstract
> In an experimental setting when outcomes are stochastically related to actions, predictions of equilibrium behavior depend not only on the participants' preference orderings of outcomes, but also on their orderings of lotteries on outcomes as well. We introduce and test a reward structure that can be utilized in any experimental setting to allow the experimenter to decree beforehand the subjects' preferences for lotteries on experimental outcomes. We show analytically that the proposed reward structure can induce subjects to behave as if they have the decreed preference function defined on experimental outcomes. Empirical tests using two different choice settings provide evidence to support this ability to control preferences.

## Summary
The paper introduces a reward-structure procedure — later known widely as the *binary lottery procedure* — that lets an experimenter induce *any* desired risk attitude over an experimental exchange unit, not merely the more-is-better ordering of Smith's Induced Value Theory. Smith (1976) controls preferences when payoffs are a deterministic function of actions; but once outcomes are stochastically related to actions, equilibrium predictions hinge on subjects' preferences over *lotteries* on the exchange units, which the experimenter cannot otherwise observe or fix. Berg, Daley, Dickhaut & O'Brien close this gap with a two-prize gamble whose probability of winning the preferred prize is a controlled function of accumulated "points." Because expected utility is linear in the winning probability of a two-prize gamble, mapping points $q$ into the win-probability $P(x_1\mid q)$ via a prespecified $G(q)$ makes the subject behave as an expected-utility maximizer with induced preference $G(q)$ over points — concave (risk averse), linear (risk neutral), or convex (risk preferring) — regardless of the subject's own (unknown) utility for the dollar prizes. The procedure is operationalized with a physical "prize wheel" and validated in two individual-decision tasks. Coverage is full: theory/proof, operationalization, design, hypotheses, and results are all represented.

## Research question
Can an experimenter exogenously *control* (induce) subjects' preferences over lotteries on units of experimental exchange — i.e., fix their risk attitude by design — so that equilibrium predictions in stochastic-outcome experiments become sharp and theory-testable, and does the induction actually take hold behaviorally?

## Method / identification
Setting: a subject chooses action $a\in A$; outcome points $q\in Q$ ($Q$ bounded) are drawn from a known conditional density $f(q\mid a)$; points map to the win-probability $P(x_1\mid q)$ of a two-prize gamble over prizes $x_1,x_2$ with $U(x_1)>U(x_2)$.

**Proposition (induction).** Assume an expected-utility subject and an experimenter who observes only that $U(x_1)>U(x_2)$. If $P(x_1\mid q)$ is built as an increasing linear (positive affine) transformation of a prespecified $G(q)$, the subject behaves as if his preferences over $q$ are $G(q)$.

*Proof sketch.* The subject solves
$$\max_a \int f(q\mid a)\,[\,P(x_1\mid q)U(x_1)+(1-P(x_1\mid q))U(x_2)\,]\,dq.$$
Utility is unique up to positive affine transformation, so set $U(x_1)=1$, $U(x_2)=0$, reducing the program to $\max_a \int f(q\mid a)\,P(x_1\mid q)\,dq$. With $P(x_1\mid q)=a+bG(q)$, $b>0$, this is equivalent to $\max_a \int f(q\mid a)\,G(q)\,dq$ — i.e., the subject acts as an EU maximizer with utility $G(q)$ over points. Risk neutrality ($G(q)=q$) is the special case requiring $P(x_1\mid q)$ linear in $q$; there the procedure coincides with the "bargaining in probabilities" device of [[@Roth1979|Roth & Malouf (1979)]]. The own-prize utility $U(\cdot)$ cancels — the key identification result.

**Operationalization.** A "prize wheel" depicts $P(x_1\mid q)=a+bG(q)$. Points are mapped onto the circle's $[0,360]$ degrees by solving $a+bG(q_L)=0$ and $a+bG(q_H)=360$ for $(a,b)$. Spinning a spinner; landing at $\le$ the won points yields $x_1$. The mechanism assumes only arithmetic competence and an "as if" reading of theory — no subject knowledge of functional forms.

**Tests.** Two individual-decision tasks: (1) a *paired-choice task* (subject picks the preferred of two point-lotteries; predicted choice maximizes $\sum_Q f(q\mid a)P(x_1\mid q)$); (2) a *value-setting task* eliciting the subject's exchange value (certainty equivalent) of a bet via the Becker–DeGroot–Marschak (1964) incentive-compatible mechanism, stated in points. Predicted values are the certainty equivalents under the induced $G(q)$.

## Data
Original lab experiment. 80 recruited from introductory accounting at the University of Minnesota; 47 participated, randomly assigned: Group 1 (risk averse) $n=23$ with $G(q)=-e^{-0.11q}$; Group 2 (risk preferring) $n=24$ with $G(q)=e^{-0.11q}$ (CARA in points). Prizes set to equalize expected pay across groups ($\$1/\$0$ averse, $\$3/\$0$ preferring); average payout $\approx\$15$. Three parts: 6 paired choices (Parts 1 and 3) and 12 value-setting decisions (Part 2), one per bet.

## Key findings
- **Paired-choice (H0: choice unrelated to induced-optimal bet).** 88.3% of choices matched the prediction overall (92.8% averse, 84.0% preferring), rejecting the 50%-random null at $\alpha<0.01$ (Chi-squared / binomial). 61% of averse and 42% of preferring subjects chose the predicted bet on all six trials; none below four. Because bets were equated on expected points, the strong directional choices reject the rival hypothesis that subjects merely maximize expected points (i.e., behave risk-neutrally).
- **Repetition.** Predicted-choice share rose from Part 1 to Part 3 (averse 87.0%→98.5%; preferring 80.6%→87.5%; not individually significant), and unpredicted choices clustered on the first pair — a sequencing/learning effect.
- **Value-setting (H0: elicited values unrelated to predicted).** Spearman correlations of average elicited value with the certainty equivalent were 0.881 (averse) and 0.860 (preferring), both significant at 0.01; the cross-group difference $(\text{PVRP}-\text{PVRA})$ vs. $(\text{AVRP}-\text{AVRA})$ correlated 0.853 — the induction reproduces both the *ordinal* and the relative *cardinal* structure across treatments.
- **Anomalies.** Risk-averse subjects systematically over-valued bets (11/12) with error rising over time ($r=0.734$); some valuations violated monotonicity (above max or below min attainable points); individual variance was large.

## Contribution
Provides the formal foundation and a practical, assumption-light implementation for *inducing arbitrary risk preferences* in the lab — extending Smith's Induced Value Theory from deterministic to stochastic-outcome environments. This enables clean equilibrium predictions in settings where risk attitudes are decisive (asymmetric information, moral hazard, adverse selection, markets under uncertainty), removing the confound that Plott & Sunder (1982) and Plott & Wilde (1982) had to assume away via risk neutrality.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Cardinal control is imperfect.** The risk-averse group's systematic over-valuation and growing error suggest an unmodeled bias; what causes the asymmetry between the averse and preferring groups, and can the wheel/elicitation be redesigned to remove it?
- **EU dependence.** The induction relies on the expected-utility hypothesis (linearity in probability of a two-prize gamble); if subjects violate EU (probability weighting, the common-ratio effect), the mapping no longer delivers $G(q)$. How robust is induction under non-EU preferences?
- **Monotonicity violations** indicate some subjects did not even satisfy more-is-better in points — open question of how much instruction/repetition is needed for reliable control.
- **External validity untested in markets.** Validation was confined to individual decisions; embedding in an experimental market yields only a joint test of market theory and the induction. Calibrating the procedure inside a market remains open.
- **Within-subject heterogeneity** is large; aggregate success masks individuals for whom induction failed (some negative individual correlations).

## Connections
The procedure descends directly from Smith's (1976) Induced Value Theory and is the EU-based "binary lottery" complement to it for stochastic outcomes. The risk-neutral special case is shown equivalent to the "bargaining in probabilities" device of [[@Roth1979|Roth & Malouf (1979)]]; see also Roth & Murnighan (1982) and Roth & Schoumaker (1983). Elicitation of certainty equivalents uses the incentive-compatible mechanism of Becker, DeGroot & Marschak (1964). The motivating market applications are Plott & Sunder (1982, 1983) and Plott & Wilde (1982), whose risk-neutrality assumptions this method aims to relax. The work connects to the broader literatures on preference elicitation and incentive compatibility, and the noted EU dependence anticipates later critiques tied to non-expected-utility behavior and the validity of binary-lottery incentives.

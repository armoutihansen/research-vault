> [!abstract] Abstract
> (no abstract in Zotero) We show that team formation can serve as an implicit commitment device to overcome problems of self-control. If individuals have present-biased preferences, effort that is costly today but rewarded at some later point in time is too low from the perspective of an individual's long-run self. If agents interact repeatedly and can monitor each other, a relational contract involving teamwork can help to improve performance. The mutual promise to work harder is credible because the team breaks up after an agent has not kept this promise, which leads to individual underproduction in the future, reducing future utility.

## Summary
Fahn and Hakenes show that forming a team can act as an endogenous commitment device for individuals with present-biased ($\beta$-$\delta$) preferences. In an infinite-horizon model with two sophisticated, time-inconsistent agents, a relational contract sustained by mutual monitoring lets agents promise each other high effort; reneging triggers permanent reversion to (too-low) individual production, and the resulting loss of future utility makes the promise credible. Strikingly, more severe self-control problems can *improve* team performance, because they lower an agent's off-path outside option, and this can hold even when teamwork carries technological benefits — so present-biased agents may outperform time-consistent ones.

## Research question
Can the formation of a team — absent any technological advantage, and despite the standard free-rider problem — serve as a self-disciplining device that helps present-biased individuals overcome their self-control problems and raise effort toward the first best?

## Method / identification
A pure theory paper: an infinite-horizon, discrete-time game with two risk-neutral agents $i\in\{1,2\}$ living over $t\in\{0,1,\dots\}$. Each period an agent chooses one project and effort $e_t$; a project returns $V$ next period with probability equal to effort, at immediate quadratic cost $ce_t^2/2$. Preferences are quasi-hyperbolic (Laibson; O'Donoghue–Rabin): a period-$t$ utility stream is valued as $u_t+\beta(\delta u_{t+1}+\delta^2 u_{t+2}+\cdots)$ with $\beta,\delta\in(0,1]$, and agents are *sophisticated*. Under individual production, current-self effort is $e^I=\beta\delta V/c$, which falls short of the long-run-preferred first best $e^{FB}=\delta V/c$ whenever $\beta<1$.

In the teamwork regime agents jointly work on a project and split output $(e_{1,t}+e_{2,t})V$ equally; effort is mutually observable but non-verifiable. A stationary symmetric relational contract specifies team effort $e^T$, and (following Abreu 1988) any deviation is met with the harshest credible punishment — permanent breakdown to individual production. The solution concept is subgame-perfect equilibrium that is also a Nash equilibrium of every subgame given the agents' time-inconsistent preferences and continuation play. The key object is the incentive-compatibility constraint $U^T\ge U^D$: the first-period free-rider gain (work zero on the team, free ride, work an individual project) must be outweighed by the discounted value of future cooperation,

$$\Big(\beta\delta e^T\tfrac{V}{2}-c\tfrac{(e^T)^2}{2}\Big)-\Big(\beta\delta e^I V-c\tfrac{(e^I)^2}{2}\Big)+\frac{\beta\delta}{1-\delta}\Big[\Big(\delta e^T V-c\tfrac{(e^T)^2}{2}\Big)-\Big(\delta e^I V-c\tfrac{(e^I)^2}{2}\Big)\Big]\ge 0.$$

Extensions add exogenous technological benefits via $e_1+e_2+2\alpha\min\{e_1,e_2\}$, and an "intrapersonal relational contract" (a relational contract with one's own future selves) under costly project experimentation.

## Key findings
- **Lemma 1:** For $\beta=1$ (time-consistent agents) no positive team effort can be enforced — the outside option is already first best, so breakup is costless and deviation always tempts.
- **Lemma 2:** No effort $e^T\le e^I$ can be enforced; hence whenever a team *is* feasible it strictly improves on individual effort, overcoming the self-control problem.
- **Proposition 1:** For every $\beta<1$ and any $e^T\in(e^I,e^{FB}]$, that effort is enforceable once $\delta$ is close enough to 1.
- **Proposition 2:** Positive team effort is enforceable iff $\delta\ge\underline{\delta}\equiv\frac{2\sqrt{4-6\beta+3\beta^2}-1}{5-8\beta+4\beta^2}$, with $d\underline{\delta}/d\beta\ge 0$ — a lower $\beta$ (more present bias) lowers the threshold, making teamwork easier to sustain.
- **Proposition 3:** First-best team effort is enforceable iff $\delta\ge\delta^{FB}\equiv\frac{1-\beta(1-\beta)}{1-\beta^2(1-\beta)}$; the fundamentals $V,c$ drop out. $\delta^{FB}$ is non-monotone in $\beta$, so for moderate initial self-control problems, *more severe* present bias makes the first best easier to sustain.
- **Extension (technological benefits, $\alpha>0$):** Even when teamwork is independently advantageous, time-inconsistent agents can outperform time-consistent ones as long as $\alpha$ is not too large — the same vulnerability mechanism.

The unifying intuition: relational contracts work better when agents are vulnerable; a present-biased agent who is "thrown back on his own" faces a particularly bad outside option and so is willing to sustain higher cooperative effort.

## Contribution
The paper unites three literatures — incentives in teams/partnerships, present-biased preferences, and relational contracts — to derive a novel, non-technological rationale for *endogenous* team formation: teams as implicit, self-imposed commitment devices against procrastination. Whereas prior work (Che & Yoo 2001 and followers) takes teams as exogenous and studies principal–agent relational contracts, here two identical individuals voluntarily team up purely to discipline themselves. The counterintuitive result that present bias can raise equilibrium performance, and that present-biased agents may beat time-consistent ones, is the paper's signature contribution. Methodologically it shows that embedding behavioral (quasi-hyperbolic) preferences into a relational-contracting framework yields new implications.

## Limitations & open questions
- The model abstracts from a **principal who assigns agents to jobs**; the authors flag studying optimal workplace design for self-control-prone employees (e.g., whether the principal can commit to output bonuses, whether agents may freely leave after a deviation) as future research.
- Restricted to **two identical agents** and symmetric equilibria; the online-appendix mixed-team and heterogeneity cases are only sketched.
- Assumes **sophisticated** agents; partial naivety (online appendix) makes enforcement harder, and full naivety is not characterized.
- **Perfect mutual monitoring** in the baseline; imperfect monitoring is only sketched.
- The empirical distinction between this relational-contract mechanism versus **peer pressure / reciprocity** is proposed but not tested — a hook for experimental work.

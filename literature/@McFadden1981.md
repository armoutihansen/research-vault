## Summary

A foundational chapter (in *Structural Analysis of Discrete Data with Econometric Applications*, eds. Manski & McFadden) that builds the modern theory of econometric discrete-choice models from first principles. McFadden frames discrete choice as a *probabilistic choice system* generated by a population of random utility maximizers, derives the necessary-and-sufficient conditions linking observed choice probabilities to random utility maximization (RUM), proves the Williams–Daly–Zachary representation tying choice probabilities to the gradient of a social-surplus function, and unifies the major parametric families — multinomial logit (Luce), multinomial probit (Thurstone), elimination-by-aspects (Tversky), and the new generalized extreme value (GEV) / nested logit class he introduces — together with their maximum-likelihood estimation and a Bay-Area travel-mode application. Because the PDF is a 75-page scanned chapter, I read it targeted: front matter and section 5.1–5.8 (theory), the model-family sections 5.9–5.16, and the estimation/application sections 5.17–5.19; appendices and proof section 5.23 were sampled only.

## Research question

When is an observed distribution of discrete choices over a population consistent with the hypothesis that each agent maximizes a (possibly random) utility function? And among the parametric specifications consistent with that hypothesis, which are flexible enough to capture realistic substitution patterns yet computationally tractable for econometric estimation and policy forecasting?

## Method / identification

The formal object is a **probabilistic choice system (PCS)** $(I,Z,\xi,\mathcal{B},S,P)$: a choice probability $P(i\mid B,s)$ for alternative $i$ from finite budget set $B\in\mathcal{B}$ given individual characteristics $s$, satisfying nonnegativity/summation and dependence only on measured attributes. The **random utility maximization (RUM)** hypothesis adds a probability measure $\mu$ over utility functions and sets

$$P(i_k\mid B,s)=\mu(\{U\mid U(i_k)\ge U(i_j)\ \forall j\},s),$$

with the "no ties" assumption guaranteeing an almost-surely unique maximizer.

Identification / consistency is attacked on three fronts. (1) **Stochastic revealed preference:** following McFadden & Richter (1970), a PCS is consistent with RUM iff it satisfies the *strong axiom of revealed stochastic preference* — for any finite sequence of trials, $\sum_m P(C^m\mid B^m,s)\le N(\cdot)$, the maximum number of successful trials consistent with a single preference order. Necessary conditions include Marschak's regularity and the triangle inequality. (2) **Williams–Daly–Zachary theorem (Theorem 5.1):** for additive-income RUM (AIRUM) forms $u(i)=(y-q_i+\varepsilon_i)/\beta(r)$, choice probabilities equal $-\partial G/\partial q_i$ where $G$ is a *social-surplus* function $G=\int[\max_i(\varepsilon_i-q_i)]f(\varepsilon)\,d\varepsilon$ satisfying convexity, linear homogeneity, additivity (SS 5.1–5.6); equivalently the PCS is a *translation-invariant probabilistic choice system* (TPCS). This is the discrete-choice analogue of Roy's identity and grounds consumer-surplus welfare analysis. (3) **GEV construction (Theorem 5.2):** any $H(y_B,z_B,s)$ that is nonnegative, linearly homogeneous, with alternating-sign mixed partials, generates a multivariate extreme-value CDF $F=\exp\{-H(e^{-\varepsilon_1},\dots)\}$, an AIRUM model with $G=\ln H(e^{-q_1},\dots)$ and $P_i=-\partial_{q_i}\ln H$.

The estimator for the nested-logit / preference-tree models is **maximum likelihood**, either full-information (FIML) on $L=\sum_h L^h$ or McFadden's **sequential estimation**: estimate bottom-level MNL, form inclusive values $y_{i\sigma}=\ln\sum\exp[\cdot]$, then estimate up the tree. Sequential is consistent and cheap but inefficient, and (per Amemiya 1976) requires corrected standard errors because regressors are estimated. RUM-consistency of the nested model requires the inclusive-value (dissimilarity) coefficients $\theta$ to lie in the unit interval, $0<\theta_{\sigma_1}\le\dots\le 1$.

## Data

The application uses survey data on **work-trip mode choice for 616 Bay-Area commuters (1975)** over four modes (auto-alone 61.4%, bus 11.0%, rail rapid transit 5.4%, carpool 22.2%), with attributes cost/wage, on-vehicle time, and access time. Otherwise the chapter is theoretical.

## Key findings

- **Strong axiom of revealed stochastic preference** is necessary and sufficient for RUM consistency; the "maximum model" of McFadden (1974) is exhibited as a PCS that violates it (so RUM imposes genuine restrictions).
- **WDZ theorem:** AIRUM ⟺ existence of a social-surplus function whose gradient gives the choice probabilities ⟺ TPCS; discrete choice imposes *no new restrictions* on consumer-surplus welfare methods.
- **MNL/Luce form** $P(i)=e^{\beta'z_i}/\sum_j e^{\beta'z_j}$ follows from i.i.d. type-I extreme-value (Weibull) utilities; it is RUM-consistent but imposes IIA / equal cross-elasticities, producing the famous **red-bus/blue-bus** failure under strong similarity.
- **GEV models** (Theorem 5.2) retain MNL's closed form while permitting flexible substitution; the **nested (tree) logit** $P(i\mid B)=P(i\mid C)\,Q(C\mid B)$ with MNL conditional choice and inclusive-value-driven nest selection is shown to be the GEV special case with $H=\sum_C a(z_C)[\sum_{i\in C} y_i^{1/(1-\sigma)}]^{1-\sigma}$.
- **EBA (Tversky)** and GEV are "roughly comparable in flexibility and complexity"; both generalize Luce.
- Empirically, value-of-time estimates are highly **sensitive to tree specification** (access time 79%–394% of wage across trees) and to estimation method (sequential vs FIML coefficients differ substantially).

## Contribution

This chapter is the canonical synthesis that established the random-utility foundations of econometric discrete choice and **introduced the GEV class and nested multinomial logit**. It connects three previously separate literatures — economic demand theory, psychological choice theory (Luce, Thurstone, Tversky), and stochastic revealed preference — under one RUM umbrella, and supplies the social-surplus/WDZ machinery that licenses welfare and forecasting use of these models. It is the theoretical backbone of McFadden's later Nobel-cited work.

## Limitations & open questions

- McFadden states he is **unaware of any general analogue, for RUM, of the integrability (sufficiency) conditions** of individual utility theory — sufficient conditions for an observed demand distribution to be consistent with a population of preference maximizers are "much less complete" than for the individual.
- The strong axiom of revealed stochastic preference yields necessary conditions but **no practical sufficient condition** for consistency.
- Extension of RUM to **history-dependent choice** (learning, habit, distinguishing intra- vs inter-individual taste variation from panel data) "awaits future research."
- The MNL log-likelihood is concave but the **nested-logit full-information likelihood is not concave** in the dissimilarity parameters and is numerically ill-behaved near $\theta=0$ (overflow/roundoff).
- EBA's behavioral motivation "provides little guidance for parametric specification of the scale function."
- Approximating MNP choice probabilities is impractical for many alternatives ($m>5$).

## Connections

Builds directly on McFadden (1974) conditional logit and McFadden & Richter (1970) on revealed stochastic preference. The psychological roots are Luce (1959) (the IIA axiom and Luce model), Thurstone (1927) (the probit/normal-utility model), and [[@Tversky1972|Tversky (1972)]] on elimination-by-aspects and simple scalability; Marschak (1960) and Block & Marschak (1960) established necessity of regularity/triangle conditions. The GEV/nested-logit derivation credits Williams (1977), Daly & Zachary (1976), Cardell (1977), and Ben-Akiva & Lerman (1977). The social-surplus and aggregation results connect to Gorman (1953) on community preferences and to Rosen & Small (1979) on discrete-choice consumer surplus; estimation draws on Amemiya (1976) and Cosslett (1978). The bounded-rationality reinterpretation of taste variation nods to Simon (1978). The volume itself is the Manski & McFadden (1981) collection; later structural and behavioral choice work (e.g. [[@Manzini2007|Manzini & Mariotti 2007]] on bounded rationality, and the GEV literature) descends from this framework.

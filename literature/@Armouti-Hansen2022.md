> [!abstract] Abstract
> The research presented in this dissertation contributes to the literature on choice and decision theory. Chapter 2 presents generalizations of two well-known boundedly rational choice procedures. Our generalization consists of defining these procedures as choice correspondences, instead of choice functions. In turn, this imposes less of a restriction on the contained rationales and allows for the decision maker to be indecisive. We provide the axiomatic characterizations of our generalizations by extending the axioms used to characterize the original procedures. Furthermore, we discuss ways in which an indecisive decision maker may arrive at a unique choice and show that our proposed generalizations can explain choice anomalies that cannot be accommodated in the original setup. Chapter 3 introduces a two-period model of individual decision-making, in which the decision maker derives utility from both future consumption and present anticipation of such consumption. Specifically, we consider a setting in which the decision maker may choose her anticipation and where this choice of anticipation, in turn, determines her reference point. In this setting, we formulate equilibrium concepts that dictate feasible choices of anticipation and consumption lotteries based on when the decision maker commits to her decision. In addition, we show that our model on the domain of choice is equivalent to a two-stage choice procedure based on the concept of consideration sets. We provide the axiomatic characterization of the choice procedure, and hence by extension, a characterization of our model of anticipation-based reference-dependent preferences. Finally, we show the extent to which the decision maker's preferences and consideration set can be identified from choice data. Chapter 4 assesses the predictive capability of simple linear social preference models by using flexible machine learning models as a benchmark. Specifically, based on experimental observations from the lab on binary dictator games and reciprocity games, we apply the recent introduced concept of a model's completeness by comparing its predictive performance to that of (i) a naive baseline model that is stripped of other-regarding preferences, and (ii) a non-parametric machine learning model capable of capturing the predictive variation in the data. In turn, this provides us with information on (i) how large a fraction of the predictable variation in the data a given social preference model captures, and (ii) how large a gain in performance the model brings by introducing other-regarding motives compared to a naive baseline model. To address the potential remaining patterns in the data that are not captured on the level of the representative agent, we also conduct the analysis in a mixture model framework allowing for heterogeneity in the estimated parameters.

## Summary
A three-essay doctoral dissertation (University of Cologne, 2021) on the behavioral foundations of choice. The unifying theme is the investigation of the "intrinsic soundness" of behavioral choice models on the domain of observable choice, pursued via two complementary tools: axiomatic characterization (Chapters 2 and 3) and predictive evaluation (Chapter 4). Chapter 2 generalizes two boundedly-rational sequential-elimination procedures to choice correspondences to accommodate indecisiveness; Chapter 3 builds an anticipation-based reference-dependent model under risk and shows it is observationally equivalent to a consideration-set choice procedure; Chapter 4 measures the *completeness* of linear social-preference models against a machine-learning benchmark.

## Research question
When standard preference maximization fails, how much can be inferred about non-standard models of choice from choice data alone? Concretely: (i) Can sequential-rationalization procedures be extended to allow an *indecisive* decision maker (multi-valued choice) while remaining axiomatically characterized? (ii) Can a model in which a DM endogenously chooses her anticipation, which then sets her reference point, be founded on, and identified from, observable choice? (iii) How *complete* are simple linear social-preference models — i.e. what fraction of the predictable variation in social-choice data do they capture relative to a flexible ML benchmark?

## Method / identification
**Chapter 2 (axiomatic).** Generalizes the rational shortlist method (RSM) of [[@Manzini2007|Manzini & Mariotti (2007)]] and the categorize-then-choose (CTC) procedure of [[@Manzini2012a|Manzini & Mariotti (2012)]] from choice functions to choice *correspondences*. The DM sequentially eliminates inferior alternatives via stage-specific asymmetric rationales; rather than requiring a unique survivor, only a nonempty surviving set is required, so the agent may be indecisive (re-asking the same problem can yield different singletons). Characterization extends the original axioms: a version of the weak WARP (WWARP) that excludes choice *re-reversals* (a reversal between two alternatives cannot itself be reversed in a subset), plus an expansion axiom adapted to correspondences. This answers Manzini & Mariotti's noted gap that conditions for general choice correspondences were lacking.

**Chapter 3 (axiomatic + identification).** A two-period decision problem under risk. Let $Y$ be a finite outcome set, $\Delta := \Delta(Y)$ the simple lotteries, and $X \subset \Delta$ the feasible physical lotteries; $r \in \Delta$ denotes an *anticipatory* lottery and $p,q,w \in X$ physical-outcome lotteries with support $\operatorname{supp}(p)$. In period 1 the DM chooses both a consumption lottery (realized in period 2) and an anticipation lottery (consumed in period 1); anticipation sets the reference point à la Köszegi & [[@Koszegi2006b|Rabin (2006)]]. Equilibrium concepts depend on the *commitment timing* and share an individual-rationality constraint barring anticipation of outcomes outside the chosen lottery's support. The choice-domain counterpart is a two-stage consideration-set procedure: a filter (satisfying internal-consistency conditions) selects a consideration set, then a preference relation picks the most-preferred considered alternative. The paper proves observational equivalence, gives the axiomatic characterization, and derives identification results stating to what extent preferences and the consideration set are recovered from choice.

**Chapter 4 (empirical / ML).** Applies the *completeness* measure of [[@Fudenberg2021|Fudenberg, Kleinberg, Liang & Mullainathan (2021)]]: completeness $= (e_{\text{naive}} - e_{\text{model}}) / (e_{\text{naive}} - e_{\text{ML}})$, the share of predictable variation captured, where the naive baseline is a purely self-interested DM and the ceiling is a flexible (non-parametric) ML model. Linear social-preference models are nested by sequentially adding other-regarding motives (inequity aversion / differentiated altruism, negative and positive reciprocity). A mixture-model extension allows latent types, with two new measures proposed: *within-type completeness* (completeness within each estimated type) and *unrestricted completeness* (benchmarked against an ML model that uses the subject identifier as a feature).

## Data
Chapters 2 and 3 are purely theoretical (None — axiomatic decision theory). Chapter 4 uses laboratory panel data on binary dictator games and reciprocity games from [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin, Fehr & Schmidt (2019)]].

## Key findings
- **Ch. 2:** The generalized RSM and CTC are characterized by the correspondence-adapted WWARP (no choice re-reversals) and expansion axioms; the indecisive formulations rationalize choice anomalies that arise precisely when more than one alternative survives elimination and that the original single-valued procedures cannot explain.
- **Ch. 3:** The anticipation-based reference-dependent model is observationally equivalent to a two-stage consideration-set procedure, is falsifiable on choice data, and its parameters (preferences and consideration set) are identifiable; worked examples show it can accommodate possibility-vs-probability effects ([[@Harless1994|Harless & Camerer 1994]]), asymmetric matching-pennies play ([[@Goeree2001|Goeree & Holt 2001]]), information aversion (Huntington's-disease testing), and defensive pessimism.
- **Ch. 4:** The full linear social-preference model attains aggregate completeness $\approx 82\%$, so richer functional forms offer limited gains on this domain. A three-type mixture is supported: one large type is well predicted (within-type completeness $88\text{–}93\%$), a second large type needs richer models ($60\text{–}65\%$), and a small minority type is not reliably estimated. Unrestricted completeness for the three-type linear models is $\approx 85\text{–}88\%$, interpreted as an upper bound.

## Contribution
Methodologically bridges two traditions for "stress-testing" behavioral models: revealed-preference axiomatics (proving falsifiability and identification) and out-of-sample predictive evaluation (completeness). Substantively: (i) fills Manzini & Mariotti's open gap on characterizing sequential procedures for correspondences and formalizes *indecisiveness*; (ii) provides the first behavioral foundation and identification for an endogenous-anticipation reference-dependent model; (iii) supplies the first completeness assessment of social-preference models and introduces within-type and unrestricted completeness for mixture settings.

## Limitations & open questions
- Ch. 4 explicitly stresses that completeness estimates are *upper bounds*: more complex ML methods could raise the benchmark and lower measured completeness.
- The second large latent type is only $60\text{–}65\%$ complete under linear models — what additional other-regarding (or non-linear) motives capture this type's behavior is left open.
- The minority type cannot be estimated due to small size — larger samples are needed.
- Findings are domain-specific to binary dictator and reciprocity games; generality to richer strategic or non-binary social environments is untested.
- Chapters 2–3 leave open how an indecisive DM ultimately *settles* on a unique action, and the empirical testability of the anticipation model's commitment-timing equilibria in the field (where expectations are hard to observe).

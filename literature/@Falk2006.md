> [!abstract] Abstract
> People are reciprocal if they reward kind actions and punish unkind ones. In this paper we present a formal theory of reciprocity. It takes into account that people evaluate the kindness of an action not only by its consequences but also by its underlying intention. The theory is in line with the relevant stylized facts of a wide range of experimental games, such as the ultimatum game, the gift-exchange game, a reduced best-shot game, the dictator game, the prisoner's dilemma, and public goods games. Furthermore, it predicts that identical consequences trigger different reciprocal responses in different environments. Finally, the theory explains why outcomes tend to be fair in bilateral interactions whereas extremely unfair distributions may arise in competitive markets.

## Summary
Falk and Fischbacher develop a formal preference-based theory of reciprocity in which players reward perceived kindness and punish perceived unkindness. The key innovation is that perceived kindness combines two ingredients: a distributional (outcome) component measured against an equity reference standard, and an intention component that depends on the alternatives the acting player could have chosen but did not. Embedded into the framework of psychological games, the model delivers a "reciprocity equilibrium" (a subgame-perfect psychological Nash equilibrium) whose predictions match stylized facts across the ultimatum, gift-exchange, best-shot, dictator, sequential prisoner's dilemma, public-goods, and competitive-market games — including the puzzle that bilateral interactions look fair while competitive markets converge to extremely unequal but "standard" outcomes.

## Research question
Can a single, formally tractable model of social preferences explain both positive reciprocity (rewarding kindness) and negative reciprocity (punishing unkindness) across many games, while capturing the experimental fact that identical material consequences trigger different responses depending on the actor's intentions and choice set — and reconcile fair bilateral outcomes with unfair competitive-market outcomes?

## Method / identification
The paper is theoretical, with supporting questionnaire evidence. A standard finite two-player extensive-form game with perfect information is transformed into a "reciprocity game," a psychological game in the sense of Geanakoplos, Pearce and Stacchetti (1989), in which utility depends on beliefs as well as strategies.

Two terms are central. The kindness term $\varphi_j(n,s_i'',s_i')=\vartheta_j(n,s_i'',s_i')\,\Delta_j(n,s_i'',s_i')$ measures how kind player $i$ perceives $j$'s action at node $n$. The outcome term is the equity comparison
$$\Delta_j(n,s_i'',s_i') := \pi_i(n,s_i'',s_i') - \pi_j(n,s_i'',s_i'),$$
so $\Delta_j>0$ when $i$'s (believed) payoff exceeds $j$'s (advantageous, hence kind) and $\Delta_j<0$ otherwise. The intention factor $\vartheta_j\in[\varepsilon_i,1]$ encodes whether $j$ could have done otherwise: it equals $1$ when a "true alternative" existed (for an advantageous outcome, an alternative giving $i$ strictly less; for a disadvantageous one, an alternative giving $i$ strictly more), and equals the pure-outcome-concern parameter $\varepsilon_i\in[0,1]$ when $j$ had no real alternative. A player with $\varepsilon_i=0$ is purely intention-driven; $\varepsilon_i=1$ is purely outcome-oriented (nesting inequity aversion).

The reciprocation term $\sigma_i(n,f,s_i'',s_i') := \pi_j(\nu(n,f),s_i'',s_i') - \pi_j(n,s_i'',s_i')$ measures how much $i$'s move alters $j$'s payoff (reward if positive, punishment if negative). Utility is
$$U_i(f,s_i'',s_i') = \pi_i(f) + \rho_i \sum_{\substack{n\to f\\ n\in N_i}} \varphi_j(n,s_i'',s_i')\,\sigma_i(n,f,s_i'',s_i'),$$
where $\rho_i\ge 0$ is the reciprocity-strength parameter; $\rho_i=\rho_j=0$ collapses the model to the standard game. First- and second-order beliefs ($s_i'$, $s_i''$) must be consistent in equilibrium. A key modeling choice is that only initial beliefs enter utility (utility components are summed node by node), which keeps the Geanakoplos et al. equilibrium concept usable for sequential games — contrasting with [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg and Kirchsteiger (2004)]], who use updated beliefs.

## Data
Primarily a theory paper. The only original data are a questionnaire study with 111 subjects at the University of Zurich / ETH Zurich, eliciting on a $-100$ to $+100$ scale how kind subjects perceive various $(\pi_j,\pi_i)$ divisions of 10 Swiss Francs across nine choice sets with systematically varied alternatives (Table 1). These elicitations motivate the equity reference standard and six observations on how the choice set shapes intention-based kindness. All game predictions are otherwise benchmarked against published experimental stylized facts rather than newly collected data.

## Key findings
The questionnaire yields six observations: (1) with a singleton choice set, intentions cannot be inferred and perceived (un)kindness is much weaker; (2) yet it is non-zero, so outcomes matter too (ruling out both pure inequity aversion and pure intention models); (3) a friendly offer is fully kind only if a less friendly alternative existed; (4) an unfriendly offer is judged less harshly if no friendlier alternative existed; (5) the cost to $j$ of being friendlier matters; (6) kindness and unkindness are asymmetric.

Model results (Propositions 1 and others, with proofs in appendices): the ultimatum game has a unique reciprocity equilibrium with acceptance probability $p^*=\min\!\big(1,\,c/[\rho_2(1-2c)(1-c)]\big)$ for $c<\tfrac12$ and offer $c^* = \max\!\Big(\tfrac{1+3\rho_2-\sqrt{1+6\rho_2+\rho_2^2}}{4\rho_2},\ \tfrac12(1-\tfrac1{\rho_1})\Big)$. The non-intentional (random-offer) version replaces $\rho_2$ with $\varepsilon_2\rho_2$, predicting higher acceptance of low offers — matching Blount (1995). In the gift-exchange game effort rises in the wage and in $\rho_2$, generating the wage–effort relation; the random-wage version weakens it (matching Charness, 2004). The reduced best-shot vs. reduced ultimatum comparison predicts a lower acceptance probability of the $(8,2)$ offer in the ultimatum game (alternative $(5,5)$ signals unkind intent) than in the best-shot game (only $(2,8)$ as alternative), confirmed by rejection rates of 44% vs. 27% (Falk et al., 2003). In competitive markets (proposer competition à la Roth et al., 1991), reciprocal proposers nonetheless bid $c_{\max}=1$, because unilateral refusal cannot achieve fairness — reconciling fair bilateral with unfair competitive outcomes. The dictator offers $c^*=\max\!\big(0,\tfrac12(1-\tfrac1{\varepsilon_1\rho_1})\big)\le\tfrac12$, shifted below ultimatum offers. The sequential prisoner's dilemma and public-goods game predict conditional cooperation but essentially no unconditional cooperation, matching [[@Fischbacher2001|Fischbacher et al. (2001)]].

## Contribution
The model unifies positive and negative reciprocity in one preference framework that jointly incorporates distributional fairness and intentions, where intentions are operationalized through the opponent's feasible alternatives. Crucially it predicts that identical consequences elicit different reciprocal responses across environments, and it accounts for the divergence between fair bilateral outcomes and unfair competitive-market outcomes — phenomena that pure inequity-aversion models (Bolton and Ockenfels, 2000; Fehr and Schmidt, 1999) cannot capture. It extends Rabin's (1993) intention-based reciprocity to multi-stage sequential games by using initial beliefs in a psychological-game equilibrium.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors note that, in its stated form, a reciprocity equilibrium does not always exist because the intention factor $\vartheta$ can be discontinuous; a minor technical modification restores existence (existence proof delegated to an appendix). The main text uses a simplified intention factor based on only four of the six observations, with the full six-observation version relegated to an appendix. The framework assumes payoffs are level-comparable and kindness rests on interpersonal comparisons — a substantive assumption distinguishing it from [[@Rabin1993|Rabin (1993)]] and [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg and Kirchsteiger (2004)]], and an empirically contestable one. Parameters $\rho_i$ and $\varepsilon_i$ are assumed common knowledge despite documented heterogeneity. Whether kindness-driven reciprocity or inequity-reduction better predicts behavior is framed as an open empirical question (Falk et al., 2001).

## Connections
The paper sits squarely in the social-preferences literature. It positions itself against the inequity-aversion models of [[@Fehr1999|Fehr and Schmidt (1999)]] and [[@Bolton2000|Bolton and Ockenfels (2000)]], which are consequentialist and nested as the $\varepsilon_i=1$ special case, and against the intention-based reciprocity models of [[@Rabin1993|Rabin (1993)]], [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg and Kirchsteiger (2004)]], [[@Levine1998|Levine (1998)]], and [[@Charness2002|Charness and Rabin (2002)]], from which it differs by retaining outcome concerns and interpersonal comparisons. It builds methodologically on Geanakoplos, Pearce and Stacchetti (1989) on psychological games. Empirical anchors include Güth, Schmittberger and Schwarze (1982) and Roth (1995) on ultimatum bargaining, [[@Fehr1993|Fehr, Kirchsteiger and Riedl (1993)]] and Fehr and Falk (1999) on gift exchange, Berg, Dickhaut and McCabe (1995) on the investment game, Fehr and Gächter (2000) on cooperation and punishment, Blount (1995) and Charness (2004) on the role of intentions, Falk, Fehr and Fischbacher (2000, 2003) on intention treatments and the reduced best-shot game, Roth et al. (1991) on market competition, and Fischbacher, Gächter and [[@Fischbacher2001|Fehr (2001)]] on conditional cooperation. It is distinct from "reciprocal altruism" in the sense of Trivers (1971) and repeated-game cooperation (Kreps et al., 1982).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Conventional wisdom suggests that more intense competition will lower firms’ profits. The authors show that this may not hold in a channel setting with exclusive retailers. They find that a manufacturer and its retailer can both become worse off if their competing manufacturer and retailer with quality-differentiated products exit the market. Put differently, in a channel setting, more intense competition can be all-win for the manufacturer, the retailer, and the consumers. Interestingly, a high-quality manufacturer can benefit from an increase in its competitor’s perceived quality (e.g., due to favorable product reviews from consumers or third-party rating agencies). In other words, a manufacturer may prefer a strong rather than a weak enemy, and the manufacturer can have an incentive to help its competitor improve product quality or remain in the market. Furthermore, the authors show that a multiproduct monopolist manufacturer with an exclusive retailer may make higher profits by spinning off a product into a competing manufacturer that has its own retail channel, even without accounting for any proceeds from the spinoff.

## Summary
In a vertically quality-differentiated market where each manufacturer sells through an *exclusive* independent retailer, a competitor's exit can hurt — not help — the surviving manufacturer and its retailer, and a *stronger* rival can be more profitable than a weak one. The driving force is double marginalization: a rival's presence keeps retail competition alive, raises pass-through, and disciplines the surviving retailer's markup, so removing the rival can worsen the channel inefficiency by more than it relieves price competition. This overturns the standard direct-selling intuition (and the Mussa–Rosen vertical-differentiation result) and yields the counterintuitive prescriptions that a firm may want to keep, strengthen, or even create an "enemy."

## Research question
When does a manufacturer (and its exclusive retailer) benefit from *less* competition? Specifically: (i) Does a competitor's market exit raise or lower the surviving manufacturer's and retailer's profits? (ii) Is competing against a stronger (higher perceived quality) rival necessarily worse? (iii) Can a multiproduct monopolist profit by spinning off a product to deliberately create a competitor, ignoring any sale proceeds?

## Method / identification
Analytical game-theoretic industrial-organization model. Two manufacturers $i\in\{1,2\}$ produce vertically quality-differentiated products with qualities $q_1>q_2$ and constant marginal costs $c_i<q_i$. Each manufacturer $i$ sells exclusively through one independent retailer $i$. Consumers have unit demand and heterogeneous willingness to pay for quality $\theta\sim\text{Uniform}[0,1]$; utility from product $i$ is $u_i=\theta q_i-p_i$, with an outside option of zero. Market size is normalized to one.

The game has three stages solved by backward induction: (1) manufacturers simultaneously set wholesale prices $w_1,w_2$ (take-it-or-leave-it linear wholesale-price contracts, no two-part tariffs); (2) retailers set retail prices $p_i\ge w_i$; (3) consumers buy the highest-nonnegative-utility product. The vertical-differentiation demand system gives, in the interior (both firms active) region,
$$D_1=1-\frac{p_1-p_2}{q_1-q_2},\qquad D_2=\frac{p_1-p_2}{q_1-q_2}-\frac{p_2}{q_2}.$$
The authors derive closed-form subgame-perfect equilibrium retail prices, wholesale prices, and profits, and characterize the parameter region $c_2\in(\underline{c},\bar c)$ in which both firms hold positive market share. The "exit" counterfactual replaces $D_1$ with the monopoly demand $\tilde D_1=1-p_1/q_1$, yielding $w_1^{**}=(q_1+c_1)/2$, $p_1^{**}=(3q_1+c_1)/4$, $\pi_{M1}^{**}=(q_1-c_1)^2/8q_1$, $\pi_{R1}^{**}=(q_1-c_1)^2/16q_1$. Results are stated as Propositions 1–6 plus Lemma 1; comparative statics in $q_2$ and $c_2$ drive every claim. Robustness is checked in a Web Appendix (integrated rival; $q_2>q_1$; retailer 2 rejoining manufacturer 1; differentiated retailers; horizontal+vertical heterogeneity; endogenous quality; >2 manufacturers; selling/service costs).

## Data
None — this is a purely theoretical paper. Anecdotal motivation only (1980s U.S. auto market; Avon, HTC, Marc Jacobs exclusive-distribution examples; an International Franchise Association survey reporting 73% of franchisors grant exclusive territories).

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1:** The rival's exit raises product 1's wholesale and retail prices, but there is a threshold $\hat c\in[\underline{c},\bar c)$ such that exit *lowers* manufacturer 1's unit sales iff $c_2>\hat c$. Mechanism: exit lowers the demand intercept (from $1+p_2^*/(q_1-q_2)$ to $1$) but also flattens demand ($dD_1/dp_1=-1/(q_1-q_2)$ becomes $-1/q_1$), since the marginal consumer's alternative drops from quality $q_2$ to the zero-quality outside option; flatter demand reduces the retailer's incentive to cut price.
- **Proposition 2:** There exist $\hat q<q_1$ and $\hat c$ such that the rival's exit makes manufacturer 1 *worse off* iff $q_2<\hat q$ and $c_2>\hat c$ — i.e., when the rival is weak, the worsened double marginalization dominates the gains from reduced competition.
- **Lemma 1:** This still holds even if manufacturer 1 could produce *both* products after exit; sole retailer 1 simply marks up both, so channel inefficiency persists.
- **Proposition 3:** Symmetrically, the rival's exit makes *retailer 1* worse off under the same weak-rival conditions, because competition served as a "commitment device" securing a low wholesale price; exit drops the retail pass-through rate from $2q_1/(4q_1-q_2)$ to $1/2$, prompting manufacturer 1 to raise $w_1$. Hence an "all-lose" outcome (firms and consumers) is possible (Figure 2, region 2).
- **Proposition 4:** There exists $\hat c$ such that if $c_2\in(\hat c,\bar c)$, manufacturer 1's profit *rises* when the rival's (perceived) quality $q_2$ increases — contradicting the Mussa–Rosen prediction. A higher $q_2$ steepens product 1's demand, intensifying retail competition and easing double marginalization enough to offset the thinner margin. A firm may thus prefer a strong rival and benefit from a competitor's favorable reviews.
- **Proposition 5:** A multiproduct monopolist with an exclusive retailer can earn more by *spinning off* its low-quality product to a new independent manufacturer-plus-retailer (creating a competitor), iff $q_2<\hat q$ and $c_2>\hat c$, even ignoring spinoff proceeds — the induced retail competition relieves double marginalization on the high-quality product.
- **Proposition 6 (robustness):** Results survive when rival 2 is vertically integrated, and an increase in $q_2$ still helps manufacturer 1 for $c_2$ in a sub-interval.

## Contribution
Contributes to the channel-competition and market-entry/exit literatures by explicitly modeling *quality differentiation* (asymmetric firms) in a setting of exclusive retailers, where prior work used reduced-form demand or horizontally differentiated symmetric firms. The novel insight is that a competing manufacturer's product can alleviate double marginalization (not just inter-retailer competition over one manufacturer's good, as in Padmanabhan & Png 1997), so a rival's exit can make the surviving manufacturer *and* its retailer worse off, a strong rival can beat a weak one, and entry can be all-win — reversing standard direct-selling and vertical-differentiation intuitions. It supplies a strategic rationale for helping competitors survive/improve, for patent sharing, and for profitable spinoffs.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors explicitly flag three caveats. (1) **Retailer bargaining power:** the model gives retailers none; with strong bargaining power the retailer secures a low wholesale price regardless of the rival, making exit *more* likely to hurt the manufacturer — qualitative results hold only if bargaining power is not too strong. (2) **Contract form:** under two-part tariffs the channel is fully efficient and the manufacturer *always* benefits from exit, so results hinge on the (empirically common) linear wholesale-price contract. (3) **No repeated interaction:** a long-term repeated game could discipline opportunistic retailer pricing via future punishment, changing the role of competition. The empirical magnitude of the channel-coordination effect (e.g., how much of the 1980s U.S. auto-profit rise it explains) is left for future systematic empirical work; quality is largely exogenous in the core model (only numerically endogenized in the appendix).

## Connections
The demand and quality-differentiation structure builds directly on Mussa & Rosen (1978), whose prediction that a high-quality firm is always hurt by a rival's quality gain is overturned here. The double-marginalization channel logic descends from [[@Jeuland1983|Jeuland & Shugan (1983)]] and the classic channel-structure work of [[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin (1983)]], [[@Coughlan1985|Coughlan (1985)]], and [[@Moorthy1988|Moorthy (1988)]]. The closest contrast is Padmanabhan & Png (1997), where inter-retailer competition for one manufacturer's product eases double marginalization; dyadic channel competition follows Subramanian, Raju & Zhang (2013), and low pass-through of price promotions follows Kumar, Rajiv & Jeuland (2001). On entry/exit benefiting incumbents, it relates to Tyagi (1999), Arya, Mittendorf & Sappington (2007), Chiang, Chhajed & Hess (2003), Thomadsen (2012), Thomadsen, Pazgal & Soberman (2016), and the entry-deterrence-via-weak-entrant logic of Ashiya (2000), which it inverts. Optimal entry/exit timing references Fudenberg & Tirole (1986); related channel work by the authors includes Diao, Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019), Jiang & Tian (2018), and Tian & Jiang (2018).

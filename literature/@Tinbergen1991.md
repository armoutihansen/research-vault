> [!abstract] Abstract
> The author believes in the measurability of welfare (also called satisfaction or utility). Measurements have been made in the United States (Jorgenson and collaborators), France (Allais), and the Netherlands (Van Praag and collaborators). The Israeli sociologists Levy and Guttman have shown that numerous noneconomic variables are among the determinants of welfare. The determinants are numerous; the author proposes a list of about fifty. Various mathematical functions have been proposed, of which the logarithm of the determinants shows the highest correlation with welfare, as measured.

## Summary

A short programmatic essay in which Tinbergen, a committed "cardinalist," argues that welfare (treated as synonymous with utility and satisfaction) is empirically measurable, surveys three national empirical traditions that have measured it, and contributes two extensions: a list of more than fifty welfare components (extending welfare beyond consumption to learning, productive activity, leisure, and national security) and a worked-out conceptualization of (inter)national security as a measurable welfare component defined via a citizen's subjective probability of national sovereignty. It is a position-and-agenda piece, not an empirical study.

## Research question

Is welfare (utility, satisfaction) measurable, and if so, what are its determinants and what functional form best relates determinants to measured welfare? A secondary question is how to broaden the list of welfare "components" beyond consumption goods, with particular attention to national security as a newly salient determinant.

## Method / identification

The paper is methodological and discursive rather than estimating a new model. It frames a common measurement procedure shared across cardinalist programs: utility is assumed to be the *same* function of a set of "determinants" or "components" across all individuals or households, with the function shaped by coefficients common to all individuals plus parameters that characterize the individual or household. Three functional approaches are contrasted:

- **Jorgenson and collaborators (US):** a translog utility function where log utility is a quadratic function of the logs of three or five consumption goods, with five household-characterizing parameters (family size, age of head, region, race, type of residence). Cardinalist identification is bought by imposing restrictions ordinalists avoid — *exact aggregation* of individual to total demand and *integrability* of demand functions.
- **Van Praag and collaborators (Dutch):** a single determinant, income, fitted against many functional forms, with a preference for the cumulated lognormal welfare function.
- **Allais (French):** a single "psychological assets" component, linear in its logs over the main interval, with satiety (an asymptote) at the upper extreme.

Tinbergen's own preferred specification is linear in the logs of the determinants $x_i$ plus unity:
$$w = \sum_i \alpha_i \ln(x_i + 1)$$
whose advantage is that it exhibits falling (diminishing) marginal utilities:
$$\frac{\partial w}{\partial x_i} = \frac{\alpha_i}{x_i + 1}$$
This form has a lower bound (for $x_i \geq 0$) but no upper bound, which Tinbergen regards as defensible. The general identifying restriction is that the verbal qualifications used in measurement (excellent, good, satisfactory, bad) carry the same meaning across the compared persons — acceptable for local policy, less so across very different populations.

For the national-security component, he proposes an operational definition: security is "a person's estimate of the probability of her or his country's sovereignty in the coming decade," to be elicited by interview surveys of citizens, with refinements (multiple successive decades or five-year periods, more precise notions of sovereignty such as control over pollution). He carefully distinguishes *determinants/components* of security from *instruments* of security policy (arms stocks, grain or high-tech supplies), the latter belonging to an optimal-policy model outside this paper's scope.

## Data

None — no new empirical estimation is performed. The paper draws on prior measurement results, most notably Levy and Guttman (1975), who across two data sets (more than 1800 interviews each) found that some twenty components explain about two-thirds of the variance in "happiness" ($E^2 \approx 0.67$), of which only about $0.13$ is attributable to economic components. The paper also references the empirical correlations reported by the Jorgenson, Van Praag, and Allais programs.

## Key findings

- Welfare is treated as measurable; Tinbergen positions measurement as a "respectable activity" analogous to the historical replacement of qualitative descriptors by quantitative ones in physics (heat → temperature, color → wavelength, pitch → wavelength).
- The logarithmic specification gives the best fit among the functions tested and yields diminishing marginal utility.
- Most explained variance in happiness is *noneconomic*: economic components account for only about $0.13$ of an $E^2 \approx 0.67$, explaining the otherwise unimpressive multiple correlations in purely consumption-based models.
- A taxonomy of more than fifty welfare components is proposed (Table 1), organized into individual consumption needs (material and spiritual), social relations, formal learning, productive activity, leisure, and national security — subject to two consistency checks: components must sum to total welfare, and must not overlap.
- National security is reconceived as a measurable welfare component via subjective probability of sovereignty, distinct from policy instruments.

## Contribution

The paper consolidates and bridges three previously isolated national cardinalist measurement traditions (American, Dutch, French) and adds noneconomist evidence (Levy and Guttman) into one agenda. Its two original contributions are (1) substantially broadening the domain of welfare determinants beyond consumption — explicitly incorporating learning, productive activity, and especially national security as a determinant made salient by nuclear "annihilators" — and (2) offering an operational, survey-based definition of (inter)national security suitable for measurement. It is an early, programmatic statement of what later became the subjective-well-being / experienced-utility measurement agenda.

## Limitations & open questions

The author flags several explicit open problems and caveats:

- The interpersonal-comparability restriction (that verbal qualifications mean the same thing across persons) is "more acceptable for local than for world-wide policies" — a "good income" means different things to an American versus a Pakistani metalworker; reconciling cross-population comparisons is left open.
- The proposed list of fifty-plus components is "submitted for discussion"; some categories may be missing or wrongly excluded (e.g., determinants not affectable by socio-economic policy are set aside but acknowledged to matter).
- Only a small number of components have so far been measured; how to actually measure most of them remains to be done.
- The security definition is offered as "the simplest version," with refinements (multiple time horizons, probabilistic determinants, richer sovereignty concepts such as pollution sovereignty, European integration, Warsaw Pact treaties) anticipated but undeveloped.
- The construction of an explicit policy model linking security *instruments* to outcomes is deliberately excluded and left for future work.

## Connections

The paper sits at the intersection of welfare measurement and what is now subjective-well-being economics. Its central empirical pillar is **Levy and Guttman (1975)** on the multivariate structure of well-being. The three measurement programs are anchored by **Christensen, Jorgenson, and Lau (1975)** and **Jorgenson, Lau, and Stoker (1980)** on translog utility and exact aggregation; **Van Praag (1971)** and **Van Praag and Kapteyn (1973)** on the individual welfare function of income (the Leyden school); and **Allais (1984)** on cardinal utility from an intrinsic invariant. It extends Tinbergen's own earlier notes on measurability of utility and on welfare of "productive consumers." The Pareto ordinalist position is the explicit foil. For a modern reader interested in social preferences and choice modeling, the paper foreshadows the experienced-utility / happiness-economics literature and the methodological debate over cardinal versus ordinal welfare and interpersonal comparability.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> It has been argued that transformational leaders increase group effectiveness by empowering followers to perform their job independently from the leader, highlight the importance of cooperation in performing collective tasks, and realign followers' values to create a more cohesive group. A study was conducted to examine whether transformational leadership would be positively related to followers' perceptions of empowerment, group cohesiveness, and effectiveness. Forty-seven groups from four Korean firms participated in this study. Results of partial least squares analysis indicated that transformational leadership was positively related to empowerment, group cohesiveness, and group effectiveness. Empowerment was positively related to collective-efficacy, which in turn was positively related to group members' perceived group effectiveness. Implications for research and practice are discussed.

## Summary
Jung and Sosik test a group-level nomological model in which transformational leadership feeds into followers' perceived empowerment, group cohesiveness, and perceived group effectiveness, with empowerment and cohesiveness in turn feeding collective-efficacy, which itself raises perceived effectiveness. Using survey data from 47 intact work teams in four large Korean firms and partial least squares (PLS) structural equation modeling, they confirm most of the hypothesized positive paths. The one notable null is the cohesiveness-to-collective-efficacy link, which is in the predicted direction but not significant. The study's contribution is to move transformational-leadership research from the individual level of analysis to the group level and to position collective-efficacy as both an outcome and a determinant of group effectiveness.

## Research question
Does transformational leadership, operating at the group level, positively shape group members' perceptions of empowerment, group cohesiveness, and group effectiveness; and do empowerment and cohesiveness raise collective-efficacy, which then raises perceived group effectiveness?

## Method / identification
The design is a cross-sectional, correlational field survey aggregated to the group level. The theoretical model (Figure 1) specifies five latent constructs and the paths among them, formalized as three hypotheses: H1 (transformational leadership $\to$ empowerment, cohesiveness, effectiveness), H2a/H2b (empowerment $\to$ collective-efficacy; cohesiveness $\to$ collective-efficacy), and H3 (collective-efficacy $\to$ effectiveness).

Measurement: transformational leadership via a 28-item Multifactor Leadership Questionnaire (MLQ; Bass & Avolio, 1990) capturing idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, and individualized consideration; empowerment via Spreitzer's (1995) 12-item scale (meaningfulness, competence, self-determination, impact); cohesiveness via Bernthal & Insko's (1993) 4-item task/social cohesion scale; collective-efficacy via an 11-item Bandura (1986) adaptation; and perceived performance via 5 self-assessment items. Items were translated and back-translated for the Korean sample (Brislin, 1986).

Identification strategy for common-method bias: each group was randomly split into two subgroups (A and B), and the constructs entering any given structural path were measured from different subgroups so that no path rested on a single rater pool. Within-group agreement was checked with $r_{wg}$ (James, Demaree & Wolf, 1984); all averaged above $.7$ (TFL $.89$, empowerment $.92$, cohesion $.90$, efficacy $.90$, effectiveness $.94$), justifying aggregation to the group level. Exploratory factor analysis (principal components, Varimax) reduced each scale. The structural model was estimated with PLS (Wold, 1985), chosen for its tolerance of small samples and lack of distributional/metric assumptions; path-coefficient significance was assessed via a blindfolding/jackknife procedure with omission distance 10. The whole analysis was re-run with the A/B subgroup assignments switched as a robustness replication.

## Data
Survey data from 217 employees forming 47 intact teams in four large Korean firms in Seoul (400 individuals across 65 teams contacted; ~54% response). Mean age 33; ~93% male; >80% held a bachelor's degree. Functional areas spanned marketing/customer service (35.1%), accounting (18%), purchasing (16.6%), strategy/planning (12.9%), R&D (10.5%), and HR (6.9%). Study variables did not differ significantly across the four firms. Analysis was conducted at the group level ($N=47$, with one group dropped to $N=46$ in the full-sample PLS run).

## Key findings
H1 supported: transformational leadership was positively related to empowerment ($\beta=.15$), group cohesiveness ($\beta=.21$), and perceived group effectiveness ($\beta=.18$). H2a supported: empowerment $\to$ collective-efficacy ($\beta=.17$). H2b not supported: cohesiveness $\to$ collective-efficacy was positive in direction but not significant. H3 supported: collective-efficacy $\to$ perceived group effectiveness ($\beta=.20$). The switched-subgroup replication reproduced the same pattern (empowerment $\beta=.14$, cohesion-to-efficacy again null, efficacy-to-effectiveness $\beta=.28$), indicating the results were not an artifact of the split. Notably, the reported $R^2$ values are very low (around $.03$–$.05$), so the paths, while statistically significant under PLS, explain little variance. ## Contribution
The paper provides one of the early empirical, group-level tests of a transformational-leadership nomological network, whereas most prior work was individual-level or purely theoretical. It treats collective-efficacy simultaneously as a criterion (predicted by empowerment) and as a predictor of group effectiveness, helping integrate Bandura's efficacy construct with leadership and group-process research. Practically, it suggests managers can lift group effectiveness by cultivating transformational behaviors and emphasizing the empowerment process that raises collective confidence.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems: (1) common-method/common-source bias persists despite the subgroup split, because carryover effects remain; future work should gather input, process, and outcome variables from independent sources. (2) The correlational design precludes causal claims; experimental and longitudinal designs are needed. (3) Omitted control variables (team history/age, leader tenure) could confound the leadership-process-outcome links. (4) Performance is a perceptual self-assessment, prone to inflation and social desirability; objective or externally sourced performance measures are recommended. (5) External validity: the all-Korean, 93%-male sample may not generalize, and the translated Western-developed MLQ may miss cultural nuances; replication in other cultural and gender-balanced settings is needed. They also call for exploring additional mediators/moderators such as task type, task interdependence, communication, workflow, and power dynamics. The unexpected null cohesiveness-to-efficacy result is itself an open question: the authors conjecture that actual successful joint experience, not cohesion alone, may be the precondition for collective-efficacy.

## Connections
The work builds directly on the transformational/transactional leadership tradition of Burns (1978) and Bass (1985, 1990, 1998), and on Bass & Avolio's (1994) four-component model operationalized through the MLQ (Bass & Avolio, 1990; Avolio, Bass & Jung, 1999). The collective-efficacy construct derives from Bandura (1986, 1997) and connects to Zaccaro, Blair, Peterson & Zazanis (1995) on collective efficacy and to Lindsley, Brass & Thomas (1995) on efficacy-performance spirals; the paper's null result runs against Zaccaro et al.'s prediction that cohesion raises efficacy. The self-concept and collectivistic-motivation mechanisms draw on Shamir (1990), Shamir, House & Arthur (1993), and House & Shamir (1993). Empowerment measurement follows Spreitzer (1995) and Conger & Kanungo (1987); cohesion measurement follows Bernthal & Insko (1993). Related group-efficacy/performance evidence includes Campion, Medsker & Higgs (1993), Guzzo, Yost, Campbell & Shea (1993), Gully, Beaubien, Incalcaterra & Joshi (meta-analysis), and the authors' own Sosik, Avolio & Kahai (1997, 1998) and Jung & Avolio (1999, 2000) studies. The PLS methodology follows Wold (1985) and its small-group application in Sambamurthy & Chin (1994).

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Most economic models are based on the self-interest hypothesis that assumes that material self-interest exclusively motivates all people. Experimental economists have gathered overwhelming evidence in recent years, however, that systematically refutes the self-interest hypothesis, suggesting that concerns for altruism, fairness, and reciprocity strongly motivate many people. Moreover, several theoretical papers demonstrate that the observed phenomena can be explained in a rigorous and tractable manner. These theories then induced a first wave of experimental research which offered exciting insights into both the nature of preferences and the relative performance of competing fairness theories. The purpose of this chapter is to review these developments, to point out open questions, and to suggest avenues for future research. We also discuss recent neuroeconomic evidence that is consistent with the view that many people have a taste for mutual cooperation and the punishment of norm violators. We further illustrate the powerful impact of fairness concerns on cooperation, competition, incentives, and contract design.

## Summary

This is a 77-page Handbook of the Economics of Giving, Altruism and Reciprocity survey (Chapter 8) by Fehr and Schmidt. It synthesizes two decades of experimental evidence refuting the self-interest hypothesis and then catalogues, formalizes, and critically compares the "first wave" of other-regarding-preference models: social-preference models (altruism, relative income/envy, inequity aversion, hybrids), interdependent-preference (type-based) models, intention-based reciprocity models built on psychological game theory, and emerging axiomatic approaches. It argues that other-regarding behavior is rational (taste-based, GARP-consistent) rather than a mistake, shows how competition can fully suppress fairness effects, and stresses that the interaction between fair and selfish types is the key to explaining why the same people look selfish in some games and fair in others. Because the chapter exceeds 50 pages, I read it targeted: front matter, the full introduction/overview, the empirical foundations (Section 2), the formal model definitions (Section 3), and the discrimination evidence (Section 4); I did not ingest the applications section (Section 5) or conclusions verbatim.

## Research question

Under which conditions do other-regarding preferences (altruism, fairness, reciprocity) have important economic effects, and what is the best way to formally model and empirically discriminate among them? The chapter takes as settled that *many* people deviate from self-interest, and asks instead how to model preferences rigorously and how to tell competing fairness theories apart with data.

## Method / identification

A survey/synthesis combining experimental evidence with formal model exposition; no new data. The methodological backbone is the use of strategically simple games where the selfish action is salient and dominant, so deviations identify other-regarding motives. The core formal models reviewed are:

- **Inequity aversion ([[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt 1999]]):** $$U_i(x_1,\dots,x_N)=x_i-\frac{\alpha_i}{N-1}\sum_{j\neq i}\max\{x_j-x_i,0\}-\frac{\beta_i}{N-1}\sum_{j\neq i}\max\{x_i-x_j,0\}$$ with $0\le\beta_i\le\alpha_i$ and $\beta_i\le 1$; disadvantageous inequality weighs more than advantageous ($\alpha_i\ge\beta_i$). Player-by-player comparison; heterogeneity of types is essential.
- **[[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]]:** $U_i=U_i(x_i,\sigma_i)$ where $\sigma_i$ is $i$'s share of total income, concave with a maximum at $\sigma_i=1/N$. Comparison is to the group *average*, not to each opponent — a sharp contrast to Fehr-Schmidt.
- **Relative income / envy (Bolton 1991, Kirchsteiger 1994):** $U_i(x_i,x_j)=u_i(x_i,x_i/x_j)$ — agent suffers only when behind.
- **Quasi-maximin hybrid ([[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin 2002]]):** convex combination of own payoff and a disinterested welfare function $W=\delta\min\{x_1,\dots,x_N\}+(1-\delta)\sum x_k$, later augmented by an endogenous demerit profile $\rho$ to add intention-based reciprocity (the "reciprocal fairness equilibrium").
- **Interdependent / type-based preferences ([[@Levine1998|Levine 1998]]):** $U_i=x_i+\sum_{j\neq i}x_j(a_i+\lambda a_j)/(1+\lambda)$ — kindness conditioned on the opponent's *type*, turning games into signaling games. Related: [[@Gul2005|Gul & Pesendorfer (2005)]], Rotemberg (2004).
- **Intention-based reciprocity ([[@Rabin1993|Rabin 1993]]):** using psychological game theory (Geanakoplos, Pearce & Stacchetti 1989), a kindness function $$f_i(a_i,b_j)\equiv\frac{x_j^f(b_j,a_i)-x_j^{f}(b_j)}{x_j^h(b_j)-x_j^l(b_j)}$$ and utility $U_i(a,b_j,c_i)=x_i(a,b_j)+f_j(b_j,c_i)\big(1+f_i(a_i,b_j)\big)$. Generalized to extensive form by [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger]] (2004, Sequential Reciprocity Equilibrium) and merged with inequity aversion by [[@Falk2006|Falk & Fischbacher (2006)]].
- **Guilt aversion (Charness & Dufwenberg 2004)** and **axiomatic foundations (Segal & Sobel 2004; Neilson 2005)**, where Segal-Sobel derive $u_i(\sigma_i,\sigma_j)=v_i(\sigma_i,\sigma_j)+a_{i,\sigma_j}v_j(\sigma_i,\sigma_j)$ from independence plus self-interest.

## Data

None of its own — this is a review. It draws on hundreds of ultimatum, dictator, power-to-take, third-party-punishment, trust, gift-exchange, and public-good experiments, including representative-population trust games in Germany (Fehr et al. 2002) and the Netherlands (Bellemare & Kröger 2003) and the 15-small-scale-society cross-cultural ultimatum study (Henrich et al. 2001, 2004).

## Key findings

- **Robust ultimatum-game facts:** modal/mean offers are 40–50% of surplus; offers below 20% are rejected with probability 0.4–0.6; rejection probability decreases in offer size. Dictator-game offers (10–25%) are much lower, so fear of rejection explains part — but not all — of proposer generosity.
- **Punishment of norm violators is real and not self-interested:** in the third-party punishment game roughly 60% of uninvolved third parties punish unfair (sub-50%) allocations at a cost to themselves; second-party punishment is stronger than third-party.
- **Stakes barely matter:** raising stakes to up to three months' (Cameron) or ten weeks' income changed behavior little or not at all.
- **Other-regarding behavior is rational, not error:** subjects satisfy GARP ([[@Andreoni2002|Andreoni & Miller 2002]]), respond to the price of giving/punishing, and adjust instantly between one-shot and reputation conditions (>80% raise acceptance thresholds when reputation is possible), refuting the "confused repeated-game heuristics" interpretation.
- **Neuroeconomic support:** reward-network activation when cooperating or punishing norm violators, and empathy circuitry (Singer et al. 2004) consistent with a genuine taste for cooperation and punishment.
- **Heterogeneity is the engine:** populations mix selfish and fair types; competition (e.g., proposer-competition ultimatum) can wipe out fairness effects entirely, while a small fraction of inequity-averse punishers can sustain cooperation among selfish agents.
- **Discrimination results:** the Bolton-Ockenfels average-reference assumption fails to explain third-party punishment and the (575,575,575) vs (900,300,600) choice (54% pick equal), favoring player-by-player comparison. Efficiency/surplus concern is real (Andreoni & Vesterlund classify ~44% selfish, 35% egalitarian, 21% surplus-maximizers; Engelmann & Strobel 2004 emphasize efficiency).

## Contribution

A canonical, formally precise taxonomy and critical appraisal of the entire first generation of other-regarding-preference theories, establishing the now-standard distinctions between distributional (social) preferences, type-based interdependent preferences, and intention-based reciprocity, and articulating the evaluation criteria — parsimony, portability of parameter estimates across games, tractability, equilibrium uniqueness — by which later models are judged. It also crystallizes the "interaction of fair and selfish types" insight and the competition-suppresses-fairness result.

## Limitations & open questions

The authors flag explicit open problems that are strong project hooks: (i) **Who are the relevant reference agents?** is "an important unsolved problem" — there is almost no theory or evidence on how social-comparison groups form. (ii) **Equality versus efficiency:** the relative weight of egalitarian vs surplus-maximizing motives is unresolved and context-dependent. (iii) Intention-based models built on psychological game theory suffer from **multiplicity of equilibria** (both kind and hostile self-fulfilling equilibria coexist) and **lack selection criteria**, plus dimensional/cardinality problems in the kindness term. (iv) Most reciprocity models are **too complex to test** and have not demonstrated **out-of-sample parameter portability** across games. (v) Whether behavior shifts toward selfishness at *extremely* high stakes remains untested. (vi) Reconciling intentions with distributional concerns in a tractable, falsifiable model.

Guilt aversion comes from Charness & Dufwenberg (2004); axiomatic treatments from Segal & Sobel (2004) and Neilson (2005), the latter linking inequity aversion to prospect theory ([[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky 1979]]). The empirical backbone includes the ultimatum game of Guth, Schmittberger & Schwarze (1982), the trust game of Berg, Dickhaut & McCabe (1995), the gift-exchange game of [[@Fehr1993|Fehr, Kirchsteiger & Riedl (1993)]], third-party punishment from Fehr & Fischbacher (2004), the power-to-take game of Bosman & van Winden (2002), cross-cultural evidence from Henrich et al. (2001, 2004), and the strong-reciprocity framing of Gintis (2000) and Fehr & Fischbacher (2003). The learning-based alternative interpretations are Roth & Erev (1995) and Binmore, Gale & Samuelson (1995). This chapter is the natural companion to [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] and a standard reference point for structural mixture-model estimation of social preferences (e.g., Bruhin, Fehr & Schmidt 2019).

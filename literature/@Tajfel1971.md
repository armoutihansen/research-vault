> [!abstract] Abstract
> The aim of the studies was to assess the effects of social categorization on intergroup behaviour when, in the intergroup situation, neither calculations of individual interest nor previously existing attitudes of hostility could have been said to have determined discriminative behaviour against an outgroup. These conditions were satisfied in the experimental design. In the first series of experiments, it was found that the subjects favoured their own group in the distribution of real rewards and penalities in a situation in which nothing but the variable of fairly irrelevant classification distinguished between the ingroup and the outgroup. In the second series of experiments it was found that: 1) maximum joint profit independent of group membership did not affect significantly the manner in which the subjects divided real pecuniary rewards; 2) maximum profit for own group did affect the distribution of rewards; 3) the clearest effect on the distribution of rewards was due to the subjects' attempt to achieve a maximum difference between the ingroup and the outgroup even at the price of sacrificing other 'objective' advantages. The design and the results of the study are theoretically discussed within the framework of social norms and expectations and particularly in relation to a 'generic' norm of outgroup behaviour prevalent in some societies.

## Summary
This is the foundational "minimal group paradigm" paper. Tajfel, Billig, Bundy, and Flament strip an intergroup situation of every conventional cause of discrimination — face-to-face interaction, anonymity-breaking, instrumental stakes, self-interest, prior hostility, anticipated future contact — and show that the bare act of categorizing schoolboys into two arbitrary groups is sufficient to make them allocate money so as to favour their own group. Most strikingly, in Experiment 2 subjects sacrifice both joint payoff and absolute ingroup payoff in order to maximize the *relative difference* in favour of the ingroup. The authors interpret this as the operation of a "generic" social norm of ingroup–outgroup behaviour, held in tension with a competing fairness norm.

## Research question
Can the very act of social categorization, isolated from all other variables, by itself produce intergroup behaviour that discriminates against an outgroup and favours an ingroup? And what are the *base-line* conditions under which such differential behaviour is to be expected — i.e. how minimal can the group be?

## Method / identification
A two-part laboratory design (the "minimal group paradigm"). Part I induces an intergroup categorization; Part II measures money-allocation behaviour via decision matrices, with subjects always allocating to *two other people* (never to themselves), under anonymity (code numbers), no face-to-face contact, and no instrumental link between the categorization criterion and the allocation task.

**Experiment 1** ($n=64$ boys, 14–15, eight groups of 8): subjects first estimated numbers of dots on a screen, then were ostensibly split by "over-/underestimation" (neutral condition) or "better/worse accuracy" (value condition) — actually assigned *at random*. Each allocation matrix presents 14 boxes, each a pair of payoffs (top row to one recipient, bottom row to another). Three choice types: **I** (both recipients ingroup), **O** (both outgroup), **D** (one ingroup, one outgroup). D choices are scored by rank 1–14, where 14 = max points to the ingroup member and a rank of $7.5$ = the fairness point; one-sample $t$-tests compare group means against $7.5$. I/O choices are re-scored under four "favouritism strategies" (simple/inverted/top/bottom) to obtain a valid comparison, and an *extremization* measure (distance of a choice from the matrix centre, ignoring direction) separates deliberate bias from a symmetry artefact.

**Experiment 2** ($n=48$, plus a pilot) replaces the categorization criterion with aesthetic preference for "Klee vs. Kandinsky" paintings (signatures hidden), again assigning subjects randomly. It orthogonalizes three payoff variables so their pulls can be measured by mean rank distance from each variable's optimum:
- $\text{MJP}$ = maximum joint payoff (largest sum to the two recipients);
- $\text{MIP}$ = maximum ingroup payoff;
- $\text{MD}$ = maximum *difference* in favour of the ingroup.

Type A matrices set $\text{MJP}$ opposite to ($\text{MIP}$ and $\text{MD}$ combined); Type B matrices set $\text{MD}$ opposite to ($\text{MJP}$ and $\text{MIP}$ combined). Differences are tested with the Wilcoxon matched-pairs signed-ranks test (two-tailed).

## Data
Original experimental data: 126 subjects in total (64 + 48 + a pilot incl. four girls), all but one from a Bristol comprehensive school, allocating real money (each point $= 1/10$ penny) over booklets of matrices. No external/observational dataset.

## Key findings
1. **Pure categorization produces discrimination.** In Experiment 1, all four groups' mean D-choice ranks lie significantly above the fairness point $7.5$ ($p<.01$); 54 of 64 subjects favoured the ingroup overall ($p<.002$). The *value* vs. *neutral* manipulation had no significant effect — discrimination appeared even with the wholly neutral criterion.
2. **It is deliberate, not an artefact.** I and O choices cluster tightly around fairness ($7.5$), whereas D choices are pulled toward ingroup favour; mean extremization is $2.81$ for D versus $1.87$ (I) and $1.72$ (O). So categorization is not merely an "organizing principle" used in the absence of guideposts — it generates a *deliberate* discriminatory strategy.
3. **Relative difference dominates absolute gain.** In Experiment 2, $\text{MIP}$ and $\text{MD}$ combined drive choices very significantly while $\text{MJP}$ (joint profit) has *no* significant effect on Type A matrices. On Type B, where $\text{MD}$ opposes both $\text{MJP}$ and $\text{MIP}$, subjects still sacrifice joint and ingroup payoff to maximize the *gap* in favour of their own group — "winning" matters more than gaining.
4. **A fairness norm coexists.** Choices everywhere hover near the fairness point; behaviour is best read as a *compromise* between a generic groupness norm and a fairness norm.

## Contribution
Establishes the minimal group paradigm and demonstrates that intergroup discrimination requires neither conflict of interest, competition, prior hostility, nor anticipated interaction — categorization alone suffices. The $\text{MJP}/\text{MIP}/\text{MD}$ decomposition isolates *maximizing relative difference* as a distinct motive, foreshadowing Social Identity Theory. For economists, it is a canonical demonstration of non-consequentialist, relative-payoff-driven allocation behaviour that pure self-interest and pure joint-surplus models cannot accommodate.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Fairness ($F$) was not manipulated** in either experiment; the authors flag that its relative weight "will have to be assessed independently" — an explicit hook for future work.
- **Cross-cultural scope.** Results are not assumed universal; the salience of the fairness norm "may provide an opportunity for interesting cross-cultural comparisons" — the generic groupness norm is posited as society-dependent.
- **Negative payoffs confound the I/O finding.** The collateral I-vs-O result did not appear in Experiment 1 matrices using penalties (1, 2, 5, 6), since taking away from the outgroup may differ psychologically from giving it less.
- **Experimenter / subject effects.** Heavy use of the word "group" in instructions may have cued behaviour; the authors argue this is part of the manipulation, but identify the key open theoretical problem as specifying conditions under which discrimination *would not* occur.

## Connections
Builds directly on Tajfel's own work on categorization and quantitative judgment (Tajfel & Wilkes, 1963) and cognitive aspects of prejudice (Tajfel, 1969, 1970), and on Allport (1954) and Campbell (1967) on the cognitive functions of categorization. It sharpens earlier intergroup-conflict studies — Sherif's Robbers Cave field experiments (Sherif et al., 1961), Blake & Mouton (1962), Bass & Duntemann — by removing competition, and answers Rabbie & Horwitz (1969) / Rabbie & Wilkens (1968), whose control conditions had suggested "group classification per se appears to be insufficient." This paper is the empirical seed of Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner). For behavioural and experimental economics it is an ancestor of the literature on social preferences, in-group favouritism, and identity (e.g. Akerlof & Kranton's economics of identity), and a key reference point for distinguishing inequity aversion and joint-surplus maximization from a pure desire for relative standing.

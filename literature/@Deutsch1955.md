## Summary
This is the foundational paper that introduces the now-canonical distinction between two kinds of social influence: **normative social influence** (conforming to the positive expectations of others) and **informational social influence** (accepting others' reports as evidence about reality). Using a modified Asch line-judgment paradigm, Deutsch and Gerard show experimentally that the conformity observed in classic "group influence" studies (Sherif, Asch) is partly normative and partly informational, and that several manipulations - group membership, anonymity, and prior commitment to one's own judgment - move conformity in exactly the directions predicted if the two influence types are separable.

## Research question
Prior conformity experiments lumped all social effects together under the vague term "group influence." Deutsch and Gerard ask whether one can conceptually and experimentally separate (a) the pressure to *agree* with others to satisfy their expectations from (b) the rational *use of others' judgments as data* about an uncertain world, and whether manipulations targeting one channel leave the predicted signature on observed conformity errors.

## Method / identification
A modified **Asch (1951) line-matching experiment** with $N=101$ NYU psychology students. Subjects match a standard line to one of three comparison lines; correct answers are objectively easy. On 12 of 18 trials, confederates ("stooges") announce unanimous, clearly wrong answers; the dependent variable is the **number of socially influenced errors** the naive subject makes in the direction of the stooges' errors. The design crosses several between- and within-subject treatments to dissociate the two influence channels:

1. **Face-to-face situation** - exact Asch replication (with 3 stooges instead of 8, unacquainted subjects). Two judgment series: a *visual* series (lines present when judging) and a *memory* series (lines removed ~3s before judgment), order counterbalanced.
2. **Anonymous situation** - partitions prevent seeing/hearing others; judgments entered by button press; no live stooges but "electrical stooges" (a hidden accomplice lights bulbs mimicking the stooge response pattern). Each naive subject believes he is "Subject No. 3" and cannot be individually identified. This removes the *normative* channel while preserving the *informational* one.
3. **Group situation** - identical to anonymous but with instructions inducing group membership motivation: subjects told they compete as a group of 20 for a prize (Broadway tickets) awarded to the five lowest-error groups, so each member's error hurts the group.
4. **Self-commitment / Magic Pad self-commitment** - subjects privately write down their own judgment before hearing others; in the *Magic Pad* version they erase it (only they ever see it), isolating purely *self-generated* normative pressure to be true to oneself.
5. **Public commitment** - subjects write, sign, and hand in their initial judgments, adding *external* normative pressure to stay consistent with their own stated view.

Significance is assessed with one-tailed $t$ tests derived from analyses of variance over the error counts.

## Data
None external - the paper reports primary experimental data collected by the authors: error counts and post-experimental questionnaire responses from 101 subjects (90 returned questionnaires).

## Key findings
Six hypotheses, all receiving "strong support":

- **H I (group vs. aggregate):** In the anonymous, no-commitment condition, group members averaged $12.47$ socially influenced errors versus $5.92$ for non-group individuals ($p<.001$) - group framing **more than doubles** conformity even though the informational channel is held constant. Seven of fifteen group subjects spontaneously reported a "felt obligation" to other members; no non-group subject did.
- **H II (identifiability):** Conformity is markedly lower in the anonymous than in the face-to-face situation ($p<.001$), confirming that removing perceived pressure to conform reduces *normative* influence. (The authors note faulty procedure left some subjects not fully convinced of their anonymity, so the true effect is likely larger.)
- **H III (own-judgment commitment reduces others' pull):** Both public and self-commitment sharply cut socially influenced errors in both situations ($p<.001$).
- **H IV (external + self normative pressure > self alone):** *Public* commitment and the indelible *self*-commitment reduce errors more than the *Magic Pad* self-commitment, where only internal self-pressure operates - i.e., contemporaneous external pressure to be true to oneself supplements internalized self-pressure.
- **H V (uncertainty raises susceptibility):** The *memory* series (less certain) produced more errors than the *visual* series ($p<.005$); 51 of 90 subjects reported greater certainty with lines present. A carryover effect: doing the memory series first raised conformity throughout both series.
- **H VI (uncertainty about others reduces informational influence):** Stated theoretically; no data collected, presented to qualify H V and counter Festinger's and Asch's claims that influence simply rises as clarity falls.

A key residual result: even in the anonymous condition (normative channel largely removed), subjects make *more* errors than Asch's alone-control (well under one error per subject), demonstrating a genuine, separable **informational** influence.

## Contribution
Coins and operationalizes the **normative vs. informational social influence** distinction that became standard in social psychology, behavioral economics, and the conformity/herding literatures. It reinterprets the Asch and Sherif results as a blend of the two channels rather than monolithic "group pressure," and supplies a clean experimental toolkit (anonymity, group-incentive framing, private vs. public commitment, visual vs. memory uncertainty) for surgically toggling each channel.

## Limitations & open questions
- **H VI is untested** - the authors explicitly present it without data, flagging the role of *uncertainty about others' competence* in gating informational influence as an open empirical question.
- **Imperfect anonymity manipulation** - "faulty experimental procedure" meant some subjects did not internalize that their judgments were unidentifiable; the authors note a cleaner implementation should *strengthen* the H II effect, leaving the true magnitude open.
- **Ambiguous self-commitment results** - the ordinary (indelible) self-commitment behaved like public commitment, presumably because subjects believed "others can see what I have written"; disentangling pure self-pressure from imagined observability is unresolved.
- The induced "group" was deliberately trivial and artificial; how much stronger normative influence becomes under real, durable group ties is left open.

## Connections
Directly builds on and reinterprets Asch (1951, 1952) on group pressure and Sherif (1935) on the autokinetic effect, and engages Festinger (1950) on informal social communication and social-reality dependence (challenging the claim that influence is purely a function of physical-reality ambiguity). The normative side draws on the first author's own Deutsch (1949) theory of cooperation and competition, and Bovard (1951) on group structure and perception. The normative/informational dichotomy became foundational for later work on conformity and information cascades, e.g., Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer & Welch (1992) on informational cascades and herding, Banerjee (1992) on herd behavior, and the broader behavioral-economics treatment of social learning versus conformity preferences (e.g., Bernheim (1994) on conformity, and experimental social-preference and peer-effects literatures).

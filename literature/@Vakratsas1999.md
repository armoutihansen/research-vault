## Summary
A landmark *Journal of Marketing* review synthesizing 100 years and 250+ studies of "how advertising works." Vakratsas and Ambler organize the literature into a taxonomy of seven model classes distinguished by which intermediate effects—cognition (C), affect (A), experience (E)—they admit and in what sequence. Their central, contrarian conclusion is that the dominant "hierarchy of effects" tradition (AIDA, Lavidge–Steiner) lacks empirical support: there is no fixed temporal sequence in which advertising moves consumers from thinking to feeling to doing. Instead they propose an **EAC space**, a three-dimensional model in which the relative weight of experience, affect, and cognition is set by context (product category, involvement, life-cycle stage, competition, target market) rather than by a universal ordering.

## Research question
What do we actually know, empirically, about the mechanism by which advertising affects consumers? Specifically: which intermediate (mental) and behavioral effects are robustly supported across the literature, and is the century-old "hierarchy of effects" assumption—that advertising operates through a fixed sequence of mental stages—warranted?

## Method / identification
This is a structured *narrative literature review* (in the tradition of Bass and Wind 1995), not a meta-analysis or content analysis—chosen deliberately because the underlying studies use heterogeneous designs (field, single-source panel, laboratory/experimental) and incommensurable measures (advertising–sales elasticities, purchase intentions, awareness, recall). The procedure has two steps: (1) build a **taxonomy** of models, using Holbrook's (1986) notation C (cognition), A (affect), augmented with E (memory of prior brand purchase/usage/advertising experience); (2) derive **generalizations** from the empirical findings. The taxonomy ranges over: market response models (no intermediate effects), cognitive-information (C), pure affect (A), persuasive hierarchy (CA: "think→feel→do"), low-involvement hierarchy (CEA: "think→do→feel"), integrative ([C][A][E], order context-dependent), and hierarchy-free (NH). Studies were sourced from ABI/Inform searches, Broadbent's (1992) synopsis of 456 studies, and networking; inclusion required a focus on behavioral and/or intermediate effects, empirical content, post-1960 date, and English language. Findings are compressed into 25 numbered conclusions (Table 2), and each generalization is required to rest on at least two distinct conclusions.

## Data
None of its own—this is secondary synthesis. The corpus is the body of 250+ advertising studies reviewed, spanning aggregate market-level econometric data (gross rating points, brand sales/share), individual/household single-source panel data (e.g., split-cable experiments such as Lodish et al. 1995a's 389 tests), and experimental psychology studies.

## Key findings
The 25 conclusions feed five generalizations:
- **G1:** Experience, affect, and cognition are the three key intermediate effects, and omitting any one leads to overestimating the others.
- **G2:** Short-term advertising elasticities are small (typically 0 to .2) and decrease over the product life cycle; promotional elasticities can be up to ~20× advertising elasticities.
- **G3:** In mature, frequently purchased packaged-goods markets, returns to advertising diminish fast; a low frequency (one to three reminders per purchase cycle) suffices for an established brand. Response often levels off after the third exposure.
- **G4:** A *space* of intermediate effects is supported, but a *hierarchy (sequence)* is not. This is the headline result: the single-hierarchy concept is "deeply flawed," consistent with attitude–behavior correlations of only 0 to .30 and with neuroscience evidence that the brain processes cognitive (neocortex) and affective (limbic) information in parallel and interactively, not serially.
- **G5:** Cognitive bias interferes with affect measurement—verbal/attitudinal elicitation forces affective responses through a rationalizing cognitive filter, distorting them.

Supporting micro-findings include: advertising is more effective for experience/credence than search goods; nonprice advertising lowers price sensitivity while price advertising raises it; product usage experience generally dominates advertising in shaping beliefs/attitudes; advertising has a stronger effect on high-loyalty consumers and is more effective when it *precedes* (frames) usage experience, especially negative experience.

## Contribution
Reframes a century of advertising theory: replaces the linear hierarchy-of-effects paradigm with the context-dependent **EAC space**, in which advertising's position is set by goal, product category, competition, marketing-mix, life-cycle stage, and target market. It provides a unifying taxonomy and a set of empirically disciplined generalizations that have become a standard reference point for advertising-effectiveness research.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit about gaps and propose directions for further research:
- **Long-term effects** are understudied and mostly measured at the market rather than individual level; they call for more individual-level, single-source longitudinal studies and better methodologies for reliably measuring long-term effects.
- **Combining intermediate and behavioral effects:** econometric (objective, behavioral) and conceptual (subjective, intermediate) streams have developed separately; they propose augmenting single-source household data with cognitive, affective, and experience measures to study main and interactive effects in a single, natural, nonexperimental setting.
- **Cognitive bias** in affect measurement remains unresolved; affect appears to have at least utilitarian and hedonic components, but no nonverbal measure (facial coding, EEG, projective) has proven satisfactory.
- **Context operationalization:** how exactly the EAC-space coordinates should be set from context variables is left open, as is the assumption of consumer homogeneity in integrative-model evidence (involvement is likely personal as well as product-driven).
- Selection is admittedly incomplete (unpublished practitioner models, non-English work excluded), introducing variable study quality.

## Connections
Builds on and overturns the persuasive-hierarchy lineage of Lavidge and Steiner (1961) and the AIDA model attributed to Strong (1925). Uses Holbrook's (1986) C/A notation. The cognitive-information branch rests on the economics of advertising-as-information—[[@Nelson1970|Nelson]] (1970, 1974), Stigler (1961), and Telser (1964)—with the market-power view from Comanor and Wilson (1974). Pure-affect models draw on Zajonc (1980) on the primacy of affect and the attitude-toward-the-ad work of Mitchell and Olson (1981), MacKenzie, Lutz, and Belch (1986), and Brown and Stayman (1992). Persuasive-hierarchy mediation invokes Petty and Cacioppo's Elaboration Likelihood Model and Cacioppo and Petty (1985), MacInnis and Jaworski (1989), and the Fishbein and Ajzen (1975) attitude framework. The low-involvement/weak-theory branch follows Ehrenberg (1974, 1994), Krugman (1965), and Jones (1990); integrative models follow Vaughn's (1980, 1986) FCB grid and Deighton (1984). Empirical generalizations connect to Assmus, Farley, and Lehmann (1984), Clarke (1976), Leone (1995), Tellis (1988), Lodish et al. (1995a, b), and Mela, Gupta, and Lehmann (1997). The attitude–behavior consistency point links to Fazio, Zanna, and Cooper (1978) and Wicker (1969).

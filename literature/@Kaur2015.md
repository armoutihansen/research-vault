> [!abstract] Abstract
> Self-control problems change the logic of agency theory by partly aligning the interests of the firm and worker: both now value contracts that elicit future effort. Findings from a year-long field experiment with full-time data entry workers support this idea. First, workers increase output by voluntarily choosing dominated contracts (which penalize low output but give no additional rewards for high output). Second, effort increases closer to (randomly assigned) paydays. Third, the contract and payday effects are strongly correlated within workers, and this correlation grows with experience. We suggest that workplace features such as high-powered incentives or effort monitoring may provide self-control benefits.

## Summary
Kaur, Kremer, and Mullainathan argue that worker self-control problems rewrite agency theory: because employer profits fall directly when workers shirk, firm and worker interests partly align around contracts that elicit future effort. A 13-month field experiment with full-time Indian data-entry workers delivers three signatures of present bias — voluntary take-up of *dominated* contracts, rising output as a randomly assigned payday approaches, and a within-worker correlation between the two effects that strengthens with experience. The implication is that high-powered incentives, production minimums, and rigid effort monitoring may function partly as employer-provided commitment devices.

## Research question
Do workers exhibit time inconsistency (present bias) in real, high-stakes, full-time effort provision, and if so, do firms and workers have a shared interest in workplace features that act as commitment devices? Specifically: will sophisticated, present-biased workers voluntarily pay for sharper incentives that constrain their own future selves?

## Method / identification
A worker $i$ has per-period quasi-linear utility $y_t - a_i c(e_t)$ over income $y_t$ and effort $e_t$, with effort cost $c(e_t)=e_t^{v}$, $v>1$ (so the output-to-piece-rate elasticity is $\varepsilon=1/(v-1)$). Output equals effort; pay for all effort in periods $1,\dots,T$ is received only at $T$. Time-consistent workers discount exponentially, $D^{C}(t)=\delta^{t}$; time-inconsistent workers have a hyperbolic factor $D^{I}(t)$ with $D^{I}(t+s)/D^{I}(t)$ strictly increasing in $t$ and $D^{I}(t+1)/D^{I}(t)\le\delta$. All workers are *sophisticated*. A contract for period $t$ is signed $k$ periods in advance, so the period-$t{-}k$ self maximizes $D^{i}(T-t+k)\,w(e)-D^{i}(k)\,c(e)$ while the period-$t$ self maximizes $D^{i}(T-t)\,w(e)-D^{i}(0)\,c(e)$; the wedge between these drives every prediction.

The *dominated contract* lets the worker pick a target $X_{t\mid t-k}$ and pays the penalty piece rate $b^{p}<b$ for output below target and the full rate $b$ at or above it:
$$v_{t\mid t-k}(e)=\begin{cases} a+b^{p}e & e<X_{t\mid t-k}\\ a+be & e\ge X_{t\mid t-k}.\end{cases}$$
It is weakly dominated by the linear contract $w(e)=a+be$ at every output level, so a time-consistent worker never strictly prefers it — making take-up a clean test for present bias.

Empirically, full-time data-entry workers in Mysore, India were paid Rs. 0.03 per accurate field plus a Rs. 15 show-up fee, i.e. $w(e)=15+0.03e$. Each day a worker was independently randomized into one of four contract treatments — control (linear), imposed dominated target, *morning* option to self-choose a dominated target ($k=1$), or *evening* option ($k=2$) — each occurring 25% of the time. Orthogonally, workers were randomized into Tuesday/Thursday/Saturday weekly paydays, identifying the pay-cycle effect free of day-of-week confounds. Exogenous piece-rate variation is used to estimate $\varepsilon$ and convert effect sizes into piece-rate equivalents and to calibrate the implied discount rate.

## Data
13-month field experiment; core analysis uses 102 workers and 8,423 daily observations over the 8-month window when both contract and payday treatments ran simultaneously. Workers averaged 24 years old, ~13 years of education, ~74% male. Auxiliary measures include a contracts-comprehension quiz (mean 93%), Raven's/Digit-Span IQ composite, an end-line monetary discount-rate elicitation (smaller-sooner vs. larger-later cash), and self-reported self-control survey items.

## Key findings
- **Proposition 1 (pay-cycle effect).** Output rises by $\varepsilon\,[\,(D^{i}(T-t-1)-D^{i}(T-t))/D^{i}(T-t)\,]$ from one day to the next; the bracket equals $(1/\delta)-1$ for exponential discounters but exceeds it under time inconsistency. Empirically output is **8% higher on paydays**, equivalent to a 24% piece-rate increase. Calibration shows no plausible exponential $\delta$ can rationalize this — it would require a ~4%/day rate (~$1.65\times10^{6}$%/year).
- **Proposition 2 (demand for dominated contracts + bound).** Time-inconsistent selves strictly prefer $v_{t\mid t-k}(\cdot)$ and set a target in $(e_{t\mid t}^{i},\,e_{t\mid t-k}^{i}]$; time-consistent workers never do. The output increase $x_{t\mid t-k}^{i}$ is a *lower bound* on time inconsistency: $x_{t\mid t-k}^{i}\le \varepsilon\,[\,D^{i}(T-t+k)/D^{i}(k)\div D^{i}(T-t)/D^{i}(0)-1\,]$, which equals $1/\beta-1$ under quasi-hyperbolic preferences. Workers chose positive targets **36% of the time**; the treatment-on-the-treated output effect is **6%**, equal to an 18% piece-rate rise, implying workers value future net effort benefits at least 18% more at contract choice than at execution.
- **Proposition 3 (correlation).** Pay-cycle and dominated-contract effects are positively correlated within workers. Empirically, above-mean-payday-effect workers are **49% more likely** to choose dominated contracts; for them the TOT output effect is **28%** (≈85% piece-rate equivalent).
- **Proposition 4 (decreasing contract effect over the cycle).** Dominated-contract effects are larger further from payday, since the self-control wedge shrinks as $T$ approaches — confirmed in the data.
- **Proposition 5 (horizon of choice).** Workers should self-impose weakly higher effort when choosing further ahead; this prediction is *not* borne out (evening vs. morning take-up is similar), plausibly because output uncertainty (network/commute shocks) is partly resolved by arrival — when such uncertainty is low, evening demand does exceed morning demand.
- **Learning.** Payday effects are stable with experience, but after ~2 months high-payday-effect workers are 20 pp (73%) more likely to choose dominated contracts, so the Proposition-3 correlation grows with experience. Notably, neither effect is predicted by conventional lab cash-discounting measures.

## Contribution
First field-experimental demonstration of present bias in real, full-time, high-stakes labor effort (rather than lab or short student tasks), with a design — the dominated contract — that isolates demand for self-control without claiming optimality. It reframes agency theory: self-control problems hurt employer profits directly, so firms have both the motive and the means to embed commitment devices in incentive contracts and job design (production minimums, fixed hours, monitoring, factory discipline). A companion paper (Kaur, Kremer & Mullainathan 2014) shows sufficiently strong present bias reverses the standard partial-insurance result and that the mere presence of present-biased workers makes time-consistent workers worse off via adverse selection.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Proposition 5 fails**: output uncertainty resolved near the effort moment confounds the horizon-of-choice prediction; the authors flag disentangling stochastic-shock timing from pure present bias as open.
- Lab cash-discounting and preference-reversal measures do **not** predict the behavioral effects — what *does* identify time-inconsistent workers ex ante is unresolved.
- The dominated contract is a diagnostic device, **not** a claimed optimal contract; designing welfare-improving real-world contracts and assessing regulation (workweek limits, monitoring rules) under adverse selection is left open.
- Naive vs. sophisticated mix would weaken the Proposition-3 correlation; the share of naïfs is not separately identified.
- The pay-period length is taken as exogenous; endogenizing pay frequency (trading off work-side commitment against consumption-side savings commitment and transaction costs) is flagged as a separate research question.
- Broader speculative hooks: whether assembly lines, rigid hours, and factory discipline (and the farm-to-factory, agriculture-to-formal-sector transitions) derive productivity gains partly from self-control benefits.

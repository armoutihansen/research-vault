> [!abstract] Abstract
> A channel of distribution consists of different channel members each having his own decision variables. However, each channel member's decisions do affect the other channel members' profits and, as a consequence, actions. A lack of coordination of these decisions can lead to undesirable consequences. For example, in the simple manufacturer-retailer-consumer channel, uncoordinated and independent channel members' decisions over margins result in a higher price paid by the consumer than if those decisions were coordinated. In addition, the ensuing suboptimal volume leads to lower profits for both the manufacturer and the retailer. This paper explores the problems inherent in channel coordination. We address the following questions. What is the effect of channel coordination? What causes a lack of coordination in the channel? How difficult is it to achieve channel coordination? What mechanisms exist which can achieve channel coordination? What are the strengths and weaknesses of these mechanism? What is the role of nonprice variables (e.g., manufacturer advertising, retailer shelf-space) in coordination? Does the lack of coordination affect normative implications from in-store experimentation? Can quantity discounts be a coordination mechanism? Are some marketing practices actually disguised quantity discounts? We review the literature and present a simple formulation illustrating the roots of the coordination problem. We then derive the form of the quantity discount schedule that results in optimum channel profits.

## Summary
A foundational marketing-science treatment of the vertical-channel coordination problem (double marginalization). In an independent manufacturerâ€“retailer channel, each member sets his own per-unit margin, and self-interested margin-setting produces a retail price that is too high and a sales volume that is too low relative to the integrated ("joint maximum") channel optimum, leaving both worse off â€” a prisoners'-dilemma structure. The paper's central contribution is to show that a properly designed *quantity discount schedule* (more generally a variable-price contract) decentralizes the joint maximum by making each member's profit a linear function of total channel profit, thereby aligning incentives even with no order or inventory costs and even with a single retailer.

## Research question
How can a vertical channel of distribution (manufacturer + retailer) achieve the profits of joint ownership while remaining decentralized? Specifically: what causes the coordination failure, how does it extend beyond price to nonprice marketing variables (quality, shelf-space), what mechanisms restore coordination, and can quantity discounts serve as such a mechanism (rather than merely transferring inventory cost or price-discriminating)?

## Method / identification
Analytical economic theory of a symmetric bilateral monopoly under certainty. Manufacturer profit $\Pi=GD-F$ and retailer profit $\pi=gD-f$, with channel margin tied to retail price by $G+g+C+c=p$ and downward-sloping demand $D(p)$, $dD/dp<0$. The integrated owner's joint maximum requires
$$\frac{\partial(\Pi+\pi)}{\partial G}=\frac{\partial\Pi}{\partial G}+\frac{\partial\pi}{\partial G}=0.$$
Because $\partial\pi/\partial G=-g\,\varepsilon D/p<0$ (where $\varepsilon=-(\partial D/\partial p)/(D/p)$ is the absolute price elasticity), the joint-optimum margin $G^{*}$ satisfies $\partial\Pi/\partial G>0$ â€” the manufacturer wants to raise his margin from $G^{*}$ toward his selfish optimum $G^{**}$ where $\partial\Pi/\partial G=0$. The paper derives the reaction-function margins
$$G^{*}=\frac{g+c+C-\varepsilon^{*}g}{\varepsilon^{*}-1},\qquad G^{**}=\frac{g+c+C}{\varepsilon^{**}-1},$$
with symmetric expressions for $g$. The same logic is extended to nonprice variables via $D(p,Q,s)$ with $\partial D/\partial Q>0$, $\partial D/\partial s>0$ and cost dependence $C(Q)$, $c(s)$. The coordination mechanism is constructed by imposing profit sharing â€” manufacturer gets fraction $k_{1}$ of channel profit plus side payment $k_{2}$, $\Pi=k_{1}(G+g)D+k_{2}-F$, $\pi=(1-k_{1})(G+g)D-k_{2}-f$ â€” so all three objectives share the same first-order condition $(G+g)\,dD/dp+D=0$. The implementing instrument is the discount/transfer schedule
$$t(D)=k_{1}\,p(D)+(1-k_{1})C-k_{1}c+\frac{k_{2}}{D},$$
where $t=G+C$ is the price the retailer pays the manufacturer and $p(D)$ inverts demand. It is shown to be genuinely declining, $dt/dD<0$. Division of the cooperative surplus is treated as a separate bargaining problem, illustrated with the Nash/equal-split solution pinning down $k_{1}^{*}$.

## Data
None â€” this is a purely theoretical/analytical paper. It offers no estimation, only a stylized model with derived equations (most proofs relegated to an Appendix). It does point to several empirically testable hypotheses (e.g., whether smaller retailers are less price-sensitive; whether negotiated schedules differ across retailers and vanish as retail competition rises).

## Key findings
1. **Coordination failure is fundamental.** Even in the simplest two-member channel under certainty, independent margin-setting yields a price above and volume below the joint maximum; the structure is the classical prisoners' dilemma (each wants the *other* to cooperate). This rederives Cournot's (1838) double-marginalization result.
2. **The distortion extends to nonprice variables.** Variables with positive demand impact (product quality $Q$, shelf-space $s$) are set *too low* by global channel standards, because the member bearing their cost captures only part of the volume benefit.
3. **A linear-sharing quantity discount restores the optimum.** Any schedule making each member's profit linear in channel profit decentralizes the joint maximum; the explicit schedule $t(D)$ above does so. It simultaneously operationalizes revenue sharing ($k_{1}p(D)$), two-sided cost sharing ($(1-k_{1})C$ and $-k_{1}c$), and a fixed side payment ($k_{2}/D$).
4. **A new rationale for quantity discounts.** Discounts are profitable purely as coordination devices â€” independent of order/inventory-cost or price-discrimination motives, and even with a single retailer. Many observed practices (promotional allowances, cooperative advertising, manufacturer display support, transfer pricing in integrated firms) are reinterpreted as disguised profit-sharing / coordination mechanisms.
5. **Implications for in-store experimentation.** Short-run retail experiments that ignore channel reactions can mislead: in a coordinated vertical channel they would (wrongly) recommend raising the retail margin and cutting shelf-space, harming long-run profit once other members react.
6. **Variable-price contracts dominate simple fixed contracts** in flexibility and stability: they let members react to temporary shocks without breaking the agreement and provide incentives to return to the optimum after deviations.

## Contribution
Provides a clean, symmetric (neither principal/agent nor leader/follower is assumed) formalization of the channel-coordination problem and the first derivation of the *form* of the quantity-discount schedule that achieves the integrated optimum through decentralized incentives. It separates the coordination problem (achieving the surplus) from the bargaining problem (dividing it), and recasts quantity discounts and a wide range of trade-promotion practices as incentive-compatible profit-sharing mechanisms rather than cost-transfer or price-discrimination tools.

## Limitations & open questions
- **Cost observability:** if members do not know each other's true costs and report biased $\hat{C},\hat{c}$, the schedule is generally suboptimal unless $(1-k_{1})(\hat{C}-C)=k_{1}(\hat{c}-c)$; an open question is whether a biased schedule beats no schedule.
- **Heterogeneous retailers:** determining optimality conditions for a *single* schedule offered to a group of retailers with a distribution of retailing costs $c$ is left to future research.
- **Uncertainty:** extending to probabilistic demand (members maximize expected profit) and to asymmetric information (e.g., a new-product manufacturer better informed than the retailer) is flagged but not solved.
- **Horizontal/competitive structure:** extension to multiple competing manufacturers and retailers, and the resulting ambiguity in the *definition of a channel* and channel profit, remains open.
- **Implicit understandings / dynamics:** whether non-cooperative multiperiod (supergame) equilibria with Pareto-optimal payoffs exist is deferred to companion work.
- **Robinson-Patman legality** of differentiated schedules across retailers is acknowledged as a real constraint outside the model.

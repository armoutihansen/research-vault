> [!abstract] Abstract
> Mixed logit models with unobserved inter- and intra-individual heterogeneity hierarchically extend standard mixed logit models by allowing tastes to vary randomly both across individuals and across choice situations encountered by the same individual. Recent work advocates using these models in choice-based recommender systems under the premise that mixed logit models with unobserved inter- and intra-individual heterogeneity afford personalised preference estimation and prediction. In this study, we evaluate the ability of mixed logit with unobserved inter- and intra-individual heterogeneity to produce accurate individual-level predictions of choice behaviour. Using simulated and real data, we show that mixed logit models with unobserved inter- and intra-individual heterogeneity do not provide significant improvements in choice prediction accuracy over standard mixed logit models, which only account for inter-individual taste variation. We make these observations even in scenarios with high levels of intra-individual taste variation and when the number of choice situations per decision-maker is large. Also, the estimation of mixed logit with unobserved inter- and intra-individual heterogeneity requires at least seven times as much computation time as the estimation of standard mixed logit. Drawing from recent advances in machine learning and econometrics, we discuss alternative modelling approaches that can capture richer dependencies between decision-makers, alternatives and attributes.

## Summary
This research note is a negative-result benchmarking study. It asks whether mixed logit with both inter- *and* intra-individual taste heterogeneity (a two-level hierarchical mixing distribution) actually improves *personalised* choice prediction relative to standard mixed logit (one level, inter-individual only). Across an extensive simulation study and a real stated-preference dataset, the answer is essentially no: the extra intra-individual level buys at most a $0.003$ improvement in Brier score on conditional predictions, even when intra-individual variation accounts for 70% of total taste variance, while costing at least seven times more computation. The authors attribute the null result to the model's reliance on *nonstructural* random heterogeneity and point instead to recommender-system methods (matrix factorisation, collaborative learning, amortised variational inference, neural/tree-based utilities) that model structured dependencies between decision-makers, alternatives, and attributes.

## Research question
Does augmenting mixed logit with an additional intra-individual heterogeneity level deliver materially better *conditional* (personalised, history-aware) out-of-sample choice prediction than standard mixed logit, justifying its use in choice-based recommender systems? Subsidiary questions: how do maximum simulated likelihood (MSL) and Bayesian MCMC estimators compare for this model, and how large is the computational penalty?

## Method / identification
The behavioural model is a random utility specification. In choice situation $t$, decision-maker $n$ derives utility $U_{ntj} = V(\mathbf{X}_{ntj}, \boldsymbol{\beta}_{nt}) + \varepsilon_{ntj}$ from alternative $j$, with i.i.d. Gumbel(0,1) errors yielding the logit kernel $P(y_{nt}=j) = e^{V(\mathbf{X}_{ntj},\boldsymbol{\beta}_{nt})}/\sum_{j'} e^{V(\mathbf{X}_{ntj'},\boldsymbol{\beta}_{nt})}$. The distinguishing feature is *case-specific* tastes: $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{nt} \sim N(\boldsymbol{\mu}_n, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_W)$ (intra-individual perturbations) with $\boldsymbol{\mu}_n \sim N(\boldsymbol{\zeta}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_B)$ (inter-individual distribution), both covariance matrices full. Standard mixed logit is the restriction $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{nt} = \boldsymbol{\beta}_n\ \forall t$. The authors stress that despite the "individual-level" labels, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_W$ is *generic*, so intra-individual variation is learned from differences across all choices of all respondents, not within a single respondent.

Two estimators are formally derived and compared:
- **MSL.** Parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}=\{\boldsymbol{\zeta},\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_B,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_W\}$ are fixed; the unconditional log-likelihood marginalises out both $\boldsymbol{\mu}_n$ and $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{nt}$ via a double integral, approximated by simulation as $SLL(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ with $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{nt,dr}=\boldsymbol{\zeta}+\mathbf{L}_B\boldsymbol{\xi}_{n,d}+\mathbf{L}_W\boldsymbol{\xi}_{nt,r}$ using $D$ inter- and $R$ intra-individual draws. Optimisation is over the Cholesky factors $\{\mathbf{L}_B,\mathbf{L}_W\}$ (preserving positive-definiteness) via L-BFGS with *analytical* gradients (Appendix A.1). Individual posteriors $\boldsymbol{\mu}_n$ are recovered post-hoc by importance-weighted reconstruction (a Bayes-theorem formula with simulated weights $w_d$).
- **MCMC.** A fully Bayesian Gibbs sampler (following Becker et al., 2018) with vague normal prior on $\boldsymbol{\zeta}$ and half-$t$ priors on $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_B,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_W$ (implemented hierarchically via inverse-Wishart plus Gamma-distributed scale parameters $\delta_k$, chosen for non-informativity). Posterior samples of $\boldsymbol{\mu}_n$ and $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{nt}$ are obtained directly.

Predictive accuracy is scored two ways. The primary metric is the **Brier score** (a strictly proper scoring rule evaluated over the whole choice set), $\text{BS}=\frac{1}{NTJ}\sum_{n,t,j}(\mathbf{1}\{y_{nt}=j\}-\hat{P}_{ntj})^2$; the secondary metric is $P^{\text{chosen}}$ (mean predicted probability of the chosen alternative), which is the metric used by the prior studies they critique. Two prediction scenarios are distinguished: **unconditional** (new individuals, no history; integrate over both levels) and **conditional** (individuals already in training with a choice history; condition on $\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_n$ and integrate only over $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_W$). Estimation accuracy is measured by RMSE of $\boldsymbol{\zeta}$ and the unique elements of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_B,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_W$ against realised sample moments.

## Data
Two sources. **(1) Synthetic data**: three unlabelled alternatives, four attributes drawn Uniform(0,2) (≈20% error rate), $N=1000$, $T\in\{10,20\}$, 20 replications per cell. Two scenarios vary the intra-individual variance share $\alpha\in\{0.3,0.7\}$ with total variance set to $2|\boldsymbol{\zeta}|$, $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_W^2=2\alpha|\boldsymbol{\zeta}|$. **(2) Real data**: a stated-preference survey on mobility-on-demand in New York City (Bansal and Daziano, 2018; Liu et al., 2018), 1507 respondents, seven pivot-efficient choice situations each, three labelled alternatives (Uber, UberPool, current mode) described by six attributes (OVTT, IVTT, trip cost, parking cost, powertrain, automation level). Training uses $T\in\{4,6\}$ with ten random splits.

## Key findings
- **No conditional-prediction gain (simulation).** The Brier-score difference between inter-intra mixed logit and standard mixed logit is at most $0.003$ in every scenario. For $T=20$ the inter-intra MCMC conditional Brier score is $0.149$ at both $\alpha=0.3$ and $\alpha=0.7$ — the intra-individual variance share $\alpha$ does not affect conditional accuracy. Standard mixed logit clearly beats MNL on conditional prediction (e.g. $0.152$ vs $0.200$ at $T=20$, $\alpha=0.3$), and both mixed logits improve as $T$ grows, but adding the intra level adds nothing.
- **No gain (real data).** Same pattern: for $T=4$ standard mixed logit gives conditional Brier $0.154$ vs the inter-intra model's $0.151$; the difference is again negligible. Estimation-table inspection shows the extra level can even *attenuate* significance (e.g. the OVTT mean is significantly negative in standard mixed logit but indistinguishable from zero in the inter-intra model).
- **No unconditional gain over MNL.** Neither mixed logit improves *unconditional* out-of-sample accuracy over plain logit, consistent with prior literature (Cherchi and Cirillo 2010; Wang et al. 2021; Zhao et al. 2020).
- **Computational penalty ≥ 7×.** Inter-intra estimation costs roughly tenfold standard mixed logit in simulation (e.g. 285 s vs ~3000 s) and at least seven times overall. The double integration is the bottleneck.
- **MSL ≈ MCMC.** The two estimators are equivalent in prediction and parameter-recovery accuracy, with close-matching point estimates and credible/confidence intervals. Crucially, MSL with *analytical* gradients is faster than or comparable to MCMC — contradicting Becker et al. (2018), who used numerical gradients. $250\times250$ draws suffice ($500\times500$ adds nothing but ~4× cost).
- **Methodological correction.** The authors show prior favourable findings (Danaf et al. 2019; Xie et al. 2020) conflated *unconditional* inter-intra probabilities with *conditional* standard-mixed-logit probabilities, and used $P^{\text{chosen}}$ rather than a proper scoring rule, never benchmarking against standard mixed logit.

## Contribution
First clean, like-for-like benchmark establishing that the intra-individual heterogeneity level does not improve personalised prediction over standard mixed logit, with the correct conditional-vs-conditional comparison and a strictly proper scoring rule. It corrects the estimation folklore that MCMC dominates MSL for this model (true only with numerical gradients) and provides analytical MSL gradients (Appendix A.1) plus open-source code. The diagnosis — that nonstructural random heterogeneity is the wrong lever — motivates a survey of four structured alternatives from the recommender-systems and ML-econometrics frontier: collaborative filtering / matrix factorisation embedded in RUM (Athey et al. 2018; Donnelly et al. 2019), collaborative learning with time-varying parameters (Zhu et al. 2020), amortised variational inference with a neural inference network (Rodrigues 2020), and neural-network / regression-tree utility specifications (Sifringer et al. 2020; Wang et al. 2020; Kindo et al. 2016).

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are explicit about several open hooks:
- **Internal vs external validation.** All prediction scenarios are *internal* validation (same dataset). Whether the null result survives *external* validation — prediction across a different time period, region, or data-collection method (Parady et al. 2021) — is left open and flagged as instructive future work.
- **Reconceptualising the model as a general hierarchical model.** Rather than only decomposing variance into inter/intra terms, future work could treat the model as a Bayesian hierarchical model (Gelman and Hill 2006) with *less granular nesting structures* — e.g. nesting observational units within schools, districts, or graduation cohorts in revealed-preference panel data. This is presented as the model's most defensible future role.
- **AVI needs benchmarking.** Amortised variational inference performs well in initial experiments but "its performance needs to be benchmarked against other competing methods" — an explicit gap.
- **Within-individual prediction in ML models.** Neural-network and tree-based RUM models "have limited focus on improving within-individual predictions"; extending them to conditional/personalised prediction is named as the step needed to make them viable for online recommenders.
- **Integrating matrix-factorisation latent structure** into collaborative-learning and RUM utility equations is suggested as an improvement path.

## Connections
The model formulation builds directly on Hess and Rose (2009) and Hess and Train (2011), with the Gibbs sampler from Becker et al. (2018); the half-$t$ prior follows Huang and Wand (2013) and Akinc and Vandebroek (2018). The study is a direct, critical rejoinder to Danaf et al. (2019) and Xie et al. (2020), who advocated inter-intra mixed logit for recommender systems. The baseline panel mixed-logit estimator is Revelt and Train (1998); the foundational mixed-logit and conditional-recovery machinery is McFadden and Train (2000) and Train (2009). The unconditional-prediction null aligns with Cherchi and Cirillo (2010), Zhao et al. (2020), and Wang et al. (2021). The forward-looking alternatives connect to the ML-econometrics literature: matrix factorisation in RUM (Athey et al. 2018; Donnelly et al. 2019; Gopalan et al. 2013), collaborative learning (Zhu et al. 2020), amortised VI (Rodrigues 2020; cf. scalable VI in Bansal et al. 2020), and neural/tree utilities (Sifringer et al. 2020; Wang et al. 2020; Kindo et al. 2016). Within a prediction-vs-explanation framing, this note is a useful counterpoint for any project comparing the predictive value of structured versus unstructured heterogeneity in discrete choice.

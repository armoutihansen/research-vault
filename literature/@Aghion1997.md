> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper develops a theory of the allocation of formal authority (the right to decide) and real authority (the effective control over decisions) within organizations, and it illustrates how a formally integrated structure can accommodate various degrees of "real" integration. Real authority is determined by the structure of information, which in turn depends on the allocation of formal authority. An increase in an agent's real authority promotes initiative but results in a loss of control for the principal. After spelling out (some of) the main determinants of the delegation of formal authority within organizations, the paper examines a number of factors that increase the subordinates' real authority in a formally integrated structure: overload, lenient rules, urgency of decision, reputation, performance measurement, and multiplicity of superiors. Finally, the amount of communication in an organization is shown to depend on the allocation of formal authority. ## Summary
Aghion and Tirole build a tractable incomplete-contracting model distinguishing *formal* authority (the contractual right to decide) from *real* authority (effective control over decisions, conferred by information). A principal with formal authority can always overrule a subordinate, but an uninformed principal rationally rubber-stamps the subordinate's proposal, so the subordinate enjoys real authority. The central trade-off is between *initiative* (the agent's incentive to acquire information) and *loss of control* for the principal. Delegating formal authority, or factors that reduce the principal's monitoring (overload, urgency, multiple principals, reputation, better performance measurement), all shift real authority toward the subordinate, raising initiative at the cost of control.

## Research question
When and why should *formal* authority over a decision be delegated within an organization, and what structural and informational factors determine the *real* authority that subordinates exercise even when the principal formally retains the right to decide? How does the allocation of authority feed back on information acquisition and on communication between layers of a hierarchy?

## Method / identification
A two-player principal–agent model with incomplete contracts (à la Grossman–Hart 1986): projects cannot be described ex ante, so the contract allocates only a control right. The agent screens $n \ge 3$ a priori identical projects; project $k$ yields verifiable profit $B_k$ to the principal and private benefit $b_k$ to the agent. The principal's preferred project gives known profit $B$; the agent's gives known benefit $b$. Congruence parameters $\alpha,\beta \in (0,1]$ capture preference alignment: if the agent's preferred project is chosen the principal expects $\alpha B$, and conversely the agent expects $\beta b$. The agent is (for the baseline) infinitely risk-averse, so the wage is constant and authority is the only instrument. Information is acquired at private convex effort cost: the agent learns all payoffs with probability $e$ at cost $g_A(e)$; the principal with probability $E$ at cost $g_P(E)$, where $g_i(0)=0$, $g_i'(0)=0$, $g_i'(1)=\infty$. Information is hard (verifiable) or soft (a pure suggestion). The focus is the *simultaneous* timing (both investigate at once), with the sequential case used for the urgency result. Under P-formal authority the principal overrules when informed and noncongruent; under A-formal authority (delegation) the informed agent cannot be overruled. Equilibrium is the intersection of the two reaction curves, assumed unique and stable.

## Data
None — this is a pure theory paper. Empirical content is restricted to illustrative anecdotes (Chandler's histories of Du Pont, General Motors, Standard Oil; General Electric's "dual top management"; U-form to M-form transitions; matrix organizations; vicarious-liability law) used to motivate and interpret the comparative statics. The authors explicitly note that conclusive evidence would require structural estimation.

## Key findings
The reaction curves under P-formal authority are $(1-\alpha e)B = g_P'(E)$ and $(1-E)b = g_A'(e)$; the agent's curve being *downward sloping* (more principal monitoring stifles initiative) is the crucial feature distinguishing this initiative model from a monitoring/costly-state-verification model. Main results: (1) the basic *initiative vs. loss-of-control* trade-off — anything raising the principal's marginal cost of effort lowers $E$ (less control) but raises $e$ (more initiative). (2) Delegation raises agent initiative ($e^d > e$, $E > E^d$) because the agent cannot be overruled. (3) The optimal allocation absent incentive effects follows the *relative willingness to pay for authority*: delegate decision $k$ iff $B_k(1-\alpha_k)/b_k(1-\beta_k) < 1/\mu$ — i.e. delegate decisions unimportant to the principal, trusted to the agent ($\alpha_k$ high), important to the agent ($b_k$ high), or where the principal cannot help hurting the agent ($\beta_k$ low). (4) Communication: an independent agent always wants the principal informed, but a *subordinate* communicates only if $\beta > e$; with low congruence there is *more* communication under delegation, with high congruence centralization can encourage it. (5) Span of control: it is *always optimal for the firm to be in a situation of overload*, because overload is a credible commitment to reward initiative. (6) Performance measurement: a higher wage *raises* real authority (aligns the agent and lowers monitoring incentive). (7) Multiple principals, urgency (the principal rubber-stamps more, the more urgent the decision), and reputation for "intervening only when justified" each raise the subordinate's real authority. Contingent delegation and delegation to an intermediary with intermediate objectives are characterized as ways to obtain intermediate authority allocations.

## Contribution
The paper's stated theoretical contribution is the *two-way interaction* between authority and information: real authority is shaped by the information structure, which is itself endogenous to the allocation of formal authority. It bridges the moral-hazard and property-rights literatures (their two polar cases), formalizes Simon's and Weber's notion of authority, and provides a unified account of *which* decisions get delegated (formal authority) and *who really decides* (real authority). It also takes a first step toward endogenizing the limits of communication by departing from team theory to allow dissonant objectives, making communication strategic.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems: (a) endogenizing the *timing* of investigation (simultaneous vs. sequential) is left to future research; (b) extending to *multilayered* hierarchies and the "complex webs of authority relationships"; (c) the result that communication never hurts an independent agent is acknowledged as unlikely to be robust once the agent responds to monetary incentives (e.g. blackmail motives); (d) the agent's search *time* in the urgency model is taken as exogenous, and whether an agent with a noncongruent project would strategically *delay* a proposal is left open; (e) congruence parameters $\alpha,\beta$ are exogenous though the authors note many channels (recruiting, training, career design, contractual rules) that could endogenize them; (f) measurement of real authority is hard and left for empirical work. The paper bills itself as "only a first step."

## Connections
The model rests on the incomplete-contracting / property-rights framework of Grossman & Hart (1986) and Hart & Moore (1990), which it generalizes by separating real from formal authority. It formalizes the authority concept of Simon (1951) and the "rational/legal authority" of Weber (1968). The observation that better information can hurt the principal connects to Riordan (1990), Schmidt (1991), and Crémer (1995), and to Arrow (1975) on vertical integration and communication; it departs from the team-theory tradition by allowing dissonant objectives. The empirical motivation draws on Chandler (1962) on strategy and structure. The multiple-principals discussion points to Burkart, Gromb & Panunzi (1994); the M-form/initiative link is developed in the authors' companion Aghion & Tirole (1995); the complete-contract counterpart appears in Aghion & Tirole (1994) and Tirole (1994). The vicarious-liability application engages Shavell (1987), Ringleb & Wiggins (1990), and Strasser & Rodosevich (1993). The paper is foundational for the later delegation and authority literature (e.g. Dessein 2002 on communication and delegation; Alonso, Dessein & Matouschek on coordination and authority).

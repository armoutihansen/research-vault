> [!abstract] Abstract
> During the first half of the twentieth century, utility theory has been subjected to considerable criticism and refinement. The present paper is concerned with certain experimental evidence and theoretical arguments suggesting that utility theory, whether cardinal or ordinal, cannot reflect, even to an approximation, the existing preferences of individuals in many economic situations. The argument is based on the necessity of transitivity for utility, observed circularities of preferences, and a theoretical framework that predicts circularities in the presence of preferences based on numerous criteria.

## Summary

May mounts a descriptive critique of utility theory by attacking its load-bearing axiom, transitivity. He argues in four moves: (i) transitivity is *not* entailed by the empirical (choice-frequency) definition of preference; (ii) it *is* necessary for a utility representation, so testing transitivity tests the existence of utility; (iii) circular preferences are empirically common, including in his own pilot experiment on 62 students choosing among marriage partners; and (iv) intransitivity is the *natural* consequence of aggregating several transitive criteria, a result he obtains by recasting Arrow's impossibility theorem as a statement about a single individual balancing conflicting components. The upshot: neither cardinal nor ordinal utility suffices, and economics needs a more general theory grounded in empirical preference patterns.

## Research question

Is transitivity of preference a descriptively valid assumption for individual choice, and if not, what conditions generate intransitivity? Concretely: does the standard utility-theoretic apparatus (cardinal or ordinal) adequately describe how individuals actually choose, given evidence of preference cycles?

## Method / identification

The paper is primarily a formal/conceptual analysis with a small supporting experiment. May builds choice theory on the algebra of binary relations. Preference is given an empirical, relative-frequency reference: writing $p(x\mid x,y,E)$ for the probability that $x$ is chosen from $\{x,y\}$ under conditions $E$, he defines $yPx$, $xIy$, or $xPy$ according as $p(x)<,=,>p(y)$. From this he derives that $P,I$ satisfy trichotomy, reflexivity and symmetry of $I$, and anti-symmetry of $P$ (his conditions (1)-(4)), but that transitivity (conditions (5)-(6)) does *not* follow, because the probabilities for different pairs are computed over different fundamental probability sets and so cannot be chained.

He then shows a utility function $\phi$ (cardinal or ordinal) forces transitivity: $xPy$ and $yPz$ give $\phi(x)>\phi(y)>\phi(z)$, hence $xPz$. Conversely, conditions (1)-(6) over a *finite* set imply a utility function, via transitive $I$ partitioning alternatives into ordered indifference classes; for infinite sets transitivity is necessary but not sufficient (order-type / cardinality obstructions, e.g. lexicographic orders).

The capstone is the aggregation model. Alternatives are vectors $X_i=(x_{i1},\dots,x_{im})$ ranked by $m\ge 3$ criteria, with $X_iP_jX_k$ iff $x_{ij}>x_{kj}$. A preference-aggregating function $Q=F(Q_1,\dots,Q_m)$ maps component patterns to an aggregate. Encoding each pattern by a skew-symmetric matrix $D(x,y)\in\{-1,0,1\}$ (value $-1,0,1$ as $yPx$, $xIy$, $xPy$), aggregation becomes a per-pair decision function $D=f(D_1,\dots,D_m)$. May imposes four weak conditions: **A** (defined and single-valued for all components), **B** (averaging/unanimity: $f(D_0,\dots,D_0)=D_0$), **C** (positive monotonicity: $D_i'\ge D_i$ for all $i$ implies $D'\ge D$), and **D** (non-dictatorship: no single component fully determines the aggregate). He then proves these, together with binariness (8), are essentially equivalent to Arrow's conditions 1-5 stripped of the transitivity requirement.

## Data

A pilot experiment: 62 college students choosing between pairs of three hypothetical marriage partners $x,y,z$, ranked $xyz$ in intelligence, $yzx$ in looks, $zxy$ in wealth. Subjects were never shown all three at once and pairs were relabelled with random letters. Choices were effectively deterministic (probabilities only 0 or 1). May also cites secondary evidence: M. M. Flood's RAND preference experiments (cycles of order four and six), the voting paradox, rat-preference experiments, and anecdotal combat/burning-plane cycles.

## Key findings

- **Transitivity is not implied by the empirical definition of preference.** Conditions (1)-(4) hold by construction; (5)-(6) must be established by observation, not assumed.
- **Necessity of transitivity for utility.** Any utility representation (cardinal or ordinal) implies transitive $P$ and $I$; hence transitivity is a *testable* necessary condition for utility's existence. On finite sets it is also sufficient; on infinite sets it is necessary but not sufficient.
- **Empirical circularity.** The marriage experiment yields a circular group pattern: $x$ beats $y$ (39:23), $y$ beats $z$ (57:5), $z$ beats $x$ (33:29). 17 of 62 individuals were individually circular ($xyzx$). Intransitivity is explained as "choose the alternative superior on two of three criteria."
- **Aggregation result.** Conditions A-D imply that some profile of transitive component orderings produces a *non-transitive* aggregate (a restatement of Arrow's General Possibility Theorem at the individual level): for $m\ge 3$ criteria, intransitivity is unavoidable unless aggregation is not non-negatively responsive (C fails) or one component dominates (D fails).

## Contribution

May reframes Arrow's social-choice impossibility theorem as a theory of the *individual* who must reconcile multiple conflicting criteria, thereby giving a principled, structural explanation for why individual preferences can be intransitive rather than treating cycles as error or irrationality. He gives one of the earliest explicit statements that the empirical/stochastic definition of preference does not entail transitivity, anticipating stochastic-choice theory, and articulates the program shift from "Are preferences transitive?" to "Under what conditions does transitivity fail?". The skew-symmetric tournament-matrix encoding of preference patterns is also methodologically influential.

## Limitations & open questions

- The pilot experiment is small ($n=62$), uses hypothetical (marriage-partner) stimuli, and was admittedly exploratory; May explicitly concedes it "does not prove that individual patterns are always intransitive."
- Condition A rules out stochastic aggregation; May flags that a "more general theory would replace Condition A" by letting each component profile determine a *probability distribution* over the aggregate - an explicit open direction.
- He leaves open the precise combinatorial structure of cycles, conjecturing (from enumeration of patterns on 4-5 alternatives) that no more than half the triples in any pattern can be circular, and that any pattern of four or more alternatives contains at least two transitive triples - stated as conjectures, not theorems.
- The broader call is for "a more general theory based on empirical knowledge of preference patterns," and the closing hope that observed patterns can always be "analyzed eventually into transitive components" is offered as plausible but unproven.

## Connections

The paper is built directly on Arrow's *Social Choice and Individual Values* (1951), reinterpreting Arrow's conditions and General Possibility Theorem for the single agent; it also extends May's own axiomatization of majority rule in May (1952). The relative-frequency definition of preference connects to Georgescu-Roegen (1936, 1950) and anticipates the stochastic-choice tradition later formalized by Luce (1959) and Block & Marschak; Marschak (1950) on measurable utility is engaged directly on the infinite-set representation issue. The multi-criteria account of cycles prefigures modern bounded-rationality and heuristic-choice models such as Tversky's (1969) "intransitivity of preferences" and additive-difference model, and rationalization-by-multiple-rationales work like [[@Manzini2007|Manzini & Mariotti (2007)]] and [[@Kalai2002|Kalai, Rubinstein & Spiegler (2002)]]. Debreu's lexicographic counterexample to utility representability is cited, linking to Debreu (1954) on representation theorems. The empirical-circularity discussion connects to Flood's RAND experiments and to McCulloch's "heterarchy of values" in neural nets.

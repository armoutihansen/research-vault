> [!abstract] Abstract
> In recent studies of channel competition, it has been found that channel intermediaries reduce the intensity of direct competition between manufacturers. The underlying channel structure in most studies consists of two manufacturers and two retailers each of whom sells only one manufacturer's product exclusively. This paper adds to this growing literature of channel competition by analyzing a channel structure with two competing manufacturers and one intermediary (a common retailer) that sells both manufacturers' products. Unlike some exclusive dealers or retail outlets of a manufacturer, however, a common retailer is often a powerful player in the market. This paper studies three noncooperative games of different power structures between the two manufacturers and the retailer, i.e., two Stackelberg and one Nash games. It is shown that some of the results depend critically on the form of the demand function. With a linear demand function, a manufacturer is better off by maintaining exclusive dealers while a retailer has an incentive to deal with several producers. All channel members as well as consumers are better off when no one dominates the market. The common retailer benefits more than the manufacturers do from a symmetric decrease in the manufacturing cost. As products are less differentiated, all channel members' prices and profits increase: a counterintuitive result. When the demand function is nonlinear, however, an exclusive dealer channel provides higher profits to all than a common retailer channel given a power structure. As products are more differentiated, a manufacturer's profit decreases when a common retailer is used, but increases when an exclusive dealer is used. These results underscore the importance of choosing a correct demand function for a channel decision.

## Summary

Choi models a two-level distribution channel in which two competing manufacturers ($M_1, M_2$) sell differentiated brands through a single *common retailer* $R$ that carries both brands, departing from the dominant exclusive-dealer paradigm (one manufacturer, one dealer). He solves three noncooperative pricing games that differ only in who has channel power, under both a linear and a constant-elasticity demand function. The central message is methodological: many qualitative conclusions about channel structure, product differentiation, and welfare *reverse* when the demand function is changed, so demand specification is itself a high-stakes channel decision.

## Research question

How do equilibrium wholesale prices, retail prices, margins, and profits in a duopoly-manufacturer / common-retailer channel depend on (i) the *power structure* among channel members, (ii) the degree of *product differentiation*, and (iii) *manufacturing-cost* changes — and how do these results compare with the exclusive-dealer channels analyzed in prior literature? A recurring sub-question is whether conclusions are robust to the *functional form* of demand.

## Method / identification

Static, complete-information game theory; equilibria via first-order conditions with Hessian/Jacobian negative-definiteness checks. The channel has manufacturer profits $\Pi_{M_i}=(w_i-c_i)q_i$ and retailer profit $\Pi_R=\sum_i m_i q_i$ with retail margin $m_i=p_i-w_i$, where $w_i$ is the wholesale price, $p_i$ the retail price, and $c_i$ the manufacturer's variable cost. Three power structures are solved:

- **Manufacturer-Stackelberg (MS):** manufacturers lead, internalizing the retailer's reaction function; manufacturers play interchannel Nash against each other.
- **Vertical-Nash (VN):** all three players move simultaneously (a Nash equilibrium across the two channel levels, distinct from interchannel Nash).
- **Retailer-Stackelberg (RS):** the retailer leads, exploiting both manufacturers' reaction functions.

Two demand systems are used. The **linear** system is
$$q_i = a - b\,p_i + \gamma\,p_j, \quad i,j=1,2,\; j\neq i,$$
with $a>0$ and $b>\gamma>0$; the spread $b-\gamma$ is inversely related to substitutability (smaller spread = closer substitutes). This yields closed-form MS/VN/RS prices and profits (collected in the paper's Table 1). The **nonlinear** constant-elasticity system generalizes Shugan (1985):
$$q_i = a\,p_i^{-\beta}\,p_j^{\delta}, \quad i,j=1,2,\; i\neq j,$$
with $\beta>2$, $1\le\delta<(\beta-1)$, and $(\beta-1)(\beta-\delta-1)>1$; here channels are *strategically independent* (a channel's optimal price ignores the rival channel's price). Under this demand the common-retailer profit is non-concave — the retailer can drive profit to infinity by charging an infinite price as $p_j\to\infty$ — so Choi imposes a symmetry assumption; the VN game is then analytically tractable while the MS and RS Stackelberg games are solved by a numerical iterative procedure (from Choi, DeSarbo & Harker 1990).

## Data

None — purely theoretical. Numerical illustrations use stylized parameters: linear case $a=50$, $b=3$, $c_1=c_2=0$, $\gamma\in[0,2.9]$; nonlinear case $a=1$, $\beta=3$, $c_1=c_2=0.5$, $\delta\in[0.1,1.5]$.

## Key findings

Under **linear demand**: the retailer's reaction function passes only half a wholesale-price change to the retail price, so the intermediary buffers manufacturer competition. Wholesale prices rank $w^{RS}<w^{VN}<w^{MS}$; retail prices satisfy $p^{VN}<p^{MS}=p^{RS}$ (the two Stackelberg structures give identical retail prices and demands). Manufacturer profit ranks $\Pi^{RS}_M<\Pi^{VN}_M<\Pi^{MS}_M$, with retailer profit in reverse order. **Proposition 1**: industry-wide profit and consumer surplus are highest with *no* leader (VN), yet each member individually has an incentive to become Stackelberg leader and capture a larger share of the constant-sum channel profit. **Proposition 2** (differentiation, linear demand): counterintuitively, all prices and profits *increase* as products become *less* differentiated, with prices diverging to infinity as products approach perfect substitutes — a flagged weakness of the symmetric linear form, which becomes strategically inelastic in the limit. Manufacturers prefer exclusive dealers while the retailer prefers to carry multiple brands.

Under **nonlinear (constant-elasticity) demand** many results flip: $\tilde w^{MS}<\tilde w^{RS}<\tilde w^{VN}$ and $\tilde p^{MS}\le\tilde p^{RS}<\tilde p^{VN}$, so prices are *highest* under VN. Profits satisfy $\tilde\Pi^{VN}\le\tilde\Pi^{MS},\tilde\Pi^{RS}$ for both manufacturer and retailer, i.e., everyone loses when no one dominates — and curiously a manufacturer's profit is largest when the *retailer* leads, the retailer's largest when *manufacturers* lead, so no party has a direct incentive to seek leadership. Greater differentiation now *raises* prices (desirable), but a manufacturer with a common retailer *loses* from more differentiation (the retailer exploits it) whereas an exclusive-dealer manufacturer gains. Exclusive dealerships dominate the common retailer for all members under nonlinear demand.

**Cost effects** (linear demand): a unilateral cost cut helps a manufacturer most when it is the leader; from a *symmetric* cost reduction the retailer benefits more than manufacturers (retail margins rise as wholesale prices fall because there is no retail-level competition), and even a competitively matched cost cut benefits all members and consumers via higher demand. Robust across both demands: Stackelberg structures are more stable, and retail prices are higher with a common retailer than with exclusive dealers.

## Contribution

Extends the channel-competition literature (McGuire & Staelin; Coughlan; Moorthy; Shugan & Jeuland) from exclusive dealers to a *common retailer* who carries competing brands, and adds the *Retailer-Stackelberg* power structure to model retail-giant dominance. It systematically demonstrates that conclusions about leadership, welfare, differentiation, and channel-structure preference hinge on whether demand is linear or constant-elasticity — a cautionary, specification-sensitivity result for applied channel modeling.

## Limitations & open questions

The paper is explicit about its restrictive assumptions and the resulting open problems: (1) only *one* retailer — no retail-level competition; the author names "a common retailer channel with a competitive retail market" as the clear next step. (2) The retailer's *brand-assortment* demand effect is ignored; incorporating it would favor the common-retailer structure. (3) Only *price* is strategic — extensions to advertising, product policy, and other marketing-mix variables are flagged as a major direction. (4) Transfer pricing as a coordination device (à la Jeuland & Shugan; McGuire & Staelin) is an unexplored extension. (5) The puzzle that under nonlinear demand no member has a direct incentive to lead is "left to future research." (6) Both demand functions are symmetric and the nonlinear one has constant elasticity over the whole price range; relaxing these may overturn results.

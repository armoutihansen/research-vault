## Summary
This theoretical book chapter (Chapter 9 of Loewenstein & Elster's *Choice Over Time*) argues that people derive utility not only from direct, present consumption but also from contemplating their own past (memory) and future (anticipation). Elster and Loewenstein develop a unified framework in which "nonprimary" experiences enter current welfare through two opposed channels: a *consumption effect* (reliving or pre-living an experience reproduces its hedonic sign) and a *contrast effect* (a past or future experience serves as a reference point that reverses its hedonic sign relative to the present). Applied backward, these effects describe memory; applied forward, they describe savoring and dread. The authors trace the rich implications of this "triple counting" of experience for sequencing, framing, lottery-and-insurance behavior, and especially for the interpretation of time discounting.

## Research question
How do memory and anticipation generate current utility, and what behavioral and welfare consequences follow once we recognize that a single experience can be counted three times—through anticipation, through direct consumption, and through recollection? In particular, can observed time discounting still be read as a measure of "pure" time preference once these backward and forward hedonic effects are present?

## Method / identification
The chapter is a piece of conceptual/theoretical economics drawing on psychology, philosophy, and intellectual history (Bentham, Hume, Leibniz, Jevons, Scitovsky, Tversky). The central analytical apparatus is a two-by-two typology of *primary* vs. *nonprimary* experiences crossed with two mechanisms:
- **Consumption effect**: contemplating a nonprimary experience produces emotion of the *same* hedonic sign, varying directly in intensity with the original experience (memory and anticipatory savoring are the backward and forward versions). This preserves hedonic sign.
- **Contrast effect**: a past or future experience acts as a reference point that *reverses* sign—a superlative past meal devalues a present mediocre one; a hoped-for future denigrates the present.

These are distinguished from neighboring constructs: Tversky's endowment effect (the contrast effect for memory), Solomon's opponent-process theory (an unconscious physiological aftereffect, not memory-mediated), Stigler–Becker "consumption capital," and Scitovsky's Leibnizian "law of hedonic contrast." A simple formal example with "mental externalities" closes the argument: a person allocating one unit of a consumption good over four periods, with current utility of a future utile = $\tfrac{1}{2}$ and memory-utility of a past utile = $\tfrac{1}{3}$, is shown to exhibit dynamically inconsistent consumption plans (planning to concentrate consumption in period 3, then revising to period 4), illustrating that intrapersonal hedonic spillovers act like internalities.

## Data
None—this is a theoretical synthesis. The authors marshal illustrative empirical findings from others (e.g., Kasl–Gore–Cobb on anticipated unemployment and illness; Loewenstein–Sicherman on preferences for increasing wage profiles; survey evidence that people prefer to eat a crummy, mediocre, then fancy meal in that order), but conduct no original estimation.

## Key findings
- A single experience can yield utility three times: by anticipation, by consumption, and by memory; but the qualitative manner differs because the past is fixed while the future is uncertain and malleable.
- Consumption and contrast effects are formally opposed; their net effect on current utility is generally indeterminate and varies by experience type and across individuals.
- Anticipatory emotions (savoring/dread) are largely *insensitive to probability* above a "threshold of conceivability," explaining the simultaneous purchase of lottery tickets ("buying a dream") and insurance ("peace of mind"), and paradoxical reframings (a 90% chance of a date becomes a 10% chance of being stood up).
- For sequencing: positive experiences should be carried forward via the consumption effect (to be remembered/savored), negative ones via the contrast effect (to lower future standards), generating a preference for improving sequences.
- **Central result for economics**: pure time preference cannot be read directly off observed behavior. Strong backward consumption effects can make a low-saver look impatient; strong forward effects can rationalize high saving. Discounting itself may partly *be* a forward contrast effect ("envy of our future selves"); absent it, Jevons-style, we might not discount at all.

## Contribution
The chapter provides one of the foundational conceptual frameworks for "utility from beliefs/contemplation" in behavioral economics, isolating savoring and dread as forward analogues of memory and recasting time discounting as partly an artifact of intertemporal hedonic spillovers ("internalities"). It draws an explicit isomorphism between interpersonal utility interdependence (sympathy vs. empathy) and intertemporal interdependence between selves.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicitly: (i) it is impossible in general to predict which of the opposed effects dominates in a specific situation; (ii) the determinants of savoring and dread, and of contrast-effect framing, are poorly understood; (iii) whether people actually *internalize* backward consumption effects when making present consumption decisions is questionable—much apparent investment in memory may be ex-post rationalization (Thaler's conjecture that people underweight memory); (iv) whether *deliberate* memory creation is even possible is dubious, given the "be spontaneous!" paradox—planning experiences for the sake of memory can undermine the spontaneity that gives them value; (v) the relative importance of intertemporal "sympathy" vs. "empathy" is seemingly indeterminate, and "pure" discounting may be effectively zero (Ramsey) once forward contrast is netted out.

> [!abstract] Abstract
> Although experimental studies have documented systematic decision errors, many leading scholars believe that experience, competition, and large stakes will reliably extinguish biases. We test for the presence of a fundamental bias, loss aversion, in a high-stakes context: professional golfers' performance on the PGA Tour. Golf provides a natural setting to test for loss aversion because golfers are rewarded for the total number of strokes they take during a tournament, yet each individual hole has a salient reference point, par. We analyze over 2.5 million putts using precise laser measurements and find evidence that even the best golfers—including Tiger Woods—show evidence of loss aversion.

## Summary
Pope and Schweitzer test whether loss aversion survives the conditions that skeptics (List, Levitt) argue extinguish biases—experience, competition, and high stakes—by studying PGA Tour putting. Although only a golfer's 72-hole total matters, each hole carries a salient reference point, par. The authors show that golfers make birdie putts (a "gain") roughly 2 percentage points less often than otherwise identical par putts (a "loss"), a gap that survives an extensive battery of controls and matching estimators, persists even for Tiger Woods, and costs the top 20 golfers an estimated $640,000 (17.6%) per year in tournament earnings.

## Research question
Does loss aversion—a core component of Prospect Theory—persist among highly experienced agents competing for large stakes in a real market, or is it, as critics claim, extinguished by experience, competition, and incentives? Secondarily: do reference points adapt to rational expectations as in Kőszegi–Rabin (2006)?

## Method / identification
A reference-dependent model treats each hole as the unit of narrow bracketing, with par as the reference point. A golfer who sinks a putt earns score $\Delta x$ (strokes relative to par); if he misses, he is assumed to make the follow-up putt for $\Delta x - 1$. Making a putt is a function of endogenous effort $e$ and observable putt characteristics $z$:
$$\Pr(\text{make putt}) = f(e,z) + \epsilon,$$
with $f'_e > 0$ and $f''_e < 0$. Utility for a putt is
$$U = \big(f(e,z)+\epsilon\big)V(\Delta x) + \big(1 - f(e,z) - \epsilon\big)V(\Delta x - 1) - \text{cost}(e),$$
with a kinked value function embedding loss aversion $\lambda > 1$: $V(\Delta x) = \Delta x$ if $\Delta x \ge 0$ and $V(\Delta x) = \lambda\,\Delta x$ if $\Delta x < 0$. The first-order condition $\text{cost}'(e) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial e}[V(\Delta x) - V(\Delta x - 1)]$ implies golfers exert higher optimal effort in the loss domain (par, bogey, double bogey) than in the gain domain (birdie, eagle). A second value function adds diminishing-sensitivity curvature parameters $\alpha, \beta < 1$ for gains and losses, generating an ordering of success probabilities (eagle < birdie; par > bogey > double bogey). Three testable predictions follow: (1) loss-domain putts are more accurate; (2) accuracy is monotone in distance-from-reference; (3) golfers are more risk averse (hit softer, leave short) in the gain domain.

Empirically, the main specification is a linear probability / logit model with a seventh-order distance polynomial:
$$\text{MakePutt}_{ijk} = \alpha_j + \delta_k + \beta(\text{ValueRelativeToPar}_{ijk}) + \gamma Z_{ijk} + \epsilon_{ijk},$$
with player and hole-in-round-tournament fixed effects (over 300,000 fixed effects in the richest spec). Identification compares birdie vs. par putts from the same position; residual variation after distance controls reflects idiosyncratic earlier-shot quality. Robustness adds controls for prior putts on the green (learning), 4/8/16 location "pie pieces" (green position), tournament score-if-made (standing), and a nonparametric matching estimator pairing par and birdie putts hit within 1–24 inches of each other on the same hole/round/tournament.

## Data
PGA Tour ShotLink laser-measurement data, 2004–2009, 239 tournaments. Lasers record $x,y,z$ ball-rest coordinates to within one inch on every shot. Restricting to eagle/birdie/par/bogey/double-bogey putts and players with ≥1,000 putts yields 2,525,161 putts by 421 golfers (47.0% par, 39.8% birdie). World Golf Rank (2007) and 2007 earnings are merged for the heterogeneity and cost analyses.

## Key findings
- **Prediction 1 (loss aversion):** Controlling for distance, birdie/eagle putts are made 2.0 pp less often than par/bogey/double-bogey putts ($t = 43.2$). In the richest OLS specification birdie putts are ~2.8–3.6 pp less accurate than matched par putts.
- **Prediction 2 (diminishing sensitivity):** Eagle putts are least likely to be made, then birdie; par > bogey, with double bogey slightly below par—consistent with curvature in both domains.
- **Robustness:** The gap survives player FE, learning controls (which cut the birdie coefficient 20–30%), hole FE (which strengthen it), fine green-location FE, and standing controls. The matching estimator finds par putts made 1.5–3.1 pp more often than birdie putts hit from near-identical spots (88.0% vs. 83.5% within one inch, $p<0.001$).
- **Round moderation:** The par–birdie gap declines monotonically from 3.8 pp (Round 1) to ~2.1 pp (Round 4), robust to selection (restricting to four-round players). This shows the bias is psychological, not mechanical, and that competing reference points (rivals' scores; "moving day") grow more salient late.
- **Prediction 3 (risk shift):** Conditional on missing, golfers hit birdie/eagle putts significantly less hard and leave them short more often than par putts—risk seeking in losses, risk averse in gains.
- **Endogenous reference points (Kőszegi–Rabin 2006):** Using average hole score as a rational-expectations reference, the par–birdie gap shrinks on difficult holes (2.9% → 2.3%) and bogey putts become relatively more accurate as hole difficulty rises—qualified support.
- **Heterogeneity:** 94% of players have negative birdie coefficients; loss aversion does not correlate with World Golf Rank ($p = 0.90$)—even Tiger Woods exhibits it.
- **Magnitude:** Hitting birdie putts as accurately as par putts would improve scores ~1 stroke per tournament, worth ~$640,000 (17.6%) annually to the top 20.

## Contribution
Provides field evidence that loss aversion—a canonical lab finding—persists among the most experienced agents facing intense competition and very large stakes, directly rebutting the List/Levitt critique that markets extinguish behavioral biases. Methodologically distinctive for enormous statistical power (2.5M observations), laser-precise controls, a clean exogenous reference point (par), and a matching design. Also one of the early field tests of expectations-based (Kőszegi–Rabin) reference-point adaptation.

## Limitations & open questions
- The authors caution that results in golf **cannot be directly generalized** to financial advising, real estate, or public policy—only suggestive.
- They cannot fully rule out that **effort/concentration are limited resources**: golfers may be unable to hit all putts with equal accuracy, so the "lost" earnings may not be fully recoverable.
- The conceptual framework **assumes golfers make the follow-up putt** after a miss (Δx − 1); this is empirically common but not automatic.
- They **cannot control the entire dynamic** of learning from the approach shot itself, since that process generates part of the identifying variation.
- Support for the **rational-expectations reference-point model is qualified**: results do not uniformly match Kőszegi–Rabin predictions (esp. for eagle putts and easy holes), partly due to thin eagle data and large standard errors.

## Connections
Builds directly on Kahneman and Tversky (1979) Prospect Theory and on narrow bracketing (Read, Loewenstein, and Rabin 1999; Benartzi and Thaler 1995). It is a field counterpoint to the market-discipline skepticism of List (2003, 2004) and Levitt and List (2008). The endogenous-reference-point analysis tests Kőszegi and Rabin (2006), joining the cabdriver labor-supply literature on reference dependence (Camerer et al. 1997; Farber 2005, 2008; Crawford and Meng 2008; Doran 2008) and other field loss-aversion studies (Genesove and Mayer 2001 housing; Odean 1998 disposition effect; Fehr and Goette 2007; Mas 2006). It rules out high-stakes "choking" accounts (Ariely et al. 2009; Beilock 2008) and overconfidence (Barber and Odean 2001), and connects to the golf-as-tournament economics literature (Ehrenberg and Bognanno 1990; Guryan, Kroft, and Notowidigdo 2009).

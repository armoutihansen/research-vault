> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper analyzes the trade-off between risk and incentives in the share contracts of the American whaling industry. Using a newly-collected panel of 5,378 individuals who sailed on whaling voyages from 1855-68, the response of sailors' compensation to an increase in risk is estimated. The risks used to identify this response resulted from the commerce-raiding naval vessels of the Confederacy during the Civil War. As the Confederate cruisers sailed primarily in the Atlantic, and therefore posed far less of a threat to whaling voyages to other oceans, a quasi-experimental approach, focussing on the differences between Atlantic voyages compared to others, is implemented. The results support the existence of a negative trade-off between risk and incentives in the industry's contracts. Moreover, evidence is found of selection among less risk-averse sailors and merchants into riskier voyages during the war.

## Summary
Hilt exploits a natural experiment in the nineteenth-century American whaling industry to test the canonical principal-agent prediction that compensation should trade off risk against incentives. During the Civil War, Confederate commerce-raiders threatened whalers' catch (but not their lives) overwhelmingly in the Atlantic, leaving Pacific and Indian Ocean voyages relatively safe. Using a difference-in-differences design on a hand-collected panel of whaling labor contracts, he finds that crews' output shares fell on Atlantic voyages during the war while their guaranteed compensation (advances) rose—exactly as theory predicts. He also documents that less risk-averse sailors and merchants selected into riskier voyages, which is the kind of confound that, when ignored, can explain the weak and contradictory risk-incentive evidence in the broader literature.

## Research question
Is there an empirically identifiable negative trade-off between risk and incentive intensity in compensation contracts, once one controls for the endogenous responses to risk—self-selection of principals and agents, and reconfiguration of the enterprise—that confound naive estimates? The whaling setting is used as a clean testbed because its share contracts closely match the linear contracts of moral-hazard theory.

## Method / identification
The theoretical scaffold is a one-period principal-agent model with negative-exponential utility for both a risk-averse merchant (principal, coefficient $\rho_p$) and a risk-averse sailor (agent, coefficient $\rho_a$), following Holmstrom and Milgrom (1987). Output is $y=e\gamma+\varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon\sim N(0,\sigma^2)$, effort cost $\frac{e^2}{2}$, and a linear contract $w=b_0+b_1 y$. The optimal share is
$$b_1=\frac{\gamma^2+\rho_p\sigma^2}{\gamma^2+(\rho_p+\rho_a)\sigma^2},$$
and the fixed component is $b_0=u+\frac{1}{2}b_1^2(\rho_a\sigma^2-\gamma^2)$. The familiar comparative static is $\frac{\partial b_1}{\partial\sigma^2}<0$ with $b_0$ rising in risk. Crucially, Hilt signs the omitted-variable biases when $\gamma,\rho_p,\rho_a$ co-move with $\sigma^2$: less risk-averse agents sorting into risky voyages ($\frac{\partial b_1}{\partial\rho_a}<0$) understates the trade-off; exit of risk-averse principals ($\frac{\partial b_1}{\partial\rho_p}>0$) overstates it; and risk-driven changes in $\gamma$ ($\frac{\partial b_1}{\partial\gamma}>0$) also overstate it.

Empirically the identification is a difference-in-differences exploiting that Confederate raiders concentrated in the Atlantic. The core specification regresses the share $b_{ijt}$ of crewmember $i$ under merchant $j$ in year $t$ on rank dummies, vessel/crew controls, and the interactions $\text{Atlantic}\times\text{prewar}$ and $\text{Atlantic}\times\text{war}$, with sailor effects $\nu_i$, merchant effects $\phi_j$, and year effects $\delta_t$. Comparing estimates with and without the sailor and merchant fixed effects lets him read off the direction and magnitude of the selection biases predicted by the model. The Atlantic risk differential is corroborated with marine war-risk insurance premia (peaking near 5% in 1863).

## Data
A newly assembled panel from manuscript "Whalemen's Shipping Papers" (customs districts of New Bedford, MA and New London, CT), 1855-68: 1,156 voyages (57% of all U.S. whaling voyages of the period), 168 merchants, 5,378 individuals in the managerial ranks (master, officers/mates, boatsteerers; ~7 per voyage), totaling 8,612 contract entries. Names, ranks, shares, and literacy were transcribed; New Bedford "Seamens Registers" supplied ages and physical descriptions to disambiguate individuals. Voyage characteristics (tonnage, rigging, itinerary, productivity, outcomes including capture) came from Starbuck (1878) and Lund (2001). A subsample of 1,004 advances from 187 New Bedford voyages was recovered from surviving accounting ledgers (advances were not recorded on the customs contracts). 32% of voyages went to the Atlantic; 3% overall (15%+ during 1861-64) were captured by Confederates.

## Key findings
1. Shares fell on Atlantic voyages during the war. The baseline $\text{Atlantic}\times\text{war}$ coefficient is about $-0.23$ percentage points of output (~8% of the mean share); adding sailor fixed effects enlarges it to $-0.29$, and the effect remains negative and significant ($\approx -0.16$ to $-0.17$) once merchant effects and vessel/crew controls are added. The relative drop was largest in 1863, the year of peak Atlantic war-risk insurance premia.
2. Advances rose. In the 1,004-observation advances sample, the $\text{Atlantic}\times\text{war}$ coefficient is positive ($\approx +20$ to $+28$ in 1855 dollars), confirming that the guaranteed component of pay increased as the incentive component fell—the joint movement predicted by theory.
3. Selection. The fact that the share effect grows when sailor effects are added is consistent with less risk-averse sailors sorting onto Atlantic voyages (a positive bias that masks the trade-off), while merchant turnover (only 37 of 168 merchants spanned all three periods) signals selection among principals operating in the opposite direction.
4. Robustness/falsification. Labor-market "slackness" (desertions before departure) did not move differentially for the Atlantic; voyage productivity, sperm-oil specialization, and length were stable within-merchant; placebo treatments (small vessels, sperm-whaling voyages interacted with the war) yield null effects.

## Contribution
Provides unusually clean field evidence for the risk-incentive trade-off where most of the literature is "weak and contradictory." Its central methodological point is that ignoring the endogenous responses to risk—agent and principal self-selection, and enterprise reconfiguration—can bias estimates in either direction, offering a coherent explanation for the conflicting prior findings. The historical contribution is a rehabilitation of the whaling share system as an efficient response to moral hazard rather than mere "exploitation."

## Limitations & open questions
- The dataset covers only the managerial ranks (career whalemen); the author cautions that for lower-ranking unskilled seamen the "exploitation" characterization may be more apt—leaving the contractual response among ordinary hands unexamined.
- About 5.3% of contracts omitted the share, and the master's share is complicated by separate vessel-ownership stakes; advances survive for only 23 merchants.
- Itineraries within an ocean cannot be precisely measured, so within-Atlantic route shifts are only indirectly ruled out via productivity.
- The model is a deliberately stylized one-period CARA-normal framework; the selection biases are signed under strong assumptions rather than structurally estimated, leaving room for a structural treatment that separately identifies $\rho_a$, $\rho_p$, and $\gamma$.

## Connections
The paper is a direct empirical response to [[@Prendergast2002a|Prendergast (2002)]] and Chiappori and Salanie (2003) on why the risk-incentive trade-off is hard to find. The selection-on-agents mechanism draws on [[@Ackerberg2002|Ackerberg and Botticini (2002)]] on endogenous matching; the comparative statics follow Holmstrom and Milgrom (1987), with scale effects on productivity referencing Baker and Hall (2004). On whaling economics it builds on and contrasts with Davis, Gallman and Gleiter (1997) (whose large sample excludes the Civil War era), Hohman (1928), Ellickson (1989), and Allen and Keay (2004). Hilt (2006) supplies the analysis of merchant-firm structure underlying the principal-selection argument.

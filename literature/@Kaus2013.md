> [!abstract] Abstract
> A century ago, Thorstein Veblen introduced socially contingent consumption into the economic literature. This paper complements the scarce empirical literature by testing his conjecture on South African household data and finds that Black and Coloured households spend relatively more on visible consumption than comparable White households. Following the approach of [[@Charles2009|Charles et al. (2009)]], this paper explores whether the differences in visible expenditures can be explained with a signaling model of status seeking. Moreover, it is assessed to which extent positional concerns motivate conspicuous consumption. Although the socially contingent share in visible consumption increases with income, different incentives to consume conspicuously seem to explain that, at every level of income, Black households spend relatively more on visible consumption than comparable White households. In contrast to the findings of [[@Charles2009|Charles et al. (2009)]] where differential spending on conspicuous consumption can be found also within each group separately, the model's core hypothesis fails to hold within the group of White South Africans.

## Summary

Kaus replicates and extends [[@Charles2009|Charles, Hurst & Roussanov (2009)]] — the U.S. study of racial gaps in "visible" consumption — to South Africa, a much poorer and more unequal setting. Using three pooled Income and Expenditure Surveys (1995, 2000, 2005), he documents that, holding permanent income and demographics fixed, Black and Coloured households spend roughly 30–50% more than comparable White households on a basket of conspicuous goods (clothing, footwear, personal care, jewelry, cars). The cross-group gap is largely absorbed once mean own-reference-group income is controlled for, consistent with a status-signaling model. But the model's consistency requirement — that the same negative relationship between reference-group income and visible spending hold *within* each group — fails for White households, suggesting different groups use different signaling environments and devices.

## Research question

Do the differences in conspicuous (visible) consumption spending across South African social groups reflect socially contingent status-seeking, as predicted by a signaling model — and does that model's core mechanism (visible spending falling in mean reference-group income) hold both across and within groups, as required by the assumption of common utility functions? Secondarily: how does the socially contingent share of visible spending change with income?

## Method / identification

The paper adopts the signaling framework of [[@Charles2009|Charles et al. (2009)]]. Individuals belong to reference groups with known income densities $f_k(y)$ on $[y^k_{\min}, y^k_{\max}]$; unobserved income is split between an observable and an unobservable good, and utility depends on both goods plus *status*, defined as society's expected value of the individual's income conditional on observed conspicuous spending and group membership. Two predictions follow: (i) conspicuous spending rises with own income; (ii) for fixed own income, conspicuous spending *falls* as mean group income rises (because a given income implies a lower relative position in a richer reference group, weakening the incentive to signal).

The empirical core is a log–log expenditure regression:
$$\ln(Vis_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Bl_i + \beta_2 Col_i + \gamma \ln(pInc_i) + \delta \, Dem_i + \theta \, Yr_i + \varepsilon_i,$$
where $Vis_i$ is visible spending, $Bl_i$ and $Col_i$ are Black/Coloured indicators (White omitted), $pInc_i$ is permanent income proxied by total expenditure, and $Dem_i$ holds area type, age, age squared, and family size. Because total expenditure is endogenous to its components, $\ln(pInc_i)$ is instrumented via 2SLS, using log current income as the preferred single instrument (passing Stock–Yogo weak-instrument thresholds), with education entered as a direct predictor. The status test adds mean own-group income at the province level:
$$\ln(Vis_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Bl_i + \beta_2 Col_i + \alpha \ln\!\left(Inc^{\mu}_{k,t}\right) + \gamma \ln(pInc_i) + \delta \, Dem_i + \theta \, Yr_i + \zeta_i,$$
where $Inc^{\mu}_{k,t}$ is mean income of one of 27 province-by-group cells in year $t$. Reference groups are defined by social affiliation × province (justified by persistently low interracial contact post-apartheid). The within-group test re-runs this specification separately for Black and White households (Coloureds omitted due to provincial concentration). Standard errors are clustered at the PSU level; Tobit is used for categories with many zeros.

## Data

Statistics South Africa Income and Expenditure Surveys (IES) for 1995, 2000, and 2005, pooled to 72,163 households (heads aged 18–100; Asians/Indians dropped for small samples; top 1% of each subgroup-year expenditure distribution trimmed). Items were recategorized to the UN COICOP structure; 2005 figures corrected for newly introduced imputed rent; the 2000 sample (which over-represents Blacks) reweighted to the 2001 census. All amounts in 2005 Rand. Robustness re-runs exclude the 2000 wave without material change.

## Key findings

- **Large unexplained gaps.** With income and demographic controls (specs 6a/6b), Black households spend about 50% more and Coloured about 35% more on visible goods than comparable Whites (≈0.50–0.54 and ≈0.33–0.35 log points). In absolute terms a comparable Black household spends ~10,966 Rand/year more. The income elasticity of visible consumption $\gamma \approx 1.32 > 1$, confirming visible goods are luxuries (prediction (i)).
- **Status mechanism absorbs the cross-group gap.** Adding mean group income (spec 7) drives the Black and Coloured coefficients to near zero and insignificance, while the coefficient on mean group income is significantly negative (≈ −0.29), consistent with prediction (ii).
- **Within-group failure.** Run separately, the mean-group-income coefficient is significantly negative for Blacks (≈ −0.26) but insignificant/positive for Whites (≈ 0.10). The common-utility assumption is therefore violated; missing income variation does not explain it.
- **Crowd-out.** Black/Coloured households spend less on health, housing/utilities, recreation, communication, and alcohol/tobacco, but more on food, education, transportation (public), and miscellaneous goods — echoing Charles et al.'s "spend less on the future" pattern.
- **Rising social contingency.** The socially contingent share of visible spending increases with income among Blacks. Including education in the visible basket leaves results qualitatively unchanged but does not improve fit.

## Contribution

First study to test Veblen's socially contingent consumption — and the Charles et al. signaling model — on consumer-expenditure microdata from a less affluent, high-inequality country. It both replicates the headline cross-group result and, by exposing the within-group failure for Whites, sharpens the externally relevant point that signaling *environments* (street vs. club/restaurant interactions) determine which goods serve as status devices, something the baseline model assumes away.

## Limitations & open questions

The author explicitly flags: (1) the signaling model should ideally *ex ante* incorporate the possibility that different groups express positional concerns through different devices/environments — a clear theoretical extension hook; (2) the "White signaling reluctancy" conjecture (being White is itself a powerful signal in post-apartheid South Africa) cannot be tested with the data; (3) the data cannot control for the number of children, clouding the positive Black education coefficient; (4) the Black negative alcohol/tobacco coefficient may reflect unrecorded home-brewed consumption rather than preferences; (5) reference groups are coarsely defined at the province level; (6) Coloured within-group estimates are infeasible due to provincial concentration; (7) IES 2000 representativeness concerns and the recall-to-diary methodology change introduce measurement caveats.

## Connections

The paper is a direct extension of [[@Charles2009|Charles, Hurst & Roussanov (2009)]] on U.S. racial differences in visible consumption, and builds on [[@Heffetz2011|Heffetz (2011)]] for the empirical measurement of good visibility. Its theoretical backbone is the status-signaling literature: Veblen (1899), Cole, Mailath & Postlewaite (1995), Corneo & Jeanne (1998), Glazer & Konrad (1996), and Ireland (1994). On conspicuous consumption as an externality with policy implications it cites Bagwell & Bernheim (1996), Frank (1985), and Duesenberry (1949). Related developing-country evidence on status spending includes Bloch, Rao & Desai (2004) on Indian weddings and Brown, Bulte & Zhang (2011) on rural China; relative-income and well-being links draw on Dynan & Ravina (2007) and Fafchamps & Shilpi (2008). Mason (1998) and Laumann & House (1970) ground the argument that social elites and newly affluent groups signal differently. The South African data and inequality context lean on van der Berg et al. (2008), Özler (2007), and Yu (2008).

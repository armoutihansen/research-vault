> [!abstract] Abstract
> Why do people vote? We design a field experiment to estimate a model of voting "because others will ask". The expectation of being asked motivates turnout if individuals derive pride from telling others that they voted, or feel shame from admitting that they did not vote, provided that lying is costly. In a door-to-door survey about election turnout, we experimentally vary (1) the informational content and use of a flyer pre-announcing the survey, (2) the duration and payment for the survey, and (3) the incentives to lie about past voting. The experimental results indicate significant social image concerns. For the 2010 Congressional election, we estimate a value of voting "to tell others" of about $15, contributing 2 percentage points to turnout. Finally, we evaluate a get-out-the-vote intervention in which we tell potential voters that we will ask if they voted.

## Summary
The paper builds a structural behavioral model of voting "to tell others": people turn out in part because they anticipate that neighbors, friends, and family will ask whether they voted, and they care about the social image conveyed by their answer (pride from saying "yes," shame from admitting "no") while facing a cost of lying. A door-to-door field experiment in suburban Chicago, tightly coupled to the model, identifies the structural parameters via three crossed randomizations (flyer content, survey pay/duration, and incentives to lie). The authors estimate stigma from not voting (about $11 disutility per ask) but essentially no pride in voting, a lying cost of roughly $7.6, and an overall value of voting "to tell others" of about $18 for voters and $13 for non-voters, contributing roughly 2 percentage points to turnout.

## Research question
Why do people vote, and what is the monetary value of the social-image component of voting? Specifically, how much do individuals value being able to tell others that they voted (versus admitting they did not), and how much does anticipated "asking" contribute to turnout? The paper also asks whether a get-out-the-vote (GOTV) intervention that merely raises the expectation of being asked can increase turnout.

## Method / identification
The core is a structural decision model. An individual votes iff
$$pV + g - c + N\,[\max(s_V, s_N - L) - \max(s_N, s_V - L)] \ge 0,$$
where the first three terms (pivotality $pV$, warm glow $g$, voting cost $-c$) collapse into a residual reason-to-vote $\varepsilon = pV + g - c$ with c.d.f. $H$, and the bracketed term is the net social-image gain from being asked once. $s_V$ and $s_N$ are the utilities of being seen as a voter / non-voter, $L \ge 0$ is the cost of lying, and $N$ is the expected number of times asked. Defining the net social-image value as a function $\Lambda(s_V - s_N, L)$ that equals $L$, $s_V - s_N$, or $-L$ depending on whether $s_V - s_N$ exceeds $L$, lies in $[-L, L]$, or falls below $-L$, voting requires $N\,\Lambda(s_V - s_N, L) + \varepsilon \ge 0$. Two special cases are "Pride in Voting" ($s_V > 0$) and "Stigma from Not Voting" ($s_N < 0$ and $s_V - L < 0$).

The model is embedded in a door-to-door survey choice problem: a respondent who sees a flyer chooses a home/door-opening probability $h^* = \max[\min[h_0 + \eta\max(s + m - c + z, -S), 1], 0]$ at cost $(h - h_0)^2 / 2\eta$, then decides whether to complete the survey given baseline survey value $s$, payment $m$, time cost $c = \tau v_s$, social pressure $S$ from refusing in person, and the image value $z$ ($z_v$ for voters, $z_{nv}$ for non-voters). Four propositions give testable signs: Pride implies voters sort in to the election flyer; Stigma implies non-voters sort out; lying is zero for voters and positive for non-voters absent incentives, with $\partial P(L)^v/\partial I \ge 0$ and $\partial P(L)^{nv}/\partial I \le 0$; and $\partial P(V)/\partial N \ge 0$ when $s_V - s_N > 0$ and $L > 0$.

Estimation uses a simulated minimum-distance estimator matching reduced-form moments (door-opening, survey completion, opt-out, and lying shares across treatments) to model predictions, explicitly modeling selection: individuals endogenously become voters or non-voters as a function of heterogeneous $(s_V, s_N, \varepsilon)$, so conditional expectations like $E^v[s_V] > \mu_V > E^{nv}[s_V]$. Identification rests on flyer sorting moments (mean image utilities), Information-vs-No-Information completion, the lying-incentive responses (heterogeneity $\sigma_{SI}$ and lying cost $L$), the baseline turnout rate (0.60, pinning $\mu_\varepsilon$), and the relative variances $\sigma_\varepsilon^2$ vs $\sigma_{SI}^2$ that tie the voter and non-voter parameters together. A separate GOTV field experiment (2010, 2012) tests the $N$-channel via OLS/linear-probability turnout regressions.

## Data
A natural field experiment run July–November 2011 in 15 towns around Chicago, using Cook County voting records. The sample is restricted to households with homogeneous 2010 turnout (all registered members voted, or none did); mixed households are excluded. 14,475 households were reached, 13,197 after exclusions; the analysis covers 60 crossed treatment cells with 50 surveyors. The turnout question concerns the 2010 congressional election; 1,738 people completed the survey. The GOTV arm covers 31,306 individuals (Nov. 2010) and 93,805 (Nov. 2012). Survey evidence pins the average number of times asked (about five). Baseline turnout is 60% (2010) and 73% (2012).

## Key findings
On average there is no pride in voting: $\mu_V = -3.9$ (voters do not sort in to the election flyer), but strong stigma from not voting, $\mu_N = -11.3$ (non-voters sort out, dropping door-opening ~20% / 6pp under the election flyer). The average value of being seen as a voter rather than non-voter per ask is $\mu_V - \mu_N \approx \$7.4$, with large heterogeneity ($\sigma_{SI} = 9.5$), so about 34% of people actually take pride. The estimated lying cost is $7.6 (close to a $7 cheap-talk benchmark from Erat and Gneezy, 2012). An 8-minute incentive to claim non-voter status cuts non-voter lying by 12pp while barely raising voter lying. Combining per-ask image values with ~5 asks yields a total value of voting "to tell others" of about $18 (voters) and $13 (non-voters), worth ~2pp of turnout. A party split shows Republican voters do sort in (pride), consistent with the 2010 results favoring Republicans. The GOTV "we will ask" flyer raised 2010 turnout by ~1.3pp (one-sided $p=0.06$) but only ~0.1pp in 2012; the model predicts ~0.3pp, within both confidence intervals.

## Contribution
Provides among the first credible monetary estimates of the private value of voting, achieved by using monetary and time inducements (survey pay, duration, lying incentives) to "price out" social-image motives. It bridges the theoretical literatures on voting and on social image with the reduced-form GOTV field-experiment literature, and demonstrates a structural-behavioral methodology in which a simple model is tightly coupled to an experimental design. It also delivers a model-based welfare evaluation of a GOTV intervention, showing the disutility imposed on respondents can dwarf administrative cost. The pricing-out design generalizes to other settings (contingent valuation, sensitive-attitude surveys) where responses carry signaling motives.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems: (1) results are specific to one election, place, and time (2010 Illinois congressional), and the absence of pride may reflect Democratic disappointment that year — applying the methodology to other elections (varying turnout, closeness, importance) is left to future work. (2) The estimate likely understates the true value, since it is based on being asked once by a previously unknown surveyor, whereas real asks come from friends/family in repeated interactions; and the linear-in-$N$ assumption (rather than concave) is untested and may bias the value downward. (3) Identification cannot cleanly separate large from very large $\sigma_\varepsilon$; the lying cost is tightly linked to the assumed $\sigma_\varepsilon$. (4) An alternative interpretation — that non-voters dislike talking about politics rather than feeling stigma — cannot be fully ruled out for the flyer-sorting moments, though it does not explain the lying patterns; allowing differential tastes loses one image parameter. (5) The signaling micro-foundation for $s_V, s_N$ is reduced-form here; estimating the underlying signaling game is left open. (6) The GOTV results are imprecise and consistent with but do not sharply confirm the model.

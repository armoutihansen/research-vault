## Summary
Asch reports a now-classic series of laboratory experiments on social conformity. A naive subject is placed in a group of confederates who unanimously give obviously wrong answers on a simple, unambiguous perceptual task (matching the length of a line). Despite the objective clarity of the correct answer, a substantial share of subjects yield to the erroneous majority. By systematically varying majority size, unanimity, and the presence of a dissenting "partner," Asch isolates what drives conformity, showing that the unanimity of the majority matters far more than its sheer size, and that even a single ally dramatically restores independent judgment.

## Research question
How strongly do the opinions of others shape an individual's own judgments, even on matters of plain fact? Specifically, will people repudiate the clear evidence of their own senses in order to agree with a unanimous and arbitrary majority of their peers, and what situational features (majority size, unanimity, presence of a supporter) govern the degree of such conformity?

## Method / identification
A controlled group experiment in visual perception. Groups of seven to nine male college students are shown two large white cards: a "standard" card with one vertical line, and a "comparison" card with three lines of differing lengths, one of which exactly matches the standard. Subjects announce aloud, in fixed seating order, which comparison line matches. The discrimination is easy: control subjects matching the lines alone err less than 1% of the time, and comparison lines differ from the standard by three quarters of an inch to one and three quarters inches.

All but one group member are confederates coached in advance to give unanimous wrong answers on prearranged "critical" trials. The single naive subject is seated near the end so he hears the majority before responding. Across 18 trials the majority responds erroneously on 12. The identification strategy is the contrast between the near-zero error rate under no pressure and the error rate under group pressure, attributing the difference to social influence. Asch then runs treatment variations: (i) varying opposition size from 1 to 15; (ii) breaking unanimity by inserting a "truthful partner" who answers correctly, or an "extremist" dissenter who picks the worst line; (iii) having the partner "desert" to the majority or simply leave the room partway through; and (iv) post-experiment interviews to probe subjects' reasoning.

## Data
Primary series: 123 naive subjects drawn from three institutions of higher learning. Outcome measures are the proportion of critical trials on which subjects accepted the majority's wrong answer, plus qualitative interview reports. No econometric model; results are descriptive percentages and error curves.

## Key findings
- Under unanimous group pressure, naive subjects accepted the wrong majority judgment on 36.8% of critical selections, versus under 1% error in control conditions.
- Wide individual heterogeneity: about one quarter were completely independent and never conformed; at the other extreme some conformed nearly always. Behavior was highly consistent within individuals across trials.
- Majority size matters only up to a point: error rose from 3.6% (one opponent) to 13.6% (two) to 31.8% (three), then plateaued; with four to fifteen opponents error stayed near 31-37%.
- Unanimity is decisive. A single truthful partner cut errors to roughly one quarter of the unanimous-majority level (subjects independent in 18 of 27 cases). An "extremist" dissenter freed subjects even more, dropping errors to about 9%; dissent per se increased independence.
- The partner's effect partly outlasted his presence: if the partner merely left the room, errors rose somewhat; if he "deserted" to the majority, errors rose more sharply, suggesting a specific cost of desertion.
- Interview themes: independents recovered from doubt and re-established equilibrium; some yielders concluded "I am wrong, they are right," some yielded "not to spoil your results," and some construed their deviation as a personal deficiency to be hidden. All yielding subjects underestimated how often they had conformed.

## Contribution
Establishes experimentally that social pressure can override the direct evidence of the senses on an unambiguous task, providing one of the foundational empirical demonstrations of conformity in social psychology. Crucially, it moves beyond the prior "suggestibility" tradition by separating the effect of majority size from the effect of unanimity, demonstrating that a lone ally or any break in consensus sharply restores independent judgment. This reframes conformity as a contingent situational phenomenon rather than uniform submission to prestige or numbers.

## Limitations & open questions
Asch is explicit that the study answers a few simple questions and raises many open ones:
- Whether independence or conformity is consistent across situations differing in content and structure, and if so how it relates to character and personality.
- How independence relates to sociological or cultural conditions, and whether leaders are more independent or merely "adept at following their followers."
- The sources of the striking individual differences "have not yet been investigated in detail."
- Why even at a seven-inch line discrepancy some subjects still yielded, the attempt to find a discrepancy so glaring that all subjects would repudiate the majority "regretfully did not succeed."
- Sample is restricted to young male college students, limiting generalizability.

## Connections
The work descends from the late-19th-century suggestibility tradition Asch cites: Charcot's hypnosis studies, Bernheim and Liebault on hypnotic susceptibility, and Gabriel Tarde's "social man is a somnambulist," along with Thorndike's reports on shifting aesthetic preferences by majority opinion. It is the canonical complement to Sherif's earlier autokinetic-effect conformity studies (which used ambiguous stimuli, whereas Asch deliberately used unambiguous ones), and it set the stage for Milgram's obedience experiments and Deutsch and Gerard's (1955) distinction between normative and informational social influence. In economics the paper anticipates later work on herding and information cascades (Banerjee 1992; Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer & Welch 1992) and on conformity and social pressure in decision-making, themes relevant to social-preference and peer-effect modeling.

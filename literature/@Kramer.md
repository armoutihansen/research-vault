> [!abstract] Abstract
> (no abstract in Zotero) This M.Sc. thesis (Univ. Bonn, supervisor Matthias Kräkel) asks whether individuals are concerned not only about appearing extrinsically motivated but also about appearing image-motivated when working on a prosocial task, and how such a concern reshapes the classic crowding-out of image motivation by monetary incentives. It extends the Bénabou–[[@Benabou2006|Tirole (2006)]] signaling model (in Sliwka's 2020 formulation) and proposes an extension of [[@Ariely2009|Ariely, Bracha & Meier (2009)]]'s click-task experiment with a BDM willingness-to-accept elicitation.

## Summary
The thesis combines a theory contribution and an experimental design. On the theory side it augments the Bénabou–Tirole/Sliwka signaling model of prosocial effort with a second-order reputational motive: agents dislike being seen as image-motivated, not only as money-motivated. This adds a countervailing term to the standard crowding-out effect, so monetary incentives can partly fail to crowd out effort under observation. On the empirical side it proposes (but does not run) a within-subject extension of [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]] that adds a BDM-elicited willingness-to-accept (WTA) for public disclosure of one's private effort, intended to detect the concern about appearing image-motivated. Reported results are the original [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]] findings plus the author's expected results for the extension.

## Research question
Are individuals concerned about being perceived as image-motivated (not merely as extrinsically motivated) when working on a prosocial task, and if so, how does this concern modulate the previously documented crowding-out of image motivation by monetary incentives?

## Method / identification
The baseline is a principal–agent signaling model (Bénabou & [[@Benabou2006|Tirole 2006]], as formulated by [[@Sliwka2020|Sliwka 2020]]). A risk-neutral principal offers base wage $a$ and performance pay $\beta>0$; a risk-averse agent chooses unobservable effort $e$ at quadratic cost $C(e)=\frac{c}{2}e^2$. The agent has three private/known traits: intrinsic motivation $v_e\sim N(\mu_{v_e},\sigma_{v_e}^2)$, money preference $v_m\sim N(\mu_{v_m},\sigma_{v_m}^2)$, and image concern $v_i$ (desire to appear intrinsically motivated). With $\mathrm{Cov}(v_m,v_e)=0$, the agent maximizes
$$\max_e\; a+(\beta v_m+v_e)e+v_i\,E(v_e\mid e)-\tfrac{c}{2}e^2.$$
Observers infer the intrinsic type from effort via the conditional expectation $E(v_e\mid e)$; because $\beta v_m$ also raises $e$, higher $\beta$ dilutes effort's signal of intrinsic motivation, generating the crowding-out of image motivation.

The extension adds a concern about being perceived as image-motivated, scaled by observability $x\in[0,1)$ (perfect observation $x=1$ is treated as infeasible). The inferred image motivation $E(v_i\mid e)$ is increasing in $e$ and decreasing in $\beta$; the agent now also wants to suppress the signal that she is image-driven, captured by an additional second-order concern $v^*$ with sensitivity $\delta_1$ alongside the image-signal sensitivity $\lambda_1$. The interior effort solution becomes
$$e^*=\frac{\beta\mu_{v_m}+\mu_{v_e}}{c}+x\!\left(\frac{\mu_{v_i}\sigma_{v_e}^2}{\beta^2\sigma_{v_m}^2+\sigma_{v_e}^2+x^2\lambda_1^2\sigma_{v_i}^2}-\frac{c\,x\,\sigma_{v_e}^2\sigma_{v_i}^2\,v^*}{(\beta^2\sigma_{v_m}^2+\sigma_{v_e}^2+x^2\lambda_1^2\sigma_{v_i}^2)^2}\right),$$
with the conditional-expectation sensitivities $\lambda_1=\frac{c\sigma_{v_e}^2}{\beta^2\sigma_{v_m}^2+\sigma_{v_e}^2+x^2\lambda_1^2\sigma_{v_i}^2}$ and $\delta_1=\frac{c^2x\sigma_{v_e}^2\sigma_{v_i}^2}{(\beta^2\sigma_{v_m}^2+\sigma_{v_e}^2+x^2\lambda_1^2\sigma_{v_i}^2)^2}$ characterized implicitly. The first bracketed term is the standard image-motivation boost (eroded by $\beta$); the second, new term subtracts effort due to the concern about appearing image-motivated, and is itself attenuated by higher $\beta$ — so it works against the crowding-out effect.

The experimental design replicates [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]]'s key-pressing donation task (pairs of clicks raising money for a charity, the "good" cause = Red Cross) under crossed visibility (public/private) and payment (with/without private monetary incentives) conditions, but moves to a within-subject visibility design and adds a Becker–DeGroot–Marschak (1964) WTA elicitation for publicly disclosing one's private-vs-public effort comparison. The design is proposed, with a calibration pilot and a high-powered control treatment to rule out a physical key-pressing ceiling; no new data were collected.

## Data
None collected by the author. The reported empirical numbers are the original [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]] results for the "good" cause; the thesis otherwise reports only expected results for its proposed extension.

## Key findings
- Theory (extended model): a concern about being perceived as image-motivated introduces a countervailing term that partially offsets the standard crowding-out of image motivation by performance pay. Both the image effect and the counter-effect grow with observability $x$, but the counter-effect scales with $x^2<x$, so it is weaker. Crucially, this does not make money effective in public — it only moderates the crowding-out.
- Replicated/baseline result ([[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. 2009]]): without monetary incentives, effort is higher in public (≈900 click-pairs) than private (≈517), supporting the Image-Motivation Hypothesis. Introducing private money raises private effort (517→737) but slightly reduces public effort (900→814), supporting the Effectiveness Hypothesis (incentives are less effective the more visible the act).
- Expected extension result: subjects are predicted to report a significantly higher WTA for public disclosure when there are no monetary incentives than when incentives are present — interpreted as evidence of a concern about appearing image-motivated (the Image-Concerns Hypothesis).

## Contribution
Adds a second-order reputational motive — aversion to appearing image-motivated — to the canonical Bénabou–Tirole prosocial-signaling model, showing analytically that it dampens monetary crowding-out. Proposes a WTA-for-disclosure instrument to identify this motive empirically and a cross-cultural (German) replication-plus-extension of [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]].

## Limitations & open questions
- All motive covariances are assumed zero ($\mathrm{Cov}(v_e,v_m)=\mathrm{Cov}(v_e,v_i)=\mathrm{Cov}(v_m,v_i)=0$); the author notes a positive $v_i$–$v_m$ link (status-seekers) or negative link (morally driven agents) is plausible for some people.
- No data: the experiment is only designed; replication and the WTA test remain to be run.
- BDM/WTA concerns: incentive-compatibility only within the calibrated bid interval ($[0.1,10]$ EUR ceiling), anchoring on the price distribution ([[@Tversky1974|Tversky–Kahneman 1974]]; Vassilopoulos et al. 2018), and the WTA–WTP gap / endowment effect ([[@Tversky1990|Kahneman et al. 1990]] vs. Plott & Zeiler 2005). No market price exists for "information about one's motivation," sharpening the anchoring worry.
- Cross-cultural generalizability: predictions are for German subjects; e.g. Lacetera & Macis (2010) find Italian blood donors respond to very non-subtle public symbolic rewards.
- Author flags social norms (Krupka & Weber 2013) as a promising lens on the crowding-out phenomenon for future work; and that effective signaling should be subtle yet sincere (Sezer et al. 2018; Nguyen et al. 2008).
- (Reader's marginal notes flag several underargued claims, e.g. why the no-incentive vs. with-incentive WTA comparison is needed rather than the absolute no-incentive WTA, what "private monetary incentives" means in the public treatment, and why an insignificant change under incentives is treated as uninformative.)

> [!abstract] Abstract
> The act of choosing can have particular relevance in maximizing behavior for at least two distinct reasons: (1) process significance (preferences may be sensitive to the choice process, including the identity of the chooser), and (2) decisional inescapability (choices may have to be made whether or not the judgemental process has been completed). The general approach of maximizing behavior can--appropriately formulated--accommodate both concerns, but the regularities of choice behavior assumed in standard models of rational choice will need significant modification. These differences have considerable relevance in studies of economic, social, and political behavior.

## Summary
Sen's Frisch Memorial Lecture argues that the *act of choosing* itself must occupy a central place in the theory of maximizing behavior. Two features motivate this: process significance (preferences can depend on *who* chooses and on the *menu* over which choice is made) and decisional inescapability (a chooser may have to act before reaching a complete ranking). Standard "rational choice" axiomsŌĆöWARP, SARP, contraction ($\alpha$) and expansion ($\gamma$) consistency, the sure-thing principleŌĆöare violated once choice acts are taken seriously, but Sen shows that *maximization* (picking an alternative not judged worse than any other) rather than *optimization* (picking a best element) can accommodate both chooser/menu dependence and incompleteness of preference, at the cost of revising the standard consistency requirements.

## Research question
How should the theory of rational, maximizing behavior be reformulated so that it can accommodate (a) the *intrinsic*, not merely instrumental, relevance of the choice actŌĆöchooser dependence and menu dependence of preferencesŌĆöand (b) the necessity of choosing under incomplete, possibly non-completable, preference rankings, while preserving an operationally useful maximizing framework?

## Method / identification
None empirical; this is axiomatic choice theory with motivating thought-experiments. Sen works with choice functions $C(S)$ over menus $S \subseteq X$ and a weak preference relation $R$ with asymmetric factor $P$. Two solution concepts are contrasted:
- Optimal set: $$B(S,R)=\{x\in S \mid \forall y\in S:\ xRy\}.$$
- Maximal set: $$M(S,R)=\{x\in S \mid \text{for no } y\in S:\ yPx\}.$$
Preferences are parameterized as $R^{j}_{i,S}$ŌĆöperson $i$'s ranking conditional on chooser $j$ and menu $S$ŌĆöformalizing *chooser dependence* (variation in $j$) and *menu dependence* (variation in $S$). Menu-independence of preference is defined as the existence of a global $R_X$ with $R_S = R_X\mid_S$; menu-independence of a *choice function* is defined via a canonical revealed-preference relation $R_C$ and binariness $C(S)=B(S,R_C)$. Section 6 introduces a *permissibility function* $K$ encoding self-imposed (e.g., Kantian/Smithian) constraints, with choice $C(S)=M(K(S),R)$, and asks when this can be mimicked by an "as if" preference $R_S$ or $R^{*}$. Game-theoretic relevance is illustrated through a "fruit-passing game" and the common knowledge of norms.

## Data
None ŌĆö this is a conceptual and axiomatic paper. Evidence is drawn from motivating examples (the garden-party chair, the mango/apple fruit basket, Dr. Chang's single dose of medicine, Frisch's two-cakes anecdote, voting, work ethics, Italian corruption, *karoshi*) rather than datasets.

## Key findings
- **Theorem 3.1:** Menu-independence of preference entails menu-independence of the generated choice function, but *not* conversely.
- **Theorem 3.2:** A choice function is binary iff it is menu-independent.
- **Theorem 3.3:** A complete choice function over a finite $X$ has a menu-independent revealed preference iff it satisfies Properties $\alpha$ and $\gamma$.
- **Theorem 5.1:** $B(S,R)\subseteq M(S,R)$, not generally conversely; divergence splits into Case 1 ($B$ empty, $M$ nonempty) and Case 2 (both nonempty with $M(S,R)\setminus B(S,R)\neq\varnothing$).
- **Theorem 5.2:** $M(S,R)$ is nonempty for every finite set iff $R$ is acyclic.
- **Theorem 5.3:** $B(S,R)=M(S,R)$ if either (I) $R$ is complete, or (II) $R$ is transitive and $B(S,R)$ is nonempty.
- **Theorem 5.4:** Every maximizing choice function can be replicated by an optimizing one via a devised $R^{+}$ with $B(S,R^{+})=M(S,R)$.
- **Theorem 5.5:** The converse failsŌĆönot every optimizing choice function can be represented as a maximizing one.
- **Theorem 5.6:** Theorems 5.1ŌĆō5.5 extend to menu-dependent preferences.
- **Theorems 6.1ŌĆō6.2:** Self-imposed constraints $K(S)$ can always be mimicked by a *menu-dependent* "as if" preference $R_S$ (6.1), but in general *not* by any menu-independent $R^{*}$ (6.2).

Substantively: chooser/menu dependence violates WARP, SARP, $\alpha$, $\gamma$ without implying irrationality or framing effects; expected-utility's sure-thing principle can be sensibly violated under fiduciary responsibility (Dr. Chang); and Simon's *satisficing* nests inside maximization.

## Contribution
Sen reorients rational-choice theory away from optimization toward the more permissive notion of *maximization*, showing it cleanly accommodates incomplete (and possibly assertively non-completable) preferences and the intrinsic relevance of the choice act. The directional asymmetry (maximization always representable as optimization, never reliably vice versa) plus its larger reach argues for maximization as the mainstay of rational choice. He sharply separates menu-independence of *preferences* from that of *choice functions*, ties the latter to binariness, and reconciles deontological (Kant, Smith) self-imposed constraints with formal choice theory via menu-dependent "as if" preferencesŌĆöwhile insisting the formal isomorphism does not erase the substantive sociological difference.

## Limitations & open questions
- The *as if* equivalences are formal only; Sen stresses that mimicking a Kantian/Smithian constraint or *karoshi*-style duty by a devised preference leaves "the sociology of the phenomenon" unexplainedŌĆöan open call to ground these constructions in social psychology and evolutionary processes.
- The distinction between *tentative* and *assertive* incompleteness is introduced but deliberately not pursued; characterizing choice and welfare under genuinely non-completable rankings remains open.
- Redefining alternatives comprehensively (e.g., outcome-cum-process) can trivially restore consistency axioms but robs them of operational content; the right *domain restrictions* limiting WARP/$\alpha$/$\gamma$ are left for further work.
- Integrating the value of *freedom/flexibility to choose* (KoopmansŌĆōKreps) with the value of the *process* of choice is flagged as ongoing (his then-unpublished Arrow Lectures).
- Empirical identification of chooser/menu dependence versus framing, and of strategic versus norm-driven "strategic nobility," is noted as practically important but hard to formalize fully.

## Connections
Builds directly on Sen's own revealed-preference and consistency work (Sen 1970a, 1971, 1993) and on Suzumura (1976, 1983); the binariness/$\alpha$-$\gamma$ apparatus connects to Hansson, Herzberger (1973), Plott (1973), and AizermanŌĆōMalishevski. The maximization-over-optimization stance engages Bourbaki and Debreu (1959) on set-theoretic maximality, and the incompleteness theme connects to Szpilrajn (1930), Arrow, and Levi (1986) on "hard choices." It departs from [[@Samuelson1938|Samuelson (1938)]] and Houthakker (1950) revealed-preference axioms and from Becker (1976)'s reduction of process concerns to a compound utility. The fiduciary/EU discussion (Dr. Chang) engages the sure-thing principle and Machina (1981) on non-EU; the menu-as-information and median-voter examples connect to Kreps (1979) preference-for-flexibility and GaertnerŌĆōXu (1995). The normative/deontological framing draws on Kant, Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments*, Rawls (1971), and Harsanyi (1976), while linking norm survival to evolutionary game theory (Axelrod, Binmore 1994, Weibull 1995). Together these make the paper a foundational reference for menu-dependent choice, reference-dependence/social-norm models, and the modern theory of incomplete preferences.

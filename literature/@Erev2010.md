> [!abstract] Abstract
> Erev, Ert, and Roth organized three choice prediction competitions focused on three related choice tasks: One shot decisions from description (decisions under risk), one shot decisions from experience, and repeated decisions from experience. Each competition was based on two experimental datasets: An estimation dataset, and a competition dataset. The studies that generated the two datasets used the same methods and subject pool, and examined decision problems randomly selected from the same distribution. After collecting the experimental data to be used for estimation, the organizers posted them on the Web, together with their fit with several baseline models, and challenged other researchers to compete to predict the results of the second (competition) set of experimental sessions. Fourteen teams responded to the challenge: The last seven authors of this paper are members of the winning teams. The results highlight the robustness of the difference between decisions from description and decisions from experience. The best predictions of decisions from descriptions were obtained with a stochastic variant of prospect theory assuming that the sensitivity to the weighted values decreases with the distance between the cumulative payoff functions. The best predictions of decisions from experience were obtained with models that assume reliance on small samples. Merits and limitations of the competition method are discussed. ## Summary
This paper reports three open "choice prediction competitions" run by Erev, Ert and Roth (EER) on binary choices between a sure payoff and a risky two-outcome gamble, under three information conditions: decisions from description, one-shot decisions from experience (sampling), and repeated decisions from experience (feedback). Each competition gave entrants an estimation dataset of 60 randomly drawn problems plus baseline-model fits, then judged submitted models by their out-of-sample fit to a separate competition dataset of 60 fresh problems drawn from the same distribution. The headline scientific result is the robust qualitative gap between description and experience: people behave as if they *overweight* rare events from description but *underweight* them from experience, and the best-predicting models differ in kind across the two regimes - stochastic cumulative prospect theory for description, small-sample/memory-retrieval models for experience. Methodologically the paper argues that generalization-criterion competitions defuse the "1-800 critique" and selection bias in quantitative modelling.

## Research question
Can leading behavioural decision models produce accurate, falsifiable *quantitative* predictions of choice proportions on randomly selected problems (not hand-picked counterexamples)? And do decisions from description versus decisions from experience differ merely in degree (same model, different parameters) or in kind (genuinely different models)?

## Method / identification
The unit task is a binary choice: a Safe prospect paying $M$ for sure versus a Risky prospect paying $H$ with probability $P_h$ and $L$ otherwise (with probability $1-P_h$). 60 problems per dataset were drawn by a fixed random algorithm (Appendix 1): $P_h$ sampled with equal probability from $(0.01\text{-}0.09)$, $(0.1\text{-}0.9)$, $(0.91\text{-}0.99)$; outcomes drawn so that $\approx 1/3$ of problems have rare high outcomes, $1/3$ rare low outcomes, and the domain is balanced across gain/loss/mixed. $M$ is centred on the expected value of the risky option, $M=\mathrm{Round}(H'P_h+L'(1-P_h))+\varepsilon$, so that risk attitude rather than EV drives choices.

The design uses the **generalization criterion methodology** (Busemeyer & Wang, 2000): entrants fit models to the estimation set, freeze parameters, submit a *computer program* mapping payoff distributions to a predicted risky-choice rate, and are scored only on the held-out competition set. Estimation sessions ran March 2008, competition sessions May 2008, predictions were due September 1 2008, and the organizers did not inspect competition results until September 2. 160 Technion students per study, 20 per cell after sub-grouping; in E-sampling subjects freely sampled two virtual card decks before one choice; in E-repeated subjects played 100 trials per problem with obtained-only (not forgone) feedback.

The scoring rule is **Mean Squared Distance (MSD)** between predicted and observed risky-choice proportions, transformed into the **Equivalent Number of Observations (ENO)** of Erev, Roth, Slonim & Barron (2007): $\mathrm{ENO}=S^2/(\mathrm{MSE}-S^2)$ where $S^2$ is pooled across-problem variance and MSE is the squared distance to individual (0/1) choices; ENO is the experiment size whose empirical mean would match the model's accuracy. The stochastic baseline for description is SCPT, in which $\Pr(R)=\frac{1}{1+e^{[WV(S)-WV(R)](m/D)}}$ with $D=|H-M|\pi(P_h)+|M-L|\pi(1-P_h)$ the distance between the CPT value distributions, so sensitivity falls as the prospects' weighted values move apart.

## Data
Two experimental datasets per condition (estimation + competition), each 60 problems answered by Technion students paid in Sheqels; aggregate R-rates and mean sample sizes are tabulated (Tables 1a, 1b). A separate "intuition" benchmark was collected from 32 Harvard students who played the competition problems for money and then predicted others' R-rates under a proper scoring rule.

## Key findings
- **Description vs. experience differ in kind, not degree.** R-rates rise with $P_h$ under experience but fall under description; with rare events ($P_h<0.2$ or $>0.8$) description and experience R-rates are strongly *negatively* correlated (e.g. $r=-0.74$ estimation, rare-event problems), while the two experience conditions correlate highly ($r\approx0.83$). No entrant used one parametric model across conditions; cross-condition transfer fails badly (SCPT, ENO $\approx 81$ in description, drops to ENO $2.34$ and $2.19$ in the two experience conditions - worse than predicting 50%).
- **Description winner:** Haruvy's linear-utility + logistic-choice regression, $T(R)=b_0+b_1H+b_2L+b_3M+g_1P_h+g_2\,\mathrm{EV}(R)+g_3\,\mathrm{Dummy1}$, $P(R)=1/(1+e^{-T(R)})$, ENO $56.4$. But the *best baseline*, stochastic CPT (SCPT), beat it (ENO $81$); the regression's logistic rule is too conservative (biased toward 50%) relative to SCPT's normalized rule.
- **E-sampling winner:** Herzog, Hau & Hertwig's **ensemble** of four equally-weighted rules (two natural-mean/primed-sampler variants, a stochastic CPT with reversed-S value function implying underweighting of rare events, and a stochastic priority heuristic with two search orders), ENO $25.9$, beating the primed-sampler-with-variability baseline (ENO $15.2$). Forecast-combination logic (low inter-model error correlation, positive bracketing rate) underlies its success.
- **E-repeated winner:** Stewart, West & Lebiere's **ACT-R** model with sequential dependencies and blended memory retrieval - activation $A_i=\ln\sum_k t_k^{-d}+\varepsilon(s)$, softmax retrieval $P_i=e^{A_i/t}/\sum_j e^{A_j/t}$ with $t=\sqrt{2}s$ - using near-default parameters ($s=0.35$, $t=-1.6$, $d=0.5$), ENO $32.5$. The *explorative sampler with recency* baseline did slightly better (ENO $47.2$); both embody high sensitivity to a small set of recent, context-similar experiences, generating a hot-stove effect.
- **Models beat human intuition decisively** (intuition median ENO only $1.88$; 97% of mean human predictions were conservative), and the hot-stove decline in R-rates is non-linear in $P_h$ (clear at high/low $P_h$, muted at medium $P_h$).

## Contribution
It supplies a rigorously held-out benchmark establishing that (i) the description-experience gap and the over-/under-weighting of rare events generalize beyond hand-picked problems to 120 randomly sampled gambles; (ii) accurate quantitative prediction of human choice is achievable and far exceeds lay intuition, answering the "1-800 critique"; and (iii) the competition format reduces author-favouring selection bias by separating fitting from prediction. It also seeds an influential genre of choice-prediction competitions and the ENO accuracy metric.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors are candid that the method only *partly* succeeded. The submitted (more creative) models beat the strong baselines in only one of three competitions, suggesting accumulated prior knowledge encoded in baselines is hard to beat. Predictive accuracy was bought at the expense of **simplicity** - the winning ensemble has four sub-models and many parameters and is "not easy to handle"; they explicitly call for future work to derive a simpler model retaining the ensemble's high ENO. They note the competition is "not a magical method" for discovering simple models and propose broadening it to wider task sets so that simpler, more general models are favoured over ones engineered to narrow conditions. Open problems flagged: building genuinely unified models (or understanding why none span description and experience), explaining the non-linear hot-stove/$P_h$ relationship, and testing whether stochastic small-sample reliance explains field phenomena such as gambling participation.

## Connections
The work sits at the intersection of prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979; Tversky & Kahneman, 1992) and the decisions-from-experience literature documenting underweighting of rare events (Barron & Erev, 2003; Hertwig, Barron, Weber & Erev, 2004; Hertwig & Erev, 2009). Its experience models draw on reinforcement learning (Erev & Barron, 2005), instance/sampling and natural-mean heuristics (Erev, Glozman & Hertwig, 2008; Hertwig & Pleskac, 2008), the priority heuristic (Brandstaetter, Gigerenzer & Hertwig, 2006), the hot-stove effect (Denrell & March, 2001), and ACT-R declarative memory (Anderson & Lebiere, 1998; Gonzalez et al., 2003). The generalization-criterion and ENO machinery come from Busemeyer & Wang (2000) and Erev, Roth, Slonim & Barron (2007); the competition framing connects to Turing tournaments (Arifovic, McKelvey & Pevnitskaya, 2006) and to market-design views of research incentives (Roth, 2008). This paper is the methodological forerunner of subsequent CPC competitions (e.g. CPC2015) on choice prediction.

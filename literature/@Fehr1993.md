> [!abstract] Abstract
> This paper reports the results of an experiment that was designed to test the impact of fairness on market prices. Prices were determined in a one-sided oral auction, with buyers as price-makers. Upon acceptance of an offer, sellers determined the quality of the good. Buyers offered prices that were substantially above the market-clearing level and expected sellers to respond with high quality levels. This expectation was, on average, confirmed by the behavior of sellers. These results provide, therefore, experimental support for the fair wage-effort theory of involuntary unemployment.

## Summary
Fehr, Kirchsteiger, and Riedl run a two-stage experimental market - a competitive one-sided oral auction followed by an unconstrained, unverifiable quality (effort) choice by the seller - to test whether fairness can sustain above-market-clearing prices. Despite a deliberate excess supply of sellers and standard theory's sharp prediction of minimum effort and market clearing, buyers paid prices more than 100% above the clearing level and sellers reciprocated with effort rising in the wage. Crucially, prices showed no convergence toward the competitive equilibrium over twelve repetitions. The results are read as experimental support for the Akerlof-Yellen fair wage-effort hypothesis of involuntary unemployment.

## Research question
Do fairness considerations - shown by psychologists in non-market settings - survive in a competitive market with repetition and excess supply, and can they prevent prices/wages from falling to the market-clearing level? Equivalently, is the Akerlof-Yellen fair wage-effort hypothesis behaviorally valid in a competitive experimental market?

## Method / identification
A two-stage game framed in goods-market terms (buyers/sellers, price/quality - never "wage" or "effort"). Stage 1 is a three-minute one-sided oral auction: employers (buyers) post wage bids in multiples of five; any worker (seller) can accept; an accepted bid concludes a binding contract; unaccepted bids may be raised but must exceed the standing high bid. Stage 2: the matched worker privately chooses effort $e \in \{0.1, 0.2, \ldots, 1.0\}$, revealed only to "their" employer, with no sanctions. Identities are hidden, parties sit in separate rooms communicating by telephone, and effort choices are coded - design choices that deliberately shut down group pressure, reputation, and partner selection so as to isolate one-shot fairness.

Payoffs use fixed parameters $v=126$, $c=26$, and an increasing effort-cost schedule $m(e)$ with $m(e_{\min})=0$. Worker $j$'s payoff:
$$u_j = p_j - c - m(e_j)$$
Employer $i$'s payoff:
$$\Pi_i = (v - p_i)\,e_i$$

The standard money-maximizing prediction: since effort is costly and unpunishable, rational employers expect $e=e_{\min}=0.1$, opportunity cost is $c=26$, and with excess supply and the multiple-of-five rule the competitive equilibrium wage is $30$ - the outcome $[p=30,\,e=0.1]$. The fair wage-effort hypothesis instead yields three testable claims: H1 effort increases in the wage; H2 average wages exceed the clearing wage $c+\tau$ and do not converge to it under repetition; H3 average effort exceeds $e_{\min}$ and does not converge. They define average relative overpayment $r = (p^o - c - \tau)/(v-c)$ and estimate effort regressions by OLS (and Tobit / log-linear robustness): the pooled $e = a + \beta p + \varepsilon$, a worker-fixed-effects version with dummies $d_i$, and a period-dummy version, using Wald tests for heterogeneity across workers and across periods. Efficiency is measured as $\phi = G_o/G_s$, the realized joint gain over the standard-prediction gain $G_s = e_{\min}(v-c-\tau)+\tau = 13.6$.

## Data
Experimental. Four sessions (Vienna, Oct 1991), deliberate excess supply: three sessions with nine workers/six employers, one with eight workers/five employers; twelve periods per session plus trial periods. 276 effort observations; 35 workers, 23 employers. Mostly computer-science/engineering students, plus some economics students; mean earnings AS 296 (~$25) per two-hour session. End-of-session questionnaires record stated motives.

## Key findings
- Average price was 72 versus a clearing wage of 30 - average relative overpayment $r=0.42$ (employers ceded 42% of surplus despite excess supply). Prices ranged 30 to 110.
- H1 (effort rises in wage): supported. Average effort was 0.4, four times the predicted $e_{\min}$; $e_{\min}$ chosen in only 16% of cases. The $\beta$ coefficient is positive and highly significant in every specification (pooled, per-session, last-period-only). Worker dummies (regression 6) roughly double the adjusted $R^2$, and Wald tests reject equality of worker intercepts at the 1% level - fairness notions differ across workers.
- H2 (no price convergence): supported. $r$ stays significantly above zero in all periods of all sessions with no downward trend.
- H3 (no effort convergence): supported. Average effort exceeds $e_{\min}$ in every period; period dummies (regression 7) are not significantly different from the intercept (Wald not rejected even at 60%) - behavior does not vary by timing.
- Efficiency: average $\phi=4.5$ (up to >6 in eight cases); reciprocity raised joint gains by several hundred percent. Employers' average profit was AS 18.9 versus the standard-prediction AS 9.6 - paying "fair" wages was profit-increasing, not profit-reducing.
- Reputation is ruled out as the driver: anonymity, no partner choice, coded effort, and a significant positive wage-effort relation even in the final period.

## Contribution
First experimental test of the "pure" fair wage-effort hypothesis inside a competitive market institution known to converge to equilibrium. It demonstrates that reciprocal fairness - sellers choosing high quality in response to high prices - persists under repetition, anonymity, and excess supply, providing a behavioral microfoundation for efficiency-wage explanations of involuntary unemployment and, more broadly, for gift-exchange in markets with non-contractible quality. The two-stage gift-exchange design became a workhorse paradigm for reciprocity research.

## Limitations & open questions
The authors flag several explicit open problems, each a project hook: (i) the design strips out durability of relationships - long-term interaction may strengthen social ties and reciprocity, but "little is known" about long-term effects in experimental markets; (ii) employers cannot incur losses, so the Darwinian "fairness driven out by competition" argument is not directly tested; (iii) the interaction between effort monitoring/punishment and fairness is unresolved - punishment may crowd out fairness, or fairness may instead govern the perceived legitimacy of punishment, with the theoretical possibility (Fehr 1991) that bonding and entrance fees fail to restore market clearing; (iv) whether fairness effects are stronger in labor than goods framing (the experiment used goods framing deliberately to bias against fairness).

## Connections
The paper operationalizes the fair wage-effort hypothesis of [[@Akerlof1982|Akerlof (1982)]] and Akerlof & Yellen (1988, 1990), and contrasts it with the shirking efficiency-wage model of Shapiro & Stiglitz (1984) and Bowles (1985). It builds on survey/field evidence of fairness norms from Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler (1986) and Blinder & Choi (1990), and on equity theory in psychology (Adams 1963; Homans 1961). The competitive-convergence benchmark draws on Smith (1964, 1976), Plott & Smith (1978), Walker & Williams (1988), and Plott (1989). It is a companion to Fehr, Kirchsteiger & Riedl (1992) on the shirking version, and connects to ultimatum-bargaining work by Fehr (1991) and Guth, Ockenfels & Wendel (1991). The gift-exchange paradigm here is foundational for later reciprocity and social-preference modeling (e.g., [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt 1999]]; [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels 2000]]) and for experimental labor-market research.

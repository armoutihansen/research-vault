> [!abstract] Abstract
> Beliefs are a central determinant of behavior. Recent models assume that beliefs about or the anticipation of future consumption have direct utility consequences. This gives rise to informational preferences, i.e., preferences over the timing and structure of information. Using a novel and purposefully simple set-up, we experimentally analyze preferences for information along four dimensions. We find evidence that the majority of subjects prefers receiving information sooner. This preference, however, is not uniform but depends on context. When the environment allows subjects to not focus attention on (negative) consumption events, later information becomes more attractive. We also identify an aversion towards piecemeal information. Variations in prior distributions do not seem to affect information preferences.

## Summary

This CESifo working paper provides clean experimental evidence on *non-instrumental* preferences for information, the behavioral signature of belief-based (anticipatory and news) utility. Falk and Zimmermann let lab subjects choose how to learn the outcome of a lottery that determines whether they will receive a real aversive consumption event (a series of 30 painful but harmless electric shocks). Because the information cannot change the outcome, demand for it must reflect direct utility from beliefs. Manipulating the information structure along four dimensions, they find: (1) a robust majority (76%) prefers information sooner; (2) this is overturned by attention — when a distracting quiz lets subjects look away from the threat, the sooner-fraction collapses to ~48%; (3) a strong aversion to piecemeal information (~90% choose clumped), beyond any preference for sooner news; and (4) no effect of the prior probability of the bad event. The findings empirically vindicate central mechanisms in Kőszegi and [[@Koszegi2009|Rabin (2009)]] (loss aversion in beliefs) and Golman and Loewenstein (2015) (attention management), while contradicting belief-manipulation ([[@Brunnermeier2005|Brunnermeier and Parker 2005]]), suspense (Ely et al. 2015), and prior-dependence (Epstein 2008).

## Research question

Do people have preferences over the *timing and structure* of non-instrumental information, as predicted by models in which beliefs about future consumption directly enter utility? Concretely, along four dimensions: (i) sooner vs. later resolution; (ii) whether the level of attention on the future event shifts the timing preference; (iii) clumped vs. piecemeal (gradual) revelation; and (iv) whether any of these preferences depend on the prior probability of the consumption event. Each dimension is designed as a discriminating test among competing belief-utility theories.

## Method / identification

Lab experiment, 376 subjects (BonnEconLab), 8 main + 5 control treatments, between-subjects. The aversive stimulus is 30 electric shocks delivered at random intervals over 4 minutes; lotteries are physically implemented with ten sealed envelopes (five red, five blue) from which the subject draws five. Treatments vary the prior of getting shocked: medium ($\pi\approx0.5$, "at least 3 red"), high ($>99\%$, "at least 1 red"), low ($<1\%$, "all 5 red"). At $t=0$ the subject chooses between two information structures; consumption occurs at $t=15$ min.

The paper formalizes the design in a 6-period framework. Consumption $c\in\{0,1\}$ is realized at $t=6$; an information structure $\sigma$ specifies $t(s_i\mid\sigma)$, the period in which each of five signals $s_i$ arrives, with all five jointly resolving uncertainty. Key structures: *sooner* $\sigma_S$ ($t(s_i\mid\sigma_S)=1\ \forall i$), *later* $\sigma_L$ ($t(s_i\mid\sigma_L)=5\ \forall i$), *clumped* $\sigma_C$ (all at $t=1$), and *piecemeal* $\sigma_P$ ($t(s_i\mid\sigma_P)=i$). Because piecemeal necessarily *also* delays, the design identifies a *specific* aversion to gradualness by differencing the CP choices against the SL benchmark.

Formal predictions are derived for five models (Appendix B). The flagship is Kőszegi and [[@Koszegi2009|Rabin (2009)]]: instantaneous utility in $t=1,\dots,5$ is gain-loss utility from belief revisions,
$$u_t=\gamma_t\,N(F_t\mid F_{t-1}),\qquad N(F_t\mid F_{t-1})=\mu(\pi_t-\pi_{t-1}),$$
with consumption utility $u_6=m(c_6)$ and a piecewise-linear $\mu(x)=\eta x$ for $x\ge0$, $\mu(x)=\eta\lambda x$ for $x<0$ ($\lambda>1$). Weights satisfy $0<\gamma_1\le\gamma_2\le\dots\le\gamma_5$ (recent news matters more), and the agent maximizes $EU_0=\sum_{\tau=1}^{6}u_\tau$. Loss aversion ($\lambda>1$) makes information ex-ante utility-decreasing and yields a weak preference for $\sigma_S\succeq\sigma_L$; sub-additivity of $\mu$ under splitting gives a *strict* $\sigma_C\succ\sigma_P$, e.g. $\gamma_4\mu(\pi_{4,5}-\pi_3)>\gamma_4\mu(\pi_{4,5}-\pi_4)+\gamma_4\mu(\pi_4-\pi_3)$. Identification of attention effects: treatments **AttMain** (a paid distracting general-knowledge quiz runs for 12 min after the choice, on a separate computer hiding the shock device) vs. **AttControl** (same 4-min quiz pre-choice, but no quiz afterward, so attention stays on the stimulus). The clean attention effect is the **AttMain − AttControl** contrast. To rule out a "stay focused on the quiz" confound, **AttMainMoney/AttControlMoney** replicate the quiz design but with a monetary lottery instead of shocks (difference-in-differences with an interaction term). Estimation is probit on the binary information choice with robust SEs; controls = age, gender, and screen-side ordering of options.

## Data

Original experimental data only — no external dataset. 376 lab participants; main treatments 29–32 subjects each (Table 1). Three controls characterize the stimulus: **ControlWTA** (mean willingness-to-accept = €8.3 to take the shocks), **ControlPerception** (mean unpleasantness 5.6/7), and **ControlCalibration** (verifies subjects do not under-report pain tolerance to soften shocks: 10.46 vs. 9.61, n.s.). Perceived-probability elicitation for a subset confirms priors were correctly understood.

## Key findings

- **Result 1 (Sooner vs. later):** 76% choose sooner information (pooled), >73% in each SL-treatment; binomial tests reject 50-50 ($p<0.01$). Consistent with Kőszegi-Rabin and Golman-Loewenstein; contradicts Brunnermeier-Parker (who predict $\sigma_L\succ\sigma_S$) and Ely et al. (indifference).
- **Result 2 (Attention management):** With the distraction available (AttMain) only ~48% prefer sooner, vs. 80% in AttControl — a large, significant drop (probit, $-0.93$ to $-0.97$, $p<0.01$). The placebo money treatments show *no* such effect (62% vs. 69% sooner), and the diff-in-diff interaction is significant: the effect is tied to anticipatory disutility of the shocks, not to wanting to stay on the quiz. Screen-ordering does not drive choices, so 48% reflects genuine heterogeneity, not pure indifference. This is the paper's headline support for Golman and Loewenstein (2015): later information is a tool to manage attention away from a dreaded event.
- **Result 3 (Aversion to piecemeal):** ~90% choose clumped (>87% in every CP-treatment, $p<0.01$). Crucially, the clumped-fraction *exceeds* the sooner-fraction (significant negative SL-dummy when pooling SL+CP, $-0.59$/$-0.62$, $p<0.05$), isolating a *distinct* dislike of gradual belief fluctuation — direct support for loss aversion in beliefs (Kőszegi-Rabin), and against the suspense-loving prediction of Ely et al.
- **Result 4 (No prior effect):** Neither SL nor CP choices vary with the prior (joint Wald tests far from significance; e.g. SL $\chi^2(2)=0.40,\,p=0.82$). Holds for perceived as well as objective priors. Contradicts the Epstein (2008) RDEU example.

## Contribution

To the authors' knowledge this is the first study to (a) empirically identify an aversion to *piecewise* information, and (b) show experimentally that *attention* shapes information demand. Methodologically, it pioneers using a real, controlled *non-monetary* consumption event (electric shocks) that plausibly triggers anticipatory utility — unlike prior money-based designs (e.g. Zimmermann 2015, who found no piecemeal aversion in the money domain). It tests five belief-utility theories in one unified framework, and links findings to applied phenomena: an aversion to piecemeal resolution offers a micro-foundation for *myopic loss aversion* ([[@Benartzi1995|Benartzi-Thaler 1995]]; Gneezy-Potters 1997), and attention-driven delay rationalizes *information avoidance* (e.g. people declining Huntington's tests; [[@Oster2013a|Oster et al. 2013]]).

## Limitations & open questions

- **Negative domain only.** All results concern an aversive event. The authors flag that extension to *positive* consumption is open: most model predictions are domain-symmetric (good vs. bad news exist either way), *except* the attention channel, and there is no validated positive lab stimulus known to trigger anticipatory utility.
- **External validity of the lab paradigm.** Whether shock-driven anticipation generalizes to real economic dread (job loss, diagnoses, market losses) is untested.
- **Mechanism for the ~50-50 split in AttMain** is interpreted via heterogeneity in $\gamma$-profiles but not structurally estimated; the relative strength of curiosity vs. attention-management is not pinned down.
- **Magnitude of news-utility loss aversion** ($\lambda$) and the $\gamma_t$ weights are not estimated — the tests are qualitative comparative statics, not structural recovery of preference parameters.
- **The myopic-loss-aversion link is conjectural**, an interpretive bridge rather than a tested mechanism here.

## Connections

The paper is a direct empirical companion to **Kőszegi and [[@Koszegi2009|Rabin (2009)]]** on reference-dependent, expectations-based news utility and to **Golman and Loewenstein (2015)** on attention/curiosity in information preferences; it adjudicates against **[[@Brunnermeier2005|Brunnermeier and Parker (2005)]]** (belief-as-choice / information avoidance), **Ely, Frankel and Kamenica (2015)** (suspense and surprise), and **Epstein (2008)** (axiomatic anxiety/hope with prior-dependence). It builds on the anticipatory-utility tradition of **[[@Loewenstein1987|Loewenstein (1987)]]** and **[[@Caplin2001|Caplin and Leahy]] (2001, 2004)**, and on **[[@Brunnermeier2005|Brunnermeier-Parker (2005)]]** optimal expectations. Empirically it sits beside **Ganguly and Tasoff (forthcoming)** and **Zimmermann (2015)** on information preferences, **Ambuehl and Li (2015)** on willingness-to-pay for instrumental information, **[[@Oster2013a|Oster, Shoulson and Dorsey (2013)]]** and **Golman et al. (forthcoming)** on information avoidance, and the **myopic-loss-aversion** literature ([[@Benartzi1995|Benartzi-Thaler 1995]]; Gneezy-Potters 1997; Bellemare et al. 2005; Fellner-Sutter 2009). The broader expectations-based reference-dependence literature ([[@Abeler2011|Abeler et al. 2011]]; [[@Crawford2011|Crawford-Meng 2011]]; Gill-Prowse 2012; Ericson-Fuster 2012) supplies the loss-aversion foundation the piecemeal result probes.

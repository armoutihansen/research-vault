> [!abstract] Abstract
> The majority of economic decisions are forward-looking and thus involve expectations of future outcomes. Understanding the expectations that individuals have is thus of crucial importance to designing and evaluating policies in health, education, finance, migration, social protection, and many other areas. However, the majority of developing country surveys are static in nature and many do not elicit subjective expectations of individuals. Possible reasons given for not collecting this information include fears that poor, illiterate individuals do not understand probability concepts, that it takes far too much time to ask such questions, or that the answers add little value. This paper provides a critical review and new analysis of subjective expectations data from developing countries and refutes each of these concerns. We find that people in developing countries can generally understand and answer probabilistic questions, such questions are not prohibitive in time to ask, and the expectations are useful predictors of future behavior and economic decisions. The paper discusses the different methods used for eliciting such information, the key methodological issues involved, and the open research questions. The available evidence suggests that collecting expectations data is both feasible and valuable, suggesting that it should be incorporated into more developing country surveys.

## Summary
This is a critical methodological review, leavened with new empirical analysis, of how to elicit and use *subjective probabilistic expectations* in developing-country household surveys. The authors argue that most economic decisions are governed jointly by preferences and by expectations of future outcomes, yet large development surveys (LSMS, DHS, IFLS) collect static, non-probabilistic data at best. Three standard objections — that poor, illiterate respondents cannot grasp probability, that elicitation is too time-consuming, and that the answers are uninformative — are each confronted with accumulated evidence and rejected. The paper surveys elicitation instruments, the practical design choices they entail, and the criteria by which one judges whether the elicited beliefs "work," then maps the open research frontier.

## Research question
Can probabilistic subjective expectations be feasibly, reliably, and usefully elicited in developing-country survey settings? Concretely: (i) Do low-education respondents understand and obey the basic laws of probability when answering? (ii) Are the questions tractable in field surveys (enumerator skill, time, incentives)? (iii) Do the elicited beliefs carry information beyond socioeconomic correlates and current outcomes — i.e., do they predict future realizations and choice behavior?

## Method / identification
This is a review paper plus original secondary analysis, not a single estimator. Methodologically it does two things. First, it catalogs elicitation designs: (a) non-probabilistic Likert / point-expectation questions; (b) direct percent-chance elicitation as in Dominitz & Manski (1997), where respondents report $\Pr(Y<Y_k)$ at several thresholds to trace a subjective CDF; and (c) visual-aid methods in which respondents allocate a fixed number of beans/stones/tokens across bins to express a discretized subjective distribution, sometimes with an explicit "one bean = one chance in ten" mapping (Delavande & Kohler 2009a) and sometimes with an analogical "20 businesses just like yours" framing (de Mel et al. 2008). Moments are then recovered by treating bin midpoints $x_i$ with elicited probabilities $f_i$ as a histogram, $\hat\mu=\sum_i f_i x_i$. Second, the validation logic is laid out as a *joint hypothesis test*: an observed inaccuracy is jointly a failure of comprehension and/or a failure of rational expectations, so trial questions with known probabilities (coin toss, rain today) and familiar-vs-unfamiliar event comparisons (McKenzie et al. 2007) are used to separate the two. Internal-consistency checks exploit probability axioms — monotonicity over nested events $\Pr(A)\le\Pr(B)$ for $A\subseteq B$, and additivity $\Pr(E)+\Pr(E^{c})=1$. Predictive-validity regressions regress realized outcomes (profits, catches, migration) on elicited moments, with and without current-outcome controls and enumerator fixed effects.

## Data
The paper reuses several field datasets as new evidence: Sri Lankan microenterprise panel (de Mel et al. 2008, ~564 owners; full profit distributions vs point expectations); Malawi HIV/mortality survey (Delavande & Kohler 2009a, ~3222 adults, 10-bean elicitation); semi-arid India monsoon-onset beliefs (Giné et al. 2009, 960 obs); coastal Tamilnadu boatowner fish-catch expectations (Giné & Klonner 2007, 232 owners); Tongan migration income expectations (McKenzie et al. 2007, ~95-128 obs); plus Colombia (Attanasio et al. 2005), Orissa India (Mahajan et al. 2008), Ethiopia/Kenya pastoralists (Luseno et al. 2003; Lybbert et al. 2007), and Uganda coffee (Hill 2007).

## Key findings
1. **Comprehension.** Respondents broadly obey probability laws. In Malawi only 19 of 3222 (0.6%) violated nested-event monotonicity; in Colombia the sum of complementary-event probabilities cannot be rejected as 1; in Orissa the mean of unconstrained probabilities summing to 1.13 (1.06 trimmed) is close to unity given the 0.1 stone granularity.
2. **Cardinal vs ordinal.** Subjective probabilities and Likert scores are positively correlated but carry independent information; subjective probabilities explain slightly more variation in true HIV status (pseudo-$R^2$ 0.082 vs 0.058) and yield cardinal quantities usable in expected-utility / dynamic models.
3. **Point expectation vs full distribution.** In Sri Lanka the casual "what do you expect?" answer correlates 0.90 with the subjective mean/median but differs materially (only 19% within 10%). The subjective mean predicts realized December profits far better: regression coefficient 0.47 vs 0.26 for the point expectation, mean absolute error 25% lower, and MSE 1.63x higher for the point question even after trimming — the point answer is more outlier-prone.
4. **Accuracy.** Beliefs are accurate for common, recurring events (monsoon onset, rainfall, rice prices) but biased for rare or unfamiliar ones — Malawians overstate near-term mortality and understate own HIV risk; Tongans underestimate New Zealand earnings.
5. **Higher moments and horizon.** The elicited standard deviation predicts realized variance of catches; accuracy rises at shorter horizons, though over very short horizons current outcomes absorb the predictive content.
6. **Choice prediction.** Expectations predict behavior conditional on covariates and current outcomes: coffee-labor allocation, monsoon planting/replanting/insurance purchase, share-tenancy contract choice ([[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare 2008]]), and Tongan migration (NZ$100 higher expected income → +10pp application probability). Information-provision experiments (Jensen 2010; Nguyen 2008) show belief changes causally raise schooling, especially for non-liquidity-constrained students.

## Contribution
The paper consolidates a scattered field-economics literature into a single methodological reference, supplies the first cross-study evidence that probabilistic elicitation is feasible and informative in low-income settings, demonstrates concretely (Sri Lanka) that eliciting full distributions outperforms point questions for prediction, and provides a disciplined framework — comprehension checks, the joint-hypothesis view of accuracy, and predictive validity — for judging elicited beliefs. It thereby furnishes the empirical warrant for embedding expectations modules in major development surveys.

## Limitations & open questions
The new evidence is secondary analysis of small, specialized, non-nationally-representative samples; results may not scale. Several open problems are named explicitly: whether explicitly linking beans to probabilities (vs vague "likely" allocations) materially changes responses, to be settled experimentally; sensitivity of nested-event consistency to question order and to whether beans are left on or removed (anchoring artifacts); whether monetary incentives / scoring rules improve accuracy, still unsettled since Grisley & Kellogg (1983) did not randomize rewards; the lack of any developing-country comparison of "what do you expect" vs formal elicitation beyond the one Sri Lanka case; enumerator effects, tested only once; the near-absence of structural decision models using these beliefs (only Kaufmann 2009) and no test of whether subjective expectations build better counterfactuals than assumed rational expectations; the difficulty of finding instruments for expectations given how little is known about their formation; and intra-household questions of *whose* expectations drive decisions.

## Connections
This is the developing-country companion to [[@Manski2004|Manski (2004)]] "Measuring expectations" and Hurd (2009) on subjective probabilities in household surveys, building directly on the percent-chance CDF elicitation of Dominitz & Manski (1997) and the point-vs-distribution comparison of Engelberg, Manski & Williams (2009). The anchoring-vignette concern echoes McFadden et al. (2005) and Kapteyn, Smith & van Soest (2007). On the formation and updating of beliefs it connects to Di Tella, Galiani & Schargrodsky (2007) and the broader behavioral program of [[@Rabin1998|Rabin (1998)]] and Kahneman, Slovic & Tversky (1982); on incentive compatibility of belief elicitation it gestures at scoring-rule work (Nelson & Bessler 1989), relevant to the proper-scoring-rule literature later formalized by [[@Gneiting2007|Gneiting & Raftery (2007)]]. The structural-modeling agenda links to Attanasio & Kaufmann (2009) and Kaufmann (2009).

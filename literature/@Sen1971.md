## Summary
Sen gives a unified axiomatic treatment of revealed-preference theory once the domain of the choice function is widened from "budget sets" (convex polyhedra) to all finite subsets of the alternative set. Under this broader domain he proves that essentially every classical rationality condition — the Weak and Strong Axioms of Revealed Preference, Richter's Congruence Axiom, normality plus an ordering, and the coincidence of competing definitions of revealed preference — collapses into one equivalence class. He then "factorizes" full rationality into independent component properties ($\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$, $\delta$) that isolate normality, transitivity, and quasi-transitivity, clarifying which weaker structures (e.g. transitive strict preference with intransitive indifference) survive when full transitivity fails.

## Research question
What is the axiomatic structure of revealed-preference theory when (i) the domain of the choice function is not restricted to budget polyhedra but includes all finite subsets, (ii) choice is set-valued rather than element-valued, and (iii) one drops the obsession with full transitivity in favor of weaker rationality requirements? Specifically, which conditions on a choice function guarantee that it is generated by ("rationalizable" by) a binary preference relation, and which guarantee that this relation is an ordering, quasi-transitive, or merely normal?

## Method / identification
Pure axiomatic / set-theoretic analysis — no data, no estimation. Let $X$ be the universe of alternatives and $C(\cdot)$ a choice function assigning a non-empty $C(S)$ to each non-empty $S$ in a domain $K$; the key working assumption is that $K$ contains all finite subsets of $X$ (Sen notes all results survive if $K$ contains merely all pairs and triples).

Three distinct binary relations are generated from $C$: the "at-least-as-good" relation $R$ ($xRy$ iff $x\in C(S)$ for some $S$ with $y\in S$); the pairwise relation $\tilde R$ ($x\tilde Ry$ iff $x\in C(\{x,y\})$); and the Arrow/Samuelson revealed-preference relation $\hat R$ ($x\hat Py$ iff $\exists S$ with $x\in C(S)$, $y\in S\setminus C(S)$, and $x\hat Ry$ iff not $y\hat Px$). The "image" $\hat C(S)=\{x\in S: xRy \text{ for all } y\in S\}$ is the choice that $R$ would produce, and $C$ is **normal** iff $C(S)=\hat C(S)$ for all $S$ — i.e. $C$ picks exactly the $R$-best elements.

Rationality conditions are stated as axioms on $C$: WARP (if $x\hat Py$ then not $y\hat Rx$), SARP (its closure under the indirect relation $\hat P^{*}$), Richter's Strong Congruence Axiom, Sen's new **Weak Congruence Axiom** (if $xRy$ then for any $S$ with $y\in C(S)$ and $x\in S$, $x\in C(S)$), and the contraction/expansion properties $\alpha$ (Chernoff: $x\in C(T)$, $S\subseteq T$, $x\in S \Rightarrow x\in C(S)$), $\beta$, $\gamma$, and $\delta$. Results are theorems (T.1–T.10) proved by direct set-theoretic argument, culminating in an implication diagram.

## Data
None — this is a theoretical paper. Sen explicitly criticizes the empirical pretension of restricting the domain to observed budget sets: only finitely many of an uncountable continuum of budget sets are ever observed, so the rationality axioms are assumptions, not established facts, whether the domain is $K$ or the budget subclass $K^{*}$.

## Key findings
- **(T.1)** WCA implies $R$ is an ordering (complete, reflexive, transitive); the converse holds when $C$ is normal.
- **(T.2)** WCA holds **iff** $R=\hat R$ (the "at-least-as-good" and Arrow revealed-preference relations coincide).
- **(T.3) Master equivalence:** the following are all equivalent — ($R$ an ordering & $C$ normal); ($\tilde R$ an ordering & $C$ normal); WCA; SCA; WARP; SARP; $R=\hat R$; ($\tilde R=R$ & $C$ normal). Thus on the full finite domain WARP, SARP, and congruence are not distinct; the classical Houthakker/Gale non-equivalence of WARP and SARP is an artifact of the restricted budget-set domain.
- **(T.4)** An element-valued (singleton) choice function is normal iff $R$ is an ordering.
- **(T.5)** Every normal choice function satisfies $\alpha$. **(T.6)** For element-valued functions, $\alpha \Leftrightarrow$ WCA. **(T.7)** $\alpha$ implies $\tilde R=R$ (but not normality in general).
- **(T.8) Factorization of full rationality:** $C$ satisfies WCA **iff** it satisfies both $\alpha$ and $\beta$ — separating contraction-consistency ($\alpha$) from the expansion-consistency ($\beta$) needed for transitivity.
- **(T.9)** $C$ is normal **iff** it satisfies $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ (an expansion/union property); $\gamma$ alone delivers normality/binariness without transitivity.
- **(T.10)** For a normal $C$, $R$ is **quasi-transitive** (strict $P$ transitive, indifference possibly intransitive) **iff** Property $\delta$ holds, giving the intermediate Armstrong/Luce semiorder-style case its own axiom.

## Contribution
Sen converts a tangle of seemingly competing rationality axioms into a single clean hierarchy. The headline is the (T.3) collapse: by enlarging the domain to all finite sets, the much-debated gap between the Weak and Strong Axioms of Revealed Preference disappears, completing the program Arrow began. Equally important is the factorization (T.8–T.10): full rationality $=\alpha+\beta$, normality $=\alpha+\gamma$, quasi-transitivity (given normality) $=\delta$. This decomposition isolated the contraction- vs. expansion-consistency properties that became foundational vocabulary for all later choice-theoretic and social-choice work, and the Weak Congruence Axiom and Property $\delta$ are original to this paper.

## Limitations & open questions
- The entire apparatus presumes the rationality axioms hold over **unobservable** choice situations; Sen openly flags the methodological tension that postulating WCA over all finite subsets has operational content (it lets one deduce $xRz$ from observed $xRy$, $yRz$) yet can never be empirically verified. He poses but does not resolve whether there is any a priori reason to expect the axioms to hold over budget sets and not elsewhere.
- The treatment of quasi-transitivity stops at Property $\delta$; the still-weaker structures (acyclicity, the existence of choice sets without quasi-transitivity, semiorders as a special case of $\delta$) are noted as open territory, pointing to Luce's semiorders and the collective-choice literature (Pattanaik, Inada, Fishburn) for further axiomatization.
- Whether the full-domain equivalences degrade gracefully under intermediate domains (richer than budget sets but poorer than all finite subsets) is left implicit.

## Connections
This paper is the formal companion to Sen's 1969 "Quasi-transitivity, Rational Choice and Collective Decisions" and his social-choice program, where intransitive indifference and merely quasi-transitive social preference matter for escaping Arrow-type impossibilities. It completes and corrects the revealed-preference lineage of Samuelson (WARP), Houthakker (SARP, indirect revealed preference), Ville, and von Neumann–Morgenstern, and engages directly with Arrow's "Rational Choice Functions and Orderings" (the (ii)/(v)/(vi) equivalence) and Richter's Congruence Axiom, showing the apparent disagreements (Richter, Gale) stem from domain or element-valued assumptions. Property $\alpha$ is Chernoff's; the quasi-transitive/semiorder strand draws on Armstrong, Georgescu-Roegen, Majumdar, and Luce. Sen credits Herzberger for the dual $\alpha^{*}$/$\gamma^{*}$ pair-of-sets reformulation that highlights the complementarity of contraction and expansion consistency — the conceptual seed of the modern $\alpha$/$\beta$/$\gamma$ taxonomy used throughout choice theory and behavioral revealed-preference work.

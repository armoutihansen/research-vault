## Scope

This topic asks how to make normative welfare statements — and design defaults, menus, and choice architecture — for an agent whose revealed choices violate the standard axioms (WARP/SARP). It spans the choice-theoretic foundations of behavioral welfare ([[@Bernheim2009|Bernheim & Rangel (2009)]], [[@Rubinstein2012|Rubinstein & Salant (2012)]], [[@Chambers2012|Chambers & Hayashi (2012)]]), the framing/status-quo machinery those foundations rest on ([[@Salant2008|Salant & Rubinstein (2008)]], [[@Samuelson1988|Samuelson & Zeckhauser (1988)]]), and the empirical/policy program of nudges, defaults, and choice overload ([[@Thaler2003|Thaler & Sunstein (2003)]], [[@Thaler2004a|Thaler & Benartzi (2004)]], [[@Johnson2003|Johnson & Goldstein (2003)]], [[@Iyengar2000|Iyengar & Lepper (2000)]], [[@Benartzi2002|Benartzi & Thaler (2002)]], [[@Bhargava2015|Bhargava & Loewenstein (2015)]]). Its boundary is the welfare/policy *use* of inconsistency; the positive procedures generating that inconsistency (limited attention, satisficing, context effects) live in sibling bounded-rationality topics and enter here only as inputs.

## Sub-themes

- **Axiom-free welfare relations.** [[@Bernheim2009|Bernheim & Rangel (2009)]]'s unambiguous-choice relation $P^{*}$ ("$x\,P^{*}y$ iff $y$ is never chosen when $x$ is available"), proven acyclic over generalized choice situations $(X,d)$, and the survey synthesis in [[@deClippel2022|de Clippel & Rozen (2022)]] situating $P^{*}$ against multi-self and model-based welfare.
- **Model-based / distortion-function welfare.** [[@Rubinstein2012|Rubinstein & Salant (2012)]]'s latent welfare ordering recovered through an explicit distortion function $D$, and [[@Chambers2012|Chambers & Hayashi (2012)]]'s subjective scoring-rule welfare functional. Their framing substrate is [[@Salant2008|Salant & Rubinstein (2008)]]'s $(A,f)$ choice-with-frames.
- **Defaults and choice architecture.** [[@Thaler2003|Thaler & Sunstein (2003)]]'s libertarian paternalism; [[@Thaler2004a|Thaler & Benartzi (2004)]]'s Save More Tomorrow; [[@Johnson2003|Johnson & Goldstein (2003)]]'s organ-donation defaults — all leaning on [[@Samuelson1988|Samuelson & Zeckhauser (1988)]]'s status-quo bias.
- **Choice overload and the menu-monotonicity failure.** [[@Iyengar2000|Iyengar & Lepper (2000)]] and [[@Benartzi2002|Benartzi & Thaler (2002)]] as counterexamples to "more options weakly dominate."
- **Methodological/programmatic scaffolding.** [[@Rabin1998|Rabin (1998)]], [[@Loewenstein2001|Loewenstein (2001)]], [[@Dekel2010|Dekel & Lipman (2010)]], and [[@Bhargava2015|Bhargava & Loewenstein (2015)]] on whether/which non-standard $U(x)$, construal, or non-choice data should anchor welfare and policy.

## Cross-paper tensions

**How discerning may a welfare criterion be without smuggling in unobservables?** This is the core fault line. [[@Bernheim2009|Bernheim & Rangel (2009)]] buy acyclicity by being *conservative*: $P^{*}$ never overrules a choice, so under pervasive conflict its optimum set bloats and it goes silent. [[@Rubinstein2012|Rubinstein & Salant (2012)]] explicitly attack this coarseness — by positing a refutable distortion function (satisficing, small errors) they recover *finer* rankings, and in their monotone-mistakes scenario their criterion can even *reverse* Pareto, declaring $a$ welfare-superior to $b$ though $b$ Pareto-dominates $a$. That is a direct contradiction of $P^{*}$'s libertarian guarantee, bought with an assumption the analyst must validate. [[@Chambers2012|Chambers & Hayashi (2012)]] reject the premise both share — that some procedure is "correct" — arguing welfare is irreducibly *subjective*: their Combination axiom forces a linear scoring rule whose weights *are* the economist's prior, and they exhibit a status-quo example where Combination itself fails. So the three flagship foundations disagree on whether welfare is recovered (Rubinstein–Salant), bounded (Bernheim–Rangel), or stipulated (Chambers–Hayashi).

**Are defaults welfare-improving or merely fate-determining?** The nudge papers presume the planner can identify the welfare-relevant option, yet their own evidence undercuts that. [[@Johnson2003|Johnson & Goldstein (2003)]] cannot separate physical response cost, cognitive effort, and loss aversion — and concede "one might draw different conclusions" depending which dominates; if it is constructed preference, the default doesn't reveal welfare, it *manufactures* it. [[@Benartzi2002|Benartzi & Thaler (2002)]] sharpen the knife: 62% prefer a peer's portfolio to their own and choices violate IIA via extremeness aversion, so the very autonomy nudges aim to preserve "is not worth much" — but then which allocation is the welfare benchmark the default should target? [[@Thaler2003|Thaler & Sunstein (2003)]] confront this as the *circularity problem*: market-mimicking is "doomed" precisely when majority choice depends on the starting point — exactly the regime [[@Samuelson1988|Samuelson & Zeckhauser (1988)]] and [[@Johnson2003|Johnson & Goldstein (2003)]] document.

**Is nudging enough?** [[@Bhargava2015|Bhargava & Loewenstein (2015)]] break with [[@Thaler2003|Thaler & Sunstein (2003)]]: choice-preserving nudges are "nano-sized," and standardizing *products* or taxing behavioral exploitation may be needed — but acting on internalities demands a "true interest" benchmark the foundational papers say is unavailable.

**What evidence may anchor the benchmark?** [[@Dekel2010|Dekel & Lipman (2010)]] argue non-choice data (response times, neuro) can legitimately inform models, while [[@Loewenstein2001|Loewenstein (2001)]] and [[@Rabin1998|Rabin (1998)]] would re-anchor welfare on construal/experienced-utility — a move [[@Bernheim2009|Bernheim & Rangel (2009)]] and [[@Chambers2012|Chambers & Hayashi (2012)]] deliberately refuse, keeping choice (or choice-plus-prior) primitive.

## Open questions

- **Principled pruning / weight selection.** Bernheim–Rangel leave line-drawing between object characteristics and ancillary conditions $d$ to the analyst; Chambers–Hayashi leave which scoring weights are "reasonable" open; Thaler–Sunstein leave "where to stop" being libertarian undefined. No paper supplies a discipline.
- **Validating the distortion function.** Rubinstein–Salant offer only consistency-based refutation and a deterministic $D$; the stochastic, maximum-likelihood version that would let competing cognitive processes be *compared* on fit is unbuilt.
- **Mechanism behind defaults.** Johnson–Goldstein and Samuelson–Zeckhauser cannot decompose the status-quo effect into cost vs. loss aversion vs. construction — yet the welfare verdict on a default hinges on which it is.
- **Which design features are load-bearing.** Thaler–Benartzi do not know whether the lag, the raise-linkage, or the auto-escalation drives Save More Tomorrow's effect; no RCT isolates them.
- **Boundaries of choice overload.** Iyengar–Lepper leave the mechanism unconfirmed and the manageable-vs-excessive threshold (somewhere in $[6,24]$) uncalibrated.

## Candidate ideas

- **Stochastic distortion functions, head-to-head.** Build the probabilistic extension [[@Rubinstein2012|Rubinstein & Salant (2012)]] flag, then ML-estimate satisficing vs. small-errors vs. status-quo distortions on panel choice data and report which best fits — turning their refutation-only test into model selection (links [[@Chambers2012|Chambers & Hayashi (2012)]], [[@Salant2008|Salant & Rubinstein (2008)]]).
- **Decomposing the default.** Design an experiment that orthogonally varies switching cost, loss-aversion framing, and recommendation salience to identify the three channels [[@Johnson2003|Johnson & Goldstein (2003)]] could not separate, and map each to a different welfare verdict on opt-out defaults.
- **When does $P^{*}$ go silent?** Quantify, on real menu data (e.g. the dominated-health-plan choices in [[@Bhargava2015|Bhargava & Loewenstein (2015)]]), how coarse [[@Bernheim2009|Bernheim & Rangel (2009)]]'s $P^{*}$ becomes vs. how sharp [[@Rubinstein2012|Rubinstein & Salant (2012)]]'s recovered ordering is — an empirical discernment/robustness frontier per [[@deClippel2022|de Clippel & Rozen (2022)]].
- **Reverse-Pareto stress test.** Search experimentally for the monotone-mistakes regime where [[@Rubinstein2012|Rubinstein & Salant (2012)]]'s welfare ranking contradicts Pareto, and ask subjects ex post which option they endorse — adjudicating Rubinstein–Salant vs. the libertarian guarantee of [[@Bernheim2009|Bernheim & Rangel (2009)]].
- **Eliciting the planner's prior.** Operationalize [[@Chambers2012|Chambers & Hayashi (2012)]]'s scoring weights from policymakers' stated priors over opportunity sets, then show how the implied default differs from market-mimicking under the circularity problem of [[@Thaler2003|Thaler & Sunstein (2003)]].
- **Calibrating the overload threshold for high-stakes menus.** Use [[@Benartzi2002|Benartzi & Thaler (2002)]]'s unlabeled-distribution yardstick to locate the assortment size at which [[@Iyengar2000|Iyengar & Lepper (2000)]]'s demotivation kicks in for retirement-plan menus, feeding a menu-restriction rule.

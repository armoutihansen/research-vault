## Scope

This topic covers the founding descriptive theory of choice under risk: reference-dependent, S-shaped valuation of gains and losses; loss aversion; nonlinear probability weighting; and the framing/elicitation effects these generate. It spans the original axiomatic statements, the heuristics-and-biases prelude, and field tests of whether the bias survives experience, competition, and stakes. Its boundary is *exogenous* reference dependence — endogenous, expectation-based reference points (Kőszegi–Rabin) live in the neighbouring [[expectations-based-reference-dependence]] topic, and transitive non-EU generalizations (rank-dependent, disappointment) sit in [[disappointment-regret-nonexpected-utility]] — though both edges are contested here.

## Sub-themes

- **Foundational theory.** The core valuation rule: [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky (1979)]] (editing + bilinear evaluation, separable $\pi(p)v(x)$) and its rank-dependent successor [[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Kahneman (1992)]] (Choquet/cumulative weights restoring dominance, $\lambda=2.25$, $\alpha=0.88$, $\gamma=0.61$). [[@Tversky1974|Tversky & Kahneman (1974)]] supplies the heuristics-and-biases scaffolding that motivates departures from the Bayesian/EU benchmark.
- **Framing and elicitation invariance.** [[@Tversky1981|Tversky & Kahneman (1981)]] (description invariance, Asian-disease, mental accounting) and [[@Tversky1990|Tversky, Slovic & Kahneman (1990)]] (procedure invariance, scale compatibility, overpricing of long-shot bets).
- **Riskless reference dependence.** [[@Bateman1997|Bateman et al. (1997)]] turns the WTA–WTP/endowment gap into eight-way elicitation tests of loss aversion over riskless goods.
- **Does it survive the field?** [[@Pope2011|Pope & Schweitzer (2011)]] (PGA putting), [[@Allen2017|Allen et al. (2017)]] (marathon bunching), [[@Benartzi1995|Benartzi & Thaler (1995)]] (myopic loss aversion and the equity premium), [[@Sydnor2010|Sydnor (2010)]] (over-insuring modest risks).
- **Rivals and the transitivity question.** [[@Loomes1991|Loomes, Starmer & Sugden (1991)]] (regret-theoretic intransitivity), [[@Conlisk1993|Conlisk (1993)]] (a tiny utility-of-gambling rescue of EU), and [[@Isler2023|Isler, Yilmaz & Dulleck (2023)]] (a preregistered replication probing the robustness of context effects).

## Cross-paper tensions

**1. Which axiom actually breaks — and is the value function even the culprit?** The deepest fault line is *what* prospect theory must abandon. [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky (1979)]] and [[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Kahneman (1992)]] keep transitivity and locate the anomalies in the curvature of $v$ and $\pi$. [[@Tversky1990|Tversky, Slovic & Kahneman (1990)]] argue the preference-reversal evidence is overwhelmingly a failure of *procedure invariance* (~90% non-invariance, only 10% intransitivity), explained by scale compatibility rather than by the value function at all. [[@Loomes1991|Loomes, Starmer & Sugden (1991)]] flatly contradict this: in a pure binary-choice design immune to BDM, valuation tasks, and random-lottery-selection loopholes, they find systematic intransitive *cycles* in the regret-predicted direction ($p<10^{-13}$). So two member papers, sharing authors and methods lineage, disagree on whether transitivity is descriptively safe — the same data territory yields "invariance fails, transitivity holds" versus "transitivity genuinely fails."

**2. Loss aversion vs. probability weighting as the load-bearing mechanism.** [[@Benartzi1995|Benartzi & Thaler (1995)]] show the equity-premium result is driven by loss aversion, not by the weighting function (replacing $w(\cdot)$ with true probabilities barely moves the equilibrium evaluation period). [[@Sydnor2010|Sydnor (2010)]] reaches the opposite conclusion for low-deductible insurance: standard prospect theory *fails* because the choice lives entirely in the loss domain away from the kink, so the standalone value-function loss aversion explains only ~half the willingness to pay — he needs probability *overweighting* of a ~4% claim rate (or a Kőszegi–Rabin "cost ≠ loss" reframing). The two field anomalies thus assign opposite weights to the same two PT primitives.

**3. Is loss aversion a deep preference or a "superficial," erodable artifact?** [[@Pope2011|Pope & Schweitzer (2011)]] and [[@Allen2017|Allen et al. (2017)]] argue the bias survives the List/Levitt market-discipline critique — even Tiger Woods and millions of marathoners show it. But [[@Bateman1997|Bateman et al. (1997)]] explicitly flag that their *conditional* endowment likely under-detects the effect and concede loss aversion "might be a relatively superficial phenomenon" eroded by trading experience. [[@Conlisk1993|Conlisk (1993)]] presses the rival: append a *tiny* separable utility-of-gambling to ordinary concave EU and you reproduce simultaneous insuring and small-stakes risk-seeking without any reference dependence — a parsimony challenge that, if subjects treat lab choices as gambling, confounds the entire risk-attitude interpretation underlying the member experiments.

**4. What is the reference point, and is it exogenous?** [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky (1979)]]/[[@Tversky1981|Tversky & Kahneman (1981)]] leave the reference point as an un-modelled status quo. [[@Pope2011|Pope & Schweitzer (2011)]] use par (an exogenous salient anchor) yet test Kőszegi–Rabin rational-expectations adaptation and get only *qualified* support; [[@Allen2017|Allen et al. (2017)]] deliberately reject expectations and use round-number *goals*, which are adopted spontaneously mid-race. So even within "exogenous PT" the members cannot agree whether the reference point is the status quo, a goal, or rational expectations — the live border with [[expectations-based-reference-dependence]].

**5. Identification of curvature in the field.** [[@Allen2017|Allen et al. (2017)]] need a diminishing-sensitivity exponent ($\alpha\approx0.15$–$0.23$) far below the lab benchmark of $0.88$ from [[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Kahneman (1992)]], and concede $\lambda$, $\alpha$, and the share of reference-dependent agents $p$ are mutually interchangeable in their deterministic calibration. Field bunching identifies *that* reference dependence operates but cannot cleanly recover the structural parameters the lab claims to pin down.

## Open questions

- **Can transitivity and invariance violations be separated in one design?** [[@Tversky1990|Tversky, Slovic & Kahneman (1990)]] and [[@Loomes1991|Loomes, Starmer & Sugden (1991)]] each leave open whether a richer (>3-option) design would reveal *joint* failures rather than forcing one diagnosis.
- **By what mechanism does market experience erode loss aversion (if it does)?** [[@Bateman1997|Bateman et al. (1997)]] explicitly demand testable hypotheses; the field papers show persistence but not the boundary conditions.
- **Are decision weights stable, or formulation/spacing-dependent?** [[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Kahneman (1992)]] flag that weights vary with outcome spacing and source of uncertainty, possibly forcing abandonment of value/weight separability.
- **Is there a formal theory of framing/editing?** Both [[@Kahneman1979|Kahneman & Tversky (1979)]] and [[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Kahneman (1992)]] concede the editing phase has no formal model; [[@Isler2023|Isler et al. (2023)]] add that context effects are themselves moderated by the *cognitive salience* of scarcity.
- **Can PT and the utility-of-gambling be empirically distinguished?** [[@Conlisk1993|Conlisk (1993)]] leaves this open — a direct identification gap.
- **Which welfare measure under reference dependence?** [[@Bateman1997|Bateman et al. (1997)]] and [[@Sydnor2010|Sydnor (2010)]] both raise but do not resolve how to do welfare/insurance-demand analysis once WTA $\neq$ WTP is a preference, not a bias.

## Candidate ideas

- **A nested design that jointly identifies intransitivity and procedure-invariance failures**, reconciling [[@Tversky1990|Tversky, Slovic & Kahneman (1990)]] (invariance) with [[@Loomes1991|Loomes, Starmer & Sugden (1991)]] (transitivity) by partitioning reversals into all four cells in a single incentive-compatible binary-choice experiment.
- **A horse race between PT and Conlisk's utility-of-gambling on field insurance data**: re-estimate [[@Sydnor2010|Sydnor (2010)]] deductible choices under both [[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Kahneman (1992)]] CPT and [[@Conlisk1993|Conlisk (1993)]] EU-plus-$\epsilon V(G,p)$, testing whether a separable gambling term out-fits probability overweighting.
- **Decompose loss aversion ($\lambda$) from probability weighting ($\gamma$) across domains** by jointly estimating on the ~4% golf/insurance regime ([[@Pope2011|Pope & Schweitzer (2011)]], [[@Sydnor2010|Sydnor (2010)]]) vs. the high-probability equity regime ([[@Benartzi1995|Benartzi & Thaler (1995)]]) to test mechanism (2).
- **Field test of reference-point source**: exploit settings with competing status-quo, goal, and expectation anchors to adjudicate [[@Allen2017|Allen et al. (2017)]] (goals) against [[@Pope2011|Pope & Schweitzer (2011)]] (par + KR expectations) and pin down which reference point the data prefer.
- **Structurally identify field curvature** by embedding a stochastic effort-control model into the [[@Allen2017|Allen et al. (2017)]] marathon-bunching estimator, breaking the $\lambda$/$\alpha$/$p$ interchangeability and recovering whether lab $\alpha=0.88$ ([[@Tversky1992|Tversky & Kahneman (1992)]]) holds in the field.
- **Manipulate scarcity salience to move loss aversion**: extend [[@Isler2023|Isler et al. (2023)]] with a *causal* scarcity-priming arm crossed with [[@Tversky1981|Tversky & Kahneman (1981)]] framing tasks, testing whether cognitive salience attenuates loss aversion itself, not just transaction-utility context effects.

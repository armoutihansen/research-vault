## Scope

This topic asks whether people *have* coherent subjective probabilities at all, and — if they do — how to get them out of a head and into a usable number. Two research communities meet here: economists who want to *measure* expectations because choice data alone cannot separate what people believe from what they want (the identification problem), and cognitive psychologists who ask whether the human assessor is a competent "intuitive statistician" or a limited-capacity heuristic device whose reported probabilities are partly artifacts of the elicitation format. The unifying question is the soundness of the object being elicited: is a subjective probability distribution a stable mental quantity waiting to be read off, or a construction whose value depends on response mode, information format, and whether the assessor is given the tools to compute it?

## Sub-themes

- **Expectations as identifying data for choice models.** Manski (2004) makes the foundational identification argument — observed choices are jointly generated by preferences and beliefs, so the same data are consistent with many $(\text{preference}, \text{belief})$ pairs; eliciting subjective probabilities directly lets one *relax* rather than assume rational expectations. Delavande, Giné & McKenzie (2011) carry this program into developing-country field surveys, showing the same elicited beliefs predict realized behavior (profits, migration, planting) beyond covariates and current outcomes.

- **Feasibility and comprehension of probabilistic elicitation.** Delavande et al. (2011) refute the claim that low-education respondents cannot handle probability, documenting near-universal obedience to monotonicity and additivity axioms (e.g., only 0.6% of Malawians violated nested-event monotonicity); Manski (2004) likewise reports that large surveys (SEE, HRS) yield internally coherent probabilistic responses, vindicating Juster's purchase-probability format over verbal scales.

- **Cognitive limits and biases in assessment.** Hogarth (1975) catalogs the deficit case — absent sampling-distribution intuitions, overconfident credible intervals (Alpert & Raiffa's "98%" intervals missing 40–50% of the time), conjunction/disjunction errors, and reliance on representativeness and availability — concluding humans are "ill-suited" to distributional assessment. Edwards (1975) and Gigerenzer & Hoffrage (1995) contest the interpretation of exactly these findings.

- **Format and representation effects.** Gigerenzer & Hoffrage (1995) show base-rate neglect largely dissolves when the same information is posed as natural-sampling *frequencies* rather than single-event *probabilities*, roughly tripling Bayesian solutions with no instruction; Edwards (1975) anticipates the format critique via Seaver's result that Alpert–Raiffa overtightness *reverses* under a fractile-given vs. point-given switch; Hogarth (1975) himself stresses sensitivity to response mode (linear scale vs. odds vs. log-odds).

- **Incentive compatibility and scoring rules.** Hogarth (1975) gives a critical exposition of strictly proper scoring rules — the condition $S(p,p) > S(r,p)$ for all $r \neq p$ — and warns many standard rules are *insensitive* and presuppose a unique true distribution; Manski (2004) cites scoring-rule elicitation (Nyarko & Schotter) as a way to break experimental identification; Delavande et al. (2011) note the incentive question for field elicitation remains unsettled.

## Cross-paper tensions

The defining friction is the **Hogarth (1975) vs. Edwards (1975) exchange**, an explicit published debate. Hogarth's review argues from a limited-capacity premise: man is a "selective, sequential information processing system with limited capacity," hence "ill-suited" to assess coherent distributions, and the documented biases are evidence of stable cognitive shortcomings. Edwards, *the originator of the conservatism literature he is discussing*, replies that this conclusion is largely an artifact of impoverished experimental "ground rules" — banning paper, pencil, and time while admitting only naive subjects "to keep the numbers small." His reductio is that Hogarth's own 181-reference review is itself high-capacity processing done with external tools, so the right unit of analysis is the human-*plus*-tools system, whose limits are unknown. Edwards demands an *invariance-across-response-modes* criterion before any bias is reified as a cognitive process, and offers Seaver's unpublished result that the Alpert–Raiffa overtightness vanishes under a format switch as a case where Hogarth's "limitation" is really an elicitation artifact.

**Gigerenzer & Hoffrage (1995) escalate Edwards's side with hard data.** Where Hogarth treats base-rate neglect as a fixed deficit, Gigerenzer & Hoffrage show it is format-dependent: the same Bayesian problem in natural-frequency form elicits up to ~50% Bayesian solutions from naive subjects, and individuals switch algorithms across menus in 96% of cases — reasoning is *constructed from* the representation, not read off a stable internal competence. This directly undercuts Hogarth's "no true prior, but biases are real" stance and vindicates Edwards's format-invariance test. Yet there is a residual tension even within the Edwards/Gigerenzer camp: Edwards still talks of "less-than-adequate tools" people fall back on, implying a normative target, whereas Gigerenzer's natural-sampling argument reframes base-rate neglect as *rational* under frequency formats — they agree the deficit reading is wrong but disagree on whether there is a deficit to repair at all.

**The economics program (Manski 2004; Delavande et al. 2011) sits uneasily with the psychology.** Manski's identification fix *requires* eliciting a coherent subjective distribution $Q$ over $\Gamma$ and assuming it "faithfully describes perceptions"; the entire payoff — relaxing rational expectations in choice models — rests on the elicited number being a meaningful belief. Hogarth and Edwards both deny there is a single "true" internal distribution to elicit, and Gigerenzer shows the elicited number flips with format. So the very object the economists treat as measurable data is the object the psychologists argue is constructed at the moment of elicitation. Manski half-concedes this — his closing move toward eliciting probability *ranges* to capture ambiguity (sets of priors à la Gilboa & Schmeidler) is an admission that a point $Q$ may not exist — but the working empirical program proceeds on the single-$Q$ assumption. Delavande et al. (2011) report strong predictive validity for elicited means (the Sri Lanka coefficient 0.47 vs. 0.26 for point expectations), which is the economists' rejoinder: whatever the philosophical status of $Q$, the elicited moments *predict*, so they are informative data. The unresolved question is whether predictive validity licenses the *cardinal, structural* use Manski wants, or only an ordinal/reduced-form one.

A quieter tension concerns **whose format helps and why.** Gigerenzer privileges absolute natural-sampling frequencies and shows *relative* frequencies do not help; Manski and Delavande et al. mostly elicit single-event percent-chance CDFs (Dominitz–Manski thresholds, bean/token allocation) — exactly the single-event probability format Gigerenzer argues is computationally mismatched to the mind. If Gigerenzer is right, the economics elicitation instruments may be systematically harder for respondents than a frequency-framed alternative, a possibility the economics papers do not engage.

## Open questions

- **Does a coherent single $Q$ exist to be measured, or only ambiguity?** Manski (2004) flags that willingness to report probabilities does not establish people *think* probabilistically; range-elicitation for ambiguity is proposed but unvalidated. Edwards (1975) leaves the "boundary problem" — how to even define capacity once tools are admitted — explicitly open.
- **Which elicitation format yields veridical distributions?** Edwards (1975) asks for a format robust across fractile-given vs. point-given framings; Gigerenzer & Hoffrage (1995) show natural frequencies help for inference but leave open how the effect is realized cognitively and why ~50% remain non-Bayesian; neither connects to the economists' percent-chance/CDF instruments.
- **Do monetary incentives / scoring rules improve accuracy?** Delavande et al. (2011) note this is unsettled (no randomized-reward study since Grisley & Kellogg 1983); Hogarth (1975) argues many proper rules are too insensitive to motivate sharp assessment and presume a unique true distribution.
- **A theory of belief formation.** Manski (2004) calls this the area "about which little is known," needed to predict behavior after belief-shifting policy; Delavande et al. (2011) echo that the absence of a formation model makes instruments for expectations hard to find. Lab Bayesian-updating results map poorly to field information environments.
- **Do elicited expectations build better counterfactuals than assumed rational expectations?** Delavande et al. (2011) note this central promise of the program is essentially untested with structural decision models (only Kaufmann 2009).
- **Process over outcome.** Hogarth (1975) calls for shifting from "how well" to "*how*" people assess uncertainty, and for a taxonomy of task characteristics matching strategies to task types — still largely unbuilt.

## Candidate ideas

1. **Re-frame the economists' percent-chance instrument as natural frequencies and test predictive validity (existing-data + new-instrument design).** Gigerenzer & Hoffrage (1995) imply the single-event CDF elicitation used by Manski (2004) and Delavande et al. (2011) is the format the mind handles worst. A project would specify a frequency-format analog of the bean/token CDF elicitation ("of 100 people just like you, how many...") and, on an existing panel with realizations, test whether frequency-framed elicitation yields better-calibrated and more predictive moments than the standard probability frame. Theory + simulation feasible now: formalize when the two formats imply the same recovered $Q$ and where they diverge, even before fieldwork.

2. **An identification result for choice models under *constructed* (format-dependent) beliefs (theory).** Manski's (2004) identification argument assumes the elicited $Q$ "faithfully describes perceptions." Gigerenzer & Hoffrage (1995) and Edwards (1975) imply the elicited number is partly a function of the elicitation format $\phi$, i.e. $\hat Q = g(Q, \phi)$. A theory project would ask what is point- vs. partial-identified in a random-utility model when beliefs are observed only through a format-distorting map $g$, deriving bounds on preference parameters — turning the psychology critique into an econometric robustness statement and extending Manski's partial-identification stance.

3. **Reconcile "ambiguity" and "format dependence" as competing explanations of the fifty–fifty heap (existing-data).** Manski (2004) reads the "50–50" response as a probabilistic summary of ambiguity and proposes range elicitation; Gigerenzer's logic suggests some 50–50 responses are format-induced computational defaults. Using surveys that elicited both point probabilities and ranges (SEE/HRS-style), a project could test discriminating predictions — ambiguity predicts wide *ranges* around 50, a format artifact predicts narrow ranges — to identify which mechanism generates heaping, sharpening Manski's open question on whether people use a single $Q$.

4. **Meta-analytic invariance audit of the heuristics-and-biases corpus (existing-data / theory).** Edwards (1975) demanded that a bias count as a cognitive process only if it survives variation in language, number system, and response mode. A project would operationalize this as a coding scheme over published probability-assessment studies, scoring which biases (conservatism, overconfident intervals, base-rate neglect, conjunction error) replicate across response modes and which collapse under a format switch — empirically adjudicating the Hogarth (1975) vs. Edwards (1975) debate with the modern literature Edwards could not yet see. Feasible without new experiments.

5. **A formation-and-elicitation model linking response mode to recovered moments (theory + simulation).** Both Manski (2004) and Delavande et al. (2011) name the absence of a belief-formation theory as the binding constraint. A project would build a generative model in which an agent holds a (possibly imprecise) belief, a natural-sampling experience stream feeds it, and an elicitation format maps it to a report; simulate how recovered means/variances and consistency-axiom violations (monotonicity, additivity) vary with format and horizon, and calibrate against the documented patterns (Delavande's accuracy-rises-at-short-horizon; Gigerenzer's frequency advantage). This yields testable predictions for the next survey wave and a microfoundation for the predictive-validity regressions.

6. **Scoring-rule sensitivity vs. coherence trade-off for field elicitation (theory + simulation).** Hogarth (1975) shows many proper rules are insensitive and presume a unique true distribution; Delavande et al. (2011) flag the unsettled incentive question. A project would characterize, for an assessor with imprecise (set-valued) beliefs, which proper scoring rules remain *sensitive* enough to motivate effort while not penalizing honest reports of ambiguity — connecting Hogarth's sensitivity critique to Manski's (2004) range-elicitation proposal and to Gneiting & Raftery's modern scoring-rule synthesis. Pure theory; directly informs incentive design for the next developing-country survey module.

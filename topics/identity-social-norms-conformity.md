## Scope

This topic covers how membership in social categories and the observed behavior of others reshape individual preferences and choices — the economics of identity, the in-group/out-group machinery that drives it, and the conformity to social norms that follows. It spans the founding formalization of identity in the utility function, lab and field tests that move "primitive" parameters (discount rates, distributional weights, equilibrium effort) via category salience, and the psychology of conformity and group influence on which those economic models rest. Its boundary: the *social determination* of preferences themselves, distinct from outcome-based distributional preferences (the inequity-aversion topic) and intention-based reciprocity, which it borrows as functional-form vehicles but does not centrally test.

## Sub-themes

- **Identity in the utility function (the founding program).** [[@Akerlof2000|Akerlof and Kranton (2000)]] augments $U_j$ with an identity term $I_j$ tied to social categories and their prescriptions; [[@Benjamin2010|Benjamin, Choi and Strickland (2010)]] operationalizes it as a convex combination $x^*(s)=(1-w(s))x_0+w(s)x_C$ and uses priming to recover the sign of $x_C-x_0$.
- **Induced group identity and social preferences.** [[@Chen2009|Chen and Li (2009)]] grafts a group dummy onto the Charness-Rabin weight ($w_A'=\rho(1+\mathbb{I}a)r+\sigma(1+\mathbb{I}b)s$); [[@Chen2011|Chen and Chen (2011)]] embeds the same weight in potential games to drive equilibrium selection. Both lean on the minimal-group seed [[@Tajfel1971|Tajfel et al. (1971)]].
- **Conformity and social influence (psychological substrate).** [[@Asch1955|Asch (1955)]] on unambiguous-task conformity, [[@Deutsch1955|Deutsch and Gerard (1955)]] separating normative from informational influence, [[@Cohen20031103|Cohen (2003)]] on party-over-policy redefinition of attitudes, and [[@Ransom1974|Ransom (1974)]] reviewing Janis's groupthink.
- **Norms in field outcomes.** [[@Clark2003|Clark (2003)]] tests the utility implication of an employment norm in panel data; [[@Ariely2000|Ariely and Levav (2000)]] shows sequential public choice generates welfare-reducing variety-seeking.
- **Endogenous/developmental origins.** [[@Hoff2016|Hoff and Stiglitz (2016)]]'s "enculturated actor" makes preferences and perception endogenous to durable social context; [[@Buttelmann2013|Buttelmann et al. (2013)]] pushes group-selective social learning down to 14-month infants.

## Cross-paper tensions

The sharpest fault line is **what mechanism identity operates through — preferences vs. beliefs/expectations — and whether it is an exogenous norm or an emergent equilibrium object.** [[@Akerlof2000|Akerlof and Kranton (2000)]] and [[@Benjamin2010|Benjamin, Choi and Strickland (2010)]] treat the prescribed norm $x_C$ as a *primitive* entering utility; [[@Chen2011|Chen and Chen (2011)]] explicitly objects that the effort norm should not be assumed but should *emerge* as the argmax of a reshaped potential function via learning. [[@Chen2009|Chen and Li (2009)]] sits between, flagging in its own limitations that it cannot say whether ingroup matching changes preferences or changes *beliefs* about partners' generosity. [[@Hoff2016|Hoff and Stiglitz (2016)]] radicalizes the dispute: even susceptibility to a prime is endogenous ($P(a_t;S,p_t)=H(p_t,a_{t-1})$), so the "primitive" $w(s)$ of [[@Benjamin2010|Benjamin et al.]] is itself a historical residue — and a "die-hard devotee of fixed preferences" can recast every result as Bayesian updating, an identification escape Hoff-Stiglitz admit they cannot close.

A second tension is **the motive behind in-group/relative behavior.** [[@Tajfel1971|Tajfel et al. (1971)]] finds subjects sacrifice both joint and absolute in-group payoff to maximize the *relative difference* $\text{MD}$ — a pure relative-standing motive. [[@Chen2009|Chen and Li (2009)]] and [[@Chen2011|Chen and Chen (2011)]] instead find ingroup *favoritism without outgroup discrimination* and increased social-welfare-maximizing behavior, i.e., higher absolute weight on others, directly contradicting the maximize-the-gap reading. So the same minimal-group manipulation yields opposite conclusions about whether identity is competitive (relative) or cooperative (efficiency-enhancing).

Third, **conformity's channel and welfare sign conflict.** [[@Deutsch1955|Deutsch and Gerard (1955)]] cleanly separate normative from informational influence and show group framing more than doubles conformity holding information fixed. [[@Ariely2000|Ariely and Levav (2000)]] explicitly *rejects* both informational social influence and conformity as drivers of sequential variety-seeking, attributing it to need-for-uniqueness and information gathering — anti-conformity, and welfare-reducing. [[@Cohen20031103|Cohen (2003)]] adds that group influence is not heuristic laziness (the natural economic shortcut story) but effortful object-redefinition, undermining a rational-inattention reading of conformity.

Finally, **the empirical strength and scope of norm effects is contested.** [[@Benjamin2010|Benjamin et al.]] find gender priming inert and immigrant-black priming null, where [[@Akerlof2000|Akerlof-Kranton]]'s gender applications assume potent gender prescriptions; [[@Clark2003|Clark (2003)]] finds the employment-norm utility effect strong for men but weak or absent for women. The gender asymmetry recurs but is unexplained in both, and identification across all field/lab tests rests on inferred rather than measured norms.

## Open questions

- Where do prescriptions, categories, and the norm content $x_C$ come from, and can the *state space of mental models* be made well-defined (Akerlof-Kranton; Hoff-Stiglitz, who note it is "infinite and not well-defined")?
- Is the ingroup effect a change in preferences or in beliefs/expectations, and does it decay over time (Chen-Li's own deterioration confound; the preference-vs-belief ambiguity)?
- Why is the norm/identity effect systematically weaker for women (Benjamin et al.; Clark)?
- Do temporary primes accumulate into persistent preference change, and which utility function — ex ante or ex post — should welfare analysis use under endogenous preferences (Benjamin et al.; Hoff-Stiglitz's normative indeterminacy)?
- At what cognitive level do group effects operate — true group categorization vs. individual (dis)similarity/comprehension (Buttelmann et al.'s unidentified language-as-group-marker)?
- Can ingroup salience tip *other* coordination mechanisms (public-good provision points) to efficient equilibria, and how do societies move *between* social equilibria (Chen-Chen; Hoff-Stiglitz)?

## Candidate ideas

- **Belief vs. preference decomposition of the ingroup effect.** Re-run the [[@Chen2009|Chen and Li (2009)]] game battery with elicited second-order beliefs about partners' generosity, structurally separating $a,b$ (preference shift) from belief shifts — resolving the mechanism ambiguity both Chen papers flag.
- **Does identity maximize the gap or the surplus?** A design crossing the [[@Tajfel1971|Tajfel et al. (1971)]] $\text{MJP}/\text{MIP}/\text{MD}$ decomposition with the [[@Chen2009|Chen-Li]] real-stakes self-other games to test whether relative-difference vs. social-welfare motives dominate within the *same* subjects.
- **Endogenous priming-susceptibility test.** Implement Hoff-Stiglitz's untested proposal (cited in [[@Hoff2016|Hoff and Stiglitz (2016)]]) by randomly assigning durable role exposure, then priming category salience à la [[@Benjamin2010|Benjamin et al. (2010)]], to see whether $w(s)$ itself shifts with history.
- **Gendered norm asymmetry.** Combine the patience/risk priming of [[@Benjamin2010|Benjamin et al. (2010)]] with the panel employment-norm design of [[@Clark2003|Clark (2003)]] to pin down why identity/norm effects are male-skewed — content of $x_C$ vs. weight $w(s)$.
- **Identity as a coordination device beyond minimum-effort.** Extend [[@Chen2011|Chen and Chen (2011)]]'s potential-game result to provision-point public goods, testing their conjecture that a weak other-regarding boost ($\alpha^g>1/3$ threshold) suffices for efficient provision.
- **Anti-conformity vs. conformity boundary.** Unify the welfare-reducing uniqueness-seeking of [[@Ariely2000|Ariely and Levav (2000)]] with the normative/informational split of [[@Deutsch1955|Deutsch and Gerard (1955)]] by manipulating observability and group entitativity to map when public sequential choice tips from conformity to differentiation.

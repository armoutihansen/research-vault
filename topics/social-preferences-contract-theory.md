## Scope

This topic studies what happens to the *optimal incentive contract* when one or both parties in a moral-hazard relationship depart from the self-interested expected-utility benchmark — embedding inequity aversion, intention-based reciprocity, spite, social attentiveness, or biased beliefs (overconfidence) into the Holmström (1979) / Grossman–Hart (1983) principal–agent model. The unifying object is the **wage schedule** $w(\cdot)$ and how a non-standard agent or principal reshapes its slope, level, and information sensitivity relative to the selfish second best. It is bounded *below* by the measurement of social preferences themselves (the [[inequity-aversion-distributional-preferences|inequity-aversion]] and [[reciprocity-gift-exchange|reciprocity]] topics supply the primitives) and *above* by pure mechanism-design / screening; the focus here is the *design* problem, not the elicitation of $\alpha,\beta,\eta$.

## Sub-themes

- **Inequity-averse agent, selfish principal (the founding case).** [[@Englmaier2010|Englmaier & Wambach (2010)]] (continuous effort/output), [[@Itoh2004|Itoh (2004)]] (binary, limited-liability), and [[@Rey-Biel2008|Rey-Biel (2008)]] (contractible effort, no uncertainty) ask how a Fehr–Schmidt agent bends linearity, the sufficient-statistics result, and team-vs-tournament structure.
- **Other-regarding *principal* and two-sided preferences.** [[@Banerjee2017|Banerjee & Sarkar (2017)]] gives the principal inequity aversion; [[@Banerjee2023|Banerjee & Chakraborty (2023)]] makes the principal *spiteful* and pairs opposite-signed preferences.
- **Reciprocity / generosity as an incentive instrument.** [[@Englmaier2012|Englmaier & Leider (2012)]] (intention-based $\eta$), [[@Fehr2007|Fehr, Klein & Schmidt (2007)]] (experimental bonus-vs-trust-vs-incentive choice), and the "socially attentive" agent of [[@Koch2021a|Koch & Weinschenk (2021)]].
- **Tournaments and relative pay under fairness.** [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]] embeds envy/compassion in the Lazear–Rosen rank-order tournament.
- **Biased beliefs as the non-standard primitive.** [[@delaRosa2011|de la Rosa (2011)]] swaps social preferences for overconfidence; [[@Koszegi2014|Kőszegi (2014)]] and [[@Sliwka2020|Zimmermann & Sliwka (2020)]] are the integrative surveys mapping the whole program.

## Cross-paper tensions

**Does a non-standard agent help or hurt the principal?** The sharpest disagreement is over the *sign* of the principal's payoff in the agent's social-preference intensity. In single-agent models fairness is pure cost: [[@Itoh2004|Itoh (2004)]] and [[@Banerjee2017|Banerjee & Sarkar (2017)]] show expected pay *rising* in $\alpha$, and [[@Englmaier2010|Englmaier & Wambach (2010)]] gives $\partial\Pi_P/\partial\alpha<0$. But the *same primitive* becomes a free incentive instrument once a second agent exists: [[@Rey-Biel2008|Rey-Biel (2008)]] and [[@Itoh2004|Itoh (2004)]] (two-agent case) show the principal manufacturing off-equilibrium inequity to pay a bonus bill *strictly below* summed effort costs, and [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]] finds profit *rising* in envy under fixed prizes. So the field cannot agree whether social preferences are a friction to be insured against or a lever to be exploited — the answer hinges entirely on whether comparisons are vertical (agent-vs-principal) or horizontal (agent-vs-agent).

**Outcome-based vs. intention-based primitives.** [[@Englmaier2010|Englmaier & Wambach (2010)]], [[@Itoh2004|Itoh (2004)]], [[@Rey-Biel2008|Rey-Biel (2008)]], and the Banerjee papers all use distributional Fehr–Schmidt utility; [[@Englmaier2012|Englmaier & Leider (2012)]] insists generosity works through *intentions* ($\eta\cdot\text{rent}\cdot\text{revenue}$). These predict different things about the same gift: [[@Fehr2007|Fehr, Klein & Schmidt (2007)]] show experimentally that the *bonus* contract (agent trusts first) beats the *trust* contract (principal trusts first) — a sequencing result that distributional models, which care only about realized payoff gaps, cannot generate. [[@Koszegi2014|Kőszegi (2014)]] sharpens the indictment: inequity aversion *fails* to predict gift exchange when the principal is much wealthier and selfish (Netzer–Schmutzler: a selfish principal's wage is "never kind"), so the two families are not observationally equivalent.

**What gets compared to what, and to whom?** The most fragile modelling choice is the comparison object. [[@Banerjee2023|Banerjee & Chakraborty (2023)]] show their clean "$\pi=\alpha\Rightarrow$ self-regarding benchmark" reversion *evaporates* once the principal nets out the agent's effort cost $e^2/2$. [[@Englmaier2010|Englmaier & Wambach (2010)]], [[@Rey-Biel2008|Rey-Biel (2008)]], and [[@Koszegi2014|Kőszegi (2014)]] all flag the absence of a theory of *reference-group formation* as the binding constraint. [[@Englmaier2010|Englmaier & Wambach (2010)]] defines inequity over *realized* output (avoiding a vNM-independence violation), while [[@Englmaier2012|Englmaier & Leider (2012)]] and [[@Sliwka2020|Zimmermann & Sliwka (2020)]] use expected/reference-adapted comparisons — so even the timing of the comparison is contested.

**When do incentives vanish, and why?** Many members "explain" weak performance pay, but the mechanisms compete. [[@Englmaier2012|Englmaier & Leider (2012)]] flattens incentives because generosity *substitutes* for spread; [[@Koch2021a|Koch & Weinschenk (2021)]] sets $\Delta t^*=0$ because attentive agents respond at rate $(1-\beta)$; [[@delaRosa2011|de la Rosa (2011)]]'s *incentive effect* lowers power because an overconfident agent overestimates $\partial p/\partial e$; [[@Sliwka2020|Zimmermann & Sliwka (2020)]] adds career/image concerns that crowd out $\beta^*$. These are observationally similar (flat wages) but imply opposite comparative statics in productivity and signal informativeness — an identification problem the survey [[@Koszegi2014|Kőszegi (2014)]] leaves open.

## Open questions

- **Reference-group endogeneity.** Every distributional member (esp. [[@Englmaier2010|Englmaier & Wambach (2010)]], [[@Rey-Biel2008|Rey-Biel (2008)]], [[@Koszegi2014|Kőszegi (2014)]]) treats *whom* the agent compares to as exogenous; no model lets the firm's information policy (pay secrecy, office layout) endogenously set the reference group.
- **Multi-agent other-regarding principals.** [[@Banerjee2017|Banerjee & Sarkar (2017)]] and [[@Banerjee2023|Banerjee & Chakraborty (2023)]] both defer the multi-agent case, flagging that defining a principal's other-regardingness *across* several agents is non-trivial.
- **Discriminating between primitives empirically.** [[@Fehr2007|Fehr, Klein & Schmidt (2007)]] fit average behavior but fail at the individual level and do not test inequity aversion against reciprocity; [[@Banerjee2023|Banerjee & Chakraborty (2023)]] note $\alpha,\pi$ are only ordinally identified.
- **Endogenous preference formation.** [[@Itoh2004|Itoh (2004)]] and [[@Koch2021a|Koch & Weinschenk (2021)]] take $(\alpha,\beta)$ as fixed; how organizational design *cultivates* or *crowds out* social attentiveness is unmodeled.
- **Loss aversion under moral hazard.** [[@Koszegi2014|Kőszegi (2014)]] notes no paper carefully embeds expectations-based loss aversion in an *insurance* model with hidden action — a clean gap bordering the [[expectations-based-reference-dependence|KR program]].

## Candidate ideas

- **Structural horse-race of contract primitives.** Re-run a [[@Fehr2007|Fehr–Klein–Schmidt (2007)]]-style contract-choice experiment with a within-subject design that exogenously varies sequencing and principal wealth, then estimate distributional ([[@Itoh2004|Itoh]]/[[@Englmaier2010|Englmaier–Wambach]]) vs. reciprocity ([[@Englmaier2012|Englmaier–Leider]]) models head-to-head, exploiting the wealth-asymmetry prediction [[@Koszegi2014|Kőszegi (2014)]] flags as the discriminating case.
- **Endogenous reference groups via information policy.** Extend [[@Rey-Biel2008|Rey-Biel (2008)]]/[[@Englmaier2010|Englmaier & Wambach (2010)]] to make pay-transparency a choice variable, deriving optimal disclosure as the lever that sets the strength of $\alpha$ — directly answering their shared open question.
- **Multi-agent spiteful principal.** Solve the deferred extension of [[@Banerjee2023|Banerjee & Chakraborty (2023)]]: a spiteful principal facing two comparing agents, testing whether the "$\pi=\alpha$" benchmark-reversion survives peer comparison or collapses as the net-payoff specification did.
- **Separating crowding-out channels.** Design a field/lab test that distinguishes the substitution mechanism of [[@Englmaier2012|Englmaier & Leider (2012)]], the attentiveness scaling of [[@Koch2021a|Koch & Weinschenk (2021)]], and the overconfidence incentive-effect of [[@delaRosa2011|de la Rosa (2011)]] via their opposite comparative statics in signal informativeness — the identification problem [[@Sliwka2020|Zimmermann & Sliwka (2020)]] leaves unresolved.
- **Sorting on beliefs and fairness.** Build a model where overconfident ([[@delaRosa2011|de la Rosa (2011)]]) and inequity-averse ([[@Itoh2004|Itoh (2004)]]) agents *self-select* across firms, since the principal's gain from each type flips with the comparison structure — connecting to [[@Koch2021a|Koch & Weinschenk (2021)]]'s pooling-optimality result under hidden $\beta$.

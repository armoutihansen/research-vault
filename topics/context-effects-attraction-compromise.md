## Scope

This topic covers the three canonical context effects that break the standard, menu-independent random-utility benchmark: the **attraction** (asymmetric-dominance / decoy) effect, the **compromise** (extremeness-aversion) effect, and the **similarity** effect, together with the phantom-decoy variant. Members span the founding experiments, the unifying behavioral principles, and the competing formal accounts — reason-based, salience, contextual-inference, dual-self bargaining, and incomplete-preference rationalizations. The boundary: phenomena driven by the *relational geometry* of the offered set (dominance, betweenness, attribute contrast). Stochastic-choice machinery and consideration-set formation appear only where they bear directly on these effects; the broader sequential-rationalizability and limited-attention programs are bordering work.

## Sub-themes

- **Founding demonstrations.** The attraction effect and its regularity violation — [[@Huber1982|Huber, Payne & Puto (1982)]]; the compromise effect and the reasons account — [[@Simonson1989|Simonson (1989)]]; the similarity effect and elimination-by-aspects — [[@Tversky1972|Tversky (1972)]]; phantom (unavailable) decoys — [[@Farquhar1993|Farquhar & Pratkanis (1993)]], [[@Pettibone2007|Pettibone & Wedell (2007)]].
- **Unifying behavioral principles.** Tradeoff contrast + extremeness aversion — [[@Simonson1992|Simonson & Tversky (1992)]]; the componential-context (relative-advantage) value model — [[@Tversky1993|Tversky & Simonson (1993)]]; reason-based choice as the general lens — [[@Shafir1993|Shafir, Simonson & Tversky (1993)]]; the estimable single-reference variants — [[@Kivetz2004|Kivetz, Netzer & Srinivasan (2004)]].
- **Mechanism contenders.** Salience/attention — [[@Bordalo2013|Bordalo, Gennaioli & Shleifer (2013)]]; rational contextual inference in markets — [[@Kamenica2008|Kamenica (2008)]]; reason-based and dual-self bargaining rationalizations — [[@Lombardi2009|Lombardi (2009)]], [[@deClippel2012|de Clippel & Eliaz (2012)]]; incomplete-preference partial dominance — [[@Gerasimou2016|Gerasimou (2016)]]; two-state random utility — [[@Manzini2018|Manzini & Mariotti (2018)]].
- **Domain generality & moderators.** Perceptual (non-hedonic) replication — [[@Trueblood2013|Trueblood et al. (2013)]]; policy/political choice — [[@Herne1997|Herne (1997)]]; assortment-type reversal (extremeness *seeking*) — [[@Gourville2007|Gourville & Soman (2007)]].

## Cross-paper tensions

The deepest fault line is **whether context dependence is irrationality or rational inference.** [[@Kamenica2008|Kamenica (2008)]] derives the compromise effect and choice overload as equilibria of *stable preferences* with rational consumers reading the product line as a signal of the hedonic value of quality $s$. [[@Bordalo2013|Bordalo, Gennaioli & Shleifer (2013)]] instead keeps preferences fixed but distorts *valuation* via ex-post salience weights $\sigma(a_k,\bar a)=|a_k-\bar a|/(a_k+\bar a)$. The behavioral camp — [[@Simonson1989|Simonson]], [[@Shafir1993|Shafir et al.]], [[@Tversky1993|Tversky & Simonson]] — denies a stable global order entirely: "preferences are constructed, not revealed." [[@Trueblood2013|Trueblood et al. (2013)]] is the sharpest blow against the *rational-inference* reading: all three effects appear when subjects merely judge the largest of three rectangles, where there is no $s$ to infer and no hedonic gain/loss to weight, implying a process-level cause that neither Kamenica's inference nor pure loss aversion can be.

A second tension is **which mechanism the data actually select.** [[@Pettibone2007|Pettibone & Wedell (2007)]] run a horse race over phantom-decoy locations and find loss-aversion relative advantage beats similarity-substitution and range-weighting — yet *no* model fits the majority "Low" subgroup, who show a positive range effect but a *negative* frequency effect, demanding a two-process account. [[@Trueblood2013|Trueblood et al.]] then argue the nonhedonic perceptual data *falsify* loss aversion as the general cause, favoring dynamic accumulators (MDFT/LCA) over the static relative-advantage model of [[@Tversky1993|Tversky & Simonson]]. So the winning mechanism in consumer data is precisely the one ruled out by perceptual data.

Third, the **formal rationalizations compete on coverage and primitives.** [[@deClippel2012|de Clippel & Eliaz (2012)]] is the only member to generate *both* attraction and compromise from one ordinal rule (fallback bargaining, $C^f_\succ(S)=\arg\max_x \min_i s_i(x,S)$) — explicitly noting that [[@Lombardi2009|Lombardi (2009)]] and [[@Gerasimou2016|Gerasimou (2016)]] capture *attraction only*, while [[@Kivetz2004|Kivetz et al.]] need cardinal structure and [[@Kamenica2008|Kamenica]] needs market equilibrium. But de Clippel & Eliaz concede their ordinal model cannot represent *distance-based* effects (a compromise at $(50,50)$ but not $(2,2)$), exactly the cardinal content [[@Kivetz2004|Kivetz et al.]] and [[@Bordalo2013|Bordalo et al.]] build in. [[@Manzini2018|Manzini & Mariotti (2018)]] sidestep mechanism entirely: dRUM reproduces attraction/compromise/phantom with two rankings and menu-dependent weights, agnostic to "the precise decoy type or causal psychology" — a parsimony that the discriminating designs of [[@Pettibone2007|Pettibone & Wedell]] treat as the whole question.

Fourth, **extremeness aversion is not a law.** [[@Gourville2007|Gourville & Soman (2007)]] show the compromise effect *reverses* into extremeness *seeking* for non-alignable assortments, and that justification magnifies the reversal — using the very [[@Simonson1989|Simonson (1989)]] accountability manipulation that was offered as evidence *for* compromise. [[@Simonson1992|Simonson & Tversky]] themselves had "no definite answer" for why extremeness aversion attaches to quality but not price (polarization), and [[@Tversky1993|Tversky & Simonson]] admit their model is "at best approximate and incomplete." Finally, the empirical identification is fragile: aggregate shares satisfy the betweenness inequality only conditional on an unobserved **ranking condition** ([[@Herne1997|Herne]], [[@Tversky1993|Tversky & Simonson]]), and [[@Gerasimou2016|Gerasimou]] notes existing between-subject one-shot data cannot even distinguish $C(\{x,y\})=\{x,y\}\to C(\{x,y,z\})=y$ (incomplete-preference-compatible) from the WARP-violating $C(\{x,y\})=x\to C(\{x,y,z\})=y$.

## Open questions

- **One process or several?** Does a single mechanism generate attraction, compromise, *and* similarity, or do the negative-frequency subgroup ([[@Pettibone2007|Pettibone & Wedell]]) and the perceptual results ([[@Trueblood2013|Trueblood et al.]]) force a two-process / accumulator account?
- **Do the effects survive incentives and complexity?** Members are overwhelmingly hypothetical, two-attribute, three-option lab choices ([[@Huber1982|Huber et al.]], [[@Simonson1989|Simonson]], [[@Gourville2007|Gourville & Soman]], [[@Herne1997|Herne]]); robustness to real stakes, richer menus, and natural environments is repeatedly flagged as open.
- **What makes a "reason" compelling a priori?** [[@Shafir1993|Shafir et al.]] concede "almost anything can be counted as a reason"; [[@Simonson1989|Simonson]] has "only limited knowledge" of which arguments dominate — so reason-based predictions remain partly post hoc.
- **Endogenizing the choice/consideration set.** [[@Bordalo2013|Bordalo et al.]] take the evoked set as exogenous and name endogenizing it the key next step; [[@Farquhar1993|Farquhar & Pratkanis]] leave optimal $k$-dominance retention unsolved.
- **Identification under incomplete preferences.** [[@Gerasimou2016|Gerasimou]]'s recovery result needs $n-2$ known universally-incomparable alternatives; recovering incomplete/dual preferences ([[@deClippel2012|de Clippel & Eliaz]], [[@Manzini2018|Manzini & Mariotti]]) from sparse menu data is open, and dRUM loses uniqueness at $\alpha=\tfrac12$.
- **An estimable cardinal model.** [[@Tversky1993|Tversky & Simonson]] note the componential model "cannot be readily estimated from aggregate data"; [[@Kivetz2004|Kivetz et al.]] call individual-level heterogeneity in concavity/loss-aversion parameters "fertile ground."

## Candidate ideas

- **A within-subject discrimination experiment that pits the formal accounts head-to-head.** Implement [[@Gerasimou2016|Gerasimou]]'s Fig.-2 designs to separate partial-dominance, [[@deClippel2012|de Clippel–Eliaz]] fallback bargaining, and [[@Lombardi2009|Lombardi]] on out-of-sample compromise/similarity predictions, resolving the attraction-vs-WARP identification gap.
- **Test the rational-inference vs. process accounts crucially.** Replicate [[@Kamenica2008|Kamenica]]'s ex-post-optimality prediction (richer-menu choices are better) against [[@Trueblood2013|Trueblood et al.]]'s nonhedonic perceptual paradigm where no $s$ exists — does the inference signature vanish while the effect persists?
- **Structurally estimate dRUM on bimodal choice data.** Build the two-component mixture [[@Manzini2018|Manzini & Mariotti]] sketch and test whether menu-dependent weight $\tilde\alpha(A)$ recovers attraction/compromise without committing to salience or loss aversion, benchmarking against [[@Kivetz2004|Kivetz et al.]]'s contextual-concavity model.
- **Map the alignability boundary of extremeness seeking.** Extend [[@Gourville2007|Gourville & Soman]] with incentivized, partially-alignable assortments to find where compromise flips to extremeness seeking, and embed the moderator in [[@Bordalo2013|Bordalo et al.]]'s salience weights or a contingent-additive value function.
- **Endogenize the consideration set in salience theory.** Close [[@Bordalo2013|Bordalo et al.]]'s flagged gap by letting the salient reference good determine the evoked set, then derive whether decoy/phantom effects ([[@Farquhar1993|Farquhar & Pratkanis]], [[@Pettibone2007|Pettibone & Wedell]]) survive endogenous attention.
- **Decoys as a manipulation channel in collective choice.** Build on [[@Herne1997|Herne]]'s policy-choice evidence to model strategic decoy insertion in voting agendas, formalizing when an asymmetrically dominated alternative shifts a social-choice outcome.

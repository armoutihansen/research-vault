## Scope

This topic collects the choice-theoretic foundations for decision makers whose underlying preference relation is *not* forced to be complete or transitive, and for the behaviors that incompleteness rationalizes: indecisiveness, choice deferral, status-quo/default bias, and cyclic choice. Its boundary is the *revealed-preference* question — what observable choice (or non-choice) data identify about an incomplete/intransitive order — rather than the welfare or market consequences of such preferences. It spans the individual decision maker (the Eliaz–Ok and Gerasimou programs) and the collective/aggregation reading of incompleteness (May, Sprumont, Xu–Zhou, Sen), unified by the refusal to recover completeness via WARP.

## Sub-themes

- **Weakening WARP to reveal incompleteness.** [[@Eliaz2006|Eliaz & Ok (2006)]] relax WARP to WARNI and pin $\beta$ (not $\alpha$) as the axiom forcing completeness; [[@Gerasimou2018|Gerasimou (2018)]] instead keeps full WARP on *active* choices and lets $C(A)=\varnothing$ carry incompleteness; [[@Ehlers2008|Ehlers & Sprumont (2008)]] weaken WARP's conclusion to permit cycles while banning context-dependence; [[@Sen1997|Sen (1997)]] reframes the whole enterprise as *maximization* (undominated) vs. *optimization* (best).
- **Representation of incomplete/intransitive preference.** [[@Gerasimou2010|Gerasimou (2010)]] gives a single odd bivariate preference function $P$ for non-complete, non-transitive $\succ$; [[@Gerasimou2013|Gerasimou (2013)]] and [[@Gerasimou2015|Gerasimou (2015)]] settle when hemicontinuity suffices for continuity once completeness is dropped.
- **Deferral, status quo, and partial dominance.** [[@Gerasimou2016a|Gerasimou (2016)]] derives deferral and status-quo bias from one acyclic incomplete $\succ$ via Extended Partial Dominance; [[@Qin2017|Qin (2017)]] re-axiomatizes the same rule under *transitive* $\succ$; [[@Gerasimou2018|Gerasimou (2018)]] separates three deferral motives (indecisiveness, undesirability, overload).
- **Intransitivity and the collective reading.** [[@May1954|May (1954)]] grounds cycles in multi-criteria aggregation; [[@Sprumont2000|Sprumont (2000)]] and [[@Xu2007|Xu & Zhou (2007)]] rationalize cyclic *collective* choice as Nash/Pareto outcomes or game-tree SPNE.

## Cross-paper tensions

**Acyclic vs. transitive vs. complete-but-cyclic as the "right" relaxation.** The members do not agree on which axiom to surrender. [[@Eliaz2006|Eliaz & Ok (2006)]] keep transitivity and drop completeness (regular incomplete $\succ$). [[@Gerasimou2016a|Gerasimou (2016)]] keeps only *acyclicity* of an incomplete $\succ$; [[@Qin2017|Qin (2017)]] argues this is too weak — under a multi-criteria reading $\succ=\bigcap_j\succ_j$, transitivity (which survives intersection) is the principled restriction, and isolates *Elimination by Eliminated* as the exact axiom marking the transitive sub-class. [[@Ehlers2008|Ehlers & Sprumont (2008)]] go the opposite way: keep a single *complete* relation but allow it to be *cyclic*, arguing cyclic choice needs no multiple rationales. [[@May1954|May (1954)]] supplies the structural reason cycles arise at all (Arrow's theorem read at the individual level), siding against transitivity as descriptively valid. These are rival primitives, not notational variants.

**The sharpest tension is the identification of indifference vs. indecisiveness, and it splits the field.** [[@Eliaz2006|Eliaz & Ok (2006)]] recover the incomparable pairs $I(c)$ *only* by exploiting a WARP violation (their criterion presumes $\{x,y\}=c\{x,y\}$ and needs a regularity restriction). [[@Gerasimou2018|Gerasimou (2018)]] explicitly contests this: under maximally-dominant choice, indifference vs. indecisiveness is read off *WARP-consistent* data via deferral, needing no regularity assumption — a direct claim of superiority over Eliaz–Ok on the same identification problem. [[@Sen1997|Sen (1997)]] casts doubt on whether either is fully identifiable, distinguishing *tentative* from *assertive* (non-completable) incompleteness and declining to formalize the latter.

**What does deferral mean?** [[@Gerasimou2016a|Gerasimou (2016)]] models deferral as the empty set and insists it is *conflict-driven* (incomparability), explicitly *ruling out* choice-overload deferral. [[@Gerasimou2018|Gerasimou (2018)]] then re-admits overload (and undesirability) as separate, axiomatically-distinguished sources — partly in tension with the earlier paper's deterministic single-relation parsimony. Both are deterministic, which Gerasimou flags as a tension with the *probabilistic* attraction/compromise evidence.

**Continuity is not free under incompleteness.** [[@Gerasimou2013|Gerasimou (2013)]] shows hemicontinuity of an incomplete preorder forces *fragility* (small perturbations make strictly-ranked options incomparable) and that on connected spaces a bi-hemicontinuous nontrivial preorder is forced *complete* (Schmeidler) — a latent threat to the whole program, since most economic domains are connected. [[@Gerasimou2015|Gerasimou (2015)]] rescues it via additivity, but at the cost of independence-type axioms with known descriptive failures — exactly the axioms the behavioral motivation rejects.

**Individual vs. collective source of the cycle.** [[@May1954|May (1954)]], [[@Sprumont2000|Sprumont (2000)]] and [[@Xu2007|Xu & Zhou (2007)]] treat cyclic/incomplete choice as the footprint of *aggregation* (criteria, agents, game trees), whereas the Eliaz–Ok/Gerasimou line treats it as a property of one agent. The observable data can look identical, so the interpretation is underdetermined.

## Open questions

- **Empirically separating the rival models is untested.** [[@Qin2017|Qin (2017)]] gives a discriminating axiom (Elimination by Eliminated) for transitive vs. acyclic PD but no experiment; [[@Gerasimou2018|Gerasimou (2018)]] gives a sharp split (inserting a dominant option into a large menu breaks overload under MDC but not under OCR) that is likewise unestimated.
- **Strong vs. weak preference reversal.** [[@Eliaz2006|Eliaz & Ok (2006)]] rationalize only the *weak* form and call for experiments separating it from the strong form, which their theory rules out.
- **Deterministic vs. stochastic deferral.** [[@Gerasimou2016a|Gerasimou (2016)]] points to a perception-adjusted Luce complement but leaves the reconciliation open.
- **Recoverability gaps.** Under [[@Gerasimou2018|Gerasimou (2018)]]'s DCR/OCR, the undesirable/too-complex tail of preferences is unrecoverable; the full preorder cannot be identified.
- **Multi-valued and large-domain extensions.** [[@Xu2007|Xu & Zhou (2007)]] cannot rationalize choice *correspondences* by game trees for $|X|\ge 4$; [[@Sprumont2000|Sprumont (2000)]] leaves full Pareto-rationalizability and mixed-strategy Nash uncharacterized.

## Candidate ideas

- **Experimentally adjudicate transitive vs. acyclic partial dominance** by designing menus where Elimination by Eliminated bites, testing [[@Qin2017|Qin (2017)]] against [[@Gerasimou2016a|Gerasimou (2016)]] on real indecisive choice.
- **A "dominant-option insertion" lab test of overload mechanisms** distinguishing incompleteness-driven from complexity-driven deferral in [[@Gerasimou2018|Gerasimou (2018)]] (MDC vs. OCR), using the breakdown prediction as the identifying contrast.
- **Separate strong from weak preference reversal** in a paradigm built to test [[@Eliaz2006|Eliaz & Ok (2006)]]'s claim that the strong form is incompatible with WARNI maximization.
- **A stochastic partial-dominance model** marrying [[@Gerasimou2016a|Gerasimou (2016)]]'s EPD with random-utility / perception-adjusted Luce, recovering an incomplete $\succ$ plus a deferral hazard from probabilistic choice.
- **Estimate an incomplete bivariate preference function** $P$ from market or panel demand data ([[@Gerasimou2010|Gerasimou (2010)]]), using observed WARP violations as the diagnostic for ambivalence and the ambivalence-aversion axiom as a falsifiable restriction.
- **Identify individual vs. collective origins of observed cycles**, designing choice data that discriminate May-style multi-criteria intransitivity ([[@May1954|May (1954)]]) from game-tree/Nash collective rationalization ([[@Xu2007|Xu & Zhou (2007)]]; [[@Sprumont2000|Sprumont (2000)]]).

## Scope

This topic covers the strategic economics of advice: how a seller or intermediary recommends a product whose suitability is privately (im)perfectly known, when the recommendation is corrupted by commissions, sales incentives, emotions, or credulous listeners, and how consumer-protection regulation (disclosure, caps, penalties, refunds, standards of proof) should respond. The unifying object is *cheap-talk or persuasion advice with an endogenous bias* — the divergence between adviser and customer is not exogenous but generated by the very institutions (compensation, liability, anticipatory utility) the model studies. The boundary excludes pure credence-goods diagnosis without communication, and excludes belief-based welfare in non-advice settings (which lives under anticipatory-utility).

## Sub-themes

- **Internal agency as the source of misselling.** [[@Inderst2009|Inderst & Ottaviani (2009)]] makes the corrupting commission an output of a *multi-task* firm–agent problem (prospecting vs. advising), with an attribution friction that prevents rewarding the two tasks separately.
- **Competition through commissions, and disclosure remedies.** [[@Inderst2012|Inderst & Ottaviani (2012)]] embeds the adviser in Hotelling-style competition where firms bid for the recommendation via kickbacks, and asks whether mandatory disclosure / caps / penalties help.
- **Self-imposed commitment substituting for honest advice.** [[@Inderst2013|Inderst & Ottaviani (2013)]] shows generous return/refund terms as a commitment device that makes cheap talk credible — until customers turn credulous.
- **Choosing the noisiness of a lie.** [[@Drugov2019|Drugov & Troya-Martinez (2019)]] adds a *second* distortion margin (vagueness) alongside bias, and a regulator with a statistical standard of proof.
- **Benevolent distortion via emotions.** [[@Koszegi2006|Kőszegi (2006)]] strips out conflicting interests entirely: a *perfectly aligned* adviser still distorts because the listener has anticipatory emotions.

## Cross-paper tensions

**Where does the adviser's bias come from?** The four "conflict" papers disagree at the root. In [[@Inderst2009|Inderst & Ottaviani (2009)]] the bias is the price of motivating hidden prospecting effort; in [[@Inderst2012|Inderst & Ottaviani (2012)]] it is the equilibrium of firms bidding commissions; in [[@Drugov2019|Drugov & Troya-Martinez (2019)]] it is a profit-maximizing lie disciplined only by an expected fine. [[@Koszegi2006|Kőszegi (2006)]] is the sharpest contrast in the whole cluster: there is *no* commission, *no* divergent interest, *no* lying motive — the adviser maximizes the listener's utility and still slants toward the good-news action, because anticipatory utility creates an informational externality the benevolent adviser fails to internalize. So bias is not intrinsically a conflict-of-interest phenomenon, which directly questions whether commission-targeting regulation addresses the right friction.

**Does transparency help?** The flagship policy tension. The naive prior — disclose commissions, advice improves — is contradicted by [[@Inderst2012|Inderst & Ottaviani (2012)]]: disclosure chills the *cost-efficient* firm asymmetrically, so for an adviser whose suitability concern $w$ exceeds a threshold $w^{D}$, mandated disclosure *lowers* welfare; caps and penalties can be perverse for the same reason. [[@Koszegi2006|Kőszegi (2006)]] supplies an independent anti-disclosure force: under certifiable disclosure the aligned adviser reveals good news but *conceals* bad news (feigning ignorance), so even forced verifiability does not unravel to truth. Yet [[@Inderst2013|Inderst & Ottaviani (2013)]] cuts the other way — a *minimum statutory refund* ($\underline q \ge k$) strictly raises consumer and social surplus for credulous buyers and never harms rational ones — a rare unambiguously-beneficial mandate, but only because the instrument is a contract term, not a disclosure.

**Rational vs. credulous listeners — and which margin moves.** [[@Inderst2013|Inderst & Ottaviani (2013)]] and [[@Drugov2019|Drugov & Troya-Martinez (2019)]] both partition consumers into rational and credulous, but draw opposite policy conclusions. For Inderst-Ottaviani, *competition policy* helps the rational and is impotent for the credulous, while *consumer protection* (refund floors) helps the credulous; for Drugov-Troya-Martinez, all rational revenue gains vanish ($\tilde\beta=\beta$ in equilibrium) so the *entire* misselling harm falls on the credulous share $c$, and the optimal standard of proof tightens as $c$ shrinks. [[@Koszegi2006|Kőszegi (2006)]] notes that a *naïve* (vs. sophisticated) principal flips his welfare ranking — naïveté can dominate — which the conflict models never reconcile.

**Binary advice vs. choosing precision.** [[@Drugov2019|Drugov & Troya-Martinez (2019)]] explicitly identifies a modeling limit of the Inderst-Ottaviani program: there advice is binary ("buy"/"don't"), so only the *bias* margin exists. Allowing the seller to also choose *noise* yields the bias–noise complementarity (vague lies), a phenomenon invisible in the binary-recommendation models — meaning the suitability-standard machinery of [[@Inderst2009|Inderst & Ottaviani (2009)]] may understate manipulation when vagueness is feasible.

**Can self-regulation reach the planner's standard?** [[@Inderst2009|Inderst & Ottaviani (2009)]] says no — a firm selling through an agent picks a strictly lower penalty and standard than the planner because the agent's rent is a private cost but a social transfer. [[@Inderst2013|Inderst & Ottaviani (2013)]] says yes for rational markets — generous refunds are *voluntarily* chosen to commit to honest talk. The reconciling variable is whether the commitment device (refund vs. liability) is contractible with the customer or only with the regulator.

## Open questions

- How to make commission/refund *disclosure credible* when firms substitute toward implicit, soft, or in-kind incentives ([[@Inderst2009|Inderst & Ottaviani (2009)]]; [[@Inderst2012|Inderst & Ottaviani (2012)]]).
- A *uniform* regulatory penalty $L$ is miscalibrated across firms that organize sales differently (single vs. separated agents, resale intensity) — what is the organization-contingent optimal policy ([[@Inderst2009|Inderst & Ottaviani (2009)]])?
- Mixed populations of rational and credulous customers are unsolved in both [[@Inderst2013|Inderst & Ottaviani (2013)]] and partially in [[@Drugov2019|Drugov & Troya-Martinez (2019)]]; the unobserved-noise ("fine print") case is "technically very hard."
- Whether reputation/competition and *endogenous adviser selection* mitigate or worsen distortion is named as future work in [[@Koszegi2006|Kőszegi (2006)]] and [[@Inderst2012|Inderst & Ottaviani (2012)]] (tied vs. independent advisers).
- Disclosure as *moral licensing* for biased advice (Cain-Loewenstein-Moore evidence) is flagged but unmodeled in [[@Inderst2012|Inderst & Ottaviani (2012)]].

## Candidate ideas

- **Vague lies meet suitability standards.** Merge the noise margin of [[@Drugov2019|Drugov & Troya-Martinez (2019)]] into the multi-task firm of [[@Inderst2009|Inderst & Ottaviani (2009)]]: does an adviser paid a commission also *blur* advice, and does a binary suitability standard under-regulate when vagueness is contractible?
- **Emotional misselling.** Replace the benevolent adviser of [[@Koszegi2006|Kőszegi (2006)]] with a commission-paid one and ask whether anticipatory-emotion distortion and commission bias *reinforce or offset* — a test of whether targeting commissions even moves the bias.
- **When is disclosure self-defeating? A unified taxonomy.** Reconcile the perverse-disclosure result of [[@Inderst2012|Inderst & Ottaviani (2012)]] with the conceal-bad-news result of [[@Koszegi2006|Kőszegi (2006)]] into a single condition on the adviser's suitability concern $w$ and listener sophistication.
- **Refund floors vs. commission caps, head to head.** Compare the unambiguously-good minimum refund of [[@Inderst2013|Inderst & Ottaviani (2013)]] against the ambiguous cap/penalty of [[@Inderst2012|Inderst & Ottaviani (2012)]] in one market with both contract and commission margins.
- **Mixed-credulity regulation.** Solve the rational/credulous *mixture* left open by [[@Inderst2013|Inderst & Ottaviani (2013)]] and [[@Drugov2019|Drugov & Troya-Martinez (2019)]] and characterize how the optimal standard of proof and refund floor co-move with the credulous share $c$.
- **Endogenous adviser choice.** Let customers select advisers (the open agenda of [[@Koszegi2006|Kőszegi (2006)]]); does competition for the relationship discipline commission-driven misselling à la [[@Inderst2012|Inderst & Ottaviani (2012)]]?

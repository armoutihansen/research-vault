## Scope

This topic covers status signaling through *visible* consumption: the idea that spending on goods others can observe (clothing, jewelry, cars) is a costly signal of unobserved income, so a household's optimal display depends on the income distribution of its reference group. It spans the canonical signaling model and its tests across populations (U.S. racial groups, South Africa), the measurement of good "visibility," and the macro-distributional implications — expenditure cascades that link rising top-income inequality to falling savings. The boundary: it is about *outcome-visible relative-position* consumption, not the inequity-aversion or peer-effects-identification literatures that border it.

## Sub-themes

- **The reference-group signaling model and its racial-gap tests.** [[@Charles2009|Charles, Hurst & Roussanov (2009)]] build the Spence/Glazer-Konrad separating model where status $s_i = E[y_i \mid c_i^*, k]$ and visible spending falls in reference-group mean income; [[@Kaus2013|Kaus (2013)]] ports the identical specification to South Africa.
- **Measuring visibility.** [[@Heffetz2011|Heffetz (2011)]] turns "visibility" from an assumption into a survey-elicited index (Vindex) and shows it predicts up to a third of cross-good income-elasticity variation — the instrument later used by everyone else.
- **Inequality, expenditure cascades, and saving.** [[@Frank2014|Frank, Levine & Dijk (2014)]] formalize the recursive cascade $C_i = k(1-a)Y_i + a\,C_{i+1}$ (comparison *up* the ladder); [[@Bertrand2016|Bertrand & Morse (2016)]] test it on the visibility gradient of nonrich budget shares.
- **Exogenous-shock identification.** [[@Kuhn2011|Kuhn et al. (2011)]] use the Dutch Postcode Lottery's randomized neighbor windfalls to identify localized social effects on visible durables (cars) cleanly.

## Cross-paper tensions

**The reference group is assumed, not estimated — and the data fight back.** The signaling model needs a *common utility function* so that the negative reference-income relationship holds both across and within groups. [[@Charles2009|Charles et al. (2009)]] find it holds within each U.S. race ($\delta_1 \approx -0.60$ for Whites). [[@Kaus2013|Kaus (2013)]] reproduces the *across*-group result in South Africa but the within-group test **fails for Whites** ($\alpha \approx +0.10$, vs. $-0.26$ for Blacks). This is the sharpest tension: the same model that fits cross-group gaps is internally inconsistent within a group, implying different groups signal through different *environments/devices* — which the model assumes away.

**Comparison up vs. comparison to peers.** [[@Frank2014|Frank et al. (2014)]] and [[@Bertrand2016|Bertrand & Morse (2016)]] insist the impulse propagates *downward* from the top (cascades, top-percentile income as regressor), explicitly distinguishing themselves from the Luttmer "keeping up with the Joneses" peer-comparison reading. But [[@Charles2009|Charles et al. (2009)]] and [[@Kaus2013|Kaus (2013)]] use *mean* own-group income, a peer aggregate, and [[@Kuhn2011|Kuhn et al. (2011)]] find effects only from *immediate* neighbors. The members disagree on whether the relevant comparator is the local top, the group mean, or the next-door house — and the geographic resolution that would adjudicate (state/province/county) is too coarse in every observational paper.

**Visibility vs. income elasticity as the organizing dimension.** [[@Heffetz2011|Heffetz (2011)]] shows visibility and elasticity are entangled — visible goods *are* luxuries by construction in his model ($e_v > 1$). [[@Bertrand2016|Bertrand & Morse (2016)]] try to break them apart and find budget-share responses sort on *visibility, not elasticity* (flat for elasticity), but the vehicle anomaly — high-visibility cars show no response — directly contradicts [[@Kuhn2011|Kuhn et al. (2011)]], for whom cars are the *only* good that responds.

**Identification: rejected-alternatives vs. randomization.** Every observational member lacks an instrument for reference-group income and rests on either the PIH proxy plus falsification tests ([[@Charles2009|Charles et al. (2009)]], [[@Kaus2013|Kaus (2013)]]) or a one-by-one rejection of noncausal stories ([[@Bertrand2016|Bertrand & Morse (2016)]]); [[@Frank2014|Frank et al. (2014)]] cannot even estimate $a$. Only [[@Kuhn2011|Kuhn et al. (2011)]] has clean exogeneity — and it cannot separate *endogenous* (imitation) from *exogenous* (neighbor-income) social effects, nor rule out resale/information channels for the car result.

## Open questions

- At what level is the reference group actually defined, and why does the within-group prediction fail for some groups (Whites in [[@Kaus2013|Kaus (2013)]]) but not others? Is "being White" itself the signal?
- Is the comparator the local top, the group mean, or immediate neighbors — and does the answer vary by good and by income level (the visibility-elasticity link bites only in upper quintiles per [[@Heffetz2011|Heffetz (2011)]])?
- Why do cars cascade for [[@Kuhn2011|Kuhn et al. (2011)]] yet not for [[@Bertrand2016|Bertrand & Morse (2016)]]? Durable-adjustment timing, resale, or measurement?
- Is context-responsive spending myopic or rational? [[@Frank2014|Frank et al. (2014)]] leave this explicitly open.
- Does the visibility ranking and the cascade transport across countries, time, and finer good categories ([[@Heffetz2011|Heffetz (2011)]], [[@Kaus2013|Kaus (2013)]])?

## Candidate ideas

- **Structurally estimate $a$** in Frank's cascade recursion using neighbor-level windfall variation à la [[@Kuhn2011|Kuhn et al. (2011)]], turning [[@Frank2014|Frank et al. (2014)]]'s illustrative parameter into an identified social multiplier.
- **A within-group-failure test of the common-utility axiom**: replicate [[@Kaus2013|Kaus (2013)]] across many countries to map *which* groups violate the negative reference-income slope, modeling group-specific signaling environments explicitly.
- **Adjudicate the comparator** (top vs. mean vs. neighbor) by combining [[@Bertrand2016|Bertrand & Morse (2016)]]'s top-income design with [[@Charles2009|Charles et al. (2009)]]'s mean-income design on the same fine-geography panel.
- **Resolve the car anomaly** by separating resale/information from status channels for visible durables, reconciling [[@Kuhn2011|Kuhn et al. (2011)]] with [[@Bertrand2016|Bertrand & Morse (2016)]].
- **Endogenize visibility**: test [[@Heffetz2011|Heffetz (2011)]]'s reverse-causality worry — do high-elasticity goods *become* more visible? — with a panel Vindex.
- **Lab/experimental relative-position test** manipulating reference-group income directly to recover the signaling vs. peer-comparison mechanism that observational members cannot separate (extending [[@Kuhn2011|Kuhn et al. (2011)]]).

## Scope

This topic covers rank-order tournaments and relative-performance evaluation (RPE) as incentive devices: schemes in which an agent's pay depends on her *ordinal rank* or her output *relative to peers* rather than on absolute output. It spans the foundational rationales for RPE (efficient effort, filtering of common shocks, monitoring economies), the contracting frictions it introduces (sabotage, doping, inequity, rating compression), and the design margins — prize spreads, penalties, differentiation — through which a principal manages them. The boundary excludes pure individualistic moral hazard (piece rates without comparison), career-concerns / reputational incentives, and the broader risk–incentive tradeoff, which form sibling topics.

## Sub-themes

- **Foundations: why pay on rank or relative output.** [[@Lazear1981|Lazear & Rosen (1981)]] establish that risk-neutral rank-order tournaments replicate the efficient piece rate, so the choice turns on monitoring cost, and that effort depends only on the prize *spread* $W_1-W_2$, not levels. [[@Nalebuff1983|Nalebuff & Stiglitz (1983)]] add the *flexibility/insurance* rationale: relative ranking filters a common environmental shock $\theta$, keeping incentives compatible without recontracting. [[@Holmstrom1982|Holmström (1982)]] subsumes both under the sufficient-statistic / informativeness principle.
- **The informational critique of competition.** [[@Holmstrom1982|Holmström (1982)]] argues "competition per se is worthless" — RPE has value only when peer output is informative about an agent's own action through common uncertainty, and cardinal aggregates dominate ordinal rank.
- **Dark-side behaviors RPE invites.** [[@Krakel2007|Kräkel (2007)]] models doping/cheating as an illegal complementary input; [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]] and [[@Krakel2007|Kräkel (2007)]] both touch sabotage; envy/compassion enter effort and design in [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]].
- **RPE in practice — subjective evaluation.** [[@Kampkotter2018|Kampkötter & Sliwka (2018)]] take RPE to field data: supervisors who *compress* ratings (low differentiation) depress effort and shrink bonus pools, with effects moderated by span of control, measurement precision, and task interdependence.

## Cross-paper tensions

The sharpest tension is **the very value of rank**. [[@Lazear1981|Lazear & Rosen (1981)]] and [[@Nalebuff1983|Nalebuff & Stiglitz (1983)]] treat ordinal rank-order prizes as (near-)optimal incentive instruments. [[@Holmstrom1982|Holmström (1982)]] cuts against this: under his sufficient-statistic logic, the optimal sharing rule conditions on a *cardinal* aggregate (a precision-weighted peer average), and ordinal rank is generally informationally wasteful — "competition per se is worthless," valuable only as a vehicle to extract information about common shocks. Lazear–Rosen instead justify rank precisely because *measuring* cardinal output is costly; the disagreement is thus about whether the relevant constraint is informational (Holmström) or a monitoring/measurement cost (Lazear–Rosen) that the theory leaves exogenous.

A second tension is **first-best implementability**. Standard symmetric tournaments are claimed to implement first-best effort under risk neutrality ([[@Lazear1981|Lazear & Rosen (1981)]]). [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]] overturn this once Fehr–Schmidt inequity aversion is added: an irreducible *participation effect* (inequity costs $(\alpha+\beta)\Delta w$) means endogenous prizes can no longer reach first-best, even though envy *raises* effort for fixed prizes. So the same friction (other-regard) is incentive-enhancing or welfare-reducing depending on whether prizes are fixed or optimized — a sign reversal the foundational papers cannot see because they assume selfish agents.

A third, subtler tension concerns **whether more competitive pressure is good**. The foundational logic says a larger spread $\Delta w$ buys more effort. But [[@Krakel2007|Kräkel (2007)]] shows a wide spread also fuels doping (and a high loser prize $w_2$ deters it via the base-salary effect), and [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]] show envy amplifies *sabotage* alongside effort. [[@Kampkotter2018|Kampkötter & Sliwka (2018)]] then find empirically that differentiation *reverses sign* at the lowest hierarchical level and where cooperation matters — directly contradicting a monotone "more spread, more output" reading and echoing Nalebuff–Stiglitz's own worry that contests "give no incentive to cooperate."

Finally, an **existence / equilibrium** tension runs through the theory: [[@Lazear1981|Lazear & Rosen (1981)]] assume Nash existence after a sufficient-variance condition; [[@Nalebuff1983|Nalebuff & Stiglitz (1983)]] show that as noise $\to 0$ the marginal incentive $\bar g\to\infty$ and *no* symmetric pure-strategy equilibrium exists ("a mile is as good as a miss"), repaired only by penalties or winning-by-a-gap; [[@Krakel2007|Kräkel (2007)]] likewise must specialize to quadratic costs and uniform noise to guarantee existence. The neat comparative statics are thus conditional on a knife-edge the literature handles unevenly.

## Open questions

- **Effort vs. skill, and effort on variance.** [[@Lazear1981|Lazear & Rosen (1981)]] model only prior skill investment, not post-skill effort, and explicitly exclude agents who manipulate the *variance* of output — a gambling-for-rank margin still open.
- **Equilibrium multiplicity under cheating.** [[@Krakel2007|Kräkel (2007)]] characterizes only the no-doping equilibrium and a binary doping choice; the mutual-doping and asymmetric configurations, and a *continuous* doping intensity trading detection against health cost, are uncharacterized. The organizer's design problem (does society even want to deter doping?) is unsolved.
- **Reference groups and the scope of inequity.** [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]] assume envy/compassion vanish under lateral promotion and that reservation utility is independent of $\alpha,\beta$; how reference groups actually form across vertical/lateral boundaries is unmodeled.
- **Disentangling the compression channels.** [[@Kampkotter2018|Kampkötter & Sliwka (2018)]] cannot separate the cooperation explanation from career-concerns (Gibbons–Murphy) for the low-level reversal, use bonus as a performance proxy, and cannot fully rule out that firms place strong differentiators where effort is profitable.
- **The adoption puzzle.** [[@Nalebuff1983|Nalebuff & Stiglitz (1983)]] flag that RPE is "less widespread than its advantages suggest," conjecturing unmodeled fairness/social-incentive frictions — an open empirical hook that [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]] and [[@Kampkotter2018|Kampkötter & Sliwka (2018)]] only partly answer.

## Candidate ideas

- **Ordinal vs. cardinal RPE, tested.** Design an experiment pitting rank-order prizes against a Holmström-style precision-weighted peer-average bonus to measure the welfare gap between ordinal and cardinal RPE, adjudicating [[@Holmstrom1982|Holmström (1982)]] against [[@Lazear1981|Lazear & Rosen (1981)]] and [[@Nalebuff1983|Nalebuff & Stiglitz (1983)]] while varying common-shock variance $\sigma_\theta^2$.
- **Continuous doping with endogenous prizes.** Extend [[@Krakel2007|Kräkel (2007)]] to a continuous doping intensity and solve the organizer's prize-design problem, deriving whether a higher loser prize $w_2$ (cheaper than testing) is the optimal cheating-deterrent and whether competitive balance can make underdog doping organizer-optimal.
- **Inequity-aware tournament design under limited liability.** Merge the participation effect of [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]] with limited-liability constraints (Demougin–Fluet) to characterize the second-best spread when both inequity costs *and* sabotage scale with $\Delta w$.
- **Differentiation, cooperation, and span of control — causal field test.** Use a randomized or quasi-experimental shock to forced-ranking policy to separate the cooperation channel from career concerns behind the low-level reversal in [[@Kampkotter2018|Kampkötter & Sliwka (2018)]], with direct output (not bonus) as the outcome.
- **The adoption puzzle revisited.** Empirically test the Nalebuff–Stiglitz conjecture ([[@Nalebuff1983|Nalebuff & Stiglitz (1983)]]) that fairness/envy frictions ([[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]]) explain the under-use of RPE, by relating measured workforce inequity aversion to firms' adoption of relative-performance pay.
- **ML-benchmarked tournament effort.** Use a predictive-completeness diagnostic to ask how much of observed tournament effort the spread-only Lazear–Rosen prediction misses versus an envy-augmented [[@Grund2005|Grund & Sliwka (2005)]] model, quantifying the behavioral residual in $\Delta w$-driven effort.

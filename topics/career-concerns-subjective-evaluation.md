## Scope

This topic covers **implicit incentives** — those that arise outside an explicit pay-for-performance contract — and the **distortions of non-contractible signals** on which any contract must ultimately rest. Two engines recur: the labour-market/reputational channel where current output reshapes future wages through Bayesian learning about ability ([[@Holmstrom1999|Holmström (1999)]], [[@Gibbons1992|Gibbons & Murphy (1992)]]), and the agency-of-the-evaluator channel where the very signal feeding the contract is reported by a biased or colluding human ([[@Prendergast1996|Prendergast & Topel (1996)]], [[@Faure-Grimaud2003|Faure-Grimaud, Laffont & Martimort (2003)]]). The boundary: these are dynamic/multi-tier agency models where the *informativeness of the performance signal is itself endogenous* to incentives — distinct from tournaments (relative pay as the device) and from the pure risk-incentive tradeoff (signal noise taken as exogenous).

## Sub-themes

- **Career concerns as signal-jamming.** Reputation in a competitive market disciplines effort because wages track perceived ability, but the effort path is generically inefficient — decaying, front-loaded, and risk-distorting: [[@Holmstrom1999|Holmström (1999)]].
- **Total incentives = implicit + explicit.** The optimal contract treats career concerns and contractual slope as substitutes, predicting steepening pay-performance toward retirement: [[@Gibbons1992|Gibbons & Murphy (1992)]].
- **Subjective evaluation and the evaluator's own agency.** When the signal is reported by a supervisor with private preferences, favoritism ([[@Prendergast1996|Prendergast & Topel (1996)]]) or risk-averse collusion ([[@Faure-Grimaud2003|Faure-Grimaud, Laffont & Martimort (2003)]]) corrupts it, and the firm responds by *throttling the contract* (pay compression, bureaucratic rules, output distortion).
- **Effort-information conflict in project/sales contracts.** A success bonus and a truthful-reporting bonus collide under hidden effort + limited liability, so firms over-fund bad products ([[@Simester2010|Simester & Zhang (2010)]]) or overstock inventory to sharpen the effort signal ([[@Dai2013|Dai & Jerath (2013)]]).

## Cross-paper tensions

**Is time/dynamics a friend or foe of incentives?** [[@Holmstrom1999|Holmström (1999)]] shows reputation can *substitute* for contracts but delivers $a^*\le\bar a$ with young workers over-investing — implicit incentives are real but inefficient. [[@Gibbons1992|Gibbons & Murphy (1992)]] turns this into a design lever: because the career-concern term $\delta(1-b_2)\sigma_\eta^2/(\sigma_\eta^2+\sigma_\varepsilon^2)$ fades with tenure, the *explicit* slope should rise toward retirement. The sharpest tension is internal to the latter: the steepening prediction (H1) holds in the cross-section but goes statistically insignificant once firm-specific elasticities are allowed ($\beta_i=.0297$, $t=1.4$) — so the model's signature comparative static survives only under the homogeneity restriction it imposes.

**Where does the noise come from — nature or the evaluator?** The clean career-concerns models treat $\sigma_\varepsilon^2$ as exogenous output noise. [[@Prendergast1996|Prendergast & Topel (1996)]] detonates that assumption: the bias variance $\tau^2\sigma_\eta^2$ is *generated by the contract itself* ($\partial^2 b/\partial\eta\,\partial\tau=1$), so a steeper slope manufactures the very noise it fights. This directly contradicts the Gibbons–Murphy logic of dialing the explicit slope up: under favoritism, steepening is self-defeating, and the firm rationally *flattens* below even the noisy benchmark. [[@Faure-Grimaud2003|Faure-Grimaud, Laffont & Martimort (2003)]] sharpens the friction's source further — the obstacle is the supervisor's risk aversion $r$, with collusion costless at $r=0$ and supervision useless at $r=\infty$ — a continuous interpolation that Prendergast–Topel's favoritism story leaves as an unmodeled altruism primitive $\eta^i$.

**Rational design vs. behavioral pathology.** [[@Simester2010|Simester & Zhang (2010)]] explicitly argues failure-to-kill is *not* escalation-of-commitment bias but an incentive incompatibility (a termination bonus $X$ erodes the success bonus $W$). Yet they concede optimism *amplifies* the tension — leaving unresolved whether the bias is truly orthogonal to the contract or interacts with it.

**Does the firm distort the real economy to buy signal quality?** [[@Dai2013|Dai & Jerath (2013)]] finds agency motivates *overstocking* ($Q>Q^{FB}$) purely to make high demand observable — inventory as an information device. [[@Faure-Grimaud2003|Faure-Grimaud, Laffont & Martimort (2003)]] symmetrically finds output of the inefficient type distorted *downward* to deter collusion. Both bend a productive decision to serve signal informativeness, but in opposite directions — a tension over whether endogenizing the signal pushes the firm to expand or contract real activity.

## Open questions

- **Combining implicit and explicit incentives dynamically.** [[@Holmstrom1999|Holmström (1999)]] explicitly leaves a full dynamic model with both contracts *and* career concerns undeveloped; [[@Gibbons1992|Gibbons & Murphy (1992)]] only proves the weak $b_t^*<b_T^*$ and conjectures monotonic steepening.
- **Internal labour markets.** Career concerns via promotion/intra-firm competition are flagged as "the most natural application" yet unmodeled ([[@Gibbons1992|Gibbons & Murphy (1992)]]); [[@Prendergast1996|Prendergast & Topel (1996)]] ties wages to *jobs* rather than people but leaves the boundary informal.
- **Does reputation discipline the evaluator?** [[@Prendergast1996|Prendergast & Topel (1996)]] asks whether a supervisor's own reputation concern (fairness, anti-discrimination) could eliminate favoritism — exactly the Holmström/Gibbons mechanism applied one tier up, but never closed.
- **Dynamics and renegotiation of collusion/censoring.** [[@Faure-Grimaud2003|Faure-Grimaud, Laffont & Martimort (2003)]] is static with strong commitment (renegotiation would re-insure the supervisor); [[@Dai2013|Dai & Jerath (2013)]] is single-period — both leave multi-period self-enforcement open.
- **Limited liability as the load-bearing assumption.** [[@Simester2010|Simester & Zhang (2010)]] notes large enough punishments dissolve the kill-tension; how negative-wage bounds drive results is open across the contracting members.

## Candidate ideas

- **Steepening-toward-retirement vs. favoritism:** build a model nesting [[@Gibbons1992|Gibbons & Murphy (1992)]] career-concern fade with [[@Prendergast1996|Prendergast & Topel (1996)]] endogenous bias, deriving when the optimal explicit slope rises vs. flattens over tenure, then re-test on the CEO panel with firm-specific elasticities.
- **Reputation one tier up:** dynamize [[@Prendergast1996|Prendergast & Topel (1996)]] by giving the supervisor [[@Holmstrom1999|Holmström (1999)]]-style career concerns, asking whether evaluator reputation substitutes for bureaucratic rules and whether it generates the conjectured *positive* discrimination.
- **Risk aversion as the unifying friction:** map [[@Faure-Grimaud2003|Faure-Grimaud, Laffont & Martimort (2003)]] continuous-$r$ collusion onto the favoritism primitive $\eta^i$ of [[@Prendergast1996|Prendergast & Topel (1996)]], testing whether evaluator risk attitude rather than altruism is what bends pay compression.
- **Real-decision distortion as a signal-buying menu:** unify the upward overstocking of [[@Dai2013|Dai & Jerath (2013)]] and the downward output distortion of [[@Faure-Grimaud2003|Faure-Grimaud, Laffont & Martimort (2003)]] into one model where the firm chooses real activity to maximize signal informativeness, predicting the sign from demand/cost primitives.
- **Bias vs. pathology, identified:** design an experiment separating the [[@Simester2010|Simester & Zhang (2010)]] incentive-incompatibility account of failure-to-kill from escalation-of-commitment by varying $W/X$ and prior optimism, measuring whether bias and contract interact as the model concedes.
- **Career concerns under demand censoring:** extend [[@Dai2013|Dai & Jerath (2013)]] to a multi-period setting where past sales also update the market's belief about the salesperson's ability, asking whether implicit incentives reduce the firm's costly overstocking.

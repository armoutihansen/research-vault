## Scope
This topic covers outcome-based theories of fairness — preferences defined over the *distribution* of material payoffs — and the experimental and structural-econometric work that tests them. It spans the two canonical difference-aversion models ([[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] pairwise inequity aversion, [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]] relative-share ERC), their social-welfare/efficiency rivals ([[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]], [[@Andreoni2002|Andreoni & Miller (2002)]], [[@Fisman2007|Fisman et al. (2007)]]), the type-based and reciprocity models that contest a purely distributional reading ([[@Levine1998|Levine (1998)]], [[@Cox2004|Cox (2004)]], [[@Engelmann2010|Engelmann & Strobel (2010)]]), and the structural estimation of preference parameters and population heterogeneity ([[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin et al. (2019)]], [[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare et al. (2008)]], [[@Bellemare2011|Bellemare et al. (2011)]]). Its boundary is *outcome-based* preference: the intention-based reciprocity program (Rabin; Dufwenberg–Kirchsteiger) enters only as the discriminating foil, not as the subject.

## Sub-themes
- **Difference-aversion / inequity-aversion models.** The reference forms in which agents dislike unequal allocations. [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] penalizes pairwise payoff gaps, $u_i = x_i - \alpha_i\max\{x_j-x_i,0\} - \beta_i\max\{x_i-x_j,0\}$ with $\alpha_i \ge \beta_i$; [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]] instead makes utility concave in one's *relative share* $\sigma_i = x_i / \sum_j x_j$, peaking at $1/n$. [[@Fehr2006|Fehr & Schmidt (2006)]] and [[@Fehr2006a|Fehr & Schmidt (2006a)]] are the canonical surveys cataloguing this class.
- **Efficiency / social-welfare and revealed-preference models.** The rival reading that people raise total surplus (weighting the worst-off) rather than shrink differences. [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]] nest competitive, difference-averse, and social-welfare preferences in one three-parameter form and find social welfare dominates; [[@Andreoni2002|Andreoni & Miller (2002)]] and [[@Fisman2007|Fisman et al. (2007)]] recover individual CES utilities over own-vs-other payoff from budget-set dictator games, GARP-consistent and tilted toward efficiency ($\rho>0$).
- **Type-based, reciprocity, and intention-control models.** Outcome-based distribution is insufficient: [[@Levine1998|Levine (1998)]] makes the weight on an opponent depend on the opponent's inferred *type*; [[@Cox2004|Cox (2004)]] decomposes transfers into trust, reciprocity, and unconditional altruism via a triadic design; [[@Engelmann2010|Engelmann & Strobel (2010)]] set inequality aversion in direct *opposition* to reciprocity.
- **Structural estimation and choice noise.** Fitting preference parameters in random-utility frameworks. [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin et al. (2019)]] estimate a piecewise-linear model fusing distribution and reciprocity at representative-agent, finite-mixture, and individual levels; [[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare et al. (2008)]] and [[@Bellemare2011|Bellemare et al. (2011)]] estimate nonlinear, heterogeneous inequity aversion by maximum simulated likelihood, jointly modeling elicited subjective beliefs.
- **Population heterogeneity and types.** Who holds which preference, and how stable. [[@Andreoni2002|Andreoni & Miller (2002)]] (selfish/Rawlsian/utilitarian), [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin et al. (2019)]] (three temporally stable types, *no* selfish type), and [[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare et al. (2008)]]/[[@Bellemare2011|Bellemare et al. (2011)]] (representative Dutch samples — young/educated are most selfish, student calibrations understate population inequity aversion).
- **Reference point and relative position.** What counts as the neutral allocation. The egalitarian split is the default in [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] and [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]], but [[@Kuziemko2014|Kuziemko et al. (2014)]] show *ordinal rank* — specifically last-place aversion — enters utility nonlinearly, and [[@Fershtman2001|Fershtman & Gneezy (2001)]] show the relevant comparison group is conditioned by social identity (ethnicity, gender).

## Cross-paper tensions
- **Pairwise gaps vs. relative share (Fehr–Schmidt vs. ERC).** The two difference-aversion models disagree on the *reference object*. [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] compares $i$ to each opponent *separately*, so $i$ cares about the distribution among others; [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]] compares only to the group *average*, so $i$ is indifferent to inequality among others holding own share fixed. [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]] (54% sacrifice to equalize three-way payoffs) and [[@Fehr2006|Fehr & Schmidt (2006)]] (third-party punishment; the $(575,575,575)$ vs $(900,300,600)$ choice) report multiperson evidence that favors pairwise comparison and breaks the ERC average-reference assumption.
- **Difference aversion vs. efficiency/social welfare.** The heart of the topic. [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]] argue the canonical ultimatum/PD menu cannot tell difference aversion from social welfare plus reciprocity because inequality-reducing Pareto damage is confounded with retaliation; their diagnostic games find difference aversion "weak and rare" (0 of 36 chose $(0,0)$ over $(800,200)$) while subjects make inequality-*increasing* efficient sacrifices. [[@Fisman2007|Fisman et al. (2007)]] independently recover a majority with efficiency-leaning CES ($\rho>0$). This directly challenges the $\sigma<0$ disadvantageous term that [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] and [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]] make load-bearing.
- **Distribution as a literal preference vs. an as-if shorthand for reciprocity.** [[@Engelmann2010|Engelmann & Strobel (2010)]] design a moonlighting game where punishing a "poor pickpocket" is reciprocal but *anti*-inequality-averse; reciprocity wins decisively, and they argue inequality aversion only "works" because designers conventionally make the kind poor and the unkind rich. [[@Levine1998|Levine (1998)]] reaches a parallel conclusion structurally — pure altruism ($\lambda=0$) *cannot* fit ultimatum data; belief/type-dependent weights are essential. [[@Cox2004|Cox (2004)]] shows raw investment-game transfers conflate trust, reciprocity, and unconditional altruism, so single games "documenting" inequity aversion test a compound hypothesis.
- **Linear vs. nonlinear inequity aversion.** [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] assume linearity for tractability but concede dictator evidence of nonlinear advantageous-inequality aversion. [[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare et al. (2008)]] estimate significant *concave* disadvantageous-inequity aversion ($\alpha_2<0$) — but, tellingly, the nonlinearity vanishes once rational expectations replace elicited subjective beliefs, so the functional-form verdict is entangled with the belief assumption.
- **Beliefs assumed vs. beliefs elicited.** [[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare et al. (2008)]] and [[@Bellemare2011|Bellemare et al. (2011)]] show that imposing rational expectations badly distorts recovered preferences (rational expectations puts 67% mass on zero offers; subjective-expectations models fit and forecast far better). This is a methodological tension cutting *across* every structural estimate that plugs in aggregate acceptance rates rather than measuring beliefs.
- **Egalitarian reference point vs. rank / identity.** [[@Kuziemko2014|Kuziemko et al. (2014)]] show second-to-last players in a six-player dictator game give *up* the rank ladder, holding constant own income, surplus, *and* the Fehr–Schmidt inequality terms — a shape feature invisible to two/three-player designs and unaccounted for by [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]], [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]], and [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]]. [[@Fershtman2001|Fershtman & Gneezy (2001)]] show the comparison group itself is identity-conditioned (stereotype-driven mistrust of Eastern men, a male-only phenomenon), which no payoff-distribution model encodes. Both pressure the unmodeled reference-group problem that [[@Fehr2006a|Fehr & Schmidt (2006a)]] flag as "an important unsolved problem."
- **No selfish type vs. selfish young/educated.** [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin et al. (2019)]] find *no* purely selfish type at any level of aggregation in a Swiss student sample, while [[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare et al. (2008)]]/[[@Bellemare2011|Bellemare et al. (2011)]] find young, highly educated subjects are the *most* selfish (even competitive, $\beta<0$) in a representative population — a direct sample-composition tension about how widespread other-regarding preference really is.

## Open questions
- **Who is the reference agent / reference group?** Flagged as unresolved by [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]], [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]], and "an important unsolved problem" in [[@Fehr2006a|Fehr & Schmidt (2006a)]]; sharpened by [[@Kuziemko2014|Kuziemko et al. (2014)]] (rank) and [[@Fershtman2001|Fershtman & Gneezy (2001)]] (identity). There is almost no theory of how social-comparison groups form.
- **What is the reference *outcome* when the equal split is infeasible?** [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]] call the 50-50 reference "perhaps too simple"; [[@Engelmann2010|Engelmann & Strobel (2010)]] show that re-centering Fehr–Schmidt on a "do-nothing" or entitlement allocation either contradicts the data, requires an ambiguous neutral action, or makes the model label-dependent and untestable.
- **How much do efficiency/maximin motives weigh against equality, and is it context-dependent?** Left unresolved by [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]], [[@Fisman2007|Fisman et al. (2007)]], and explicitly by [[@Fehr2006|Fehr & Schmidt (2006)]].
- **Can intentions/reciprocity and distributional concern be combined in a tractable, *falsifiable*, out-of-sample-portable model?** Hybrid models (Falk–Fischbacher; the Charness–Rabin RFE) fit well but are too parameter-heavy and equilibrium-bound to test cleanly ([[@Fehr2006|Fehr & Schmidt (2006)]], [[@Fehr2006a|Fehr & Schmidt (2006a)]]); [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin et al. (2019)]] find distribution dominates reciprocity but flag understated reciprocity in non-salient frames.
- **What is the true curvature of inequity aversion, and is it separable from beliefs and efficiency?** [[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare et al. (2008)]] cannot separate efficiency concern from disadvantageous-inequity disutility, and their nonlinearity is contingent on the belief model.
- **Do canonical models extrapolate beyond two/three players and beyond linear budgets?** [[@Kuziemko2014|Kuziemko et al. (2014)]] expose rank features only at $n=6$; [[@Fisman2007|Fisman et al. (2007)]] note that by Afriat's theorem linear budgets can never reject well-behavedness, and their step-shaped design surfaces a ~17% selfish-plus-difference-averse blend the [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]] model cannot accommodate.
- **Are recovered preferences stable agent characteristics across games and time, and how few types suffice?** [[@Cox2004|Cox (2004)]] explicitly leaves cross-game stability open; [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin et al. (2019)]] show three types predict out-of-sample almost as well as 160 individual estimates, but the cross-game/cross-population generality of that taxonomy is untested.
- **Is the lab-vs-representative gap a cohort effect or an age effect?** Left open by [[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare et al. (2008)]].

## Candidate ideas
- **A horse-race re-estimation under elicited vs. assumed beliefs.** Re-estimate Fehr–Schmidt, ERC, and the Charness–Rabin social-welfare form on a common multiperson dataset, toggling rational vs. elicited subjective expectations à la [[@Bellemare2008|Bellemare et al. (2008)]]/[[@Bellemare2011|Bellemare et al. (2011)]] — testing whether the difference-aversion-vs-efficiency verdict of [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]] survives once beliefs are measured, not imposed.
- **A reference-point identification experiment.** Build the clean three-treatment design [[@Engelmann2010|Engelmann & Strobel (2010)]] propose (identical budget set after a take, a send, and a neutral move) with elicited first- and second-order beliefs, to pin down whether the neutral reference is the equal split, the status quo, or an entitlement — directly attacking the unsolved reference-outcome problem in [[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt (1999)]] and [[@Bolton2000|Bolton & Ockenfels (2000)]].
- **Rank-augmented structural estimation.** Add the ordinal-rank / last-place term of [[@Kuziemko2014|Kuziemko et al. (2014)]] to the piecewise-linear mixture model of [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin et al. (2019)]] and estimate on $n\ge6$ allocation data, testing whether a rank parameter displaces or complements the Fehr–Schmidt $\alpha,\beta$ and whether a distinct "rank-averse" type emerges.
- **Step-shaped budgets for the not-well-behaved minority.** Scale the [[@Fisman2007|Fisman et al. (2007)]] step-shaped design to a representative sample to quantify the ~17% selfish-plus-difference-averse blend that escapes the [[@Charness2002|Charness & Rabin (2002)]] canonical form, and ask whether it correlates with the behindness-averse type of [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin et al. (2019)]].
- **Identity-conditioned distributional preferences.** Combine the [[@Fershtman2001|Fershtman & Gneezy (2001)]] ethnic-signal manipulation with the [[@Andreoni2002|Andreoni & Miller (2002)]] budget-set dictator task to estimate how the CES self-weight $\alpha$ and curvature $\rho$ shift with the recipient's group identity — operationalizing the reference-group problem as a measurable preference shifter.
- **Cross-game type portability test.** Use the [[@Cox2004|Cox (2004)]] triadic decomposition jointly with the [[@Bruhin2019|Bruhin et al. (2019)]] three-type taxonomy to test whether type membership estimated from dictator/reciprocity games predicts trust-vs-altruism shares in the investment game — settling Cox's open question of whether distributional preferences are stable agent characteristics across fairness games.

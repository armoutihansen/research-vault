## Scope

This topic covers the economics of how a product reaches consumers through a vertical chain of manufacturers and retailers: the canonical double-marginalization distortion, the contractual instruments that coordinate a channel (quantity discounts, two-part tariffs, all-units discounts), and the *strategic* reasons a manufacturer might deliberately decentralize — surrendering control of the retail price — rather than integrate forward. The boundary is the *make-or-buy / sell-through-whom* margin: it includes channel-structure choice under downstream competition, product positioning interacting with channel form, and behavioral demand frictions that reshape vertical pricing, but excludes pure horizontal oligopoly, antitrust foreclosure of rivals, and intra-firm agency.

## Sub-themes

- **The coordination problem and its instruments.** The successive-monopoly markup-stacking origin in [[@Spengler1950|Spengler (1950)]]; channel coordination via a derived quantity-discount schedule in [[@Jeuland1983|Jeuland & Shugan (1983)]]; the rebuttal that coordination is a generic *decentralization* problem solved equally well by a two-part tariff in [[@Moorthy1987|Moorthy (1987)]]; all-units discounts as an alternative coordinating/screening device in [[@Kolay2004|Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004)]]; and the quantity-discount/two-part-tariff isomorphism under a dominant retailer in [[@Kolay2013|Kolay & Shaffer (2013)]].
- **Strategic decentralization under competition.** The foundational result that vertical separation softens price competition in [[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin (1983)]], generalized to broad demand classes in [[@Coughlan1985|Coughlan (1985)]], mechanized via "raising the demand curve" and the demand-vs-strategic-dependence coupling in [[@Moorthy1988a|Moorthy (1988)]], and extended to a common retailer carrying both brands in [[@Choi1991|Choi (1991)]].
- **Endogenous product positioning and the channel.** Inefficient vertical differentiation under duopoly in [[@Moorthy1988|Moorthy (1988)]]; cost-reducing process innovation as a competing channel determinant in [[@Gupta1998|Gupta & Loulou (1998)]]; and endogenous quality interacting with channel structure in [[@Zhao2009|Zhao, Atkins & Liu (2009)]].
- **Competition, entry/exit, and behavioral demand as channel forces.** Comparison-shopping as a third rationale for decentralization in [[@Moorthy2018|Moorthy, Chen & Tehrani (2018)]]; the "bright side of an enemy," where a rival's exit worsens double marginalization in [[@Harutyunyan2019|Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019)]]; and reference-price fairness concerns alleviating double marginalization in [[@Diao2023|Diao, Harutyunyan & Jiang (2023)]].

## Cross-paper tensions

The sharpest fault line is whether *decentralization is a coordination failure to be fixed or a strategic asset to be exploited.* [[@Spengler1950|Spengler (1950)]] and [[@Jeuland1983|Jeuland & Shugan (1983)]] treat the independent channel as a pure efficiency loss — stacked markups, $p$ too high and $Q$ too low — that a clever contract should eliminate. [[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin (1983)]] flip the sign: with two competing manufacturer–retailer dyads, inserting an independent retailer commits each firm to a higher effective marginal cost, *softening* Bertrand competition, so $DD$ is a Nash equilibrium for substitutability $\theta>0.931$. The two literatures even disagree on what the benchmark *is*: J&S's reduced-form demand implicitly bakes in a price leader, which is precisely why integration looks unambiguously optimal there — a point McGuire & Staelin make explicitly.

A second tension is **which contract is the "right" coordinating instrument**, and whether it is even special. [[@Jeuland1983|Jeuland & Shugan (1983)]] derive a declining quantity-discount schedule and claim it as a new rationale for observed discounts; [[@Moorthy1987|Moorthy (1987)]] dissolves this, showing the necessary-and-sufficient condition is merely that effective marginal cost cut marginal revenue from below at $q^*$ — satisfied by an *infinity* of schemes including quantity *surcharges*, so a two-part tariff dominates and the discount has no special claim. [[@Kolay2004|Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004)]] partly re-opens the case: under asymmetric demand information the discontinuous all-units-discount outlay schedule strictly out-screens any menu of two-part tariffs ($\pi^{AU}=\pi^{2PT}+(1-\alpha)\varepsilon$). Yet [[@Kolay2013|Kolay & Shaffer (2013)]] restore indifference between quantity discounts and two-part tariffs once full surplus extraction is feasible with a dominant retailer — so the contract ranking is not robust but *regime-dependent* (complete info, two-type screening, continuum-of-types, dominant-retailer).

Third, **what drives strategic decentralization is contested.** The early reading (substitutability) is sharpened by [[@Moorthy1988a|Moorthy (1988)]], who shows demand substitutability is *neither necessary nor sufficient*: what matters is the coupling of demand dependence with strategic complementarity/substitutability — economies of scale can kill $DD$ at arbitrarily high $\theta$. [[@Gupta1998|Gupta & Loulou (1998)]] add a contradicting force: endogenous cost-reducing R&D introduces a vertical externality (decentralized manufacturers under-invest), shrinking the decentralization-equilibrium region as cost reduction gets cheaper — an efficiency-vs-strategic-incentives trade-off the constant-cost models cannot see. [[@Zhao2009|Zhao, Atkins & Liu (2009)]] push further: once *quality* is endogenous, $DD$ is no longer an equilibrium at all — full integration $II$ is unique — because firms reposition quality to soften competition instead of decentralizing. So the equilibrium channel structure is fragile to exactly which margin (cost, quality) is held fixed.

Fourth, **the welfare/antitrust verdict is genuinely split.** [[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin (1983)]] and [[@Zhao2009|Zhao, Atkins & Liu (2009)]] find integration yields the *lowest* consumer prices, so regulatory presumptions favoring divestiture can harm consumers; [[@Harutyunyan2019|Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019)]] and [[@Diao2023|Diao, Harutyunyan & Jiang (2023)]] instead surface "all-win" regions where competition or even consumer fairness concerns *relieve* double marginalization, so more rivalry helps everyone. Crucially, several papers turn on the **assumed contract space**: the strategic-softening and exit-hurts results of [[@Moorthy1988a|Moorthy (1988)]] and [[@Harutyunyan2019|Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019)]] hold only under *linear wholesale-price* contracts; allow two-part tariffs and the channel is efficient and the conclusions flip. Demand form is just as load-bearing — [[@Choi1991|Choi (1991)]] shows leadership, differentiation, and structure-preference rankings *reverse* between linear and constant-elasticity demand.

## Open questions

- **Beyond the duopoly / single-dyad straitjacket.** [[@McGuire1983|McGuire & Staelin (1983)]], [[@Coughlan1985|Coughlan (1985)]], and [[@Choi1991|Choi (1991)]] all flag $n>2$ manufacturers, multi-brand retailers, and competitive retail markets as unresolved; [[@Choi1991|Choi (1991)]] specifically wants a common retailer facing a *competitive* retail fringe.
- **Which contract regime governs reality?** [[@Moorthy1987|Moorthy (1987)]] and [[@Kolay2004|Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004)]] leave open how to *empirically* disentangle coordination from price-discrimination motives for observed discounts, and whether the all-units-discount advantage survives a continuum of types and downstream rivals.
- **Asymmetric information and observability.** [[@Jeuland1983|Jeuland & Shugan (1983)]] flag unobservable costs; [[@Gupta1998|Gupta & Loulou (1998)]] note unobservable contracts can nullify strategic delegation entirely — when does the commitment value of decentralization actually survive?
- **Robustness to demand and contract primitives.** [[@Zhao2009|Zhao, Atkins & Liu (2009)]] and [[@Choi1991|Choi (1991)]] caution that core conclusions are functional-form-specific; [[@Harutyunyan2019|Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019)]] and [[@Diao2023|Diao, Harutyunyan & Jiang (2023)]] caution they hinge on linear contracts and no repeated interaction.
- **Empirical content.** Almost every member is pure theory; [[@Coughlan1985|Coughlan (1985)]] is the lone (crude, binary-proxy) field test. The magnitude of the channel-coordination effect — e.g., how much of the 1980s U.S. auto-profit pattern [[@Harutyunyan2019|Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019)]] attributes to it — is unmeasured.

## Candidate ideas

- **A horse-race of coordinating contracts under richer information.** Nest two-part tariffs, quantity discounts, and all-units discounts in one screening model with a continuum of types and downstream competition to map exactly when each strictly dominates, reconciling [[@Moorthy1987|Moorthy (1987)]], [[@Kolay2004|Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004)]], and [[@Kolay2013|Kolay & Shaffer (2013)]].
- **Does the contract space overturn "exit hurts the survivor"?** Re-solve [[@Harutyunyan2019|Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019)]] under two-part and all-units tariffs to test whether the bright-side-of-an-enemy and profitable-spinoff results are linear-contract artifacts, bridging to [[@Kolay2004|Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004)]].
- **Endogenous channel structure with both quality and cost-reducing investment.** Combine the R&D externality of [[@Gupta1998|Gupta & Loulou (1998)]] with endogenous quality of [[@Zhao2009|Zhao, Atkins & Liu (2009)]] and [[@Moorthy1988|Moorthy (1988)]] to see whether $DD$ ever survives when firms can move on cost *and* position simultaneously.
- **Structural estimation of the strategic-decentralization margin.** Take a multi-brand-retail panel (cars, electronics) and estimate the demand-vs-strategic-dependence coupling of [[@Moorthy1988a|Moorthy (1988)]] to recover whether observed decentralization is competition-softening or efficiency-driven — the empirical test [[@Coughlan1985|Coughlan (1985)]] could only crudely attempt.
- **Behavioral demand as a coordination device.** Generalize the reference-price "cost-reduction incentive" of [[@Diao2023|Diao, Harutyunyan & Jiang (2023)]] to a *competitive* channel and to the comparison-shopping format of [[@Moorthy2018|Moorthy, Chen & Tehrani (2018)]], asking when consumer-side frictions substitute for contractual coordination.
- **An ML-completeness diagnostic for channel models.** Use predictive benchmarking on simulated/experimental channel-pricing data to ask how much of decentralization choice the substitutability-only McGuire–Staelin map misses once [[@Moorthy1988a|Moorthy (1988)]]-style strategic-dependence and [[@Choi1991|Choi (1991)]]-style demand-form sensitivity are present.

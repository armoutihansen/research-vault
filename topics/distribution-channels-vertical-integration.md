## Scope

This topic covers the economics of how a product reaches consumers through a vertical chain of manufacturers and retailers: the canonical double-marginalization distortion, the contractual instruments that coordinate a channel (quantity discounts, two-part tariffs, all-units discounts), and the *strategic* reasons a manufacturer might deliberately decentralize — surrendering control of the retail price — rather than integrate forward. The boundary is the *make-or-buy / sell-through-whom* margin: it includes channel-structure choice under downstream competition, product positioning interacting with channel form, behavioral demand frictions that reshape vertical pricing, and — newly — channel choice as a *self-commitment* device when sales effort is wasteful, but excludes pure horizontal oligopoly, antitrust foreclosure of rivals, and intra-firm agency.

## Sub-themes

- **The coordination problem and its instruments.** The successive-monopoly markup-stacking origin in Spengler (1950); channel coordination via a derived quantity-discount schedule in Jeuland & Shugan (1983); the rebuttal that coordination is a generic *decentralization* problem solved equally well by a two-part tariff in Moorthy (1987); all-units discounts as an alternative coordinating/screening device in Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004); and the quantity-discount/two-part-tariff isomorphism under a dominant retailer in Kolay & Shaffer (2013).
- **Strategic decentralization under competition.** The foundational result that vertical separation softens price competition in McGuire & Staelin (1983), generalized to broad demand classes in Coughlan (1985), mechanized via "raising the demand curve" and the demand-vs-strategic-dependence coupling in Moorthy (1988), and extended to a common retailer carrying both brands in Choi (1991).
- **Endogenous product positioning and the channel.** Inefficient vertical differentiation under duopoly in Moorthy (1988); cost-reducing process innovation as a competing channel determinant in Gupta & Loulou (1998); and endogenous quality interacting with channel structure in Zhao, Atkins & Liu (2009).
- **Competition, entry/exit, and behavioral demand as channel forces.** Comparison-shopping as a third rationale for decentralization in Moorthy, Chen & Tehrani (2018); the "bright side of an enemy," where a rival's exit worsens double marginalization in Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019); and reference-price fairness concerns alleviating double marginalization in Diao, Harutyunyan & Jiang (2023).
- **Channel choice as self-commitment under quality uncertainty.** A fourth rationale for decentralization that sits orthogonal to the competition-softening tradition: in Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) a manufacturer of an experience/trust good faces a *signal-jamming* equilibrium trap — costly, demand-exaggerating sales activity $a$ is fully anticipated by consumers ($\hat a=a^*$) so it yields no demand gain yet cannot be cut. Handing the product to a retailer (or a low-powered intermediary in *hybrid selling*) is a self-commitment device against that wasteful effort, and double marginalization becomes a *tool* that dampens the agent's exaggeration incentive rather than a pure loss.

## Cross-paper tensions

The sharpest fault line is whether *decentralization is a coordination failure to be fixed or a strategic asset to be exploited.* Spengler (1950) and Jeuland & Shugan (1983) treat the independent channel as a pure efficiency loss — stacked markups, $p$ too high and $Q$ too low — that a clever contract should eliminate. McGuire & Staelin (1983) flip the sign: with two competing manufacturer–retailer dyads, inserting an independent retailer commits each firm to a higher effective marginal cost, *softening* Bertrand competition, so $DD$ is a Nash equilibrium for substitutability $\theta>0.931$. Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) flip the sign for a third, independent reason — and the most pointed new tension is here: their decentralized retailer's double-marginalized markup is *welcome to the manufacturer even under monopoly*, because the same wedge that raises the consumer price also blunts the retailer's incentive to burn money on signal-jamming sales activity ($a^*_{Dec}<a^*_{Cen}$). Where Spengler and Jeuland & Shugan want the wedge gone, Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel sometimes want it *positive*.

The most direct collision is over the **two-part tariff as the canonical fix**. Moorthy (1987) is categorical: the necessary-and-sufficient coordinating condition is that effective marginal cost cut marginal revenue from below at $q^*$, the two-part tariff achieves it by setting the wholesale price to marginal cost ($w=c$) and extracting surplus through the fixed fee, and double marginalization is thereby eliminated. Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026, Corollary 3) reach the opposite design conclusion in their setting: with a wealth-unconstrained retailer the *optimal* two-part tariff has a strictly positive $w^*>0$, deliberately *retaining* a slice of double marginalization to trade off against the signal-jamming equilibrium trap. The two-part tariff still makes decentralization strictly dominate — but as a self-commitment instrument tuned to leave some markup, not as a coordination instrument tuned to erase it. This is the explicit break with classic channel coordination: the "coordinated" $w=c$ contract is no longer optimal once the retailer's effort is socially wasteful rather than productive.

A second tension is **which contract is the "right" coordinating instrument**, and whether it is even special. Jeuland & Shugan (1983) derive a declining quantity-discount schedule and claim it as a new rationale for observed discounts; Moorthy (1987) dissolves this, showing the condition is satisfied by an *infinity* of schemes including quantity *surcharges*, so a two-part tariff dominates and the discount has no special claim. Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004) partly re-open the case: under asymmetric demand information the discontinuous all-units-discount outlay schedule strictly out-screens any menu of two-part tariffs ($\pi^{AU}=\pi^{2PT}+(1-\alpha)\varepsilon$). Yet Kolay & Shaffer (2013) restore indifference between quantity discounts and two-part tariffs once full surplus extraction is feasible with a dominant retailer — so the contract ranking is not robust but *regime-dependent* (complete info, two-type screening, continuum-of-types, dominant-retailer). Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) add a further regime where even the *direction* of the optimal wholesale markup flips, underlining that "the coordinating contract" presupposes a coordination objective that signal-jamming can negate.

Third, **what drives strategic decentralization is contested.** The early reading (substitutability) is sharpened by Moorthy (1988), who shows demand substitutability is *neither necessary nor sufficient*: what matters is the coupling of demand dependence with strategic complementarity/substitutability — economies of scale can kill $DD$ at arbitrarily high $\theta$. Gupta & Loulou (1998) add a contradicting force: endogenous cost-reducing R&D introduces a vertical externality (decentralized manufacturers under-invest), shrinking the decentralization region. Zhao, Atkins & Liu (2009) push further: once *quality* is endogenous, $DD$ is no longer an equilibrium at all — full integration $II$ is unique. Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) sit athwart this debate: decentralization there does *not* require demand dependence at all (it holds for a monopoly manufacturer) and survives — indeed strengthens — once manufacturer competition is added (their Proposition 2 favors decentralization for both substitutes and complements, $\alpha\in[-1,1]$). So the field now has four non-nested drivers of $DD$ — competition-softening, comparison-shopping, fairness, and self-commitment against wasteful effort — that need not coincide and can even point in opposite directions on whether competition matters at all.

Fourth, **the welfare/antitrust verdict is genuinely split.** McGuire & Staelin (1983) and Zhao, Atkins & Liu (2009) find integration yields the *lowest* consumer prices, so divestiture presumptions can harm consumers; Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019) and Diao, Harutyunyan & Jiang (2023) instead surface "all-win" regions where competition or consumer fairness *relieve* double marginalization. Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) complicate the welfare reading from a new angle: their preferred decentralization raises the consumer price (double marginalization is retained on purpose) yet *reduces* socially wasteful sales activity, so the consumer-surplus verdict turns on whether one counts the suppressed signal-jamming as a real social saving — a margin none of the price-only welfare comparisons in this topic measure. Several papers also turn on the **assumed contract space**: the strategic-softening and exit-hurts results of Moorthy (1988) and Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019) hold only under *linear wholesale-price* contracts, and Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel show that even when two-part tariffs are allowed the conclusion (here, that decentralization wins) need not flip — but for an entirely different reason than coordination. Demand form remains load-bearing: Choi (1991) shows leadership, differentiation, and structure-preference rankings *reverse* between linear and constant-elasticity demand, and Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel's closed forms rest on linear demand plus normal signals.

## Open questions

- **Beyond the duopoly / single-dyad straitjacket.** McGuire & Staelin (1983), Coughlan (1985), and Choi (1991) all flag $n>2$ manufacturers, multi-brand retailers, and competitive retail markets as unresolved; Choi (1991) specifically wants a common retailer facing a *competitive* retail fringe. Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) solve only a two-manufacturer duopoly extension and leave the competitive-fringe and multi-retailer signal-jamming channel open.
- **Which contract regime governs reality?** Moorthy (1987) and Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004) leave open how to *empirically* disentangle coordination from price-discrimination motives for observed discounts; Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) add a third motive — a deliberately positive $w^*$ as self-commitment — so observed wholesale markups could reflect coordination failure, screening, *or* effort suppression, with no obvious way yet to tell them apart in data.
- **Static vs. dynamic, and endogenous brand loyalty.** Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) flag their *static* model as the headline limitation: the reference point $\hat q$ (inverse brand loyalty) is exogenous, and endogenizing it as a function of past quality realizations is the explicit future-research hook — a dynamic dimension absent from every other (static) member here.
- **Asymmetric information and observability.** Jeuland & Shugan (1983) flag unobservable costs; Gupta & Loulou (1998) note unobservable contracts can nullify strategic delegation; Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) assume consumers observe the wholesale price / intermediary compensation *exactly* (a footnote-level relaxation only), so when the commitment value of decentralization survives partial observability is open across all three.
- **Robustness to demand and contract primitives.** Zhao, Atkins & Liu (2009) and Choi (1991) caution that core conclusions are functional-form-specific; Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019) and Diao, Harutyunyan & Jiang (2023) caution they hinge on linear contracts and no repeated interaction; Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) rest on linear demand, a single additive signal, quadratic effort cost, and a knife-edge zero-commission hybrid contract that they themselves call too extreme.
- **Empirical content.** Almost every member is pure theory; Coughlan (1985) is the lone (crude) field test. The channel-choice prediction of Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) is illustrated only by an anecdotal Apple/Samsung channel-share table and has no structural test, just as the magnitude of the channel-coordination effect Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019) attribute to the 1980s U.S. auto market is unmeasured.

## Candidate ideas

- **The optimal wholesale wedge: coordination vs. effort-suppression.** Reconcile Moorthy (1987)'s $w=c$ "kill double marginalization" two-part tariff with Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026)'s Corollary-3 optimal $w^*>0$ by nesting a productive-effort term and a signal-jamming (wasteful-effort) term in one principal–agent channel model, and characterize the boundary in primitive space where the optimal wholesale markup crosses zero. This is the user's own paper's sharpest contact point with the classic literature; pure theory, no new data, and the closed forms already exist on both sides.
- **A horse-race of coordinating contracts under wasteful retail effort.** Extend the screening setup of Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004) and Kolay & Shaffer (2013) to a retailer whose effort is partly signal-jamming, asking whether all-units discounts, quantity discounts, or two-part tariffs best trade off surplus extraction against effort suppression — does the all-units advantage survive once the principal *wants* a markup rather than wanting it gone? Theory, building directly on existing models.
- **Endogenous brand loyalty as a dynamic channel determinant.** Take the static self-commitment model of Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) and endogenize the reference point $\hat q$ as a function of realized past quality, simulating whether the centralized/decentralized boundary cycles or converges and whether dynamic learning eventually undoes the self-commitment rationale — the explicit open hook in the paper. Theory plus simulation; no new experiments.
- **Does the contract space overturn "exit hurts the survivor"?** Re-solve Harutyunyan & Jiang (2019) under two-part and all-units tariffs to test whether the bright-side-of-an-enemy and profitable-spinoff results are linear-contract artifacts, and connect to Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026)'s finding that a positive optimal $w^*$ can survive richer contracts — i.e. distinguish "linear-contract artifact" from "intentional markup." Theory, bridging to Kolay, Shaffer & Ordover (2004).
- **Unifying the four rationales for decentralization.** Build one model that simultaneously hosts competition-softening (McGuire & Staelin 1983; Moorthy 1988), comparison-shopping (Moorthy, Chen & Tehrani 2018), fairness/reference-price relief (Diao, Harutyunyan & Jiang 2023), and self-commitment against signal-jamming (Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel 2026), then ask which dominates in which region and whether they reinforce or cancel — the current field has four non-nested drivers that have never been put in the same room. Theory; existing primitives can be merged.
- **Structural identification of the wholesale-markup motive.** With a multi-brand-retail panel (electronics, autos) where experience-good quality uncertainty is plausibly high, attempt to separate coordination-failure, screening, and effort-suppression explanations of observed wholesale markups, operationalizing the Armouti-Hansen & Kräkel (2026) prediction that suppressed sales activity should accompany decentralization — the structural test the paper's Apple/Samsung anecdote only gestures at, and the empirical content Coughlan (1985) could only crudely attempt. Existing-data only, but identification is the hard part and worth scoping before committing.

## Scope

This topic covers the theory of the firm's *objective function* once ownership and control are separated: why a profit-maximizing owner deliberately hands a manager a distorted objective — sales-weighted, relative-performance, or simply non-profit — as a strategic instrument. Two distinct mechanisms anchor it: (i) *external* commitment, where a publicly observed managerial contract shifts the firm's reaction function and manipulates product-market rivals (the Cournot/Bertrand delegation program), and (ii) *internal* delegation, where ceding formal or real authority to a subordinate trades the principal's control for the agent's initiative or learning. The boundary is the deliberate, optimizing choice of a non-profit objective; pure agency-cost frictions and incentive-intensity questions live in neighboring topics.

## Sub-themes

- **Strategic delegation as oligopoly commitment.** The canonical trio [[@Fershtman1987|Fershtman & Judd (1987)]], [[@Sklivas1987|Sklivas (1987)]], and [[@Vickers1985|Vickers (1985)]] independently establish that owners write contracts on a profit/sales mix ($O_i=\alpha_i\pi_i+(1-\alpha_i)S_i$) or a cost-shifter ($\theta_i$) to commit managers to aggression (Cournot, $\alpha<1$) or softness (Bertrand, $\lambda>1$). [[@Tirole1988|Tirole (1988)]] embeds this in the broader "is profit-maximization defensible?" question.
- **Authority and initiative.** [[@Aghion1997|Aghion & Tirole (1997)]] separate *formal* from *real* authority: delegation raises agent initiative at the cost of principal control, via an endogenous information-acquisition channel.
- **Delegation as investment and empowerment.** [[@Sliwka2001|Sliwka (2001)]] reframes delegation as experimentation on unknown ability and as an implicit-incentive (career-concerns) device, generating a reputation-driven holdup problem.
- **Foundations / selection critique.** [[@Vickers1985|Vickers (1985)]] and [[@Tirole1988|Tirole (1988)]] both confront whether evolutionary "natural selection" rescues profit-maximization once delegation is possible.

## Cross-paper tensions

The sharpest tension is **what makes a distorted objective credible.** The oligopoly trio's entire result rests on the contract being *publicly observable and committed*: [[@Sklivas1987|Sklivas (1987)]] is explicit that "unobservable or renegotiable contracts are not analyzed," and [[@Vickers1985|Vickers (1985)]] assumes the agent's type is "common knowledge." Yet [[@Fershtman1987|Fershtman & Judd (1987)]] needs *ex ante uncertainty* to give the manager a genuine observing role and to escape the focal-point objection that, under certainty, quantity-forcing contracts simply reproduce the Cournot outcome — so the very informational assumption that makes the manager useful also threatens the observability the commitment requires. This is unresolved across the trio and silently assumed away.

A second tension is **the sign and source of the optimal distortion.** In the external models distortion is pinned to strategic substitutes vs. complements: aggression under Cournot, softness under Bertrand. In the internal models the "distortion" is preference *congruence* ($\alpha,\beta$ in [[@Aghion1997|Aghion & Tirole (1997)]]) or *forgone competence* (the principal in [[@Sliwka2001|Sliwka (2001)]] voluntarily acquires lower ability $a_P^{\max}$ to commit not to interfere). These are not the same object: oligopoly delegation distorts the manager *away* from the owner to manipulate a third party (the rival), whereas authority delegation aligns or hands control to the agent to manipulate his *own* effort/initiative. No member reconciles whether a single firm facing both rivals *and* a subordinate would choose objectives that point in compatible directions.

Third, **welfare signs conflict.** [[@Fershtman1987|Fershtman & Judd (1987)]] find Cournot delegation *improves* allocative efficiency (output up, price toward marginal cost, low-cost firm's share up), while [[@Sklivas1987|Sklivas (1987)]] frames the same aggression as a prisoner's-dilemma that lowers both firms' profits, and the Bertrand case pushes prices toward collusion — socially *worse*. So delegation is welfare-improving or welfare-destroying depending purely on the competition mode, with no member offering a unified welfare statement.

Fourth, **what kills the distortion.** All external models agree $\alpha,\lambda,\theta \to$ profit-maximization as $n\to\infty$ — competition restores profit-maximization, exactly the Friedman/Alchian selection logic. But [[@Vickers1985|Vickers (1985)]] turns this against selection: profit-maximizers need *not* earn maximum profits, so selection could *eliminate* them. The trio's $n\to\infty$ limit and Vickers's anti-selection argument sit in unresolved tension over whether selection disciplines or undermines the profit motive.

## Open questions

- **Unrestricted contracts.** Every external model imposes *linear* profit/sales contracts for tractability; [[@Fershtman1987|Fershtman & Judd (1987)]] flag that unrestricted contracts may admit *no* equilibrium (Myerson 1982). The optimal contract space is uncharacterized.
- **Genuine moral hazard.** [[@Sklivas1987|Sklivas (1987)]] and [[@Fershtman1987|Fershtman & Judd (1987)]] abstract from hidden action entirely; integrating strategic delegation with a Holmström moral-hazard structure is left open.
- **Endogenous congruence and timing.** [[@Aghion1997|Aghion & Tirole (1997)]] take $\alpha,\beta$ and investigation timing as exogenous, and conjecture the "communication never hurts" result breaks once the agent responds to money. Multilayered hierarchies are untreated.
- **Bargaining power and horizon.** [[@Sliwka2001|Sliwka (2001)]] assumes equal Nash bargaining and a two-period horizon; unequal power has an "ambiguous" twofold effect, unresolved.
- **Empirics.** [[@Sklivas1987|Sklivas (1987)]] explicitly calls for a direct test of the delegation prediction; [[@Tirole1988|Tirole (1988)]] notes "unforeseeability" and real authority are hard to measure.

## Candidate ideas

- **Observability meets uncertainty.** Resolve the [[@Fershtman1987|Fershtman & Judd (1987)]] tension by modeling delegation when contracts are *imperfectly* observed and demand is uncertain — does the commitment value survive noisy contract signals?
- **Unified objective under dual manipulation.** Build a model where a firm delegates both a product-market objective (à la [[@Sklivas1987|Sklivas (1987)]]) and decision authority (à la [[@Aghion1997|Aghion & Tirole (1997)]]) to the *same* manager, and characterize whether oligopoly aggression and internal initiative call for compatible distortions.
- **Strategic delegation with real moral hazard.** Embed the linear profit/sales contract of [[@Sklivas1987|Sklivas (1987)]] in a hidden-action environment, testing whether the substitutes/complements sign survives an incentive-compatibility constraint.
- **Empirical test of the competition-mode prediction.** Use executive-compensation data to test [[@Sklivas1987|Sklivas (1987)]]'s and [[@Fershtman1987|Fershtman & Judd (1987)]]'s sign prediction (sales-weighting in Cournot-like vs. Bertrand-like industries), answering Sklivas's explicit call.
- **Selection vs. distortion dynamics.** Formalize [[@Vickers1985|Vickers (1985)]]'s anti-selection claim against the $n\to\infty$ limit: simulate an evolutionary oligopoly to see whether profit-maximizing objectives are selected *out*.
- **Empowerment as commitment, structurally estimated.** Take [[@Sliwka2001|Sliwka (2001)]]'s forgone-competence result and test whether managers strategically under-invest in expertise to credibly delegate, using the real-authority proxies [[@Tirole1988|Tirole (1988)]] and [[@Aghion1997|Aghion & Tirole (1997)]] suggest.

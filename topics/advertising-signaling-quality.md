## Scope

This topic covers the economics of how product-quality information reaches consumers through advertising and reviews, with a focus on **experience goods** whose quality is unknowable before purchase. It spans the informative/persuasive trichotomy, the formal signaling tradition in which dissipative advertising or introductory price credibly transmit quality, and the newer **signal-jamming** view in which firms bias the noisy signals (ads, online reviews) consumers receive. The boundary is the *transmission of quality information itself* — search-for-price advertising, pure persuasion, and welfare accounting enter only where they bear on whether a quality message is credible. Adverse-selection/certification mechanisms and behavioral-IO exploitation sit in neighbouring topics.

## Sub-themes

- **The informative-advertising foundation and the search/experience taxonomy.** [[@Nelson1970|Nelson (1970)]] establishes the search-vs-experience primitive via an optimal-sampling model; [[@Nelson1974|Nelson (1974)]] turns it into the claim that for experience goods the *fact of advertising* — not its content — is the signal, because high-repeat-purchase (efficient) firms gain most from being sampled.
- **Formal quality signaling.** [[@Milgrom1986|Milgrom & Roberts (1986)]] endogenizes both introductory price and dissipative advertising as signals in a refined separating equilibrium, correcting Nelson's "marginal initial sale" intuition. The two handbook surveys [[@Bagwell2007|Bagwell (2007)]] and [[@Renault2015|Renault (2015)]] embed this in the broader persuasive/informative/complementary map and the disclosure/unraveling literature.
- **Signal jamming and review manipulation.** [[@Grunewald2017|Grunewald & Kräkel (2017)]] and [[@Grunewald2022|Grunewald & Kräkel (2022)]] move from honest signaling to *biasing* a noisy public signal; [[@Akoz2020|Aköz, Arbatlı & Çelik (2020)]] adds endogenous price and a high-type "counter-advertising" arms race; [[@Mayzlin2014|Mayzlin, Dover & Chevalier (2014)]] is the lone empirical member, detecting hotel-review fakery via an organizational-form discontinuity.
- **What advertising actually does to consumers.** [[@Vakratsas1999|Vakratsas & Ambler (1999)]] reviews 250+ marketing studies and rejects a fixed "hierarchy of effects," proposing an experience–affect–cognition space — a behavioral counterweight to the economists' rational-Bayesian buyer.

## Cross-paper tensions

The sharpest fault line is **who manipulates and what it signals**. In the classic signaling models only the low type wants to mislead, so positive advertising/price *separates* types and is informative: [[@Milgrom1986|Milgrom & Roberts (1986)]] make the high type "burn money" precisely because the low type cannot profitably imitate. Signal-jamming flips this. [[@Akoz2020|Aköz et al. (2020)]] and [[@Mayzlin2014|Mayzlin et al. (2014)]] both find that in equilibrium *both* types manipulate — the high type counter-advertises, and a firm that abstained would be (mis)read as low quality — so the signal no longer cleanly separates. Whether advertising is a Spence-style separating device or a pooling arms race is therefore unresolved and turns on whether the signal is dissipative-and-observable (Milgrom-Roberts) or hidden-and-additive (jamming).

A second tension is **the comparative statics of the advertising–quality correlation**. Nelson's mechanism predicts a *positive* correlation because efficient/high-utility firms advertise most. [[@Grunewald2017|Grunewald & Kräkel (2017)]] reproduce a positive correlation but through an entirely different channel — the ex-ante quality leader advertises more to widen the posterior spread $\bar q_{i1}-\bar q_{j1}$ that relaxes downstream price competition — and crucially show optimal intensity can *decrease* in quality uncertainty $\sigma_{i0}^2$, overturning the textbook signal-jamming prediction that more noise means more jamming. The same competition channel drives [[@Grunewald2022|Grunewald & Kräkel (2022)]]'s backfiring result: stricter anti-manipulation institutions (lower $\beta$) can *raise* manipulation in balanced races. Bagwell's survey notes the empirical advertising–quality link is in fact "weak," so the field has three competing rationales (Nelsonian efficiency, Milgrom-Roberts cost-monotonicity requiring $c(H)>c(L)$, and Grunewald-Kräkel differentiation) for a correlation the data barely support.

A third tension is **monopoly vs. competition**. Almost every formal member — [[@Milgrom1986|Milgrom & Roberts (1986)]], [[@Akoz2020|Aköz et al. (2020)]] — solves a *monopoly* model and explicitly flags oligopoly as "significant additional problems." Yet [[@Grunewald2017|Grunewald & Kräkel (2017)]] and [[@Grunewald2022|Grunewald & Kräkel (2022)]] show downstream competition *overturns* the single-agent jamming intuitions, and [[@Renault2015|Renault (2015)]] reports competition can even *reduce* disclosure (Board 2009). The monopoly results may thus be exactly the wrong benchmark.

Finally, a **rationality tension** cuts across the corpus: the economic members assume Bayesian consumers who perfectly discount anticipated bias (so in [[@Mayzlin2014|Mayzlin et al.]]'s symmetric-information benchmark manipulation changes no choices), whereas [[@Vakratsas1999|Vakratsas & Ambler (1999)]] document that experience and affect — not a cognitive hierarchy — drive response, and [[@Renault2015|Renault (2015)]] concedes the informative view "presupposes substantial consumer sophistication."

## Open questions

- **Oligopoly signaling/jamming with endogenous quality.** [[@Akoz2020|Aköz et al. (2020)]] and [[@Milgrom1986|Milgrom & Roberts (1986)]] leave oligopoly open; [[@Grunewald2017|Grunewald & Kräkel (2017)]] ask whether ex-ante identical firms adopt asymmetric (high-quality/heavy-ad vs. low-quality/no-ad) strategies once quality is *chosen* rather than given.
- **Welfare and consumer impact of manipulation.** [[@Mayzlin2014|Mayzlin et al. (2014)]] cannot measure whether fake reviews change purchases; [[@Akoz2020|Aköz et al. (2020)]] and [[@Grunewald2017|Grunewald & Kräkel (2017)]] leave consumer-surplus accounting for oligopoly open.
- **Combining observable signaling and hidden jamming.** [[@Grunewald2017|Grunewald & Kräkel (2017)]] flag the unsolved problem of how a firm allocates spend across dissipative-but-observable advertising and hidden signal-biasing.
- **Detecting manipulation in text/content.** [[@Mayzlin2014|Mayzlin et al. (2014)]] deliberately ignore review *content*; textual attacks (a fabricated bed-bug claim) escape their organizational-form measure.
- **Behavioral foundations for the signal.** [[@Vakratsas1999|Vakratsas & Ambler (1999)]] leave open how context sets the experience–affect–cognition weights, and [[@Bagwell2007|Bagwell (2007)]] reopens *which* preferences anchor welfare when ads activate affective systems.

## Candidate ideas

- **Oligopoly review-manipulation model with welfare.** Extend [[@Akoz2020|Aköz et al. (2020)]]'s price-plus-jamming monopoly to duopoly using the [[@Grunewald2017|Grunewald & Kräkel (2017)]] vertical-differentiation stage-2 game, to test whether competition reverses the "high prices ⇒ pro-consumer manipulation" result.
- **Structural test of the advertising–quality correlation's mechanism.** Use a discrete-choice/structural design to discriminate Nelson's efficiency channel, Milgrom-Roberts' $c(H)>c(L)$ requirement, and Grunewald-Kräkel's differentiation channel — directly answering the "weak link" puzzle flagged in [[@Bagwell2007|Bagwell (2007)]].
- **Empirical backfiring of review regulation.** Exploit a platform policy change (verified-purchase rules, mandatory deletion) à la [[@Mayzlin2014|Mayzlin et al. (2014)]] to test [[@Grunewald2022|Grunewald & Kräkel (2022)]]'s prediction that stricter institutions raise manipulation in balanced competitive markets.
- **Behavioral signal-jamming with non-Bayesian consumers.** Replace the perfectly-discounting Bayesian buyer of [[@Akoz2020|Aköz et al. (2020)]] with the experience–affect–cognition response documented by [[@Vakratsas1999|Vakratsas & Ambler (1999)]], and ask when partly-naive consumers make manipulation *more* profitable than separation.
- **Joint observable-signaling and hidden-jamming spend.** Build the two-instrument model [[@Grunewald2017|Grunewald & Kräkel (2017)]] propose — dissipative advertising plus covert bias — and characterize how product observability splits a firm's budget and its welfare cost.
- **Endogenous quality choice under jamming.** Endogenize $q_i$ in the [[@Grunewald2017|Grunewald & Kräkel (2017)]] framework to test whether ex-ante symmetric firms self-sort into high-quality/advertise vs. low-quality/silent niches, a moral-hazard dimension absent from [[@Milgrom1986|Milgrom & Roberts (1986)]].

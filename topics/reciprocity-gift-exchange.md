## Scope

This topic covers **intention-based reciprocity** — the idea that people reward perceived kindness and punish perceived unkindness, where (un)kindness is judged against a belief-dependent reference point — and its flagship application, **gift exchange** in labor markets, where above-market wages buy non-contractible effort. Its boundary is the *intentions* primitive: it holds the foundational reciprocity models (psychological games) together with the lab and field tests of whether wage/effort reciprocity is real, how strong it is, and what mechanism drives it. Purely outcome-based distributional fairness ([[@Fehr1999|Fehr & Schmidt 1999]], ERC) lives in the inequity-aversion topic and enters here only as the rival null. Expectations-based reference dependence and contract-theoretic embeddings are bordering topics.

## Sub-themes

- **Foundational intention-based models (psychological games).** [[@Rabin1993|Rabin (1993)]] builds the first normal-form fairness equilibrium; [[@Camerer2011|Camerer, Loewenstein & Rabin (2011)]] reprints it. [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger (2004)]] extends it to extensive form (sequential reciprocity equilibrium) with belief *updating*; [[@Falk2006|Falk & Fischbacher (2006)]] adds a distributional component and uses *initial* beliefs.
- **Lab gift exchange and contract enforcement.** [[@Fehr1993|Fehr, Kirchsteiger & Riedl (1993)]] launches the two-stage gift-exchange market; [[@Fehr1997|Fehr, Gächter & Kirchsteiger (1997)]] shows two-sided reciprocity enforces contracts; [[@Fischbacher2001|Fischbacher, Gächter & Fehr (2001)]] documents conditional cooperation as the public-goods analogue.
- **Adjudicating intentions vs. outcomes.** [[@Falk2008|Falk, Fehr & Fischbacher (2008)]] is the clean moonlighting-game test rejecting both pure-outcome and pure-intention models.
- **Field tests of gift exchange.** [[@Kube2013|Kube, Maréchal & Puppe (2013)]] (wage cuts), [[@Ockenfels2015|Ockenfels, Sliwka & Werner (2015)]] and [[@Sliwka2017|Sliwka & Werner (2017)]] (timing of kindness), [[@Esteves-Sorenson2018|Esteves-Sorenson (2018)]] (a confound-stripped null), [[@DellaVigna2022|DellaVigna, List, Malmendier & Rao (2022)]] (structural pay-rate estimation), [[@DellaVigna2018|DellaVigna & Pope (2018)]] (gift exchange among 18 motivators), and [[@Armouti-Hansen2024|Armouti-Hansen et al. (2024)]] / [[@Armouti-Hansen2024a|its GEB version]] (mission + biased principal beliefs).
- **Reciprocity as one explanation among rivals for one-shot anomalies.** [[@Goeree2001|Goeree & Holt (2001)]] and [[@Eichberger2011|Eichberger & Kelsey (2011)]] test whether reciprocity (vs. noisy introspection or ambiguity aversion) accounts for one-shot game deviations.

## Cross-paper tensions

**Do intentions or outcomes drive reciprocity? — the central axis.** The intention-based camp ([[@Rabin1993|Rabin]], [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger]]) makes kindness belief-dependent and predicts $b=0\,\forall a$ when intentions are absent; the outcome-based camp (Fehr–Schmidt, Bolton–Ockenfels) makes it $\Delta$-payoff-dependent and indifferent to intent. [[@Falk2008|Falk, Fehr & Fischbacher (2008)]] adjudicate cleanly: matching the random device to the human choice distribution, reciprocity *weakens* but does not vanish under non-intention, rejecting *both* poles. This is exactly the empirical content [[@Falk2006|Falk & Fischbacher (2006)]] designed for via their $\varepsilon_i$ (pure-outcome) vs. intention factor. [[@Kube2013|Kube et al. (2013)]] read the same way from a different margin: the cut/raise asymmetry follows naturally from action-based reciprocity but needs an auxiliary *narrow-bracketing* assumption to survive under inequity aversion.

**Initial vs. updated beliefs.** [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger]] use history-indexed *updated* beliefs (delivering retaliation but admitting dynamically inconsistent preferences and dollars-squared, scale-dependent utility), whereas [[@Falk2006|Falk & Fischbacher]] sum node-by-node from *initial* beliefs to retain the GPS equilibrium concept. They make non-nested predictions: [[@Falk2008|Falk et al. (2008)]] note D&K's multiplicity and higher-order-belief dependence makes it under-tested without elicited beliefs.

**Does gift exchange survive outside the lab? — the sharpest tension.** [[@Fehr1993|Fehr et al. (1993)]] and [[@Fehr1997|Fehr et al. (1997)]] show robust, profit-increasing reciprocity in lab markets. The field record then splits violently. [[@Kube2013|Kube et al.]] find a large, persistent *negative* response to cuts but *no* positive response to raises; [[@Esteves-Sorenson2018|Esteves-Sorenson (2018)]] strips eight confounds and finds noncontingent raises produce a near-zero elasticity while a cheaper piece rate works, concluding the standard model wins; [[@DellaVigna2022|DellaVigna et al. (2022)]] find gifts move *extra work* but not *productivity*, and effort is warm-glow-like, not Beckerian-altruistic and *not* return-sensitive; [[@DellaVigna2018|DellaVigna & Pope (2018)]] find only a "small, weak" reciprocity arm. Against this near-null tide, [[@Ockenfels2015|Ockenfels et al.]] and [[@Sliwka2017|Sliwka & Werner]] recover sizable (~11–15%), *persistent* effects — but only by manipulating the *timing/incidence* of increases at fixed budget, attributing it to an adaptive (narrow-bracketing) reference standard rather than the level of generosity. So the field disagreement may be less "does reciprocity exist" than "what is the reference point, and is the margin productivity or discretionary effort."

**Is the wage even mispriced, or are beliefs wrong?** [[@Armouti-Hansen2024|Armouti-Hansen et al.]] reframe low one-shot efficiency: agents *are* reciprocal, but principals systematically *underestimate* the effort–wage slope and underpay — a belief failure, not a preference failure, which a social mission happens to de-bias.

**Is "reciprocity" even the right label for one-shot deviations?** [[@Goeree2001|Goeree & Holt (2001)]] show reciprocity fails as a general account of one-shot anomalies; [[@Eichberger2011|Eichberger & Kelsey (2011)]] reproduce the same data with ambiguity aversion under calibrated parameters — a reminder that payoff-magnitude effects often attributed to social preferences may be boundedly-rational introspection.

## Open questions

- **What sets the reference point?** Every model leans on a reference standard nobody derives: [[@Rabin1993|Rabin]] omits status-quo/history; [[@Fehr1997|Fehr et al.]] state there is "no empirical method" for locating it; [[@Sliwka2017|Sliwka & Werner]] show an *adaptive* ($w_{t-1}$) standard fits better than a fixed one but mispredicts one transition. Fixed vs. adaptive, and broad vs. narrow bracketing, remain unresolved.
- **Productivity vs. extra-work margin.** [[@DellaVigna2022|DellaVigna et al.]] leave open why gifts move discretionary effort but not core productivity — elasticity artifact, habituation, or a needed reciprocity "channel"/request.
- **Persistence and horizon.** Lab/field effects are mostly one-shot; [[@Fehr1993|Fehr et al.]], [[@Ockenfels2015|Ockenfels et al.]], and [[@Sliwka2017|Sliwka & Werner]] all flag unknown dynamics over long, repeated relationships.
- **Why don't lab-prosocial types reciprocate in the field?** [[@Esteves-Sorenson2018|Esteves-Sorenson]] finds zero lab-to-field transfer of measured prosociality when effort is unobservable.
- **Do biased beliefs persist?** [[@Armouti-Hansen2024a|Armouti-Hansen et al.]] conjecture pessimistic principals never see the disconfirming counterfactual; untested under learning/rematching.
- **Incompetence vs. unkindness, and off-path inference.** [[@Falk2008|Falk et al.]] leave open whether bad outcomes from incapacity are punished like unkind intent; [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger]] leave off-path belief revision unsolved.

## Candidate ideas

- **Reference-point horse-race.** Estimate fixed vs. adaptive reference standards (and broad vs. narrow bracketing) structurally from a multi-period wage-profile design, extending [[@Sliwka2017|Sliwka & Werner (2017)]] and [[@Kube2013|Kube et al. (2013)]] — the standard nobody has pinned down.
- **De-biasing employers.** Test whether giving principals counterfactual feedback (or the [[@Armouti-Hansen2024|Armouti-Hansen et al. (2024)]] mission frame) durably corrects the underestimation of the effort–wage slope under repeated rematching.
- **Channel vs. elasticity.** Disentangle why gifts move extra work but not productivity ([[@DellaVigna2022|DellaVigna et al. 2022]]) by adding an explicit reciprocation "request" arm to a [[@DellaVigna2018|DellaVigna & Pope (2018)]]-style menu.
- **Intention vs. incompetence.** A moonlighting/gift-exchange design ([[@Falk2008|Falk et al. 2008]]) varying whether a bad outcome reflects laziness vs. genuine incapacity, eliciting beliefs to test [[@Dufwenberg2004|Dufwenberg & Kirchsteiger (2004)]] vs. [[@Falk2006|Falk & Fischbacher (2006)]] directly.
- **Lab-to-field transfer.** Replicate [[@Esteves-Sorenson2018|Esteves-Sorenson (2018)]] but vary observability continuously to map where lab-measured prosociality stops predicting field effort.
- **Reciprocity vs. ambiguity in one-shot play.** Pre-elicit ambiguity attitudes, then run gift-exchange/trust games to adjudicate the [[@Goeree2001|Goeree & Holt (2001)]] / [[@Eichberger2011|Eichberger & Kelsey (2011)]] introspection account against reciprocity at the individual level.

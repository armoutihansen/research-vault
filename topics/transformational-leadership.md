## Scope

This topic covers the transformational/transactional leadership paradigm — Bass's "Full Range of Leadership" model (idealized influence/charisma, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, individualized consideration vs. contingent reward and management-by-exception) — and its claimed effects on follower and organizational outcomes. The members are bound by the *augmentation hypothesis*: that transformational behaviors add explanatory power $\Delta R^2$ over and above transactional and older "structure–consideration" measures. The boundary is the Multifactor Leadership Questionnaire (MLQ) measurement tradition and its incremental-validity tests; it stops short of pure charismatic-leadership theory, leader–member exchange, and the motivation/contracting models that sit in neighboring topics.

## Sub-themes

- **The augmentation hypothesis and factor structure.** The conceptual anchor [[@Bass1999|Bass (1999)]] synthesizes two decades of MLQ research, fixing the vocabulary and defending augmentation, while leaving the three- vs. six-factor structure unsettled. [[@Seltzer1990|Seltzer & Bass (1990)]] supplies the sharpest direct test, showing transformational scales augment the Ohio State initiation/consideration model, not just transactional leadership.
- **Incremental validity for firm-level performance.** [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]] carries the augmentation claim from follower ratings up to *organizational* performance in Russian firms, with a behavior-only MLQ Form 10.
- **Group-level mediating mechanisms.** [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]] (empowerment, cohesiveness, collective-efficacy) and [[@LeBrasseur2002|LeBrasseur et al. (2002)]] (organizational learning, strategy/structure alignment in CQI adoption) open the black box, asking *how* transformational leadership translates into effectiveness rather than *whether* it correlates.
- **Levels of analysis.** [[@Seltzer1990|Seltzer & Bass (1990)]] (criss-cross, leader–individual vs. leader–group) and [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]] (aggregation to intact teams) both wrestle with whether the construct operates at the individual or the group level.

## Cross-paper tensions

The deepest tension is **whether transformational leadership has incremental explanatory content at all, or whether the reported $\Delta R^2$ is an artifact of common-method variance.** [[@Bass1999|Bass (1999)]] treats augmentation as established theory; but every empirical member is candid that same-source bias inflates it. [[@Seltzer1990|Seltzer & Bass (1990)]] confronts this head-on with the criss-cross design (one subordinate rates the leader, a *different* one rates outcomes) — and augmentation survives for effectiveness ($\Delta R^2=.06$) and satisfaction ($\Delta R^2=.08$) but **collapses for extra effort** ($\Delta R^2=.00$, n.s.). [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]] and [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]] attack the same threat via temporal/source separation and A/B subgroup splits, yet [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]]'s significant PLS paths sit on $R^2$ values of roughly $.03$–$.05$ — statistically present, substantively near-zero. So the effect sizes diverge wildly: $R=.85$ in [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]] vs. trivial variance in [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]], with method (single-firm-self-report vs. multi-source) plausibly driving the gap rather than the construct.

A second tension is the **sign and value of intellectual stimulation and management-by-exception.** [[@Seltzer1990|Seltzer & Bass (1990)]] find intellectual stimulation turns *negative* on satisfaction once charisma is partialled out ($-.26$, $p<.05$); [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]] find it flips sign by context — positive under high support for innovation, negative under low. Worse for the paradigm's activity-effectiveness ordering, [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]] reports *both* active and passive management-by-exception predicting performance *positively*, directly contradicting [[@Bass1999|Bass (1999)]]'s claim that management-by-exception (especially passive) is near the bottom of the hierarchy. The construct's nomological net thus does not hold fixed across cultures.

A third tension concerns **level of analysis.** [[@Seltzer1990|Seltzer & Bass (1990)]] find charisma operates as a leader–*individual* effect that vanishes under group aggregation — contradicting the standard view that charisma draws "the attention and support of many." [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]] presume a clean group-level construct, yet their null cohesiveness→collective-efficacy path suggests the group-level model is incompletely specified.

## Open questions

- **The mechanism deficit.** [[@Bass1999|Bass (1999)]] flags that "relatively little basic research" explains *how* transformational leadership works (the compliance → identification → internalization path). [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]] and [[@LeBrasseur2002|LeBrasseur et al. (2002)]] begin to fill this, but with perceptual, cross-sectional data that cannot identify causal mediation.
- **Hard outcomes.** Every empirical member relies on perceptual, self/other-reported performance. [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]], [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]], and [[@LeBrasseur2002|LeBrasseur et al. (2002)]] all call for objective financial/clinical metrics; none has them.
- **Extra effort at the group level.** [[@Seltzer1990|Seltzer & Bass (1990)]] leave the dyadic-but-not-group nature of effort as an explicit "fertile topic."
- **Factor structure and multicollinearity.** [[@Bass1999|Bass (1999)]]: whether three (or one) factors suffice "remains an open question."
- **Cultural contingency.** The Russian management-by-exception reversal [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]] and the all-Korean, 93%-male sample [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]] both leave the universality claim of [[@Bass1999|Bass (1999)]] untested against gender-balanced, individualist benchmarks.
- **Pseudotransformational vs. authentic leadership.** [[@Bass1999|Bass (1999)]] notes the ethical boundary separating genuine from self-aggrandizing leaders is barely studied.

## Candidate ideas

- **An experimental causal test of augmentation.** Use a confederate-leader lab design (the Sosik–Avolio template [[@Bass1999|Bass (1999)]] cites) delivering standardized transformational vs. transactional scripts, with *behavioral* effort and incentivized real-effort output as outcomes, to recover $\Delta R^2$ free of common-method variance — directly resolving the [[@Seltzer1990|Seltzer & Bass (1990)]] extra-effort null.
- **Meta-analytic decomposition of method vs. construct.** Code MLQ studies by source-separation design and estimate how much of the $R=.85$ [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]] vs. $R^2\approx.04$ [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]] gap is explained by single-source bias rather than substantive effect size.
- **Context-moderated sign of intellectual stimulation.** Model the satisfaction-reducing component [[@Seltzer1990|Seltzer & Bass (1990)]] and the support-for-innovation flip [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]] jointly, testing whether intellectual stimulation is uniformly positive only above a measurable contextual threshold.
- **Re-examining the management-by-exception anomaly.** Replicate [[@Elenkov2002|Elenkov (2002)]]'s positive active/passive MBE result in another high-power-distance transition economy to test the "authoritarian legacy" conjecture against [[@Bass1999|Bass (1999)]]'s activity-effectiveness ordering.
- **Identified mediation of the empowerment→efficacy→performance chain.** Combine the [[@Jung2002|Jung & Sosik (2002)]] nomological model with a panel/longitudinal design and objective team output to identify whether collective-efficacy genuinely mediates, addressing the [[@LeBrasseur2002|LeBrasseur et al. (2002)]] organizational-learning longevity gap.

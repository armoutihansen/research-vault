## Scope

This topic covers why people act prosocially *when observed*: the signaling logic by which a visible good deed conveys a favorable type to an audience (or to oneself), and the resulting fragility of that supply to monetary or "prosocial" incentives. The unifying engine is the multidimensional signal-extraction model of [[@Benabou2006|Bénabou & Tirole (2006)]]: an observed action $a$ reveals the *sum* of intrinsic, extrinsic, and reputational marginal motives, so rewards muddy the inference and can crowd out the very behavior they target. The boundary is reputational/image-based prosociality and its incentive design — not pure distributional preferences (see *inequity aversion*), not intention-based reciprocity for its own sake (see *reciprocity & gift exchange*), and not identity/conformity as such (see *identity, social norms & conformity*), though all three border it closely.

## Sub-themes

- **Foundations: signaling and motivated belief.** The signal-extraction model and its self-signaling twin — [[@Benabou2006|Bénabou & Tirole (2006)]], its survey/CSR companion [[@Benabou2010|Bénabou & Tirole (2010)]], and the much earlier belief-choice template of [[@Akerlof1982|Akerlof & Dickens (1982)]], which first put preferences over one's own beliefs into a competitive equilibrium.
- **Visibility as the on/off switch (lab + field tests).** Using observability $x$ to turn image on or off — [[@Ariely2009|Ariely, Bracha & Meier (2009)]], the German replication-plus-extension [[@Kramer|Krämer (2023)]], and [[@Zafar2011|Zafar (2011)]] on conformity-driven image effects.
- **Structural field decomposition of "why give / why vote".** Pricing out the social motive with experimental inducements — [[@DellaVigna2012|DellaVigna, List & Malmendier (2012)]] (social pressure in door-to-door giving) and [[@Dellavigna2017|DellaVigna, List, Malmendier & Rao (2017)]] (voting "to tell others").
- **Prosocial incentives in firms and markets.** The instrumental-use backfire of charitable incentives — [[@Cassar2021|Cassar & Meier (2021)]] — and the equilibrium market behavior of socially responsible consumers — [[@Kaufmann2024|Kaufmann, Andre & Kőszegi (2024)]].

## Cross-paper tensions

**What kills the signal — dilution vs. pressure vs. perceived intent?** The members agree visibility matters but disagree on the mechanism, and the disagreement is testable. For [[@Benabou2006|Bénabou & Tirole (2006)]] and [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]] the channel is *signal dilution*: money raises the noise-to-signal ratio $\theta=\sigma_y/\sigma_a$, so $\rho(y)$ falls and good deeds are reattributed to greed. For [[@DellaVigna2012|DellaVigna et al. (2012)]] the operative force is *social pressure* — a cost $S\approx\$3.75$ of saying no to a face-to-face ask, with most giving being welfare-*reducing* avoidance rather than image-seeking; their flyer-sorting design explicitly argues against (but cannot fully exclude) the Bénabou–Tirole self/other-signaling reading. For [[@Cassar2021|Cassar & Meier (2021)]] the channel is *perceived employer intent*: a performance-conditioned donation reads as instrumental and hence unkind ($k^p$ falls), depressing reciprocal effort — a reciprocity story, not a signaling one. Three models, three different objects ($\theta$, $S$, $k^p$), all consistent with "incentives backfire under visibility."

**Does image always raise prosocial supply?** [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]] find public > private effort for a "good" cause — the canonical image boost. [[@Zafar2011|Zafar (2011)]] flatly contradicts the optimistic reading: once a *low*-contribution descriptive norm sets in, social comparison and image concern push giving *down*, and subjects respond only to friends. [[@Dellavigna2017|DellaVigna et al. (2017)]] sharpen this asymmetry structurally — they estimate strong *stigma* from being seen as a non-voter ($\mu_N\approx-\$11$) but essentially *no pride* in voting ($\mu_V\approx-\$4$). So the sign of the image term is endogenous to the norm and to whether honor or stigma dominates — exactly the strategic complement/substitute knife-edge of [[@Benabou2006|Bénabou & Tirole (2006)]]'s Propositions 6–7, but the empirical papers leave $\Delta'(v_a)$ unmeasured.

**Self-signaling vs. social-signaling — an identification gap nobody closes.** [[@Benabou2006|Bénabou & Tirole (2006)]] note their model is *formally identical* for self- and other-image (imperfect recall), and [[@Akerlof1982|Akerlof & Dickens (1982)]] is purely about beliefs one holds about oneself. Yet every empirical member identifies image off *observability by others* — [[@Zafar2011|Zafar (2011)]] concedes self-signaling "cannot be separated" from social signaling, and [[@Kramer|Krämer (2023)]] holds self-image constant by construction. The visibility lever cannot, in principle, isolate the self-image channel that the foundational theory says is half the story.

**Is the responsible agent even a price/behavior taker?** [[@Kaufmann2024|Kaufmann et al. (2024)]] break a hidden assumption shared by the rest: that one agent's prosocial choice maps one-to-one onto the outcome. In a market, cutting one's demand lowers price and induces others to buy more, dampening own impact to $q_c\in(0,1)$ — so even a fully internalizing consumer ($k=K$) overconsumes. This reframes "image" as possibly chasing an impact that equilibrium erases, a tension the individual-decision members (who hold the audience and the cause fixed) never confront.

## Open questions

- **Monetary vs. non-monetary rewards.** [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]] explicitly leave open whether non-cash perks (carpool-lane access, medals) dilute the signal as much as cash; [[@Kramer|Krämer (2023)]] and the symbolic-reward field evidence suggest not.
- **A worked equilibrium / signaling model of prosocial incentives.** [[@Cassar2021|Cassar & Meier (2021)]] explicitly flag that their backfire is a one-shot, off-equilibrium result and call for a signaling or intention-based equilibrium model and a repeated-relationship test.
- **Aversion to appearing image-motivated.** [[@Kramer|Krämer (2023)]] adds a second-order motive — not wanting to look image-driven — but it is theory-plus-design with no data; the WTA-for-disclosure instrument is untested.
- **Generalizing the "pricing-out" estimates.** [[@Dellavigna2017|DellaVigna et al. (2017)]] caution their no-pride result may be election-specific; [[@DellaVigna2012|DellaVigna et al. (2012)]] identify only *marginal* (not infra-marginal) giving and lean on strong functional forms.
- **Can beliefs about impact be moved?** [[@Kaufmann2024|Kaufmann et al. (2024)]] leave open whether the 54% who naively expect one-to-one impact can be shifted by framing — and whether doing so helps or (in multi-good markets) hurts.

## Candidate ideas

1. **Horse-race the three backfire channels in one design.** Cross visibility (image), an opt-out ask (pressure), and performance-conditioning (intent) in a single field experiment to estimate $\theta$, $S$, and $k^p$ jointly — adjudicating [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]] vs. [[@DellaVigna2012|DellaVigna et al. (2012)]] vs. [[@Cassar2021|Cassar & Meier (2021)]].
2. **Identify self- vs. social-image by varying memory/recall, not just observability** — operationalize the imperfect-recall equivalence in [[@Benabou2006|Bénabou & Tirole (2006)]] and [[@Akerlof1982|Akerlof & Dickens (1982)]] to break the gap [[@Zafar2011|Zafar (2011)]] concedes.
3. **Endogenize the sign of image: a norm-elicitation experiment** measuring $\Delta'(v_a)$ (honor vs. stigma) to predict when publicity raises vs. lowers giving — reconciling [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]] with [[@Zafar2011|Zafar (2011)]] and [[@Dellavigna2017|DellaVigna et al. (2017)]].
4. **Build and test the equilibrium signaling model of prosocial incentives** that [[@Cassar2021|Cassar & Meier (2021)]] call for, embedding perceived intent $k^p$ in a repeated employment relationship.
5. **Image motivation under demand dampening** — embed [[@Ariely2009|Ariely et al. (2009)]]-style visibility into [[@Kaufmann2024|Kaufmann et al. (2024)]]'s market, asking whether responsible consumers signal harder when their real impact $q_c$ shrinks.
6. **Field-test the "appearing image-motivated" aversion** with [[@Kramer|Krämer (2023)]]'s BDM willingness-to-accept-disclosure instrument, calibrating it against the [[@Dellavigna2017|DellaVigna et al. (2017)]] lying-cost benchmark.

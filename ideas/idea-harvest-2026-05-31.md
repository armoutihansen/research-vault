### How complete and restrictive are social-preference contract models? (#24) — composite 3.5
- **Pitch:** Behavioral contract theory has rival primitives — distributional inequity aversion (Itoh; Englmaier-Wambach), intention-based reciprocity (Englmaier-Leider), agent overconfidence (de la Rosa) — that generate observationally-similar flat wages but opposite comparative statics, a gap Koszegi (2014) leaves open. Deliver a **restrictiveness-first** yardstick: using Fudenberg-Gao-Liang simulation alone (no new data), measure how much arbitrary wage-schedule behavior each primitive rules out over random permissible cost/noise/effort environments, and test whether the families separate on restrictiveness even where they pool on realized wages. Then, as a second leg, score predictive completeness of each primitive against an ML frontier on Fehr-Klein-Schmidt-style data.
- **Source topics:** social-preferences-contract-theory, ml-evaluating-economic-theories, reciprocity-gift-exchange, inequity-aversion-distributional-preferences
- **Key papers:** Fehr2007, Englmaier2010, Englmaier2012, Itoh2004, delaRosa2011, Koszegi2014, Fudenberg2020, Mullainathan2023
- **Scores:** novelty 4 / feasibility 3 / fit 5 / grounding 5 / **composite 3.5**
- **Closest prior work:** No one applies the completeness/restrictiveness/anomaly toolkit to behavioral contract theory — genuinely unoccupied. Adjacent: Fudenberg-Gao-Liang restrictiveness (choice-under-risk, not contracts); a 2026 arXiv "Model Restrictiveness in Functional and Structural Settings" (verify it doesn't pre-empt the wage-schedule method); Jesper's own pred_comp_soc_pref (same method, non-contract data); Fehr-Klein-Schmidt fit inequity aversion to averages but never against reciprocity/overconfidence.
- **First step:** START WITH THE RESTRICTIVENESS LEG — computable today, zero new data. Code the optimal-contract solver for each primitive (the Itoh/Englmaier-Wambach and Englmaier-Leider Grossman-Hart programs are fully specified; de la Rosa is closed-form), define a conceivable set of permissible cost/noise/outside-option environments, draw M random environments, compute each model's f-discrepancy, report restrictiveness r. Only then attempt completeness on Fehr-Klein-Schmidt data (request from authors); defer the within-subject-sequencing + principal-wealth experiment to paper two.
- **Risks:** Completeness leg needs data that may be too sparse (~200 offers) for an estimable ML frontier — fall back to relative completeness. Treat Mullainathan-Rambachan anomaly generation as an explicit stretch goal. Identification of the discriminating case rests on the Netzer-Schmutzler wealth-asymmetry prediction ("a selfish principal's wage is never kind").

### A predictive-complexity index for procedural rationality (#45) — composite 3.5
- **Pitch:** KRS's minimal-rationale count is NP-hard or behaviorally uninformative; the field asks for a measure penalizing the *descriptive complexity* of the menu-to-rationale map, not raw count. Propose that the structure-penalizing measure is a **restrictiveness** measure applied at the level of the procedure-as-model-class, paired with a deterministic-choice completeness. The central contribution is a THEOREM (or clean counterexample) relating restrictiveness to KRS-descriptive-complexity on the second-best vs. median benchmarks — that divergence result *is* the paper, whether the answer is "they agree" or "no, here's the third (MDL/Kolmogorov) object you need."
- **Source topics:** sequential-rationalizability-shortlists, ml-evaluating-economic-theories, revealed-preference-foundations
- **Key papers:** Fudenberg2020, Cherepanov2013, Dutta2015, Manzini2018, Mullainathan2023
- **Scores:** novelty 4 / feasibility 3 / fit 5 / grounding 5 / **composite 3.5**
- **Closest prior work:** KRS (2002) defines r(c) and explicitly calls for structured penalties; Apesteguia-Ballester make the count constructive and prove NP-hardness. Fudenberg-Gao-Liang restrictiveness + Fudenberg-Liang completeness supply the borrowed machinery. No paper builds a restrictiveness-/completeness-graded complexity index for deterministic multi-rationale choice — genuinely unbuilt, and listed as an open question in the vault's own topic note.
- **First step:** Theory-first, ~2 weeks, go/no-go: define a procedural family as a parametric class generating deterministic choice functions on small finite X (N≤6), compute restrictiveness by brute-force enumeration (NP-hardness doesn't bite at tiny |2^X|), then compute the restrictiveness-index for second-best vs. median (whose r(c) KRS computed analytically). If restrictiveness reproduces the intuitive ordering, the thesis holds; if not, you have a sharp counterexample paper — compute the MDL/Kolmogorov complexity of the partition and compare all three.
- **Risks:** The big conceptual one: "complexity = restrictiveness" is a contestable thesis, not a definition (the paper lives or dies defending it). Unit-of-analysis mismatch (individual choice functions vs. model class). Completeness on deterministic single-valued choice degenerates (lookup-table ceiling collapses). The empirical arm is the weakest — "real cyclic-choice data" is hand-waved.
